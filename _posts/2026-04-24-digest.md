## Themes

**Shared representations can homogenize or specialize.** When agents share training data, they converge—"When Agents Look the Same" shows Kimi-K2 mirroring Claude's optional tool-use patterns more closely than Claude's own sibling model. When they share structured memory instead, the effect flips: MALMAS's agents each specialize across rounds because the memory prevents redundant exploration. Same mechanism—sharing—cuts both ways depending on what flows through it.

**The communication medium is a learnable bottleneck.** DiffMAS replaces English with continuous KV-cache traces and trains end-to-end, gaining +26.7% on AIME24. Explicit Trait Inference replaces implicit partner-modeling with structured warmth–competence profiles and cuts payoff losses by up to 77%. Both papers ask the same question—are your agents communicating in the right substrate?—and both answer no.

**More compute has a specific failure mode per lever.** The scaling survey names four: overthinking for step scaling, premature consensus for round scaling, lost-in-the-middle for input scaling, and hard ceilings for model optimization. DiffMAS's finding that 10 latent steps beat 200 is a live instance of the overthinking pattern. The three-round cap on multi-agent debate that the survey cites is a live instance of premature consensus. The levers are real, and so are the ceilings.

**Explicit structure beats implicit hope.** Explicit Trait Inference writes down partner traits instead of hoping agents infer them. MALMAS writes down what worked and why instead of restarting each round. The similarity paper separates mandatory from optional actions instead of lumping them. DiffMAS makes the gradient path explicit instead of letting it die at the token boundary. Every paper today arrives at the same move—write it down, structure it, make it optimizable.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag, one shared problem: multi-agent LLM systems are bottlenecked by things we assumed were neutral. The communication medium. The memory architecture. The similarity metric. The scaling dimension.

Two of today's papers show that natural language between agents is costing us. DiffMAS proves it with gradients—if you let the medium be learned, the agents discover protocols no human would design. Explicit Trait Inference proves it with payoffs—if you give agents structured partner models, they stop fumbling through ambiguity. English is not the worst communication protocol, but it is unoptimizable, and two independent teams just showed you what happens when you replace it with something that isn't.

The scaling survey is the quiet backbone of the day. It gives you the vocabulary for failure—overthinking, premature consensus, lost-in-the-middle, hard ceilings—and then DiffMAS and MALMAS walk in and demonstrate two of those failures live. More rounds of debate don't help after three. More latent steps don't help after ten. The survey didn't predict these specific numbers, but it gave you the frame to expect them.

The similarity paper is the one that should make you uncomfortable. If Kimi-K2 is functionally a painted-over Claude, then every "diverse ensemble" built on top of it is diversity theater. The mandatory/optional split is the diagnostic. Run it on your own pipeline before you trust the variance you think you have.

What ties the batch together is a pattern: each paper takes something the field was treating as fixed—language, memory, similarity, scale—and makes it a design variable. The gains come not from more compute but from better structure. MALMAS's mean rank of 1.12 doesn't come from a bigger model. DiffMAS's +26.7% doesn't come from more parameters. ETI's 77% payoff reduction doesn't come from fine-tuning. The structure was the lever all along.
