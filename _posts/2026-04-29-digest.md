## Themes

**Coordination overhead is now a measured pathology.** For months we've intuited that throwing more agents at a problem doesn't make them smarter. Today's batch replaces that intuition with hard numbers. The MoltBook study puts 90,704 agents on a Reddit-style platform and watches 93.5% go silent while group work underperforms solo work by nearly a full standard deviation (Cohen's d = −0.88). The multi-principal paper shows conflict slicing execution accuracy from 0.86 to 0.62. These aren't anecdotes—they're baselines future systems have to beat.

**Apparent consensus hides dangerous disagreement.** Two of today's papers expose the same fault line from different angles. The Inter-LLM Communication study finds that five models agree on about half the relevant APIs but collapse to 33% overlap on which ones matter most—agreement on *what*, divergence on *what first*. The multi-principal paper shows a similar pattern: agents that appear to serve two users simultaneously are quietly leaking private information across turns. What looks like alignment is often latent misalignment, and single-agent evaluation can't see it.

**The right architecture matters more than the right number of agents.** The cyclic-subtask-graph paper demonstrates that full connectivity—one of the most flexible topologies you can build—helps on exploration-heavy ALFWorld tasks but actively hurts on sequential TextCraft chains. Adding edges to a workflow graph is not free; it costs tokens, router calls, and sometimes correctness. SSRP makes the same point from the other direction: a two-role Architect/Executive split, deliberately *constraining* the Executive's access to history, lifts success from 0.1% to 71.6%. Less connectivity, more structure.

**Attention lock-in, not capability, is the ceiling.** The SSRP paper pins the 0.1% collapse on attention weighting, not reasoning. Reflexion—the same model with post-hoc corrective feedback—scores 100% on the same multi-hop task. The model can reason. It just can't attend past stale priors buried in long context. The multi-principal paper echoes this from a different angle: privacy scores degrade gradually across turns, not in a single catastrophic jump. The Transformer's attention bottleneck is the common villain across both failures.

## Editorial

Every paper in today's batch is asking some version of the same question: what breaks when you stop evaluating LLMs one at a time?

The answer, across five different experimental setups, is remarkably consistent. More agents making more connections does not produce more intelligence. It produces more overhead, more disagreement hidden behind surface-level agreement, and more places for attention to latch onto the wrong signal.

What works instead is deliberate constraint. SSRP achieves its 715× resilience lift by *removing* the Executive's access to conversation history and replacing it with a compressed protocol. The cyclic-graph paper finds that simpler, benchmark-generic graphs outperform instance-specific ones under adversarial pressure. The MoltBook paper's most striking number—93.5% of agents doing nothing—is what happens when you give agents freedom without structure.

The genuinely new insight this week is the quantification of the gap between *retrieval agreement* and *ranking agreement* in the Inter-LLM Communication paper. Models agree on what's relevant but not on what matters most. That's a failure mode that only appears at the system level—you'd never see it testing a single model—and it's exactly the failure mode that will bite hardest in deployed multi-agent pipelines where agents route tasks by priority.

What warrants a closer second look: the SSRP paper's Grounding Paradox—98.8% scaffold adherence but only 71.6% task success. The protocol is followed; the attention trough still swallows signal. That 27.2% gap is where the next real advance lives.

The architectures that survive multi-agent deployment won't be the most connected ones.
