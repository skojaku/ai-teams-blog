*TL;DR: DiffMAS makes inter-agent communication a learnable, differentiable channel by threading KV caches through a shared latent trace — and a single epoch of SFT on 210 math samples rockets Qwen3-8B from 50% to 76.7% on AIME24.*

## The Bottleneck No One Optimized

Ask a team of LLM agents to solve a hard math problem together and they'll pass notes — plain-text notes.

Agent 1 writes a paragraph. Agent 2 reads it, re-encodes it, writes another paragraph. Every handoff crushes rich internal representations into discrete tokens and slams the gradient door shut.

The system can learn to reason. It cannot learn to *talk*.

DiffMAS asks a deceptively simple question: what if the channel itself were on the gradient path?

## From Text Pipes to Latent Traces

The core idea: replace the text pipe with a shared KV-cache trace that agents append to, never overwrite.

Think of it as a whiteboard that accumulates continuously.

Agent 1 writes its KV blocks into slots 1–T. Agent 2 reads those blocks, then writes its own into slots T+1–2T, and so on.

The final agent reads the whole board and decodes an answer.

Because nothing is erased, every upstream agent's contribution remains explicit and addressable.

![Architecture diagram showing the two-stage DiffMAS pipeline: upstream agents sequentially prefill and append KV blocks to a shared latent trace, then the final agent decodes autoregressively with SFT gradients backpropagating through the accumulated trace]({{ "/assets/figures/cf368958f102/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

This is not just an engineering trick. The concatenative design has a structural consequence spelled out in Proposition 3.1.

In an overwriting scheme where each agent compresses prior state into a fixed-dimensional carrier *h*, gradients decay geometrically: the signal reaching agent *j* shrinks by a factor $\rho^{K-j}$.

DiffMAS's concatenative trace introduces **no depth-dependent multiplicative factor** at all:

$$\left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Z^{(j)}_{1:T}}\right\|_2 \leq \left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Z_{1:N_K}}\right\|_2$$

Every upstream agent receives comparably accessible gradient signal, no matter how deep the pipeline.

## Two-Stage Training

DiffMAS trains in two stages, both running on the same forward pass.

**Stage I — Build the trace.** Agents 1 through K−1 sequentially prefill and append KV segments to the shared trace. No gradients flow here. The trace just accumulates.

**Stage II — Decode and backprop.** The final agent performs autoregressive decoding conditioned on the entire KV trace. Cross-entropy SFT loss backpropagates through the accumulated trace, updating only the final agent's LoRA parameters.

Thanks to the non-overwriting interface, those gradients reach all the way back through every upstream agent's KV contribution — even though upstream weights stay frozen.

The whole thing is standard supervised fine-tuning. No RL, no separate communication module, no distribution alignment step. Just SFT over a multi-agent trajectory.

## What the Numbers Say

On Qwen3-8B:

- AIME24: 50.0% → **76.7%** (+26.7 points)
- GPQA-Diamond: 39.9% → **60.1%** (+20.2 points)
- HumanEval+: 74.5% → **81.5%** (+7.0 points)

![Bar chart comparing accuracy across Single, TextMAS, LatentMAS, C2C, and DiffMAS on multiple benchmarks, with DiffMAS consistently highest and C2C collapsing to 0% on AIME tasks]({{ "/assets/figures/cf368958f102/fig_007_02.png" | relative_url }})

These gains hold across model scales from 4B to 32B, and across math, science, code, and commonsense benchmarks.

Meanwhile, Cache-to-Cache (C2C) — the closest latent-communication prior, which shares KV caches through learned fusion modules trained on instruction-following data — collapses to **0%** on AIME24 for both Qwen3-4B and Qwen3-8B.

Instruction-following is not mathematical reasoning. The distribution mismatch kills it.

Only **210 math samples** for one epoch are enough. Code tasks need just 50 samples for 10 epochs. This is parameter-efficient SFT, not a data-hungry regime.

### The Sweet Spot Is Narrow

Ten latent communication steps push Qwen3-8B from 50.0% to 76.7% on AIME24.

But stretching to 40 steps drops accuracy to 63.3%. Two hundred steps sits at 63.3% too.

The trace needs just enough room for a compact conversation — more steps just add noise.

### Stability, Not Luck

Self-consistency sampling (4 independent runs per problem) shows DiffMAS shifting the distribution toward reliably correct outcomes: most problems land at 3–4/4 correct.

![Distribution plot comparing self-consistency across methods, with DiffMAS skewed toward high-consistency outcomes (3–4/4 correct) while LatentMAS and TextMAS show polarized distributions]({{ "/assets/figures/cf368958f102/fig_008_03.png" | relative_url }})

LatentMAS and TextMAS produce polarized results — either all wrong or all right, with little in between.

On Qwen3-4B, DiffMAS achieves mean perplexity 1.24 vs. LatentMAS's 1.31, with a tighter distribution and far fewer outlier spikes.

The gradients aren't just flowing; they're making the model's decoding meaningfully more stable.

## The Lineage

Sukhbaatar et al. (2016) proved that differentiable communication channels between neural agents can be learned end-to-end — but at the scale of toy games, not language models.

MALT (Motwani et al., 2025) trained multi-agent LLM teams with RL for collaborative reasoning, yet kept the communication channel as discrete text.

Cache-to-Cache (Fu et al., 2025) moved closest to DiffMAS by sharing KV caches between agents, but trained its fusion modules on instruction-following data and never jointly optimized the communication with the reasoning task.

DiffMAS closes the loop: latent trace + end-to-end SFT over full multi-agent trajectories.

## The Honest Limits

The LoRA update only touches the final agent. Upstream agents receive no direct gradient signal — they benefit only through the trace they helped build.

That means very deep pipelines may hit diminishing returns, and scaling to chains of 20 or 50 agents is an open question.

The adapters are task-specific. Each benchmark gets its own LoRA, trained on a small in-domain set.

Out-of-distribution generalization is untested; you need to retrain for each new domain.

And the 10-step sweet spot suggests the learned communication is compact but fragile. We don't yet know whether the protocol generalizes across task types or whether it degrades gracefully under distribution shift.

## Why It Matters

DiffMAS shows that the communication channel in a multi-agent system is not plumbing — it's a learnable component, and optimizing it unlocks gains that no amount of prompt engineering or RL on the agents alone can reach.

The concatenative-trace insight gives a clean theoretical reason: when you stop overwriting, gradients stop decaying.

Multi-agent LLM systems have been running with the handbrake on. This paper releases it.
