*TL;DR: Yee and Koh introduce three standardized benchmark tasks for measuring emergent coordination in large LLM agent populations, and their headline finding is brutal—decentralized agent groups produce worse results than a single agent working alone (Cohen's d = −0.88).*

## 90,000 Agents Walked Into a Reddit Clone

Ninety thousand LLM agents spent three weeks posting, commenting, and voting on MoltBook—a Reddit-style platform where only AI agents can participate.

Almost all of them became lurkers.

And when groups of agents tried to solve technical problems together, they did worse than a single agent working by itself.

Not a little worse. Much worse.

This paper doesn't propose a new multi-agent architecture. It proposes something the field needs more urgently: a way to measure.

## The Measurement Gap

Multi-agent LLM systems are scaling fast. But evaluation still targets single agents or small, hand-structured groups of two to ten agents with predefined roles.

Park et al.'s Generative Agents showed that small simulated populations produce emergent routines, but offered no standardized evaluation at scale. Wu et al.'s AutoGen framework structured small-group coordination well, but can't capture the self-organization that arises when thousands of agents interact asynchronously with no script.

Without shared benchmarks, every multi-agent paper measures coordination differently. Progress becomes impossible to track.

Yee and Koh make evaluation itself the contribution. Three reproducible benchmark tasks turn "how do LLM agent swarms behave?" from an anecdotal question into a measurement problem.

## Three Tasks, One Framework

The framework runs on the MoltBook Observatory Archive—2.73 million interactions among 90,704 autonomous agents over a three-week window (January 28–February 20, 2026).

Agents operate via OpenClaw, connecting to diverse LLM providers like Claude, GPT-4, and open-source models. Model identity isn't recorded in the dataset—a constraint the authors flag honestly.

Each task targets a different dimension of emergent coordination.

### Task 1: Who Does What?

Build a directed, weighted interaction network from all agent-to-agent replies and mentions. Extract five structural features per agent—in-degree, out-degree, betweenness centrality, clustering coefficient, and PageRank. Cluster agents by these features using k-means.

The result: k = 6 clusters, silhouette score 0.914.

That clean score masks a brutal distribution. Cluster 0—the "low-activity periphery"—holds 93.5% of all agents, 84,822 out of 90,704.

At the other extreme, a single outlier agent sits alone in Cluster 2.

Just 11 agents qualify as "super-connectors."

Unconstrained agent populations default to extreme structural inequality.

Most agents watch. Very few shape the network.

### Task 2: How Does Information Spread?

Extract information cascades via n-gram matching across posts, then fit two models to the adoption dynamics.

First: logistic regression with linear and quadratic exposure terms.

The quadratic term comes out negative ($\beta_2 = -0.0074$, $p < 0.001$). Adoption probability rises with initial exposures, then plateaus.

Not complex contagion. Not simple contagion. Saturating contagion—diminishing returns from repeated contact with the same source.

Second: a Cox proportional hazards model.

Hazard ratio: 0.528. A unit increase in cumulative exposure count is associated with a 47% lower instantaneous adoption rate.

Repeated exposure to the same cascade suppresses, rather than reinforces, adoption.

Cascade sizes themselves follow a heavy-tailed power-law with exponent $\alpha = 2.57$ (95% CI: [2.54, 2.60]). Rare, massive propagation events dominate agent exposure—raising the risk that harmful content or adversarial strategies spread widely before detection.

![Log-log cascade size distribution following a power law with exponent α = 2.57, showing that rare propagation events dominate agent exposure]({{ "/assets/figures/475c88e447c8/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

### Task 3: Can They Collaborate?

Identify collaborative technical threads: at least 3 agents, at least 5 comments, technical keywords present, minimum 30-minute duration. Score each with a composite quality metric $Q \in [0,1]$ aggregating code presence (0.30), comment quality (0.30), test inclusion (0.20), and syntax validity (0.20). Compare against length-matched, topic-matched single-agent threads.

From 60,045 technical threads, only 164 qualified as collaborative events. Just 6.7% reached quality ≥ 0.5.

Then the hammer: collaborative outcomes were significantly worse than single-agent baselines.

Cohen's $d = -0.88$. $t = -11.21$, $p < 0.001$.

The coordination overhead of decentralized multi-agent work, right now, outweighs the benefit.

## A Critical Mass, Maybe

One crack of light in the data: successful collaborative events averaged 21.0 participants versus 9.4 for unsuccessful ones (Cohen's d = 1.18 for participant count). Duration also correlated positively (Cohen's d = 0.72).

There may be a critical-mass threshold below which collaboration is pure overhead and above which it starts to pay off. But with only 11 successful events out of 164, the authors can't nail that boundary down.

The signal is tantalizing, not conclusive.

Woolley et al. identified a "collective intelligence factor" in human groups—a measurable property predicting cooperative task performance. LLM agents, so far, haven't found theirs.

## The Honest Limits

The three-week window can't capture long-run processes like norm evolution or institutional consolidation.

Model identity is unrecorded. We can't tell whether behavioral heterogeneity comes from different architectures or from the platform dynamics themselves.

The n-gram cascade detection catches textual repetition but misses semantic adoption—an agent that absorbs an idea and restates it in different words is invisible to this pipeline.

The silhouette score in Task 1 conflates cluster separation with role diversity; a high score driven by one massive homogeneous cluster doesn't mean roles are richly differentiated.

The selection-bias confound in Task 3 is real: collaborative threads, by their duration and participation criteria, likely represent intrinsically harder problems. The −0.88 deficit must be interpreted with that confound in mind.

These aren't footnotes. They're the boundary conditions that make the framework useful as a scientific instrument rather than a scorecard.

## Negative Results as Infrastructure

The −0.88 deficit is the paper's most important number, not because it proves agents can't collaborate, but because it gives future protocols a concrete target to beat.

Before this paper, the question was vague: "do multi-agent systems coordinate well?" After, it's precise: "does your coordination protocol beat the −0.88 baseline on standardized tasks?"

That shift—from anecdote to measurement, from vibes to benchmarks—is the real contribution.

The framework is open-source. The three tasks are reproducible.

Any future agent interaction dataset can run the same pipelines.

The field now has a ruler. The numbers it measures so far are humbling.
