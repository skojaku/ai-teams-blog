*TL;DR: Groups of LLM agents that should be smarter together actually get dramatically worse—managing only 30% accuracy when critical facts are distributed across members, versus 81% for a single agent holding all the cards. The bottleneck isn't reasoning; it's that agents never explore what others might know.*

## The Flood and the Tunnel

Imagine a flood evacuation. Four AI agents each hold a fragment of the picture. One knows the river level is just below the bridge. Another knows the dam upstream is releasing water. A third knows a supply truck was heading into the tunnel. The fourth knows a massive fire blocks that same supply track.

All four share the same seven facts: the bridge exists, East Town has shelter, the mudslide blocks the hill trails. The shared evidence points away from North Hill. The hidden evidence, combined, points straight to it. Will the agents figure it out?

They won't. They converge on an incorrect answer before anyone mentions the dam, the fire, or the river level.

This is the paper's central finding: multi-agent LLMs fail not because they can't reason, but because they can't *explore*. They converge on what everyone already knows and never surface the critical facts held individually by other agents at the table.

## The Hidden Profile Paradox

The authors borrow a classic diagnostic from social psychology. In 1985, Stasser and Titus showed that human groups neglect unshared information—a failure they called the *Hidden Profile* problem. Everyone discusses what everyone already knows; the unique facts that would flip the decision stay buried.

Li, Naito, and Shirado ask the obvious next question: do LLM agents inherit this same pathology?

To answer it, they need a benchmark that cleanly separates *coordination failure* from *reasoning failure*.

Existing multi-agent evaluations don't do this—they conflate task skill with collective reasoning.

Jones and Steinhardt (2022) showed that single-LLM failures mirror human cognitive biases. Du et al. (2024) showed that multi-agent debate can improve factuality, but never isolated distributed information as a variable.

Nobody had built a controlled test where the *only* variable is whether agents can pool scattered facts.

## Building HIDDENBENCH

HIDDENBENCH is a 65-task benchmark where every task is a Hidden Profile. Each agent gets shared evidence (favoring a wrong answer) plus unique evidence that only it holds. Only by combining all the unshared pieces can the group reach the correct answer.

The construction pipeline is rigorous. GPT-4.1 generates 200 candidate tasks.

Each candidate must survive a two-threshold filter: $\geq 80\%$ accuracy when a single agent holds all facts (Full Profile), and $\leq 20\%$ when facts are distributed (Hidden Profile).

Just 57 of 200 pass—28.5% make the cut. Combined with 3 manually designed tasks and 5 adapted from prior human-group studies, the final benchmark is 65 scenarios.

This filtering is the whole point. Every task guarantees that the correct answer *can* be reached by reasoning alone, and that it *won't* be reached without coordinating across agents.

## The Gap

Fifteen frontier models across four families—GPT, Gemini, Qwen, Llama—all fail the same way.

Under distributed information, multi-agent accuracy is 30.1%. Under full information, a single agent hits 80.7%. The gap is not subtle.

$$Y_{\text{post}} - Y_{\text{full}} \in [-0.750,\, -0.310]$$

Even Gemini-2.5-Pro, the strongest collective reasoner, falls 31 percentage points short of its own full-information ceiling.

GPT-5 variants, despite stronger individual reasoning, fail to outperform smaller models like GPT-4.1-Mini in multi-agent settings. Scale does not save you.

## Why the Gap Persists

The authors run a battery of ablations to find out where the bottleneck sits.

More communication rounds don't help. GPT-4.1 peaks at 15 rounds ($Y_{\text{post}} = 0.233$) and *declines* at 20—extended discussion reinforces the wrong consensus.

Prompting strategies don't help. Cooperative, conflictual, chain-of-thought, even explicit instructions to "share all information" top out at 0.467. More than half the gap to Full Profile performance still remains.

Larger groups make it *worse*. Improvement from communication drops from $+0.348$ at $N=3$ to $+0.006$ at $N=7$. Adding agents amplifies the shared-information bias, not the collective intelligence.

Structured dissent is a Goldilocks problem. One adversarial agent helps GPT-4.1 ($0.233 \to 0.492$), but too many destroy convergence.

The optimal dissent level varies by model—there is no universal recipe.

## The Bottleneck, Isolated

The decisive intervention is brutally simple: *force every agent to disclose all its facts in round one*.

$$\text{GPT-4.1: } 3.7\% \to 92.6\%$$
$$\text{Gemini-2.5-Flash: } 17.3\% \to 98.2\%$$

Nearly Full Profile performance. The agents could always integrate information once they had it—the failure was in *surfacing* it, not reasoning over it.

Passive summarization (a "secretary" agent) helps Gemini substantially but barely moves GPT-4.1, confirming that the bottleneck is active exploration, not aggregation.

Top-performing models signal asymmetry unprompted: "Your point about the dam is new to me."

Lower-performing models converge immediately: "I agree—let's choose East Town."

![Effect of interventions on model accuracy: baseline, Reveal-All, and structured communication protocols showing that forcing disclosure nearly eliminates the performance gap]({{ "/assets/figures/72061bc48b55/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

## A Lightweight Fix

The authors test a structured protocol called Exchange-then-Decide. Two rounds of sharing facts plus challenging the front-runner, then one round of summary and vote.

No disclosure of who knows what—just a scaffold for exploration.

| Model | Baseline | Structured |
|---|---|---|
| GPT-4.1 | 3.7% | 80.0% |
| Gemini-2.5-Flash | 17.3% | 72.7% |
| Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite | 4.3% | 74.3% |

A 3-round protocol that doesn't even know which facts are missing lifts accuracy from single digits to 70–80%. Engineering the coordination mechanism matters more than scaling the model.

## Honest Limits

These are artificial tasks with clean ground truth. Real-world collective reasoning—diagnosis, incident response, intelligence analysis—often involves ambiguous evidence and no single correct answer.

The structured protocol was only tested on 18 of 65 tasks across 3 models. Its robustness across larger groups, mixed-model teams, and open-ended domains remains unknown.

And forced disclosure, while diagnostic, sidesteps the real problem: you don't know in advance which facts matter. In practice, the space of relevant information is itself latent—it must be discovered through interaction.

## What This Changes

HIDDENBENCH gives the field its first theory-grounded, reproducible benchmark that cleanly isolates coordination failure from reasoning ability in multi-agent LLMs.

The diagnosis is sharp: the bottleneck is *collective information exploration*, not individual inference.

And the bottleneck is *actionable*—a simple structured protocol yields large gains, suggesting that the path forward is engineering coordination, not just scaling parameters.

The agents in our flood scenario didn't need to think harder. They needed to ask what they were missing.
