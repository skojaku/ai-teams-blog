*TL;DR: A "cognitive companion" that reads an LLM agent's hidden states during its own forward pass can detect reasoning loops with zero extra compute — and it works, but only on the right kind of task.*

## 30% of steps, wasted

Step 5 makes the same argument as step 3. Step 7 restates step 4 in different words.

The output keeps flowing, so anyone watching assumes progress — but the agent is going in circles.

Pipis et al. showed that 1.5B-parameter models enter these repetitive states on roughly 30% of challenging tasks.

This paper asks: can you catch that spiral *for free*, by reading the model's own internal states as it generates?

## Two ways to watch

The Cognitive Companion is a parallel monitor that sits alongside a primary agent. Three parts: the agent itself (unchanged), a Companion Observer, and an Intervention Handler that injects corrective guidance when things go wrong.

![Architecture diagram showing the three-component Cognitive Companion with Primary Agent, Companion Observer monitoring hidden states, and Intervention Handler injecting guidance]({{ "/assets/figures/c0eaee001340/fig_005_01.png" | relative_url }})

The observer comes in two flavors.

The **LLM Companion** periodically pauses the agent and asks the same model to assess recent steps via a structured prompt — `STATUS: LOOPING` or `DRIFTING` or `STUCK` or `ON_TRACK`.

This is the LLM-as-judge pattern Zheng et al. formalized in their MT-Bench work. It works, but it costs ~11% overhead because you're running a second inference pass.

The **Probe Companion** is where this paper gets interesting. Instead of calling the model again, it reads hidden states extracted during the agent's *existing* forward pass — the one that was going to happen anyway.

It then runs a single logistic regression on mean-pooled hidden states from layer 28. Burns et al. showed that linear probes can pull latent knowledge signals out of hidden states; this paper extends that logic to degradation detection.

The probe runs one matrix multiply on activations that were already computed.

Zero additional model calls. Zero measured inference overhead.

## How the probe works

For each reasoning step, the system extracts the final-position hidden states from layer $\ell$ across the last $w = 10$ generated tokens, then mean-pools them:

$$\phi_{t,\ell} = \frac{1}{w} \sum_{i=n-w+1}^{n} h_{i,\ell}[-1, :]$$

A binary logistic classifier maps that 2560-dim vector to $P(\text{DEGRADED})$:

$$P(y_t = 1 \mid \phi_{t,\ell}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}_\ell^T \cdot \phi_{t,\ell} + b_\ell)$$

They trained classifiers at layers 20, 28, 34, and 40. Layer 28 won with cross-validated AUROC 0.840.

When the degradation probability crosses a threshold (0.55), the Intervention Handler fires. In all experiments it uses **Whisper mode** — silently injecting guidance into the agent's context without the agent knowing a monitor exists.

## What the numbers show

On loop-prone tasks like the Liar Paradox, the Probe Companion hit Cohen's $d = +2.04$ on quality — a large effect — at zero overhead.

On the Consciousness task, $d = +0.87$.

The LLM Companion reports 52–62% repetition reduction on loop-prone tasks, but at 11% overhead. Across all six E2 tasks, its mean quality effect was just $d = +0.047$.

The Probe Companion's mean effect was $d = +0.471$ — ten times the LLM Companion's — at zero cost.

Then the story turns.

## Where it breaks

On structured tasks like DB Decision, the LLM Companion *hurt*: $d = -0.82$.

The Probe Companion landed at $d = 0.00$.

![Task-type dependency showing companion effects cluster by category — strong on loop-prone tasks, neutral or negative on structured ones]({{ "/assets/figures/c0eaee001340/fig_009_05.png" | relative_url }})

Companions don't just fail to help on structured tasks — they can actively damage them.

And on small models — Qwen 2.5 1.5B and Llama 3.2 1B — neither companion improved quality at all.

Interventions fired. Guidance was injected. Nothing changed.

This suggests a scale boundary: models below ~3B parameters may lack the capacity to act on corrective signals, echoing Huang et al.'s finding that small models cannot self-correct through prompting.

![Quality improvement by model size showing zero gain for sub-3B models despite successful intervention firing]({{ "/assets/figures/c0eaee001340/fig_013_08.png" | relative_url }})

Companions are selective tools, not universal supervisors.

## Honest limits

The probe was trained on 35 proxy-labeled examples — labels generated by the same LLM Companion being evaluated, not by humans. One data collection run produced only 3 DEGRADED examples, causing cross-validation AUROC to hit NaN.

The v5 probe evaluation is in-domain: training and evaluation draw from the same task bank. Quality scores in E1/E2 are self-referential — the model family judges its own output.

No significance testing, no confidence intervals, no external annotations. Effect sizes from three-run experiments are suggestive, not conclusive.

The authors are transparent about all of this. They frame the paper as a feasibility study, not a validation.

## What to carry forward

The task-type routing insight may outlast the probe numbers. If you can classify your task as loop-prone or drift-prone before you start, you activate the companion; if it's structured, you don't.

That simple heuristic turns what looked like a negative finding — companions hurt on some tasks — into a deployment rule: *selective activation over universal supervision*.

Zero-overhead semantic monitoring via hidden states is still early. But the idea that you can read an agent's cognitive state from the activations it was already producing — that's a handle worth carrying.

The companion watches for free; it just needs to know when to show up.
