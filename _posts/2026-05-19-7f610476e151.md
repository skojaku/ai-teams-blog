*TL;DR: Multi-agent LLM teams beat human teams on creative problem-solving by a large margin (Cohen's d = 1.50), and the way they explore ideas—direct semantic jumps rather than smooth conversational pivots—turns out to be engineerable via discussion structure and model choice.*

## The Gap at the Top

The top LLM-generated idea in this study scored 0.77 on creativity.

The top human idea scored 0.48.

That is not a rounding error—it is a 58% gap at the upper tail of the distribution. And it holds across all six tasks, from plastic waste to a hypothetical "sorry pandemic."

Hu et al. ran 71 experimental conditions across six open-ended problem-solving tasks, collecting 4,541 ideas from multi-agent LLM teams and 341 ideas from human teams.

Five blind raters scored every idea on novelty and usefulness. The verdict: LLM teams substantially outperformed human teams on creativity (M = 0.297 vs. M = 0.151, Cohen's d = 1.50, p < 0.001).

The advantage was driven almost entirely by novelty (d = 1.29). Usefulness was comparable (d = 0.08, p = 0.14). LLM teams generated ideas that raters found genuinely surprising without sacrificing practicality.

This matters. Creativity has been called AI's "final frontier"—the cognitive domain where human superiority seemed secure.

Research on collective intelligence shows that human groups have emergent smarts no individual member possesses. Multi-agent LLM systems extend this idea: multiple AI instances discuss, critique, and refine ideas together. But no one had systematically benchmarked LLM teams against human teams on applied creative tasks until now.

## How They Tested It

Six tasks spanned societal challenges (plastic waste, educational inequality), business problems (supply chain, employee attrition), and deliberately absurd hypotheticals (a pandemic transmitted by saying "sorry," singing in the shower for health). The hypothetical tasks were designed to defeat memorization—no training data contains pre-baked solutions.

Human teams of three or six people had 12 minutes per task and submitted one idea each. LLM teams of three or six agents (running GPT-4.1, o3-low, o3-high, or mixed ensembles) worked under five discussion structures: open brainstorming, instructed cycles, iterative refinement, progressive improvement, or no discussion at all.

Every idea—human and LLM—was paraphrased into a standardized format to strip stylistic fingerprints before evaluation.

Five trained raters scored novelty and usefulness on 10-point scales using the Consensual Assessment Technique. Creativity was computed as novelty × usefulness.

The entire study was pre-registered.

## What the Numbers Say

The headline effect is large and consistent. Across all six tasks, LLM teams outperformed human teams.

![Bar chart comparing mean creativity scores across human and LLM team configurations, with LLM teams consistently above the human baseline across every task and model type]({{ "/assets/figures/7f610476e151/fig_005_01.png" | relative_url }})

The 95th percentile of LLM ideas scored 0.47 vs. 0.31 for humans—a 53% increase at the competitive tail.

Even single LLM agents beat human teams (single-agent M = 0.253–0.306 vs. human M = 0.151).

But multi-agent discussion lifted models further above their baselines. For GPT-4.1, the best multi-agent configuration reached M = 0.298 vs. single-agent M = 0.253 (d = 0.52). For o3-low, M = 0.345 vs. 0.289 (d = 0.61).

The novelty–usefulness space tells a clear story. LLM ideas occupy the upper-right quadrant—both novel and useful—while human ideas cluster along a trade-off curve where novelty and usefulness compete.

## Direct Jumps vs. Connected Zigzags

This is where the paper gets genuinely interesting. The authors didn't just compare outputs—they compared *processes*.

They represented each team's conversation as a path through neural embedding space and extracted nine geometric features: path length, semantic spread, max distance, local coherence, global coherence, convergence ratio, curvature, topic switching rate, and revisit score.

Both LLM and human teams produced more creative ideas when conversations ranged widely rather than staying centered on one theme (negative global coherence). But the secondary predictors diverged sharply.

LLM team creativity was predicted by **efficient exploration**: high semantic spread (β = +0.227) combined with short paths (β = −0.122) and constrained maximum distances (β = −0.276). Local coherence—smooth conversational flow—had zero relationship with LLM creativity (β = −0.053, n.s.).

Human team creativity was predicted by **conversational flow**: high local coherence (β = +0.233) and high trajectory curvature (β = +0.207). Humans zigzag through connected pivots. LLMs make direct semantic jumps.

![Semantic trajectory visualization showing high-creativity conversations making wide exploratory leaps across embedding space and low-creativity conversations staying narrowly centered]({{ "/assets/figures/7f610476e151/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

Consider two conversations from the same condition. In a high-creativity GPT-4.1 exchange (rating = 0.551), the discussion leapt from economic incentives to blockchain to biotechnology to living organisms.

Global coherence: −0.91. Wide open.

A low-creativity conversation from the same condition (rating = 0.090) stayed locked on one concept, iterating minor refinements.

Global coherence: +2.20. Locked in.

## Two Knobs to Turn

If you want to engineer a more creative AI team, you have two knobs: **model choice** and **discussion structure**. They operate through different mechanisms, and together they explain 26.8% of variance in conversation trajectories.

Model choice primarily controls coherence patterns (local coherence: 31.4% variance from model vs. 11.4% from discussion). Discussion structure primarily controls exploration breadth (max distance: 40.6% variance from discussion vs. 2.7% from model).

The practical takeaway is a dose-response relationship: reasoning models (o3-high) already perform internal deliberation, so external scaffolding barely helps.

![Creativity scores across five discussion structures for GPT-4.1 and o3-high, showing iterative refinement dramatically boosting GPT-4.1 but barely moving o3-high]({{ "/assets/figures/7f610476e151/fig_008_03.png" | relative_url }})

Iterative refinement boosted GPT-4.1 creativity by 45.3% (M = 0.308 vs. 0.212) but moved o3-high by only 6.5% (M = 0.343 vs. 0.322). The stronger the model's internal reasoning, the less it needs structured discussion to perform well.

This is actionable.

If a model produces narrow, highly coherent paths by default, impose iterative refinement to force broader exploration.

If a model already generates divergent leaps, use structures that help agents synthesize those leaps into coherent solutions.

Run a few pilot conversations, compute trajectory features, and select the discussion structure that corrects the model's geometric deficits. No more trial-and-error.

## What Doesn't Work

Bigger teams didn't help. Three-agent teams performed comparably to six-agent teams for both humans and LLMs. Coordination overhead appears to eat the marginal benefit—echoing classic process-loss findings from organizational psychology.

Persona assignment also didn't move the needle on overall creativity (F = 2.09, p = 0.124). Giving agents distinct personas shifted the novelty–usefulness balance—ideas became more useful but less novel—but left the product unchanged. The creative gain has to come from *how* agents discuss, not *who* they pretend to be.

## Where This Breaks Down

Creativity here means novelty × usefulness on applied problem-solving tasks, judged by humans using the Consensual Assessment Technique.

That captures a real and important form of creativity.

It does not capture aesthetic judgment, paradigm-shifting theoretical insight, or the kind of disruptive innovation that rewrites a field. Different domains may need different metrics.

The human sample—341 ideas from 114 teams—is modest relative to the LLM conditions. Generalization to expert or professional populations is untested.

And this study compares LLM teams to human teams, not human-AI collaboration. Human-AI teams introduce distinct challenges around real-time coordination, complementary knowledge, and dynamic task allocation. The authors acknowledge this explicitly.

## Beyond the Score

The real contribution isn't the headline effect size. It's the trajectory framework.

By showing that conversation dynamics are measurable, predictable, and manipulable, the authors turn multi-agent creativity from something you discover by accident into something you can engineer.

Trajectory features predict 32.6% of creativity variance in LLMs. Design choices explain 26.8% of trajectory variance.

The features most strongly associated with creativity—global coherence and max distance—are also the most design-responsive.

That loop—from design choices to trajectory features to creative outcomes—is what makes this more than a benchmark result. It is a diagnostic tool.

Compute a few geometric features from pilot conversations, identify the model's default exploration pattern, and select the discussion protocol that patches its weaknesses.

Direct jumps beat connected zigzags—at least when you can engineer the jump.
