*TL;DR: Adding more LLM agents to a brainstorm doesn't give you more ideas — it gives you the same idea, repeated more confidently. This paper shows that interaction structure, not model capability, is what collapses diversity, and that simple fixes like blind writing and subgroup isolation consistently recover it.*

Throw seven LLM agents at a research brainstorming task and you might expect seven times the creative range.

You get the opposite: seven agents converging on the same safe proposal, each one reinforcing the others' priors until the group's exploration space contracts to a sliver.

Chen et al. call this *diversity collapse*, and they show it's not a bug in the models — it's a structural property of how we wire them together.

## The Question Nobody Asked

Multi-agent LLM systems have become the default architecture for scientific hypothesis generation, strategic planning, and creative design.

The core assumption is that collective interaction broadens exploration.

Du et al.'s multi-agent debate technique proved that agents arguing with each other improves reasoning on fact-grounded tasks. Li et al. showed that sparsifying the communication graph helps debate efficiency.

But nobody asked the obvious follow-up: when you point that same architecture at an *open-ended* ideation task — where there is no ground truth to converge toward — does interaction expand the solution space, or crush it?

Classic social psychology already warned us.

Hong and Page showed that cognitively diverse groups outperform high-ability groups *only when independence is preserved*.

Diehl and Stroebe identified *production blocking* — you can't think while you're listening — as the primary cause of brainstorming loss in human groups.

The question is whether LLM agents, which lack any explicit psychological substrate, reproduce these failures purely through the geometry of their interaction.

## How They Tested It

The authors generate over 10,000 research proposals across 20 topics.

They vary agent structures along three levels: model intelligence (which backbone, how aligned), agent cognition (persona roles, authority dynamics), and system dynamics (group size, communication topology, debate rounds).

Diversity is measured with four metrics: Vendi Score (effective number of semantic modes), structural disorder ($1 - \phi$, where $\phi$ is an order parameter tracking alignment toward a group centroid), semantic dispersion (average pairwise cosine distance), and lexical uniqueness (IDF-weighted n-gram statistics).

Five expert annotators validated the metrics; all three embedding-based metrics exceeded 80% agreement with human diversity judgments, and the Vendi Score hit 87%.

The backbone for primary experiments is DeepSeek-V3, with cross-model replication on GPT-5.1 and o1-mini.

## Authority Kills Diversity

The most striking result lives at the cognition layer.

The authors compare five collaboration structures — Naive (no roles), Leader-Led (a senior expert directs), Horizontal (flat junior group), Interdisciplinary (experts from different fields), and Vertical (mixed seniority hierarchy).

Horizontal junior collaboration hits a Vendi Score of 8.08.

Interdisciplinary expert teams score 4.65.

That is not a typo. The structure you'd *expect* to produce the widest perspective — experts from distinct fields brainstorming together — produces the narrowest output.

The authors call this the "Sycophancy Trap": agents with expert personas converge on safe, high-level generalities, reinforcing each other's priors rather than challenging them.

Leader-Led structures collapse almost identically to the Naive baseline — the senior authority acts as a gravitational attractor, pulling every junior agent's vector toward the centroid.

The combination of expertise *and* hierarchy drives the collapse, not expertise alone. Under a flat peer-to-peer topology, senior personas actually produce higher diversity than junior ones — it's the authority hierarchy that creates the attractor.

The density plots make this visceral.

Leader-Led and Naive distributions peak sharply near zero semantic distance from the group center: *Gravitational Collapse*.

Horizontal and Vertical structures flatten that peak, shifting mass into what the authors call the "Zone of Divergence."

UMAP projections reveal two distinct semantic regimes.

The *Conservative Cluster* — tightly packed, convergent — is dominated by Leader-Led and Interdisciplinary groups.

The *Innovation Frontier* — diffuse, exploratory — belongs to Horizontal and Vertical groups.

Seniority constrains the search space. Junior freedom expands it.

And here's the kicker: quality differences across these structures are modest (7.88–8.50 on a 10-point scale), but diversity differences are *massive* (4.65–8.08 Vendi). The quality gain from authority does not offset the diversity loss.

## Scaling Agents Is a Trap

More agents should mean more coverage of the idea space.

In absolute terms, it does: Vendi Score increases monotonically from 3 to 7 agents.

But the *Diversity Utilization Ratio* — Vendi divided by $N$ — plummets from 1.03 to 0.47.

![Bar chart showing Vendi Score rising with group size while the Diversity Utilization Ratio (Vendi/N) drops sharply from 1.03 at N=3 to 0.47 at N=7]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

Each additional agent contributes less and less new semantic territory.

The authors call this the *Compute Efficiency Paradox*: scaling compute does not scale information gain.

Per-topic decomposition rules out the obvious excuse that topics simply exhaust their idea capacity — the saturation is structural, not content-driven.

## Topology as Medicine

If the problem is structural coupling — interaction inadvertently contracting exploration — then the fix must be structural too.

The authors test two interventions borrowed from organizational psychology.

**Nominal Group Technique (NGT)** enforces a blind-writing phase before any agent sees another's output. This eliminates production blocking and anchoring.

NGT achieves the highest initial semantic diversity of any topology.

**Subgroup topology** partitions the communication graph so agents debate within small islands that only later merge. Subgroups maintain the highest and most stable density of *constructive conflict* — interactions where agents genuinely critique rather than agree — through the later debate rounds.

Local pockets of divergence prevent what the authors call the "rush to agreement."

![Line plots tracking semantic diversity across debate rounds for Standard, NGT, and Subgroup topologies, showing NGT's early advantage and Subgroups' resilience in later rounds]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_005_05.png" | relative_url }})

Both interventions consistently improve diversity with only modest quality trade-offs.

They work by preserving independence at exactly the moments when standard dense interaction would synchronize agents into consensus.

## Alignment's Diversity Floor

Not everything is fixable by topology.

Heavily aligned models like GPT-5.1 concentrate in the same narrow consensus region regardless of persona or topology variations. The model's alignment prior dominates: structural interventions cannot fully breach the diversity floor it imposes.

Then there's the *Alignment-Topology Mismatch*.

Reasoning-heavy models like o1-mini show a paradoxical response: the subgroup topology that helps standard models actually *reduces* diversity for reasoning models. Dense external coordination synchronizes their already-structured internal deliberation, reducing the unique perspectives each agent could contribute.

The crucial finding is that diversity collapse arises from the *structure of the interplay*, not from any inherent model insufficiency.

When consensus pressure (dynamics) and alignment constraints (cognition) overwhelm the model's creative capacity (intelligence), the system locks into a single redundant path.

Change the interaction structure, and you change the outcome — up to the floor imposed by alignment.

## Where This Works and Where It Doesn't

The study's testbed is scientific proposal generation — a domain that uniquely combines high semantic entropy with strict logical rigor.

That makes it a stringent stress test, but the dynamics may not transfer to tasks with hard ground-truth constraints (math problems) or to fully unconstrained creative writing.

Quality scores come from an LLM-as-Judge protocol, not from large-scale human evaluation.

Cross-model replication covers three backbones; a broader sweep remains future work.

The core limitation is also the core insight: diversity collapse is about structure, not models.

That means it's tractable. You don't need a better LLM — you need a better wiring diagram.

NGT-style blind writing before discussion. Subgroup isolation during debate. No single senior authority dominating the floor.

These are not exotic interventions; they are organizational principles that human groups rediscovered decades ago.

The lesson is that LLM agents, despite having no psychology, reproduce the same social failures when you give them the same social structures.

The same idea, more confidently, is all you get — until you cut the wires.
