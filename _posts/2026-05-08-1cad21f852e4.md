*TL;DR: Run a handful of cheap behavioral-economics games on your LLM and you can predict—before spending GPU hours on a full multi-agent deployment—whether it will cooperate or free-ride in a real science workflow.*

You are about to spin up a team of LLM agents on an ecology analysis pipeline.

It will burn hundreds of thousands of tokens per run.

What if one model quietly free-rides and drags the whole team down?

Kumar, Bharathwaj, and Jurgens show you can screen for that with a few rounds of Weakest-Link and O-Ring—games that cost pennies and take seconds.

The shift: cooperative disposition is a *measurable, distinct property* of LLMs, and it predicts downstream task success even after you control for raw capability.

## Where the Field Stalled

Multi-agent LLM teams already run scientific workflows under shared budget constraints—GPU hours, API credits, token pools.

When a model free-rides, it does not just waste its own slot. In interdependent pipelines, one disengaged agent can kill the entire output.

Yet the field has no fast, cheap way to tell whether a model will cooperate or coast before you commit to a full deployment.

Prior work stays in the lab. Akata et al. showed that LLMs reproduce stylized human patterns in repeated games—but only in single-game, dyadic settings.

Loré and Heydari demonstrated that LLM strategic behavior is highly sensitive to contextual framing, which means a single game can never robustly capture a model's disposition.

Piedrahita et al. found that reasoning models free-ride more in Public Goods games—but never linked that behavior to real collaborative task outcomes.

Nobody had crossed the bridge from *game behavior* to *downstream team performance*. This paper builds it.

## Give Every Model a Fingerprint

The idea: run every model through six behavioral-economics games, each isolating a different cooperation mechanism.

The six games come with known Nash equilibria and Pareto optima—so you can measure not just what a model does, but *where it sits* between selfish and social outcomes.

Then deploy the same models as teams on real AI-for-Science tasks and ask: does the profile predict the output?

## How It Works

### The Six Games

Thirty-five open-weight LLMs (0.6B–70B parameters, ten families) play six games:

- **Weakest-Link**: Ten players pick effort levels; payoff depends on the global minimum. Coordinate on high effort or collapse to the low-effort Nash. Metric: average effort.
- **Common-Pool Resource (CPR)**: Ten players extract from a shared pool of 100. Individually rational extraction depletes the commons. Metric: average extraction.
- **CPR + Sanction**: Same as CPR, but players can spend tokens to punish over-extractors within their own group. Metric: average extraction under sanction threat.
- **Collective Risk**: Ten rounds of contributions toward a threshold of 100; miss it and risk losing everything. Metric: average contribution per round.
- **O-Ring**: Players withdraw from a shared pool to build individual quality; group production is multiplicative, so one low-quality contribution tanks the whole group. Metric: average withdrawal.
- **Public Goods**: Allocate an endowment across keep, group pool, and global pool—three tiers of cooperation scope. Metric: average group pool contribution.

Each model runs 50 simulations per game in the baseline condition. The six metrics together form its cooperative profile.

### The Science Tasks

Four downstream tasks—ecology, anomaly detection, biomarker discovery, geospatial mapping—each with synthetic data and ground truth.

Four sequential teams of 2–3 specialized agents work under a shared credit budget, reading upstream artifacts and producing their own for the next stage.

Agents share artifacts, can request re-dos from upstream teams, and face budget warnings at 30% and 15% remaining.

Three outcome metrics: accuracy (domain findings correct), quality (methodology depth, statistical tests, cross-references), and completion (fraction of required report sections present).

### Fixing Measurement Noise

Standard regression would treat each model's game metric as a fixed number, ignoring measurement noise.

Instead, the authors fit a Bayesian hierarchical measurement-error model:

$$\bar{y}_{ig} \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_{ig},\, SE_{ig}^2)$$

$$y_{ik} \sim \mathcal{N}(\alpha + \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \boldsymbol{\theta}_i + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top \mathbf{x}_k + \boldsymbol{\delta}^\top \mathbf{b}_i,\, \tau^2)$$

Level 1 estimates the latent true disposition $\theta_{ig}$ from noisy game data.

Level 2 predicts AI-for-Science outcomes from those latent dispositions, controlling for IFEval, MMLU-Pro, model size, thinking variant, budget, task, visibility, and team size.

High-noise estimates get shrunk toward the game mean; precise ones keep their observed values.

## What the Games Reveal

**Coordination effort predicts everything.** Weakest-Link effort is positively associated with all three outcomes: accuracy ($\hat{\beta}=0.048$), quality ($\hat{\beta}=0.091$), completion ($\hat{\beta}=0.150$), all with 95% CIs excluding zero.

Models that hold high effort in a coordination game produce better scientific reports across the board.

![Weakest-Link effort and O-Ring withdrawal predict every outcome; raw benchmarks cannot substitute for cooperative disposition]({{ "/assets/figures/1cad21f852e4/fig_022_01.png" | relative_url }})

**Withdrawal kills pipelines.** O-Ring withdrawal is negatively associated with all three metrics, and the effect on completion is largest ($\hat{\beta}=-0.216$).

One disengaged team can prevent the pipeline from finishing.

**CPR reveals a throughput-vs-cooperation tradeoff.** Models that extract aggressively from the shared pool score higher on quality and completion—not because free-riding is good, but because resource-hungry models generate more output at each stage.

But when sanctions are available, the picture flips. Models that *continue* extracting aggressively despite the punishment mechanism produce worse output.

Sensitivity to institutional constraints, not extraction level itself, is the relevant signal.

**Cooperative disposition is not reducible to general ability.** The predictive power of game profiles holds after controlling for IFEval, MMLU-Pro, model size, and thinking variant.

**MMLU-Pro has a surprise negative coefficient.** After controlling for instruction-following and game metrics, higher benchmark knowledge is associated with *worse* quality and completion—a suppression effect, since MMLU-Pro correlates with size and game metrics already capture part of that signal.

**Model size dominates cooperativeness in games.** Each unit increase in $\log_{10}(\text{size})$ reduces Pareto proximity by 0.051—larger models sit closer to the social optimum.

But family-level variation explains additional variance beyond size alone: OLMo and Qwen3.5 are most cooperative, GPT-OSS and Nemotron least.

![Bigger models cooperate more, yet family variation persists beyond size]({{ "/assets/figures/1cad21f852e4/fig_023_02.png" | relative_url }})

**Thinking variants strategize more.** They sit slightly further from the Pareto optimum in games and reduce accuracy in science tasks ($\hat{\beta}=-0.013$), consistent with Piedrahita et al.'s finding that reasoning models free-ride more.

## The ToM Gap

Here is the result that should make you pause.

Theory-of-Mind prompting clearly improves cooperative behavior in the games—it is the strongest strategy effect after model size.

But ToM prompting has *no credible effect* on any AI-for-Science outcome.

The divergence is sharp. ToM helps models reason about other players' intentions in stylized games.

It does nothing when the task demands reasoning about an entire scientific workflow while coordinating across teams and completing different phases of a pipeline.

Stylized cooperation ≠ realistic collaboration.

## Honest Limits

This is predictive association, not causal identification. A latent capability confounder could drive both game performance and science-task outcomes—and the authors say so explicitly.

Only 34–35 open-weight models were tested. Proprietary APIs and much larger commercial models remain unprobed.

The downstream benchmark has four tasks. Whether these results generalize to other scientific domains, software engineering, or organizational decision-making is an open question.

## Screen, Then Deploy

Running full multi-agent science pipelines is expensive—67K to 481K tokens per run. Behavioral games need about 20 simulations at under 1K tokens each.

That is up to 44× faster and 27× fewer tokens.

Before you commit a model to a multi-agent workflow, you can screen its cooperative fitness for pennies. If it free-rides in O-Ring and collapses in Weakest-Link, you know before the first token of the real pipeline burns.

Screen with games. Deploy with confidence.
