*TL;DR: SILO-BENCH shows that when LLM agents must compute with distributed information rather than merely exchange it, even the best model fails nearly two-thirds of the time—because agents can talk but can't integrate.*

## 100 Agents, One Star Topology, Zero Correct Answer

Give 100 LLM agents a simple task—find the global maximum across a set of numbers split between them—and something striking happens. The agents spontaneously organize into a near-perfect star topology. One agent emerges as the central aggregator, everyone sends their data to it, and the communication pattern looks exactly like what a distributed systems designer would prescribe.

And then they get the wrong answer.

Agent-77 submits after contacting only 28 of 100 agents, producing 208 when the ground truth is 114. Others converge on different values entirely. The topology was right. The reasoning was not.

This is the central finding of SILO-BENCH, and it reveals a fault line running through every proposal to scale AI via multi-agent systems.

## The Communication-Reasoning Gap

The promise of multi-agent LLM systems is seductive: distribute information across agents, let them coordinate, and sidestep the context limits of a single model. CAMEL assigned agents fixed personas like "Manager" or "Coder." Park's Generative Agents simulated social behavior in a town. AgentBench tested single-agent competence across tasks.

But none of these benchmarks ask the hard question: can agents actually *compute* with siloed information, or do they just trade messages?

SILO-BENCH is deliberately role-agnostic. No manager agent, no worker agent, no prescribed workflow. Each agent gets a local data shard and a task description, then has to figure out coordination from scratch. This strips away inductive bias and isolates the core capability in question: distributed reasoning, not social skill.

The result is a gap the authors name precisely. Agents are competent communicators and poor distributed reasoners. On Level-I tasks—the simplest aggregation problems—DeepSeek-V3.1 achieves a Partial Correctness Score of 88.0% but a Success Rate of only 62.0%. That 26-percentage-point chasm means the agents collectively hold nearly all the required information but still cannot synthesize it into a correct final answer.

## How SILO-BENCH Works

The benchmark grounds its task design in communication complexity theory—Yao's 1979 framework for quantifying the minimum communication needed for distributed parties to compute a function. Thirty algorithmic tasks sit at three levels:

- **Level I — Aggregation** ($O(N)$ communication). Embarrassingly parallel followed by a reduction. Find the global max, count word frequencies, vote by majority. The optimal pattern is a star: one collector, $N{-}1$ senders.

- **Level II — Mesh Network** ($O(N)$ communication). Each agent depends on its neighbors. Compute prefix sums, moving averages, or the trapping-rainwater problem. The optimal pattern is a chain of pairwise exchanges.

- **Level III — Global Shuffle** ($O(N \log N)$ to $O(N^2)$ communication). Any agent's output may depend on any other agent's data. Distributed sorting, graph connectivity, matrix multiplication. The optimal pattern is dense all-to-all exchange.

$$\tau_k = (f_k, X_k, y^*_k)$$

Each task instance is defined by its function $f_k$, global input $X_k$, and ground-truth answer $y^*_k$. Input data is partitioned equally across $N$ agents so no agent holds privileged information.

### Four metrics, one key insight

The evaluation suite tracks four quantities. Success Rate $S$ measures how many agents reach the correct answer. Partial Correctness Score $P$ measures continuous answer quality. Token Consumption $C$ tracks computational cost per round. Communication Density $D$ captures interaction intensity.

$$S = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}[\hat{y}_i = y^*]$$

$$P = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} q_i$$

The gap $P - S$ is where the diagnosis lives. When $P$ is high and $S$ is low, agents possess the information but fail at integration. When both are low, they fail even to communicate. SILO-BENCH finds that the dominant failure mode is the former.

Three communication protocols span the design space. Peer-to-peer (P2P) requires agents to address specific recipients. Broadcast (BP) sends each message to everyone. Shared File System (SFS) routes coordination through a common key-value store—agents never speak directly.

Across six agent scales ($2, 5, 10, 20, 50, 100$), three protocols, and three frontier open-source models (DeepSeek-V3.1, GPT-OSS-120B, Qwen3-Next-80B-A3B), the benchmark covers 54 configurations and 1,620 total experiments—the largest systematic study of multi-agent LLM collaboration to date.

## The Topology Is Right. The Answer Is Wrong.

This is the most surprising result in the paper. The communication heatmaps tell a clear story. For Level-I tasks, message traffic flows into a single aggregator column: a star topology emerges organically. For Level-II tasks, a diagonal band appears: agents communicate with immediate neighbors. For Level-III tasks, the matrix is uniformly dense: agents attempt all-to-all exchange.

The agents discover task-appropriate topologies without instruction.

![Communication heatmaps showing emergent star topology for aggregation, diagonal pattern for mesh tasks, and dense all-to-all connections for global shuffle]({{ "/assets/figures/347bd0b0007b/fig_008_04.png" | relative_url }})

But topological correctness does not guarantee computational correctness. The star is there for global max, but agents over-communicate within it, distribute load unevenly, and—most critically—fail to synthesize the aggregated data into the right answer. The mesh shows the diagonal, plus off-diagonal noise from redundant broadcasts. The dense all-to-all looks right structurally but executes as uncoordinated flooding rather than structured exchange.

## Where Coordination Breaks

The authors classify errors through a hybrid procedure: rule-based detection verified by two independent annotators (Cohen's $\kappa = 0.87$). Three modes account for nearly all failures.

**Premature Submission (37.2%).** Agents submit before gathering sufficient information. Agent-77 contacted only 28 of 100 agents and produced 208 instead of 114. They did not check whether they knew enough.

**Consensus Failure (29.9%).** Agents communicate actively but cannot converge. In one 100-agent XOR checksum run, 12 distinct answers emerged, with 86 agents settling on 146 while the remainder scattered from 42 to 238.

**Computation Error (28.6%).** Agents collect all required data and still compute incorrectly—submitting 619 instead of 620, an off-by-one during final aggregation.

These modes compound. Sixty-seven runs exhibit both Premature Submission and Consensus Failure, because early exits prevent subsequent consensus-building.

## Scale Kills, Complexly

Performance degrades multiplicatively with both task complexity and agent count. DeepSeek-V3.1 drops from 62% success on Level-I to 11.7% on Level-III. At $N \geq 50$ on Level-III, success rates fall to near zero across all models.

![Success rate by agent scale and difficulty level, showing steep degradation as both task complexity and team size increase, with Level-III tasks falling to near zero at N≥50]({{ "/assets/figures/347bd0b0007b/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

The Relative Coordination Cost quantifies the damage. Defined as $\text{RCC} = 1 - \text{SR}(N{=}k) / \text{SR}(N{=}1)$, it measures how much single-agent performance is lost to coordination overhead. For Level-III tasks at $N \geq 50$, RCC reaches 100%: a single agent with full data outperforms the entire distributed team.

More agents can be strictly worse.

This is not because the tasks themselves are harder. The single-agent success rate differs by only about 15 percentage points between Level-I and Level-III. But the multi-agent gap balloons to over 18 percentage points. Coordination failure, not task difficulty, drives the collapse.

### The leader trap

Perhaps the most counterintuitive finding: spontaneous leader emergence actively hurts performance on Level-III tasks. The agent that naturally becomes the aggregator gets overwhelmed by the volume of global data it must process. Self-organized centralization creates bottlenecks rather than resolving them.

Protocol preference is model-dependent. DeepSeek-V3.1 performs best with broadcast (40.4% success rate), while GPT-OSS-120B prefers peer-to-peer (20.3% vs. 14.4% with broadcast). Shared file systems underperform in most configurations—despite information transfer volume comparable to broadcast—suggesting the bottleneck is reasoning about shared state, not communication volume.

## Why More Agents Won't Save You

SILO-BENCH establishes that the critical bottleneck in multi-agent LLM systems is not communication infrastructure but reasoning-integration capability. Agents format messages, respond to peers, and organize information flows—then fail to determine whether they have enough information, and fail to synthesize what they have.

The benchmark has clear limits. Only three communication protocols are tested—no hierarchical, gossip-based, or hybrid mechanisms. Only homogeneous agent teams are evaluated, not the heterogeneous compositions common in real deployments. Only three open-source models, no closed-source ones due to cost and reproducibility concerns.

But the core finding stands: naively adding agents to a multi-LLM system does not circumvent context limitations. The Communication-Reasoning Gap is the ceiling, and right now it is low.

The agents know how to talk. They do not yet know how to think together.
