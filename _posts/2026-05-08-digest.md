## Themes

Screen before you spend. Every paper today shares the same instinct: running a full multi-agent deployment to find out whether it works is too expensive. Cooperative Profiles Predict Multi-Agent LLM Team Performance shows that behavioral-economics games costing pennies forecast cooperation failures before you burn hundreds of thousands of tokens. Active Learning for Communication Structure Optimization makes the same argument for training-task selection—random draws waste your budget on uninformative cases. Uno-Orchestra learns when *not* to decompose, collapsing a complex orchestration to a single cheap dispatch when the task allows it.

Structure that bends. The old choice was brittle pipelines or chaotic peer teams. LATTE rejects it: a shared adaptive task graph lets workers claim frontier nodes like work-stealing schedulers, and the Lead steps in only for stragglers and verification. Active Learning for Communication Structure Optimization builds its graph-optimization loop on the same logic—learn the topology, don't prescribe it. The pattern is clear: fixed coordination structure is a liability; the architectures that adapt their wiring mid-flight win.

Stress as diagnostic, not just damage. CAFE reframes what looks like failure—output quality dropping a third under semantic stress—as a measurement opportunity. The ensemble that degrades most also exposes the richest statistical signal; its stress footprint is screaming with structure. Cooperative Profiles Predict Multi-Agent LLM Team Performance finds the same logic in microcosm: the games that expose whether a model free-rides are the ones you run *before* the real task, precisely because stress reveals disposition. In both cases, the point is not that the system survives the hit—it is that the hit carries information you can act on.

Coordination made legible. LATTE's task graph does not just coordinate agents; it makes every coordination decision visible as a graph operation—overwrites, claims, releases, verifications. Cooperative Profiles Predict Multi-Agent LLM Team Performance turns a model's strategic behavior in six games into a quantitative fingerprint. CAFE maps stress response into a four-dimensional signature that differs by architecture. When you can read the coordination, you can debug it, predict it, and eventually learn from it.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag: ai-teams. The field has stopped asking whether LLM agents should collaborate and started asking how to make the collaboration cheap, stable, and honest about its limits. That is genuine progress.

Uno-Orchestra's blind-worker protocol is the design detail you should copy today. The moment the router sees model names during training, per-query cost jumps fivefold and accuracy barely twitches. The policy takes the brand-name shortcut and charges you for it. For any production system routing across a price-diverse model pool, this is not optional hygiene—it is the difference between a viable service and a burn rate.

LATTE's contribution is structural, not incremental. Borrowing claim-based work-stealing from distributed computing and putting coordination decisions on a shared DAG is a genuine transfer, and the numbers back it: half the token cost, a fifth of the overwrites, and emergent verification that fires precisely when it should. The evaluation gap they expose—nobody was counting how often agents undo each other's work—is real and overdue.

The cooperative-profiles paper delivers a solid empirical finding withone result that should worry anyone banking on prompting strategies: Theory-of-Mind prompting improves game behavior but transfers zero to actual science tasks. Stylized cooperation ≠ realistic collaboration. If you are prompting for cooperation and measuring success in a toy setting, you may be optimizing the wrong thing.

CAFE is conceptually the freshest paper today and the one with the thinnest empirical footing. The response-surface R² sits in the mid-forties; the domain is single (banking risk); the adaptation loop is open. But the core inversion—stress deforms the response surface, the deformation carries architecture-specific structure, and that structure can be measured before anyone closes the loop—deserves a second look precisely because it reframes the problem.

The active-learning paper is competent engineering in service of a real problem: random task selection for graph optimization is unsound, and the worst-case failures it causes are the ones that matter most when agents might be adversarial. The EKI trick is clean. But the contribution is a narrower fix compared to the structural moves in LATTE or Uno-Orchestra.

The through-line: measuring multi-agent systems before you commit to them—whether through games, active task selection, stress diagnostics, or task-graph legibility—is no longer a nice-to-have. It is the only way to tell the difference between an architecture that degrades gracefully and one that just degrades quietly.
