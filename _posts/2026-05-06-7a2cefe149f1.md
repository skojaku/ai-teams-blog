*TL;DR: Most multi-agent LLM failures come from bad coordination, not weak models—and this paper shows you can predict which coordination pattern produces which failure by treating coordination as a separable architectural layer and decomposing the resulting error signatures.*

## The 41–87% Problem

Multi-agent LLM systems fail in production at rates between 41% and 87%.

That number should sting. These are not toy benchmarks. These are deployed systems with real users.

And the culprit, overwhelmingly, is not the base model. It is coordination: ambiguous specifications, misaligned agents, verification gaps.

Cemri et al. catalogued 14 distinct failure modes across 1,600+ execution traces in their MAST study—79% of failures traced back to specification and coordination issues, not model capability.

But here is the gap. MAST tells you *that* coordination breaks.

It cannot tell you *which* coordination pattern breaks *how*. A practitioner staring at an architectural diagram still cannot predict which failure mode their system is most likely to exhibit.

Nechepurenko and Shuvalov argue that this gap exists because coordination has never been treated as a configurable architectural layer—named, isolated, and varied independently from the model and the information it can access.

## Three Layers, Not Two

The paper proposes splitting a multi-agent LLM system into three layers:

1. **The information layer** — tools, retrieved context, external sensors. What data any agent can reach.
2. **The coordination layer** — who exists, who talks to whom, who decides, when things stop, and how outputs combine. Pure structure.
3. **The agent layer** — the per-agent LLM call, role prompt, per-agent tool wrappers. Individual capability.

The coordination layer is specified by seven elements: agent endpoints, communication topology, authority distribution, synchronization regime, aggregation rules, termination conditions, and failure-handling policy. It refers to agents only by interface, not by internal model.

This is not a new framework. You can implement it on top of AutoGen, CrewAI, LangGraph, or any existing system without modification. The contribution is conceptual: once you name and isolate the coordination layer, you can make *predictions* about it.

## What Becomes Predictable

Once coordination is an explicit spec, four things move from implicit-in-code to explicit-in-spec.

**Decision provenance** — every output traces back to a specific agent acting under a specific delegation. Audit logs become structurally complete.

**Failure-mode signatures** — each configuration induces a characteristic error pathway.

Peer exchange can be error-amplifying (reinforcing a shared misconception). Orchestrator structures can be authority-cascading (one bad call propagates through subtasks).

Consensus loops can be drift-collapsing (diverse views collapse to a single anchor). Sequential pipelines can be upstream-fragile (early errors cascade downstream with no chance of correction).

**Cross-system comparability** — same coordination + different agents means you can compare agents. Same agents + different coordination means you can compare architectures. Today neither comparison is well-defined because both axes vary together.

**Heterogeneity handling** — when different roles need different tool access or content policies, the explicit layer makes the mismatch specifiable rather than silently broken at runtime.

## The Information-Confound Problem

Before we get to the predictions, we need to understand why clean experiments are so hard.

Ao et al. established a non-identifiability theorem: when two architectures differ simultaneously in tool access, retrieved context, or transcript length, no observed performance difference can be attributed to coordination rather than information. "Any observed gain may reflect added information rather than improved delegation."

This critique applies to the bulk of published multi-agent LLM evaluations. A planner-worker setup compared against a single-agent baseline typically differs in at least three of: total inference budget, distinct context windows, tool calls, and LLM-LLM message exchanges.

Without fixing these, attributing differences to "better coordination" is methodologically unsound.

No published study had previously satisfied the information-fixing requirement. Nechepurenko and Shuvalov treat it as a hard constraint.

## The Information-Controlled Design

The experiment holds five things constant across all configurations: the underlying model (claude-opus-4-6), the available tools, the prompt template, the per-call output token cap (1,500 tokens), and the question set (100 Polymarket binary markets resolved after the model's training cutoff).

What varies? Only the coordination layer.

Compute is deliberately *not* held constant. A sequential pipeline that propagates context across three stages necessarily consumes more tokens than three parallel agents seeing only the question. Treating total compute as an endogenous output of each architecture—reporting it and incorporating it into cost–quality analysis—preserves architectural integrity rather than breaking it.

Prediction markets are the testbed for a reason. Each question resolves to a binary outcome at a known future date, independent of any judge.

Questions about future events cannot have leaked into training data. And the Brier score is a strictly proper scoring rule: it cannot be gamed by misrepresenting beliefs.

## Five Configurations, Five Predictions

The paper spans the design space along two axes—centralization and information sharing—using five reference configurations:

![Five coordination configurations arranged by centralization and information-sharing axes, showing independent ensemble, peer-critique debate, orchestrator-specialist, sequential pipeline, and consensus alignment topologies]({{ "/assets/figures/7a2cefe149f1/fig_011_02.png" | relative_url }})

**Independent ensemble.** Three agents respond independently; outputs are averaged. Predicted signature: moderate calibration error (REL), high discriminative power (RES).

Diversity is preserved through to aggregation. Failure mode: if agents share correlated biases, averaging produces a confidently wrong consensus.

**Peer-critique debate.** Agents observe each other's outputs and revise over two rounds. Predicted signature: REL improves (cross-correction), RES declines (alignment pressure suppresses dissent).

Failure mode: premature convergence on a plausible-but-wrong answer.

**Orchestrator-specialist.** A planner decomposes the question, dispatches to specialists, and integrates. Predicted signature: low REL, moderate RES.

The orchestrator imposes a final calibration step. Failure mode: the orchestrator is a single-point error source; mistakes cascade through delegated subtasks.

**Sequential pipeline.** Fixed-order stages (research → analysis → forecast), each consuming its predecessor's output. Predicted signature: both REL and RES depend critically on stage 1.

Downstream stages cannot recover from upstream miscalibration. Failure mode: early errors cascade with no architectural opportunity for correction.

**Consensus alignment.** Agents iterate until disagreement falls below a threshold (ε = 0.05 in probability units) or three rounds expire. Predicted signature: very low REL, very low RES.

Forced agreement collapses diversity; the system speaks with one voice, often anchored on the market consensus itself.

The predictions are qualitative—low, moderate, high relative to a market-consensus baseline—because the empirical question is the *relative ordering* of configurations.

## Reading the Murphy Decomposition

The key diagnostic tool is Murphy's decomposition of the Brier score:

$$B = UNC + REL - RES$$

UNC is the irreducible uncertainty of the question set. REL is reliability—calibration error, the squared gap between stated probability and realized frequency. RES is resolution—discriminative power, the squared gap between bin-conditional frequency and the base rate.

These are independent properties. A forecaster can be well-calibrated but uninformative (low REL, low RES) or overconfident but discriminating (moderate REL, high RES).

A single Brier number can show no overall difference between two configurations that produce qualitatively different failure modes. The decomposition recovers the architectural signal that the aggregate score hides.

## What They Found

Three of five pre-specified Murphy-signature predictions are upheld in the predicted direction. The other two are reported as failed—no burying.

A Pareto frontier of two configurations dominates the others on cost-adjusted accuracy within this information regime. The paper's abstract does not name the two, but the structural logic suggests architectures that preserve diversity or impose a single calibration step fare better than those that collapse diversity or propagate early errors.

![Murphy decomposition scatter plot showing calibration error versus discriminative power for the five configurations with error bars]({{ "/assets/figures/7a2cefe149f1/fig_018_03.png" | relative_url }})

Bootstrap power-projection suggests the clearest separation is consensus alignment versus the rest—but pairwise tests do not survive Bonferroni correction at n = 100. The sample is large enough to make architectural separation visible, not large enough to reach corrected significance on adjacent pairs.

All five configurations were also deployed as live agents on Foresight Arena under web-search-enabled conditions, providing an independent on-chain replication channel.

## Honest Limits

n = 100 is the elephant in the room. It is enough to see the architectural separation, but not enough for statistically robust pairwise comparisons under Bonferroni correction.

The authors are explicit: this is a methodology-validating first instantiation, not a general claim about cross-model or cross-domain architectural laws.

Compute is not held constant across configurations, by design. A sequential pipeline burns ≈3.6× the tokens of an independent ensemble; an orchestrator ≈3.2×. These cost differences are real, and the paper folds them into the Pareto analysis rather than pretending they do not exist—but it means absolute token-count comparisons are confounded with architectural structure.

The prompt templates are fixed, and prompt sensitivity is a known unknown. Different role prompts might interact with the model's training in unpredictable ways. The released harness permits external re-runs with alternative formulations, but the paper does not sweep over prompt variations.

Web search is disabled throughout the main experiment—a choice that maximizes internal control but sacrifices ecological validity. The on-chain deployment with web search enabled partially addresses this, but that data accumulates in parallel and is not the primary analysis.

## Why This Matters

The architecture-information separation is the methodological contribution that could outlast the specific results. Every prior multi-agent evaluation that confounded coordination with information access—and Ao et al. showed that is essentially all of them—now has a clear corrective.

The information-fixing principle is implementable today, on any framework, at the cost of experimental discipline rather than engineering effort.

The coordination-layer specification itself is immediately adoptable. It can be implemented atop existing frameworks without modification, turning an implicit tangle of who-calls-whom into an explicit, auditable, predictable structure.

The declarative orchestration wave—AWS Strands and Microsoft Foundry exposing YAML-driven workflow definitions, Daunis et al.'s DSL cutting development time by 67%—already built the plumbing. This paper shows what that plumbing is *for* beyond shipping faster: it enables architectural reasoning about failure.

The Murphy decomposition as a diagnostic for coordination failure is a conceptual move that costs nothing to adopt and reveals what aggregate scores hide. Two configurations with the same Brier score can have diametrically opposed failure modes—one overconfident and discriminating, the other well-calibrated and inert. If you are deploying multi-agent systems, you need to know which kind of wrong you are.

Coordination is not a downstream implementation detail. It is an architectural layer—and once you name it, you can predict how it fails.
