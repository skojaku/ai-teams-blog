*TL;DR: A single LLM agent beats a multi-agent team in 43.3% of tested scenarios, and the paper shows you can predict which outcome you'll get just by watching entropy in the first round—uncertainty dynamics, not agent count, govern success.*

You spin up a debate-style multi-agent system—multiple agents, five rounds, full inter-agent visibility—on a math problem. A single agent with no collaborators solves it correctly. The team doesn't.

This isn't a rare edge case. Across five open-source models and six benchmarks, single-agent systems achieve the highest accuracy in 13 out of 30 scenarios and match or exceed multi-agent systems in 26 out of 30. More agents, more rounds, more compute—and worse results nearly half the time.

![Grouped bar chart comparing single-agent (SAS) accuracy versus multi-agent (MAS) accuracy across models and datasets, showing SAS often outperforming MAS]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

The question isn't whether multi-agent systems fail. Cemri et al. already catalogued the failure modes: communication breakdowns, inter-agent misalignment, insufficient verification. The question is *can you see it coming?*

Zhao, Chen, and Su say yes—and the signal is entropy.

## From Accuracy Scores to Entropy Traces

Prior scaling work from Kim et al. established that adding agents yields diminishing or negative returns. But it relied on aggregate metrics like accuracy and latency, which tell you *that* performance degrades, not *why*.

He et al. offered an information-theoretic view—only for the compression-prediction pipeline.

This paper broadens the lens: full entropy trajectories across four topologies, five models, and six tasks.

The core insight: **uncertainty dynamics—not architecture, not agent count—are the governing factor**, and they lock in early.

## Reading Entropy Across the Team

For every decoding step of every agent in every round, the authors log the token-level entropy:

$$H(s) = -\sum_{v \in \mathcal{V}} \pi_\theta(v \mid s) \log \pi_\theta(v \mid s)$$

Then they stack these raw signals into a hierarchy:

- **Agent-level**: per-agent trajectory statistics, inter-agent divergence, and round-to-round variation (156 features)
- **Round-level**: aggregate entropy per round and relative changes across rounds (27 features)
- **Sample-level**: entropy aggregated across all agents for a given problem (29 features)
- **System-level**: global uncertainty patterns compared across topologies (10 features)
- **Base-model entropy**: how uncertain the underlying LLM is when solving alone, and how much entropy shifts when it enters a team (17 features)

Plus 15 computational features covering reasoning time, token usage, and base-model correctness. After removing experimental identifiers: **245 trainable features** from entropy traces and computational metrics combined.

## Turning Entropy into a Verdict

Here's the clever move. The authors reformulate MAS evaluation as supervised learning.

For each sample, they have entropy features $\mathbf{x}_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ and a binary correctness label $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$. They train an ensemble of XGBoost and LightGBM to predict $f(\mathbf{x}_i) \in [0, 1]$—the probability the sample was solved correctly. Then they crack open the models with SHAP values to see which features actually drive predictions.

Two metrics per feature: mean importance $\bar{I}_j$ (how much the feature matters across both models) and SHAP correlation $\rho_j$ (the sign tells you whether higher values help or hurt).

**Entropy alone is highly predictive of correctness.** Using only MAS-derived features, the Entropy Judger achieves cross-validation accuracy of 0.726 on LLaMA and 0.791 on Qwen. Add base-model features and it climbs to 0.812 and 0.916.

## The First Round Writes the Story

This is the paper's most striking finding.

With $R = 5$ rounds of interaction, you'd expect later rounds to carry real diagnostic weight. They don't. Round-1 features dominate the top predictors.

Peak cumulative agent entropy in round 1 ranks second by importance ($\bar{I} \approx 0.60$, $\rho \approx -0.91$). Upper-quartile inter-agent entropy dispersion in round 1 also lands in the top five.

The entropy trajectory tells the story: maximum, mean, and total entropy all drop sharply from round 1 to round 2, then nearly flatline from round 2 through round 5.

![Round-by-round entropy evolution showing that maximum, mean, and total entropy drop sharply after round 1 and stabilize by round 2]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_019_08.png" | relative_url }})

Agents stabilize after the second round. Additional rounds just amplify noise without refining reasoning.

The principle is stark: **MAS effectiveness is largely determined in the first round, and additional deliberation cannot reliably recover from initial misalignment.**

## Three Things Entropy Tells You

### Certainty Preference

Reducing peak entropy and maintaining stable uncertainty across agents strongly correlates with correctness. On GSM8K, the stability index—measuring consistency of entropy across agents—has $\bar{I} \approx 0.44$ and $\rho \approx -0.79$.

High entropy variance across agents during problem solving is the top predictor of failure for Qwen ($\rho \approx -0.92$, $\bar{I} \approx 0.83$).

Low, stable entropy wins.

### Base Uncertainty

The uncertainty of the underlying model constrains the team. For LLaMA, base-model entropy has $\rho \approx -0.73$ with MAS accuracy; for Qwen, $\rho \approx -0.64$.

Higher base entropy consistently drags the whole system down.

Interesting divergence in reasoning styles, though. LLaMA tends to reuse others' answers without verification, propagating errors. Qwen self-corrects before finalizing, suppressing error propagation despite higher absolute entropy.

### Task Awareness

The optimal entropy profile shifts with problem difficulty.

Simple math (GSM8K) demands rapid convergence to low, stable entropy. Medium problems (MATH500) benefit from moderate exploration—some uncertainty aids discovery, but uncontrolled divergence kills consensus. Hard problems (AIME) require long reasoning with bounded uncertainty: early exploration must be contained, and agents must stay aligned.

Knowledge Q&A (MMLU) is different entirely—success hinges on inter-agent agreement, not deliberation length.

## When More Rounds Hurt

Extending from 2 to 5 rounds rarely helps and often degrades accuracy, especially for smaller models on hard benchmarks.

The only architecture that consistently gains from more rounds is centralized—because a strong orchestrator can re-aggregate longer trajectories.

Debate and hybrid architectures suffer from prolonged disagreement: repeated interactions without convergence amplify noise.

Token consumption goes up. Accuracy goes down.

The entropy diagnostic explains why: nothing new is happening after round 2.

## The RL Plot Twist

The authors test Qwen2.5-7B-SimpleRL-Zoo—a model fine-tuned with zero-shot reinforcement learning—as the base model. The results flip the script.

With a standard base model, SAS beats MAS 43.3% of the time. With the RL-trained base, **MAS never loses to SAS.** Not once.

The entropy-accuracy relationship inverts too. For a standard base model, higher entropy monotonically reduces accuracy. For the RL base, accuracy peaks at near-zero entropy (easy problems needing little reasoning), dips as entropy rises, then *recovers*—because RL training amplifies longer deliberation, letting agents explore structured solution paths under controlled uncertainty.

![Plot showing that for the RL-trained base model, accuracy peaks at near-zero entropy, dips at moderate entropy, then recovers—inverting the standard monotonic decline]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_019_07.png" | relative_url }})

**Better base models through RL reshape uncertainty dynamics.** If your base model produces reliable entropy signals, the team can coordinate effectively. If it can't, no topology will save you.

## Putting the Judger to Work

Beyond predicting correctness, the Entropy Judger enables label-free pass@k selection. When a multi-agent system generates $k$ candidate solutions, the Judger picks the one with the highest predicted correctness—no ground-truth labels required at test time.

It consistently improves accuracy across all MAS configurations and all tasks.

The training phase does need per-sample correctness labels, which limits applicability in domains without verifiable answers. But for math, code, and factual Q&A—where correctness is checkable—it's a cheap, effective filter.

## Honest Limits

The study is restricted to small open-source LLMs ($\leq$8B parameters).

Whether these findings transfer to frontier proprietary models is genuinely unknown, and not just for the usual reason—token-level probabilities are unavailable through APIs. You simply cannot compute entropy the same way from a GPT-4 endpoint.

The Entropy Judger's training requires ground-truth correctness labels. Selection at test time is label-free, but you need labeled data to train the selector. Domains without verifiable answers are out of scope.

And there's a subtlety in the entropy-performance relationship across architectures: single-agent systems show *higher* average entropy than multi-agent systems yet achieve *better* accuracy.

MAS coordination pressure can prematurely suppress productive uncertainty, yielding lower entropy without improved accuracy. The relationship between entropy and performance depends on the architecture's capacity to *channel* uncertainty, not just minimize it.

## Where to Invest Your Effort

This paper gives practitioners something they didn't have before: a cheap, principled diagnostic for when multi-agent systems will help versus hurt.

Monitor early-round entropy. If agents disagree loudly in round 1, more rounds won't fix it. If base-model entropy is high, adding collaborators makes things worse, not better.

The deeper lesson is about where to invest effort. The field has spent enormous energy on topology design—debate versus centralized versus hybrid. This paper says the bigger lever is the base model.

RL fine-tuning that produces reliable uncertainty estimates does more for multi-agent performance than any amount of architectural orchestration.

The entropy doesn't lie. You just have to listen before round two.
