*TL;DR: When LLM agents debate on social networks, they drift systematically toward agreement regardless of which side holds the majority — an intrinsic bias called "agreement drift" that could invalidate a whole class of social simulations.*

## A Hundred Agents Walk Into a Network

Picture 100 LLM agents arranged on a scale-free social network, each holding an opinion on the Ship of Theseus paradox.

Half say "it's the same ship"; half say "it isn't."

You'd expect a tug-of-war — maybe convergence, maybe polarization, maybe some messy middle.

Instead, the agents slide uphill on the opinion scale toward *agree*, even when nobody has a numerical advantage.

Not because one side argued better.

Because the models find "I agree" fundamentally more persuasive than "I disagree."

Cau, Failla, and Rossetti call this **agreement drift**, and it changes how we should read every LLM-based social simulation published so far.

## Why This Matters Now

A growing body of work treats LLM agents as stand-ins for human populations in computational social science.

The premise is seductive: prompt an army of LLMs with demographic traits, drop them on a network, and watch opinion dynamics unfold. Several recent studies do exactly this, simulating opinion dynamics with networks of LLM-based agents and showing that multi-agent debate can improve factuality and reasoning.

But if convergence in LLM societies is partly an artifact of model-intrinsic persuasive asymmetry — not genuine consensus — then every downstream policy or behavioral inference built on top is on shaky ground.

Minority voices may be suppressed not by any social mechanism the researchers intended to model, but by a directional bias baked into how the models process persuasive arguments.

## Building the Debate Graph

The authors place 100 LLM agents on scale-free networks generated with a **BA-homophily model** — a Barabási–Albert preferential-attachment skeleton augmented with a homophily parameter.

Each agent starts with an opinion on a 7-point Likert scale ($0$ = strongly disagree, $6$ = strongly agree) about the Ship of Theseus paradox — chosen precisely because it has no factual answer, so convergence can't be driven by correctness.

The key knobs the authors turn:

- **Homophily** $h \in \{0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1\}$ — from pure heterophily (agents only befriend the out-group) to pure homophily (birds of a feather, no cross-group links).
- **Class imbalance** — 50-50, 70-30, 90-10 splits between groups.
- **Forward vs. reversed** — which side holds the majority? If outcomes flip when you swap, it's structural. If they don't, something deeper is going on.
- **Neighborhood awareness** — some agents get to see the opinion distribution of their local ego-network before they decide.

At each iteration, every agent picks a random neighbor to debate. One plays the discussant, the other the opponent. The opponent argues persuasively. The discussant may **accept** (shift opinion toward the opponent by $\pm 1$), **reject** (shift away — a backfire effect), or **ignore** (no change).

## How Agreement Drift Works

Here's the core finding, stated bluntly: when two agents with opposing views interact, movement toward the positive end of the opinion scale is more likely than movement toward the negative end.

This is not sycophancy.

Sycophancy is an LLM aligning with a user's *explicitly stated belief* to seem helpful. Agreement drift is different — it is a directional susceptibility in which positions expressing *endorsement* of the discussion statement exert systematically greater persuasive pull than positions expressing rejection.

You can see it in the transition probability matrices. Even in perfectly balanced 50-50 populations, upward transitions beat downward ones.

The asymmetry survives across homophily levels, class distributions, and model variants.

It is not a majority effect.

It is not an artifact of network structure.

It is baked into the interaction dynamics of the models themselves.

## What They Found

**Balanced populations converge fast.**

For $h \leq 0.75$, the system snaps to strongly agree within 20–40 iterations.

Lower homophily accelerates the slide; higher homophily slows it but doesn't stop it.

Only at $h = 1$ — complete homophily, where agents almost never meet someone from the other side — does the system settle into polarized segregation.

![Opinion trajectories in a balanced 50-50 population across homophily levels, showing rapid convergence toward strongly agree within 20–40 iterations]({{ "/assets/figures/17df23a3ead7/fig_007_02.png" | relative_url }})

**Minorities vanish faster.**

At 70-30, consensus hits in 20–30 iterations.

At 90-10, the minority disappears almost immediately for low homophily. The larger the imbalance, the quicker the minority dissolves.

**But reversed majorities don't behave symmetrically.**

This is the tell.

If you make the *disagree* side the majority instead of the *agree* side, the outcome changes.

![Forward vs. reversed opinion trajectories showing that disagreeing majorities resist full convergence, revealing the asymmetric agreement drift]({{ "/assets/figures/17df23a3ead7/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

Disagreeing majorities are more resistant to full convergence — they form a dominant cluster that doesn't reach consensus.

The drift toward agreement is real, but a large disagreeing majority can resist it by sheer volume, limiting cross-opinion encounters.

**Gemma drifts harder than Llama.**

The authors replicate key conditions with Gemma 3 and find a systematically stronger drift toward positive opinions. Negatively oriented agents disappear rapidly for all $h \leq 0.75$.

Even at $h = 1$, where Llama agents stay polarized at the extremes, Gemma agents reinforce the positive side. Upward transition probabilities approach $1$ in some conditions.

### Neighborhood Awareness Acts Like Peer Pressure

When agents can see their neighbors' opinion distribution, the dynamics shift.

Convergence still happens, but it stops at *moderate* agreement (agree, not strongly agree). Opinion change becomes contingent on local alignment — agents shift when their neighbors support the shift and resist when they don't.

It is analogous to classical peer-pressure effects: the ego-network acts as a selective amplifier, reinforcing locally consistent changes while still permitting occasional counter-aligned moves.

Under extreme class imbalance, the minority slowly moves toward the majority's opinion — unlike the base scenario, where minorities simply vanish.

## The Honest Limits

One discussion topic. The Ship of Theseus is deliberately non-empirical, but different prompting strategies or semantically grounded statements may alter drift strength.

One network model. 100-agent scale-free networks from one generative process. Larger populations and alternative topologies could introduce new mesoscopic effects.

Two LLM families. The stark gap between Llama and Gemma demands a systematic cross-model audit. The paper acknowledges this directly: "the strong differences observed between LLM families call for a systematic analysis across models."

Single simulation per configuration. The authors run one simulation on a fixed network realization for each setting, isolating structural effects but not capturing stochastic variance.

## What This Means Beyond This Paper

If you are building LLM-based social simulations, agreement drift is a confound you must characterize before trusting any result about consensus, polarization, or minority survival.

The paper provides the tools: the **LLM-OD framework** and the methodology of conditional transition probability matrices validated against permutation-based null models give you a concrete audit procedure. Estimate $P(\text{disc} \to \text{opp} \mid o_{\text{disc}}, o_{\text{opp}})$, compare it to a shuffled null, and see whether the persuasive asymmetry is significant.

The broader lesson is uncomfortable. LLMs don't just simulate social behavior — they introduce a directional bias that has no clear analogue in human opinion dynamics, where persuasive influence is typically assumed to be directionally neutral.

Convergence in an LLM society is not the same thing as consensus.

The ship stays the same only if the agents can't talk.
