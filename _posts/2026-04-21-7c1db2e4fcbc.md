*TL;DR: When a majority of agents in your LLM ensemble are compromised, majority voting doesn't just fail—it confidently returns the wrong answer. This paper replaces vote-counting with token-level round-robin collaboration, where honest agents can interrupt and correct corrupted logic mid-generation, surviving even super-majority adversarial corruption.*

## The Hook That Sinks the Vote

Three out of five agents in your ensemble are quietly hijacked.

Maybe a travel-planning assistant retrieved the same poisoned search result. Maybe a sponsor bought prompt injections across two independent LLM providers.

The two honest agents reason beautifully—and their correct answer gets outvoted 3-to-2.

Majority voting returns the wrong answer with full confidence, and there is nothing the minority can do about it.

That is the consensus trap.

It is not a hypothetical edge case; it is a structural inevitability whenever corrupted agents form a majority.

## When the Count Betrays You

Wang et al.'s Self-Consistency (2023) popularized majority voting for chain-of-thought reasoning: sample multiple reasoning paths, take the mode.

The math works beautifully when errors are independent—a Condorcet jury theorem for LLMs, where more samples monotonically push accuracy toward 100%.

But that independence assumption is the load-bearing wall.

Conitzer and Sandholm (2005) formalized voting rules as maximum likelihood estimators under *independent* noise. Correlated adversarial manipulation shatters that premise.

When a sponsor's prompt injection shifts the entire distribution for three out of five agents, voting does not average out the bias—it amplifies it.

Du et al. (2024) tried multi-agent debate as an alternative.

It works when the distortion is mild.

Under majority corruption, debate degrades into a sycophantic echo chamber: the corrupted majority generates persuasive rationalizations, and the honest minority gets talked down.

The paper proves this is not fixable at the outcome level.

Proposition 1—the Impossibility Trinity—establishes that no anonymous, symmetric outcome-level aggregation can be robust to both minority and majority corruption simultaneously.

You cannot vote your way out.

## One Chain, Many Authors

The escape hatch is deceptively simple.

Stop having agents vote on final answers. Instead, have them write a single shared answer together—one token chunk at a time.

Here is how Token-Level Round-Robin (RR) works.

Agent 1 writes K tokens into a shared context. Agent 2 reads the full context so far, then writes the next K tokens. Agent 3 continues, and so on in a fixed rotation.

When an honest agent encounters corrupted logic in the shared prefix, it can interrupt mid-derivation—writing something like "Wait, the previous calculation was erroneous, so 3+1=4."

Once that correction lands in the context, it structurally constrains what every subsequent agent (including corrupted ones) can coherently generate.

You then run M independent RR trajectories and majority-vote across *those* outputs (called RRMaj).

The defense is two-layer: microscopic correction within each trajectory, macroscopic noise reduction across trajectories.

The cost? Exactly zero extra decoding steps.

N agents each generating L tokens independently under MAJ costs N × L sequential decoding steps. N independent RR trajectories generating L tokens each costs the same N × L. Context-switching adds negligible pre-fill overhead that modern GPUs handle in parallel.

## How the Attractor Works

The key insight is a shift from linear counting to non-linear operator composition.

Under majority voting, you are counting votes for each answer:

$$\sum_i \mathbb{1}(a_i = y)$$

This is a linear tally—corrupted votes and honest votes carry identical weight.

Under RR, the shared trajectory is a product of transition operators:

$$h_{t+1} = T_i \, h_t$$

where each $T_i$ is the generative step of agent $i$ in latent semantic space. The honest operator $T_H$ contracts the state toward the truth direction $\theta$; the corrupted operator $T_C$ deviates from it.

Crucially, $T_C$'s deviation is bottlenecked by sycophancy.

Because LLMs compute next tokens via softmax, the probability of generating tokens that contradict an established factual context decays exponentially with the logit gap.

The paper formalizes this as the Sycophancy Bottleneck. Define a Logistic Lyapunov function:

$$V(h_t) = \ln\!\bigl(1 + \exp(-\langle h_t, \theta\rangle)\bigr)$$

Honest agents strictly dissipate this potential ($\Delta V < 0$), pulling the trajectory toward a Truth Attractor.

Corrupted agents can only deviate by an amount bounded by $\delta(V(h_t))$—and $\delta \to 0$ as the state aligns with truth.

The more correct the prefix, the less runway the adversary has.

Theorem 1 then proves: under realistic parameters—$\gamma_H = 0.03$ (honest recovery rate per token), $\delta(V) = 0.004V$ (adversarial drift), $K = 100$ (chunk size)—the system is Lyapunov stable up to corruption ratio $\rho_{\max} \approx 66.0\%$.

That is super-majority resilience. The honest minority can overpower the corrupted majority because autoregressive coherence is a stronger force than injected instructions.

## Where the Count Flips

The numbers are stark.

Under moderate advisory injection with Llama-3.3-70B in a 5-agent ensemble (3 corrupted, 2 truthful), majority voting on Track7 collapses to **0.8%** accuracy.

RRMaj rescues it to **89.1%**—an +88.3% gain.

On GSM8K, MAJ drops to 19.4%; RRMaj recovers to 87.6%.

Averaged across six reasoning benchmarks for Llama-3.3-70B under majority corruption, RRMaj achieves +49.6% accuracy recovery.

Under the stronger imperative injection ("You MUST choose (C)"), MAJ plummets to near-zero—1.2% for Llama-3.3-70B on GSM8K.

RRMaj still manages +68.1% and +69.9% gains on GSM8K for Llama-3.3-70B and Llama-4-Scout-17B respectively.

The most striking finding: **weak truthful agents can rescue strong corrupted models, but not the reverse.**

Two truthful 8B agents paired with three corrupted 70B agents recover to 78.7% via RRMaj.

Flip it—four corrupted 8B agents with one truthful 70B—and you gain only +1.8%.

When a small model injects a valid mathematical step, the large model's own reasoning engine and perplexity penalty force it to abandon the adversarial prompt and complete the proof.

The small model lacks the logical inertia to resist the crowd.

## The Chunk-Size Sweet Spot

Not every K works.

Below $K \le 30$, context-switching fragments reasoning so badly that even honest agents cannot articulate a coherent deduction.

Above $K \ge 500$, corrupted agents get enough semantic runway to complete a full malicious payload before anyone can intervene.

The sweet spot sits at $K \approx 100$–300 tokens: enough room for an honest agent to articulate a corrective step, fast enough to interrupt before the adversary locks in a biased trajectory.

## The Condorcet Death Spiral—in Reverse

Adding more compute to a broken system makes it worse.

When MAJ samples more trajectories under majority corruption, accuracy *decreases*—from 46.2% down to 25.9% as M scales to 40. The law of large numbers amplifies the wrong answer.

RRMaj flips this.

Because token-level interleaving mechanically pushes each trajectory's expected accuracy above 0.5, scaling M from 1 to 40 yields a monotonic climb from 59.5% to 74.9%.

More compute becomes a truth amplifier instead of a failure multiplier.

## Where the Attractor Fails

Two caveats matter.

First, the truth-attractor mechanism relies on verifiable intermediate reasoning steps. On tasks without objective step-wise structure—open-ended generation, subjective QA—the corrective loop has nothing concrete to latch onto.

Second, the paper evaluates static, non-adaptive adversaries. An adaptive adversary that monitors the shared context and strategically overrides corrections could undermine the approach. The defense works because corrupted agents are constrained by sycophancy; a smarter attacker might find prompts that resist the context's logical gravity.

## From Counting to Sharing

Response-level aggregation treats agents as black boxes that emit final answers and then counts votes.

Token-level collaboration treats them as co-authors of a single reasoning chain—where every correction becomes a constraint on what comes next.

You don't vote your way out of a consensus trap. You write your way out.
