*TL;DR: Adding a reviewer agent that inspects tool calls before they execute—no retraining required—boosts irrelevance detection by 5.5% and multi-turn task scores by 7.1%, and the authors introduce a Helpfulness-Harmfulness framework that shows reasoning models make surprisingly good referees.*

## The Bug You Can't Un-Send

An agent deletes a user's alarm instead of updating it. By the time self-correction kicks in, the state is gone. No amount of context-window heroics will bring it back.

That's the state-recovery problem in a nutshell: once a destructive tool call executes, you're not fixing the error—you're doing damage control. Self-Refine and Reflexion, two well-known inference-time correction methods, ask the *same* model to generate *and* critique its own calls. That's a conflict of interest, and it still can't pre-empt the call before it hits the API.

Reinforced Agent proposes a cleaner split: a separate reviewer agent evaluates every provisional tool call *before* execution. No retraining. No complex context management. Just a second opinion at the gate.

## Two Agents, One Checkpoint

The architecture is straightforward. A primary tool-calling agent (GPT-4o throughout the experiments) generates a provisional call. A specialized reviewer agent inspects it and either approves it or injects feedback for revision.

![Architecture diagram showing the progressive feedback loop: a base agent generates provisional tool calls, the reviewer evaluates them before execution, and either approves or sends feedback for revision]({{ "/assets/figures/7645a37fcdf4/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

Three review strategies are tested:

- **Progressive Feedback (rN):** The reviewer flags errors and sends feedback back. The base agent revises. This loops up to N times or until the reviewer approves.
- **Best-of-N Selection:** Generate N candidates at different temperatures, then a selector picks the best one—no iteration, no feedback.
- **Best-of-N Grading:** Same idea, but the grader assigns explicit scores (0.0–1.0) with rationales before choosing.

Progressive Feedback wins. On τ²-Bench, it hits 55.8% vs. the baseline's 48.7%. Selection and grading trail by 3–8 percentage points. The iterative loop simply catches more than a one-shot vote.

![Bar chart comparing Progressive Feedback, Best-of-N Selection, and Grading on τ²-Bench, where Progressive Feedback leads at 55.8% against the 48.7% baseline]({{ "/assets/figures/7645a37fcdf4/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

## How Helpful Is Helpful, Really?

Here's the subtlety that makes this paper valuable: a reviewer can *introduce* errors while fixing others. No prior work quantifies this tradeoff systematically.

The authors introduce two metrics:

- **Helpfulness** — the percentage of base-agent errors the reviewer corrects.
- **Harmfulness** — the percentage of correct responses the reviewer degrades.

Their ratio is the benefit-to-risk ratio, and it's the number you should stare at.

o3-mini as reviewer: 36.8% helpfulness, 11.7% harmfulness → **3.1:1 ratio**.

GPT-4o as reviewer: 34.9% helpfulness, 12.9% harmfulness → **2.7:1 ratio**.

![Helpfulness vs. Harmfulness for o3-mini and GPT-4o reviewers, showing o3-mini corrects more errors while breaking fewer correct calls]({{ "/assets/figures/7645a37fcdf4/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

The reasoning model doesn't just catch more errors—it breaks fewer correct calls. Systematic verification reduces over-correction.

## The Over-Skepticism Bug

The most common reviewer failure isn't missing errors. It's *inventing* them.

On BFCL, 23% of review loops were redundant: the reviewer demanded elaboration on a tool-call-only response, complaining it lacked a "follow-up explanation for the user." But BFCL evaluates tool-call accuracy, not conversational flair. The call was already correct.

A single explicit prompt guideline slashes this from 23% to 8%:

> [CRITICAL] Tool-only responses are complete. Do not mark tool-only responses as incomplete for lacking user-facing answers.

This over-skepticism pattern recurs on τ²-Bench in a different guise. The reviewer catches policy violations (−13 percentage points) and missing context awareness (−9pp), but *introduces* over-verbalization errors (+17pp) by demanding conversational padding where the benchmark expects concise tool calls.

The lesson: domain mismatch between reviewer expectations and evaluation criteria is the primary failure mode. Generic prompts don't generalize across benchmarks.

## Letting the Machine Write the Rules

Manual prompt engineering only gets you so far. The authors turn to GEPA — a genetic-Pareto prompt evolution method — to automatically refine reviewer prompts from failure cases. These experiments use GPT-5 mini as the reviewer, swapping in the newer reasoning model for optimization.

GEPA takes the manually-written v2 prompt (358 tokens) and evolves it into v3-gepa (1,599 tokens — roughly 4.5× longer). The optimized prompt includes detailed error criteria, edge-case handling instructions, and checklists that a human would be unlikely to write from scratch.

The gains on BFCL Non-Live:

- **+1.5%** on the relevance suite (91.0% → 92.5%)
- **+2.8%** on irrelevance detection (87.6% → 90.4%)
- **+2.1%** on the hardest category, `parallel_multiple`, catching mismatches and missing calls that manual prompts miss

The kicker: GEPA discovers strategies that are difficult to anticipate manually. The optimizer doesn't just scale up your instructions — it finds blind spots you didn't know existed.

## What It Costs

Latency is the obvious deployment concern.

| Benchmark | Baseline | With Reviewer | Multiplier |
|-----------|----------|---------------|------------|
| BFCL (single-turn) | 1.27s | 7.87s | 6.2× |
| τ²-Bench (multi-turn) | 158.7s | 384.3s | 2.4× |

Single-turn pays a steep price because the reviewer overhead dominates a single inference call. Multi-turn amortizes the cost across roughly 40 turns per episode.

For real-time, high-volume APIs, 6.2× latency may be prohibitive. For complex multi-turn workflows where a wrong call cascades into expensive remediation, 2.4× is a reasonable insurance premium.

## The Honest Boundaries

Three limits are worth naming sharply.

**Only GPT-4o was tested as the base agent.** The modular architecture doesn't impose model constraints in principle, but generalization to Llama, Mistral, or smaller models remains empirically unvalidated.

**GEPA and Helpfulness-Harmfulness analysis only cover BFCL.** Extending to τ²-Bench requires adapting for partial-credit scoring and multi-turn error propagation — not a trivial translation.

**Cross-benchmark generalization is limited.** BFCL-optimized prompts applied directly to τ²-Bench introduce errors. The domain mismatch between single-turn function calling and multi-turn stateful agents is real, and no single prompt solves both.

## Why Decoupling Wins

The core insight isn't about one reviewer beating another. It's about *separation of concerns* as a design principle for agent pipelines.

When execution and review are decoupled, you get a modular knob: swap the reviewer model, optimize its prompt independently, or eventually distill it into a lightweight classifier — all without touching the base agent. The Helpfulness-Harmfulness framework gives you a principled way to ask "is this knob actually helping?" before you commit to it.

The reviewer doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to be *net positive*. And now we have the ratio to prove it.

A second opinion that arrives before the damage is done.
