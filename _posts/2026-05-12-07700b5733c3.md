*TL;DR: A new benchmark called EntCollabBench forces 11 role-specialized agents to collaborate across permission boundaries in a simulated enterprise—and even the best model tops out at 62% accuracy, revealing that cross-agent handoffs, not individual skill, are the bottleneck.*

## The All-in-One Fiction

Pick any enterprise agent benchmark today and you'll find the same assumption: one omniscient agent with keys to every system. Real companies don't work that way. HR can't approve a procure-to-pay request. IT can't modify a customer escalation ticket.

Permission boundaries exist for a reason—compliance, security, accountability—and every handoff between roles is a place where context can silently die.

EntCollabBench is the first benchmark that takes this seriously.

## What EntCollabBench Builds

The authors simulate a full organization: 11 agents across 6 departments—IT, HR, Customer Service, Shared Services, Engineering, and an Approval Center—each with their own tool access and identity-authenticated service calls. No agent can call another department's tools directly. If the IT Service Desk needs HR to update a case, it must *delegate*, passing enough context for HR to act.

![Permission-isolated organization architecture showing six departments with role-specialized agents and cross-department communication flows]({{ "/assets/figures/07700b5733c3/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

The benchmark contains 300 tasks split into two structurally different subsets.

The **Workflow subset** (200 tasks) covers operational work: creating incidents, updating HR cases, scheduling meetings, submitting pull requests. Evaluation uses database state diffs and execution traces—objective, reproducible checks on whether the right systems ended up in the right state.

The **Approval subset** (100 tasks) covers policy-grounded decisions by finance, legal, and procurement specialists. Here, the ground truth comes from 290 deterministic policy rules extracted from the GitLab Handbook and GDPR articles, not from natural-language judgment.

Multi-step versions of both subsets require at least three cross-agent delegations across a full chain—so no single agent can solve the task alone.

## The Math of Permission Isolation

Formally, the setup is a constrained distributed execution problem defined by a four-tuple $(A, T, \pi, D)$:

- $A = \{a_1, \dots, a_{11}\}$, partitioned into operational and approval agents.
- $T = T_{\text{svc}} \cup T_{\text{del}}$, enterprise service tools plus a delegation tool $t_{\text{del}}^{a_j}$ for each reachable peer.
- $\pi: A \to 2^T$ maps each agent to its permitted tools; calls outside $\pi(a_i)$ are rejected at the server.
- $D: A \to 2^A$ specifies who each agent may delegate to.

The constraint $\{t_{\text{del}}^{a_j} : a_j \in D(a_i)\} \subseteq \pi(a_i)$ means delegation targets are explicitly part of an agent's permission set—you can only hand off work to roles you're authorized to reach.

This is not a game mechanic. It models the real organizational structure where `ask_hr_service_specialist_by_http` is a first-class action, and the message you send along with it determines whether the downstream agent can even begin its work.

## Skills Are Not the Bottleneck

Here is the central finding: individual agents are competent. Strong models exceed 80% accuracy at the role level. The catastrophe happens between roles.

DeepSeek-V4-Pro leads overall at 62.00%. Its subtask accuracy on multi-step workflow tasks reaches 78.33%—but full task accuracy drops to 50.00%. That 28-point gap is the cost of the delegation chain. One misrouted handoff, one missing parameter, one dropped context item, and the entire workflow collapses downstream.

![Grouped bar chart showing subtask accuracy versus full-task accuracy for multi-step workflow tasks across models, with DeepSeek-V4-Pro's 28-point gap highlighted]({{ "/assets/figures/07700b5733c3/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

Approval tasks show the same pattern in miniature. Single-step approval accuracy hits 80.00% for DeepSeek-V4-Flash. Multi-step approval? 40.00% at best (GPT-5-mini). The gap between local competence and end-to-end success is the story of this benchmark.

It is unambiguous: the problem is not whether an agent *can* use its tools. It is whether the right context survives the handoff.

## Where Handoffs Break

Trace analysis reveals five recurring failure modes.

**Delegation gaps.** Agents omit required handoffs, delegate before prerequisites are ready, or pass insufficient context. Downstream agents receive tasks without the article body, branch name, or business object they need—failure becomes inevitable even if the downstream agent itself is capable.

**Parameter-level grounding errors.** The correct tool family is selected, but wrong enum values, mixed-up relationship labels, or incorrect user fields fill the arguments. Enterprise tool use demands precision at the parameter level, not just the action level.

**Prefix decay.** Multi-step tasks degrade as the chain proceeds. Approval workflows mainly fail at the second step. Workflow tasks fail at the final handoff.

The agent at the end of the chain must bind upstream artifacts, its own role, and the downstream target—a triple conjunction that current models handle poorly.

**Collaboration tools as bottlenecks.** Email, Calendar, and Teams operations cause more failures than core business systems. Agents misuse sender identities, confuse account keys with email addresses, and misformat payloads. The tools that should close workflows are the ones that break them.

**Weak decision commitment.** In approval tasks, some models retrieve relevant policy evidence but never convert it into a final decision. MiMo-V2-Flash loops over the same policy documents until its context window fills—retrieving without resolving.

## What Other Benchmarks Miss

Existing enterprise benchmarks sidestep the problem entirely. AgentBench evaluated LLMs as single agents across diverse environments. TheAgentCompany built a simulated company with GitLab and RocketChat—but still gave one agent all the roles. EnterpriseOps-Gym introduced stateful enterprise tools, yet kept the single-agent, unconstrained-access assumption.

On the multi-agent side, SMAC and Overcooked-AI study cooperation in games. COMMA tests communication under information asymmetry. None of these confront stateful business systems, policy-based approvals, or permission isolation—the very constraints that determine whether agents can actually deploy in organizations.

EntCollabBench forces the confrontation. Every task requires at least two departments. Every handoff must carry enough context. Every approval must cite specific policy rules. The evaluation is grounded in database snapshots and execution traces, not free-form LLM judgments.

## The Honest Limits

The simulated organization has 8 service systems and 11 roles—orders of magnitude smaller than a real enterprise. The delegation protocol (`ask_<peer>_by_http`) is more structured than the ad-hoc Slack threads and email chains that real employees navigate.

The policy rules were extracted semi-automatically using GPT-5.4-Mini and GPT-5.4, introducing potential extraction errors despite citation-verification checks. The three-model majority-vote judge improves consistency, though the paper defers a detailed consistency analysis to an appendix and does not report a single headline number in the main text.

Finally, token costs matter. DeepSeek-V4-Pro's successful runs consume substantially more tokens than weaker models', suggesting that current reliability comes at a coordination cost that may not scale.

## A Measuring Stick for Real Organizations

EntCollabBench does not claim to model every enterprise. It claims to model the *hardest part*: the space between roles, where context must survive a handoff and permission boundaries are not suggestions but hard walls.

The best model clears 62%. That number is the distance between multi-agent systems today and the organizations they aim to replace.
