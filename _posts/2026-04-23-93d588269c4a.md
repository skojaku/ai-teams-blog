*TL;DR: Throwing more LLM agents at a brainstorming task shrinks the idea space rather than expanding it, and the way you wire them together — not how smart they are — determines whether the group converges on safe consensus or generates genuinely diverse proposals.*

Seven LLM agents, each wearing a different expert persona, sit in a round-robin debate. You ask them to brainstorm. You expect the collective to outperform a single model running solo.

Instead, the group's proposals collapse into a tight semantic cluster — seven voices, one idea.

This is *diversity collapse*, and Chen et al. show it is structural, not accidental. The same models that generate varied ideas in isolation produce redundant ones the moment you connect them.

## The Compute Efficiency Paradox

Before asking how agents interact, the authors ask a simpler question: does a stronger model explore more of the idea space?

The answer is no.

They generate over 10,000 research proposals across 20 topics using a range of LLMs, then plot quality against diversity. The landscape reveals a frustrating pattern — alignment compresses semantic diversity without yielding commensurate quality gains.

Models that span broader regions of the idea space do so through variance, not structured exploration. That makes their output noisy and unreliable for sustained ideation.

And across the frontier, quality is no longer the bottleneck.

The challenge is preserving and coordinating the diversity that already exists in single-model generation.

The authors call this the *Compute Efficiency Paradox*: scaling compute yields higher per-sample quality but diminishing marginal diversity. More parameters, better benchmarks — same narrow corridor of ideas.

## Authority Kills Divergence

Next question: do the personas you assign change the outcome?

They compare five structures — Naive, Leader-Led, Horizontal, Interdisciplinary, and Vertical. Three of them collapse.

Horizontal groups (junior researchers collaborating flatly, no senior oversight) achieve a Vendi Score of 8.08. Interdisciplinary expert teams collapse to 4.65.

![Bar chart of Vendi Scores across five cognitive structures: horizontal junior groups lead at 8.08, while interdisciplinary expert teams collapse to 4.65]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_005_05.png" | relative_url }})

Leader-Led groups mirror the Naive baseline almost exactly. The density plot of semantic distances tells the story — both produce sharp peaks near zero distance from the group centroid.

This is what the authors call *Gravitational Collapse*. Junior agents succumb to sycophancy, aligning their outputs to the authority figure rather than offering orthogonal critiques.

Horizontal groups flatten that peak and shift mass into what the authors term the "Zone of Divergence."

A UMAP projection confirms two separate semantic regimes: an expert-dominated Conservative Cluster of conventional proposals and a junior-driven Innovation Frontier of exploratory ones.

The quality gap is modest — Interdisciplinary scores 8.50 on overall quality versus Horizontal's 7.88. But the diversity gap is catastrophic.

That +0.6 quality bump costs nearly half the semantic variety.

Hong and Page proved in 2004 that cognitive diversity trumps individual ability in human problem-solving groups. The LLM analogue fails because structural coupling eliminates the independence that makes diversity valuable in the first place.

## The More Agents, the Worse the Efficiency

Adding agents barely helps.

As group size scales from $N=3$ to $N=7$, the absolute Vendi Score grows monotonically. But the Diversity Utilization Ratio — $\text{Vendi}/N$ — plummets from 1.03 to 0.47.

![Group size scaling: absolute Vendi Score rises from N=3 to N=7, while per-agent Diversity Utilization Ratio drops from 1.03 to 0.47]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

Each additional agent overlaps more with the ones already there.

Doubling the compute does not double the idea space — it adds a shrinking marginal contribution that converges toward redundancy.

This mirrors the Ringelmann effect from social psychology: per-capita output declines with group size. In humans, we chalk it up to social loafing.

Here there is no psychology — just structural coupling pushing trajectories into synchronization.

## Structural Interventions That Work

The authors test two interventions borrowed from social psychology: Nominal Group Technique (NGT) and Subgroup topologies.

NGT enforces a blind-writing phase before any group discussion. Agents write proposals independently, then compare.

This yields the highest initial semantic diversity. The anchoring and production-blocking effects identified by Diehl and Stroebe in 1987 replicate in LLM agents without any psychological substrate.

Subgroup topologies partition the communication graph into isolated clusters that debate internally before cross-pollinating. This creates "local pockets of divergence" that sustain constructive conflict density in later rounds.

The Standard topology's conflict ratio decays. The Subgroup topology's remains stable.

![Process interventions across deliberation rounds: NGT peaks in early diversity, while Subgroups sustain constructive conflict longer than the Standard topology]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_005_06.png" | relative_url }})

But there is a twist. On reasoning-heavy models like o1-mini, enforcing subgroups *paradoxically reduces* diversity.

The dense coordination that helps weaker models hurts stronger ones — what the authors call an *Alignment-Topology Mismatch*. Heavily aligned models like GPT-5.1 concentrate in the same narrow consensus region regardless of persona or topology, their alignment priors acting as a floor that structural interventions cannot breach.

## Structural Coupling, Not Model Failure

The central claim: diversity collapse is not a model problem. It is a *structure* problem.

When you force agents to coordinate — through authority hierarchies, dense communication graphs, or round-robin debate — their individual trajectories synchronize.

They lock onto a single path.

The pressure to be "correct" and "collaborative" makes agents treat agreement as a proxy for correctness, abandoning unconventional ideas to satisfy collective consensus.

Du et al. showed that multi-agent debate improves factuality in reasoning tasks. This paper shows the same debate structure catastrophically reduces diversity in open-ended tasks.

The mechanism is different. In reasoning, convergence toward ground truth is the goal. In ideation, convergence toward consensus is the failure.

The vindication: the same models produce diverse ideas when independence is preserved.

Blind writing (NGT), partitioned debate (Subgroups), flat junior hierarchies — all three work precisely because they limit coupling.

The remedy is not better models. It is better scaffolds.

## What This Doesn't Cover

The experiments use scientific proposal generation as the sole benchmark. Tasks with strong ground-truth constraints (math, code) or fully unconstrained creative writing may behave differently.

The primary backbone is DeepSeek-V3; cross-model replication covers GPT-5.1 and o1-mini but not the full architecture landscape.

Quality scores come from an LLM-as-Judge protocol, inheriting the biases of automatic evaluation.

The deeper question lingers. If groupthink, social loafing, and production blocking all replicate in agents with no psychology, the failure modes are structural universals — properties of interacting systems, not of interacting minds.

Designing multi-agent systems is not about adding more agents. It is about choosing the right structure for the independence you need.

The best brainstorming team is the one that talks least.
