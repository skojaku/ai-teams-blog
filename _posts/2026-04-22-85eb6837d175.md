*TL;DR: Giving LLM agents an explicit warmth–competence rating of their partners after every interaction cuts payoff deviation by up to 77% in coordination games and lifts MultiAgentBench performance by 3–29% — because structured social perception turns out to be as useful for agents as it is for humans.*

## A Partner Who Keeps Failing

Three agents are building a web app. One agent's code is riddled with syntax errors. Another consistently delivers clean modules.

A baseline agent sees the same history but treats every round as if it were the first. The trait-aware agent notices — low competence here, high warmth there — and reshuffles the plan to cover the weak spot.

That reshuffling is what Explicit Trait Inference (ETI) makes systematic. Instead of letting agents vaguely "consider their teammates," ETI forces them to rate every partner on eight specific traits after each round, then carry those ratings forward.

## Why Multi-Agent Systems Stumble

Multi-agent LLM systems don't usually fail because individual agents can't do their jobs. They fail because they can't coordinate — goal drift, error cascades, misaligned actions.

Cemri et al. (2025) catalogued exactly these structural failures: agents duplicate work, ignore each other's output, spiral into contradictory plans.

Role scaffolding and chain-of-thought prompting organize workflows.

Reputation scores track success rates.

Theory-of-mind approaches model transient beliefs.

None of these give agents a *structured model of who their partners are* — stable traits like "this agent is cooperative but unreliable" that persist across rounds and adaptively guide decisions.

## What Humans Already Know

Social psychology has known since Fiske et al. (2007) that we evaluate others along two dimensions: warmth (are they on my side?) and competence (can they deliver?).

Hackel et al. (2020) showed that trait inference specifically — not just outcome tracking — is what improves human coordination.

ETI asks: can we give LLM agents the same capacity, grounded in behavioral evidence rather than surface cues?

## The Eight-Trait Scaffold

ETI operationalizes warmth and competence as eight behaviorally anchored traits:

- **Warmth**: goal alignment, collaboration, trustworthiness, maliciousness
- **Competence**: ability, reliability, adaptability, efficiency

Each trait has an explicit definition that prevents warmth and competence from collapsing into a single "is this agent good?" signal. Ability is defined solely as successful execution of intended actions — independent of intentions. Maliciousness captures active sabotage, not mere incompetence.

After every interaction round, each agent rates every partner on each trait using a 1–7 Likert scale and writes a short evidence justification. Those profiles are appended to the agent's context for the next planning step.

This is a prompting and context-management procedure. No fine-tuning, no extra data, minimal overhead.

## Walking Through the Mechanism

Consider a Stag Hunt round. The agent sees its partner chose "Hare" (the safe solo option) last round, and the realized payoff was low — maybe the partner *tried* to coordinate on Stag but failed, or maybe they defected.

An ETI agent reasons like this:

- **Warmth inference**: Partner chose Hare → low goal alignment → warmth rating drops.
- **Competence inference**: Partner intended Stag but failed → ability rating drops.
- **Action**: Low warmth + low competence → play Hare to protect my payoff.

A baseline agent sees the same raw history. Without the trait scaffold, it often defaults to cooperation regardless of evidence, or flips randomly.

The trait profile forces the agent to commit to a structured read, then act on it.

Under noise (opponents who sometimes cooperate, sometimes don't), this structure matters enormously.

In the noisy Prisoner's Dilemma, cooperation F1 jumps from 0.02 (baseline) to 0.49 (ETI); competence F1 from 0.06 to 0.71. The baseline literally cannot tell what kind of partner it faces.

ETI can.

## Where the Ground Truth Is Clear

ETI agents were paired with scripted opponents whose traits the experimenter controlled, allowing ground-truth evaluation of inference accuracy.

Two findings stand out.

Payoff deviation drops by 45–77%. In Stag Hunt, baseline agents deviate 65% from optimal payoff; ETI agents deviate just 15%.

After roughly five rounds of evidence collection, ETI agents converge to near-optimal strategies.

In Prisoner's Dilemma, deviation falls from 135% to 78%.

![Payoff deviation across interaction rounds for baseline vs. ETI agents in economic games, showing ETI converging to near-optimal after roughly five rounds]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Trait inference accuracy is remarkable under noise. In Stag Hunt under noise, cooperation F1 rises from 0.34 to 0.80.

When opponents occasionally cooperate but are mostly selfish, the baseline's cooperation F1 collapses to 0.02 — essentially random guessing — while ETI holds at 0.49.

## When the Rules Get Messy

Four scenarios: collaborative coding, research proposal writing, bargaining, and Werewolf (nine-player social deduction).

ETI improves task performance by 3–29% and coordination by 6–42%, depending on scenario and model.

![MultiAgentBench task performance across four scenarios, showing QWEN-generated profiles producing the largest gains]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

But the most surprising finding is the cross-model effect.

## The Profile That Helps Your Neighbor

The authors tested what happens when one model generates trait profiles and a different model acts on them.

A GPT-4o-mini agent using QWEN-generated profiles sees large gains — coding coordination jumps from 57.01 to 74.38, Werewolf task score from 25.26 to 36.46.

But GPT-generated profiles often provide little or no benefit regardless of which model acts on them.

Why? QWEN's ratings are high-variance and diagnostic — they spread across the 1–7 scale and differentiate agents clearly. GPT's ratings cluster near the top, suffused with positivity bias.

The profiles are too generic to be actionable.

This is a striking result: the *content* of the profiles matters more than the mechanism. Simply adding structured context is not enough — that context has to say something pointed.

In Werewolf, the trait-driven mechanism is especially clear. Logistic models show that higher maliciousness ratings increase the chance of an agent being voted out ($\beta = +2.74$), while higher trustworthiness reduces it ($\beta = -5.25$).

Agents literally quote their trait inferences in decisions: *"Ethel's persistent focus on Mae and high maliciousness rating suggest she may be trying to deflect suspicion; I should target her."*

## Honest Limits

The authors tested on two small models: QWEN3-8B and GPT-4o-mini. Larger or fine-tuned models may behave differently.

GPT-4o-mini's positivity bias is a real calibration problem: it means ETI's gains depend on which model generates the profiles, not just on the framework itself.

The fixed eight-trait taxonomy may miss domain-specific nuances. ETI's emphasis on stable patterns can slow adaptation when a partner abruptly changes behavior; it needs new evidence to overwrite the old profile.

And there's a token cost. In communication-heavy settings, ETI can nearly double token usage, though the dollar cost stays modest ($0.01–$0.13 per run at GPT-4o/5 rates).

## What This Changes

ETI is the first systematic evidence that LLM agents can reliably infer others' traits from interaction histories and *use* those inferences to coordinate better. That's not a marginal engineering trick — it's a lightweight import of a mechanism humans have used for millennia, and it works.

The finding that profile quality is the bottleneck, not the prompting structure, reframes the problem. Better trait inference becomes a diagnostic for model suitability in multi-agent deployments.

QWEN's sharper profiles help even GPT agents. We should be selecting models for their social perception, not just their task performance.

Know your partners. Act on what you know.
