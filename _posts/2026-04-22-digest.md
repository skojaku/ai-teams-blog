## Themes

**Structure beats scale.** All five papers converge on one finding: throwing more parameters or more agents at a multi-agent system is not the dial that matters. *Rethinking Scale* shows that a 0.5B model with tools outperforms a bare 10B model on financial tasks, and single-agent setups beat multi-agent ones on most benchmarks. *Diversity Collapse* documents that per-agent idea diversity more than halves when you scale from three agents to seven. *OMAC* demonstrates that optimizing the interaction graph compounds small per-dimension gains into large overall wins. The right wiring matters more than the right model.

**More agents can make things worse.** This is the uncomfortable thread running through the batch. *Rethinking Scale* reports completion rates dropping from 99.67% (base) to 72.01% (multi-agent) and token usage inflating sixfold. *Diversity Collapse* names the mechanism: structural coupling locks groups into premature consensus. Even *AgentElect* finds an equality paradox—elected leadership grows the total pie but distributes slices more unevenly. Adding agents introduces new failure vectors—delegation loops, context exhaustion, gravitational collapse—rather than collective intelligence.

**Social perception is infrastructure.** Two papers formalize what humans do instinctively and show it works for machines too. *AgentElect* gives agents Social Value Orientation profiles and elections, producing a 55.4% welfare gain. *Explicit Trait Inference* forces agents to rate partners on warmth and competence after every round, cutting payoff deviation by up to 77%. Both treat knowing who your teammates are as a first-class design primitive—a structured model of others, not a fuzzy afterthought.

**Independence must be preserved before coordination.** *Diversity Collapse* shows that Nominal Group Technique's blind-writing phase rescues more ideas than any persona prompt. *AgentElect* shows elections let groups fire bad leaders instead of being stuck with fixed ones. *Explicit Trait Inference* works because trait profiles give agents a structured read on partners, freeing them from default cooperation. The pattern is consistent: preserve independent judgment first, then coordinate. Coordinate first, and you get convergence dressed up as collaboration.

**Content matters more than container.** *Explicit Trait Inference* finds that QWEN's high-variance trait profiles help even GPT agents, while GPT's own clustered, positivity-biased profiles help no one. *Diversity Collapse* shows that assigning fancy expert personas doesn't broaden ideas—it narrows them. *OMAC*'s contrastive comparator works because it extracts specific lessons from performance gaps, not because the loop structure is novel. The substance of what flows through the system beats the shape of the pipes.

## Editorial

Today's batch reads like a coordinated rebuttal to the "just add more agents" school of thought.

Every single paper finds that naïve scaling—more parameters, more team members, more communication channels—makes things worse, not better. *Rethinking Scale* gives the cleanest numbers: completion rates crater, tokens balloon, and the multi-agent tax erases the gains. *Diversity Collapse* explains why: interaction that should diversify instead synchronizes. The mechanism is structural, not incidental.

What actually works is the opposite of throwing resources at the problem. Give a small model tools and stop at one agent. Let agents vote before they talk. Rate your partners on eight traits and carry those ratings forward. Write blind, then debate. Optimizing five dimensions by contrastive reasoning.

The genuine insight across these papers is that the *shape* of coordination—elections, trait scaffolds, blind-writing phases, sparse communication graphs—determines whether agents amplify or cancel each other. The container is less important than the content flowing through it, as ETI's finding that QWEN profiles rescue GPT agents makes clear.

What's incremental: OMAC's five-dimension framework is systematic but builds on existing ideas (DyLAN, ADAS, AFlow). AgentElect's common-pool resource game is a well-studied setting; the Losing Voice is the surprising finding, not the leadership dividend itself.

What warrants a second look: the interaction between model type and topology in *Diversity Collapse*. NGT helps standard models but hurts reasoning-heavy ones. That's a boundary condition that anyone deploying multi-agent systems needs to map carefully. And *Explicit Trait Inference*'s cross-model profile transfer—QWEN profiles helping GPT agents more than GPT's own—suggests that social perception is a model property worth measuring and selecting for, not just a prompt trick.

The lesson that sticks: independence is the scarce resource. Everything else is plumbing.
