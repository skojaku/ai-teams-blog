*TL;DR: When you match inference budgets fairly, multi-agent setups—especially Mixture-of-Agents—beat single-agent baselines on the Pareto front, and the biggest gains land on the hardest questions.*

You have a fixed inference budget and a hard question. Do you spend it on more parallel samples, more refinement rounds, or more agents?

The answer turns out to matter a lot—and the conventional wisdom is wrong.

Wunderlich et al. run 34 configurations across four inference pipelines on MMLU-Pro and BBH, plot accuracy against estimated runtime, and trace the Pareto front. Runtime here means FLOPs *plus* memory-transfer cost, not just FLOPs alone.

The result: multi-agent systems don't just score higher—they deliver more accuracy per compute unit than any single-agent alternative.

## The Trouble with Unmatched Budgets

Most inference-scaling papers report raw accuracy without equating compute.

Self-consistency with many parallel samples looks impressive until you realize it costs that many times a single chain-of-thought call. Debate with four agents and two rounds looks even better—until you count the bill.

Without matched budgets, you cannot tell whether a method is genuinely efficient or just expensive.

Snell et al. showed that test-time compute can beat scaling model parameters, but they compared within single paradigms. Self-consistency and debate had been compared before—Huang et al. tried it—but never under Pareto-optimal scaling with matched compute ceilings.

Until now.

## Four Pipelines, One Yardstick

The authors evaluate four inference strategies:

- **Self-consistency**: sample $N$ CoT paths, vote on the answer (parallel scaling).
- **Self-refinement**: generate, then iteratively self-criticize and revise (sequential scaling).
- **Debate**: $A$ agents argue across $R$ rounds, voting at the end (parallel + sequential, with memory).
- **Mixture-of-Agents (MoA)**: $M$ proposer models generate across $L$ layers; a final aggregator synthesizes (parallel + sequential, no memory accumulation).

The scaling levers are the pipeline itself, its parameters ($N$, $A$, $R$, $M$, $L$), and the model size (8B vs. 70B). Compute cost is estimated as theoretical runtime—FLOPs plus GPU memory-transfer time—so a method that burns FLOPs but stays memory-friendly gets fair credit.

## The Pareto Front Has a Clear Winner

MoA dominates the Pareto front on both benchmarks and both model sizes.

On MMLU-Pro with the 70B model, MoA reaches 71.4% accuracy at roughly 20× the CoT budget. Self-consistency tops out at 68.7%; debate at 70.0%.

Under comparable compute, debate beats self-consistency by 1.3 percentage points; MoA beats it by 2.7—solely from choosing a different scaling paradigm.

Self-consistency saturates early: past ~10 sequences, gains flatten.

Debate saturates around 4 agents.

MoA keeps climbing nearly linearly up to 5 models and 4 layers.

![Accuracy versus estimated runtime for all four inference pipelines on MMLU-Pro, with the Pareto front connecting the most efficient configurations]({{ "/assets/figures/e47390c25e4b/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

And self-refinement? It underperforms the CoT baseline despite requiring more compute.

Self-correction without external feedback cannot improve reasoning—a finding that echoes Huang et al.'s earlier result.

## Why MoA Scales Further

The architecture explains the gap.

In MoA, adding layers does not grow the context each model processes. Each layer receives a fixed set of proposals and aggregates them.

In debate, every extra round inflates the context because agents retain all prior exchanges in memory. More context means more tokens, more attention cost, and diminishing returns.

This is why the authors find a crisp design rule for MoA: **use one more proposer model than layers**.

Configurations like 3 models / 2 layers, 4 / 3, and 5 / 4 land on the Pareto front. Beyond that ratio, extra models or layers hurt.

For debate, the rule is even simpler: **scale agents, not rounds**.

The best debate accuracy consistently uses only two rounds. Adding agents helps; adding rounds wastes compute.

![Debate accuracy when scaling agents (parallel) versus rounds (sequential) on MMLU-Pro—more agents climb, more rounds stagnate]({{ "/assets/figures/e47390c25e4b/fig_013_06.png" | relative_url }})

## Hard Tasks Get the Most Out of Extra Compute

Not all questions benefit equally.

The authors bin MMLU-Pro questions by CoT solve rate: easy (>75%), medium (25–75%), hard (<25%).

At 15–20× CoT budget, easy tasks gain +2.2 pp.

Medium tasks gain +8.5 pp.

Hard tasks gain +9.0 pp—more than doubling their baseline accuracy of 8.4%.

![Accuracy gains across easy, medium, and hard tasks as compute scales from 1× to 20× the CoT baseline]({{ "/assets/figures/e47390c25e4b/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

The classic diminishing-returns curve, flipped: the questions where CoT falls flat are exactly the ones where inference scaling pays off most.

It also suggests an adaptive routing strategy—spend more test-time compute on hard questions, skip it on easy ones.

## Model Size Beats Extreme Scaling

The 8B model, even with the most aggressive multi-agent scaling, never cracks 53% on MMLU-Pro.

The 70B model with plain CoT hits 64.3% using less compute.

Scaling a small model cannot replace using a capable one.

Inside MoA, the picture sharpens. Swap the 70B proposers for 8B while keeping a 70B aggregator: accuracy crashes from 71.4% to 52.9%.

Swap only the aggregator to 8B while keeping 70B proposers: accuracy drops just to 69.6%.

Proposer quality dominates because it determines what evidence the aggregator sees.

A strong aggregator cannot recover quality that was never generated.

## Honest Limits

Sample size is 1,000 questions per benchmark. At accuracies around 65–72%, the 95% binomial confidence interval is roughly ±3%, so some pairwise differences may not be statistically significant on their own.

Compute costs are theoretically estimated, not directly measured. Real-world latency depends on batching, framework overhead, and GPU memory constraints that could shift the Pareto front—especially for the 70B model, which demands more memory per card.

The study also uses non-reasoning models (Llama 3.1). Whether reasoning-specialized models change the cost-quality tradeoff remains open.

## What to Take Away

Three rules for practitioners:

1. If your budget is ≤3× CoT, use self-consistency. Below that threshold, MoA is not worth the overhead.
2. If you can afford 5× CoT or more, switch to MoA with one more proposer than layers. A 5× budget captures most of MoA's gains.
3. Scale agents (parallel), not rounds (sequential). More rounds in debate or more iterations in self-refinement rarely help.

You have a fixed budget and a hard question. Spend it on agents, not rounds.
