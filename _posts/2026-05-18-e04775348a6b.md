*TL;DR: This survey introduces the LIFE progression—Lay, Integrate, Find, Evolve—as a causal framework linking individual capabilities, collaboration, failure attribution, and self-evolution in multi-agent LLM systems, and argues that the gap between diagnosing failures and structurally improving from them is the field's central unsolved problem.*

A team of agents works through a travel-planning task. One hallucinates a flight number; another routes the itinerary through that bad datum.

A third verifies the plan against constraints and flags something vague—without pinpointing the root cause.

The system knows something went wrong. It can't fix the structure that let the error propagate.

That gap—between knowing what failed and evolving the system so it won't fail the same way again—is what this survey from Qi et al. argues is the defining unsolved problem in LLM-based multi-agent systems.

## Why Treating Stages in Isolation Misses the Point

Existing surveys have covered single-agent capabilities, multi-agent collaboration, or self-evolution—but each in its own silo.

Park et al.'s Generative Agents gave us the memory-stream architecture and multi-factor retrieval, foundational ideas that belong to what this survey calls the "Lay" stage.

Shinn et al.'s Reflexion introduced iterative self-correction through verbal reinforcement—an early sketch of agentic self-evolution—but it operates on a single agent, not on the coordination topology.

The problem: none of these threads connect.

Collaboration shapes what failures become observable. Attribution narrows the search space for improvement. Evolution closes the loop. Treat them separately and you miss the causal dependencies between them.

This survey proposes a single causal pipeline called LIFE—**L**ay the capability foundation, **I**ntegrate agents through collaboration, **F**ind faults through attribution, **E**volve through autonomous self-improvement.

It is not four topics stacked side by side. It is a progression where each stage constrains and depends on the next.

## Walking Through the LIFE Progression

### Lay: What a Single Agent Brings

Before agents collaborate, each one needs the machinery to perceive, reason, remember, plan, and act. The survey formalizes an individual agent as a tuple:

$$a = (X, U, O, M, R, P, T)$$

where $X$ is the observation space, $U$ the action space, $O$ tool-returned observations, $M$ the memory state space, and $R$, $P$, $T$ are the reasoning, planning, and tool-execution functions.

Decision-making is factorized: retrieve context from memory, reason over the observation, plan a multi-step action, execute via tools, then update memory.

This formalization matters because it names the modules that later stages must attribute failures to and evolve. An agent that can't remember can't be held accountable for repeating a mistake. An agent without tool use narrows what collaboration can even distribute.

### Integrate: How Agents Organize

Collaboration introduces four organizational axes: **role** (who does what), **communication** (what gets said and how), **orchestration** (centralized, distributed, or hybrid topology), and **interaction** (information flow and turn-taking patterns).

Here's where the causal dependency starts to bite. The orchestration topology—whether a single manager routes tasks or agents negotiate peer-to-peer—determines what information flows where. That, in turn, determines which failures are even *observable*.

A distributed mesh might never surface a routing error that a centralized orchestrator would catch immediately.

The collaboration structure constrains the attribution problem before it begins.

### Find: Diagnosing Failure

When a multi-agent system produces a bad outcome, you need to trace the failure back to a responsible agent and a decisive step. The survey identifies three methodological families:

- **Data-driven methods**: pattern mining over execution logs, statistical anomaly detection—effective when you have many failure traces but limited causal structure.
- **Constraint-guided methods**: encode domain or system constraints and flag violations—precise when constraints are available, brittle when they are not.
- **Causal-inference methods**: construct causal graphs over agent interactions and estimate counterfactual impact—theoretically powerful, but the causal chain linking a failure to its downstream consequences "becomes increasingly opaque" as execution trajectories grow and inter-agent dependencies deepen.

No existing survey systematically treats multi-agent failure attribution as its own area. This one does, and it finds the area is growing fast—but the methods are still early. Many taxonomic categories have only a handful of representative systems.

### Evolve: From Diagnosis to Structural Change

This is where the gap becomes stark. The survey organizes self-evolution into three levels:

- **Agentic**: an agent refines its own prompts, memory, or tool use—Reflexion lives here, as do most current systems.
- **Systemic**: the system reorganizes role assignments, communication topologies, or collaboration policies—almost no deployed systems do this automatically.
- **Meta**: the system evolves its own evolution process—largely speculative at this point.

The asymmetry is sharp. We can build sophisticated multi-agent teams. We can sometimes diagnose why they fail. We almost never have the machinery for them to reorganize themselves in response.

## The Attribution–Evolution Closed Loop

The survey's central thesis is stated plainly: "Without reliable diagnosis, improvement efforts lack direction; without the ability to act on diagnostic results, attribution yields limited practical value."

These two deficits—attribution and self-improvement—are not separate problems. They are coupled.

Failure attribution narrows the search space for targeted evolution. Evolution changes the collaboration structure, which changes what failures are observable, which changes what can be attributed. A closed loop.

Right now, that loop is open.

![The attribution–evolution feedback loop connecting failure diagnosis to structural self-improvement, with collaboration topology as a mediating variable]({{ "/assets/figures/e04775348a6b/fig_053_07.png" | relative_url }})

The survey finds that current multi-agent systems "largely lack the ability to translate diagnostic insights into structural adaptation, whether by reorganizing coordination topologies, revising role assignments, or refining collaboration policies." The LIFE progression gives you a vocabulary for where the loop is broken and what it would take to close it.

## The Honest Limits

This is a survey, not an experimental paper. The causal dependencies between LIFE stages are argued conceptually, not empirically validated.

The framework may become a standard organizing lens, but that claim rests on synthesis and clarity, not on benchmark numbers.

The failure-attribution and self-evolution sections cover literature that is growing but still early. Many of the proposed taxonomic categories—especially systemic and meta self-evolution—have sparse implementations to point to. The hierarchies are reasonable, but tentatively populated.

And there is a deeper conceptual question the survey raises but cannot yet answer: whether the attribution–evolution closed loop can even be fully automated, or whether certain structural adaptations will always require human engineering. The survey proposes a "cross-stage research agenda for closed-loop multi-agent systems" but does not pretend the agenda is solved.

The public repository (github.com/mira-ai-lab/awesome-mas-life) collects structured literature, taxonomy visualizations, and comparative tables—useful scaffolding, but scaffolding for work that is still mostly ahead.

From knowing what went wrong to rewriting the structure that caused it—LIFE names the distance, and the distance is still considerable.
