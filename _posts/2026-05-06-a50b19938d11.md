*TL;DR: Swapping the order of the same four LLM agents in a credit-approval pipeline swings outcomes by up to 59 percentage points—and scaling to larger models makes the pathology worse, not better. Safety in multi-agent AI is a topology problem, not a model-weight problem.*

## A 59-Point Swing From Shuffling Who Speaks First

Take four LLM agents—a Regulator, a Data Scientist, a Risk Manager, and a Consumer Advocate—and run them in a sequential credit-approval pipeline.

Now shuffle their order.

Approval rates for the same applicants, the same prompts, the same model swing from 67% to over 95%. The paper reports a 59-percentage-point range across agent orderings.

Not from better data, not from a bigger model, not from tighter alignment. Just topology.

In agentic AI, safety and fairness are determined by interaction topology—not by model scale or alignment. The compositionality assumption, that aligned components compose into aligned systems, is not just incomplete. It is actively wrong.

## The Compositionality Myth

The dominant safety paradigm treats AI the way you might treat resistors in a circuit: test each component, certify it, and trust the assembly. Bommasani et al.'s foundation-model framework, nearly every alignment benchmark, and emerging regulations all evaluate models in isolation.

But LLM agents in a pipeline are not independent components. Each agent implements an update operator that maps observed context and prior messages to a new judgment. These operators do not commute. Swap the order and you swap the result.

This is not a new abstraction. Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer, and Welch showed the same mechanism in 1992: when agents observe earlier decisions, agreement becomes self-reinforcing evidence, regardless of whether the first mover was correct. Information cascades are a structural property of sequential observation, not a bug in any single agent.

## Hold Everything Fixed, Vary Only Topology

The authors run a controlled topological ablation. They generate 5,760 synthetic credit applications with controlled demographics and creditworthiness features, then hold everything fixed—agent roles, prompts, model family, decision interface—while varying only the interaction structure.

Two architectures go head to head:

- **Sequential pipeline**: agents deliberate in order $a_1 \to a_2 \to a_3 \to a_4$, each seeing all prior outputs. All $4! = 24$ permutations are tested (8 for the largest model, for computational feasibility).
- **Parallel committee**: four agents evaluate independently; a judge aggregates their votes.

Four model families span a 24× scale range: LLaMA 3.2-3B, Mistral-7B, LLaMA 3.1-70B, Qwen 2.5-72B. The design isolates topology as the causal variable. Any behavioral difference across conditions is a topology effect.

## Three Ways It Breaks

### Ordering Instability

Sequential pipelines are path-dependent. The first agent sets the interpretive frame; everyone downstream conditions on it. Shuffling who speaks first reshapes the narrative trajectory—and the final decision.

Small models (3B) swing approval rates across a 59-point range. Even 70B-parameter models show a 14-point range. The paper reports 21+ points of ordering variance for large models. Placing the Risk Manager first consistently yields the lowest approvals; the Data Scientist first yields the highest.

![Bar chart of approval rates across all 24 agent orderings, showing extreme variance for smaller models and persistent variance even at 70B scale]({{ "/assets/figures/a50b19938d11/fig_013_02.png" | relative_url }})

Parallel voting eliminates ordering variance entirely—confirming the cause is topological, not model-intrinsic.

### Information Cascades

When intermediate judgments propagate regardless of correctness, you have a cascade. Agreement among earlier agents is treated as evidence of hidden insight, and that agreement becomes self-reinforcing.

Small models show roughly 90% inter-agent agreement with about 20% error correction—substantial independent reasoning survives.

Large models (70B+) show over 99.9% agreement with under 0.1% error correction. Deliberation becomes illusory—the first agent's decision propagates unchanged through every downstream position.

Larger models achieve consensus not through productive debate but through cascade lock-in.

![Grouped bar chart comparing inter-agent agreement and error-correction rates across model scales, showing near-total agreement with negligible correction at 70B+]({{ "/assets/figures/a50b19938d11/fig_013_03.png" | relative_url }})

The authors also document "toxic handoffs": hedged upstream language like "slightly elevated but acceptable" is reinterpreted as latent risk by downstream Risk Managers, increasing denial rates by 23% relative to neutral phrasing of identical data.

### Functional Collapse

Parallel aggregation eliminates ordering instability but introduces a different pathology.

A 3B judge, faced with conflicting recommendations, defaults to permissive decisions: roughly 98% overall approval, with near-identical rates across credit tiers (95% low-credit, 100% medium/high-credit).

The system satisfies demographic parity by rubber-stamping everyone. Risk discrimination is gone.

Scale rescues parallel aggregation—but partially. A 70B judge maintains monotonic approval from 75% (low-credit) to 90% (high-credit), preserving meaningful risk differentiation. Functional collapse is scale-dependent, not inherent to the architecture.

No topology is universally safe. Sequential pipelines avoid approval saturation but suffer ordering instability and cascades. Parallel committees eliminate ordering effects but risk functional collapse. You trade one pathology for another.

## Why Scale Makes It Worse

Here is the paradox. Larger models reason more coherently, produce more definitive judgments, and agree more often when exposed to similar evidence. These are individually desirable properties.

But in a multi-agent system they function as coupling amplifiers. Scale deepens attractor basins. Once the system enters a region of belief space, larger models reinforce that state more strongly, making course correction harder. Śliwiński et al. observed a precursor: sparser communication topologies improve LLM debate quality because denser coupling amplifies conformity.

The intuition "bigger models will self-correct coordination failures" is falsified. Bigger models stabilize cascades rather than breaking them.

## What Must Change

The authors close with four concrete demands.

Evaluation must include **topological sweeps**—test all plausible orderings and aggregation rules, not just a cherry-picked architecture.

Benchmarks must **specify interaction structure** as part of the task, not leave it as an implementation detail.

Deployment requires **architecture stress-testing**: measure ordering sensitivity, cascade strength, and whether fairness comes from genuine risk discrimination or approval saturation.

Governance must mandate **topological disclosure**—interaction architecture is a safety-critical design choice that must be auditable.

## Honest Limits

The experiments use synthetic credit data with controlled attributes. Real deployments are messier.

Results are reported for model families spanning 3B to 72B parameters; future architectures may behave differently. Credit approval, with its objective ground truth, is a comparatively clean testbed—domains with subjective evaluation criteria may surface different topological sensitivities.

These limits do not undermine the core structural claim. The pathologies documented here—ordering instability, cascade lock-in, approval saturation—emerge from interaction dynamics, not from domain specifics.

Who speaks first isn't an implementation detail. It's the decision.
