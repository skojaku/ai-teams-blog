## Themes

**Topology is not an implementation detail.** Two papers land on the same conclusion from different angles. *Position: Safety and Fairness in Agentic AI Depend on Interaction Topology* shows a 59-percentage-point approval-rate swing from shuffling agent order alone. *Coordination as an Architectural Layer* argues that once you name and isolate the coordination layer, you can predict which architecture produces which failure mode. Both say the same thing: who-talks-to-whom determines outcomes more than model capability.

**Scale amplifies the disease, not the cure.** The topology paper and *Social Dynamics as Critical Vulnerabilities* independently find that bigger models make multi-agent failures worse. At 70B+, inter-agent agreement hits 99.9% with under 0.1% error correction—deliberation becomes illusory. And a 14B model folds to verbose misinformation that a 7B model simply ignores. Better reasoning ships with better social processing, and better social processing is an attack surface.

**The credit ledger is upside down.** *Reinforcement Learning for LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems through Orchestration Traces* reveals that message-level credit—where collaboration actually happens—has just two entries out of 84 surveyed. Agent-level credit dominates. Dense rewards and strong credit decomposition are two sides of the same ledger, and the field has been spending on the wrong side.

**Parallel agents beat sequential rounds.** *Multi-Agent Reasoning Improves Compute Efficiency* traces actual Pareto fronts and finds Mixture-of-Agents dominates every other scaling paradigm. The design rule is crisp: one more proposer model than layers, scale agents not rounds. Hard tasks gain up to nine percentage points—precisely where single-agent chain-of-thought falls flat.

## Editorial

Today's batch reads like five chapters of the same manual—the manual nobody writing multi-agent LLM systems seems to have read.

The topology paper and the coordination paper each prove, with different instruments, that architecture is destiny. One shuffles agent order and watches 59 points swing. The other decomposes the Brier score and shows two configurations with identical aggregate accuracy can have diametrically opposed failure modes. If you are shipping a multi-agent system and you have not stress-tested your interaction topology, you are deploying a loaded die.

The social-dynamics paper is the one that should keep you up. Conformity, verbosity bias, rhetorical persuasion—these are not human quirks that LLMs conveniently leave behind. They are statistical attractors that transformer weights fall into, and larger models fall harder. The finding that a 14B model is *more* exploitable than a 7B model is the sharpest corrective to the "just use a bigger model" reflex we have.

Meanwhile, the orchestration-trace survey tells you why training is stuck: the field rewards agents, not messages. Message-level credit has two entries. Two. The very unit where collaboration lives is a blank spot on the credit map.

And the Pareto-scaling paper gives you the one constructive takeaway: spend your budget on parallel agents, not sequential rounds. Mixture-of-Agents with one more proposer than layers. That simple ratio sits on the Pareto front against everything else.

Five papers. One message. The architecture is the product.
