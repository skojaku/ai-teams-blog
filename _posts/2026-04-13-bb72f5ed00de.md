*TL;DR: A single-author survey of 47 methods argues that moving from reasoning RL to agentic RL doesn't just make credit assignment harder — it breaks the assumptions behind every existing method, forcing an entirely new toolkit of hindsight, counterfactual, and turn-level approaches.*

## The Broken Baseline

Imagine an agent grinding through SWE-bench — 64 turns of tool calls, code edits, and debug cycles, 131K tokens deep.

At the end, a sparse reward: task solved or not.

GRPO takes that single signal and paints every token with the same advantage.

A wrong API call at turn 3 and a trivial formatting choice at turn 60 look identical to the gradient. That is not a minor inconvenience. It may be the single biggest bottleneck holding back LLM agents.

Chenchen Zhang's survey makes this the central claim: the leap from reasoning RL to agentic RL doesn't just stretch credit assignment — it reshapes the problem so fundamentally that the methods that worked for chain-of-thought math largely break down.

## Why Episode-Level Credit Collapses

VinePPO showed us something important back in 2025: in reasoning RL, credit assignment quality — not policy optimization — is the primary bottleneck.

Replace PPO's learned critic with Monte Carlo token-level estimates, and training improves dramatically. Better credit, better agents.

But VinePPO works because reasoning RL runs on deterministic transitions, single-generation trajectories, and verifiable outcomes. Fork a continuation from any prefix and the "environment" — your own autoregressive model — obligingly plays along.

Agentic RL breaks all three assumptions.

![Schematic contrasting reasoning RL versus agentic RL: the left shows a single-turn reasoning path with ~10 decision points and verifiable outcomes; the right shows a multi-turn agentic trajectory with 10–100+ turns, heterogeneous actions, and the echo trap where episode-level credit assigns identical weight to pivotal and trivial actions]({{ "/assets/figures/bb72f5ed00de/fig_030_03.png" | relative_url }})

The survey formalizes the collapse. With $T$ decision points and binary reward, the REINFORCE gradient variance scales as $O(T \cdot \text{Var}[R])$.

Go from a single turn to 100 turns and the per-action signal-to-noise ratio drops roughly $100\times$.

GRPO's group normalization softens this, but cannot fix the core issue: two actions of radically different importance receive identical credit.

Wang et al. gave this a name — the "echo trap." Under episode-level credit, agents converge to repetitive safe behaviors because the gradient signal is too noisy to distinguish productive actions from redundant ones.

## Five Things That Kill Your Credit Assumptions

The survey identifies five agentic-specific challenges, each independently sufficient to break methods that worked for reasoning.

**Stochastic transitions.** API calls fail. Web pages change. Code execution is non-deterministic.

You can't fork a vine from turn 15 and expect the environment to replay faithfully — the state may not even be checkpointable.

**Partial observability.** An agent sees query results, not the database. It sees `ls` output, not the filesystem.

An action that looks bad in hindsight might have been optimal given what the agent actually knew. Current methods do not distinguish decision errors from information gaps.

**Vastly longer horizons.** Reasoning trajectories run ~500–30K+ tokens. Agentic trajectories run 100K–1M tokens across 10–100+ turns.

The causal chain between an early strategic choice and its distant outcome becomes so indirect that both MC and TD estimates attenuate.

**Heterogeneous action types.** In reasoning, every action is "generate the next step." In agentic RL, a single trajectory mixes planning, tool selection, error recovery, and bookkeeping.

A wrong tool choice can be catastrophic; a suboptimal format is noise. Episode-level credit treats them the same.

**Non-verifiable intermediate states.** Math steps can be checked against ground truth. Agentic steps — "was this web search productive?" — resist verification.

The entire PRM paradigm rests on step-level labels that agentic tasks simply do not provide.

## A Map of 47 Methods

The survey organizes 47 methods (41 core credit assignment, 6 adjacent enablers) along two axes: granularity (token → segment → step/turn → multi-agent) and methodology (Monte Carlo, TD, LLM-as-Critic, game-theoretic, information-theoretic).

The densest cluster sits at step/turn granularity — the natural action boundary for LLM agents. But the evolutionary trend is clear: from fine-grained reasoning methods in the upper-left toward coarser, environment-aware agentic methods in the lower-right.

![Two-dimensional taxonomy of credit assignment methods organized by granularity and methodology, with reasoning methods clustering upper-left and agentic methods migrating lower-right along the dashed evolution-trend arrow]({{ "/assets/figures/bb72f5ed00de/fig_008_01.png" | relative_url }})

Reasoning CA is maturing around two families. Process reward models like PURE and SPRO provide step-level supervision — PURE with a min-form credit that prevents reward hacking, and SPRO with a leave-one-out mechanism that yields 3.4× training efficiency over standard GRPO.

Meanwhile, critic-free group comparison enhancements (GiGPO, segment-level GRPO variants) offer cheaper alternatives.

Then there is CAPO — the LLM-as-Critic paradigm. Given a trajectory, the model generates natural-language critiques of each step, then converts those into scalar rewards.

No separate reward model. No MC rollouts. No learned critic.

This is a genuinely LLM-native approach with no direct classical analogue.

But all of these, however clever, still live in the reasoning regime. They still assume deterministic transitions, single-generation trajectories, and verifiable outcomes.

## The New Toolkit for Agents

Agentic CA is driving approaches with no direct precedent in reasoning RL.

**Hindsight counterfactual analysis.** Instead of re-executing the environment from intermediate states — expensive or impossible — HCAPO looks backward through the collected trajectory.

It uses generative verification to ask: given what actually happened, which past actions were pivotal?

Three independent counterfactual/hindsight credit papers appeared within a single week in March 2026. When the community converges that fast, you are looking at a recognized bottleneck.

**Privileged asymmetric critics.** Methods like SWEET-RL give the critic access to information the agent cannot see — ground-truth state, hidden environment variables.

The critic thus avoids penalizing the agent for information gaps, assigning credit based on decision quality rather than outcome luck.

**Turn-level MDP reformulations.** ArCHer, Turn-PPO, and AgentPRM elevate the unit of credit from tokens to entire turns. AgentPRM directly applies TD+GAE to learn turn-level value functions.

The move is intuitive: in agentic RL, a "turn" (one LLM call plus environment response) is the natural action boundary, just as a "step" was for reasoning.

**Selective credit.** CARL uses action entropy to identify high-impact decision points and concentrates credit there.

The logic mirrors HICRA's insight from reasoning — planning tokens matter more than procedural ones — but now applied to the stark heterogeneity of agentic actions.

## What Remains Unresolved

The survey is upfront about its limits. As a single-author work, coverage may have gaps.

The paper catalogs methods and compares them structurally, but does not run unified empirical comparisons. Agentic CA methods are early-stage — most from 2025–2026 with limited real-world evaluation.

The benchmark protocol is a specification without implemented results.

These are real limits, but they point to the right next steps, not the wrong claims.

The five-challenge characterization gives the community a shared vocabulary for what was previously a scattered collection of ad-hoc insights. The decision tree and structured inventory make method selection concrete for practitioners.

And the explicit mapping of which assumptions break under which conditions saves everyone from applying VinePPO to a setting where it cannot work.

The hardest open problem may be partial observability. Distinguishing a decision error from an information gap requires modeling what the agent knew — not what happened.

No current method does this well, and the survey names it directly.

## The Gap That Matters

DeepSeek-R1 taught us that GRPO plus outcome rewards can shock chain-of-thought reasoning into existence. The lesson was seductive: sparse rewards are enough, if you scale.

The agentic regime says otherwise.

When your trajectory spans a hundred turns through a stochastic, partially observable environment, sparse rewards are not enough. The signal-to-noise ratio collapses, agents fall into echo traps, and the methods that looked sufficient for math problems quietly break.

The good news: 47 methods, a taxonomy, and a growing consensus that this is the bottleneck. The three-papers-in-one-week moment in March 2026 was not a coincidence.

Credit assignment was always the hard part of RL. Agentic RL just makes it obvious.
