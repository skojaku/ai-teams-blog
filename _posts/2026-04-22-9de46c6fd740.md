*TL;DR: OMAC is the first framework that treats multi-agent LLM system design as a unified optimization problem across five dimensions—agent prompts, new agent creation, team selection, dynamic participation, and communication routing—using contrastive reasoning over supervised performance signals, and it beats every baseline on code generation, reasoning, and math benchmarks.*

## Handcrafted Teams, Meet the Optimizer

You have seven LLM agents. You picked their roles, wired who talks to whom, decided the order. It works—until it doesn't. You swap in a different task and the whole topology falls apart.

So you tweak prompts. Reshuffle the team. Rewire edges. Trial and error, again and again.

That ad-hoc loop is how almost every multi-agent LLM system is built today. OMAC asks: what if we automated the whole thing?

## The Five Levers You Can Pull

Think of a multi-agent system as an information-flow graph. Agents are nodes. Communication channels are edges.

OMAC identifies exactly five dimensions you can optimize—two for node capabilities, three for graph structure:

- **Fun-1:** Refine an existing agent's prompt (and few-shot examples, if any).
- **Fun-2:** Construct a brand-new agent tailored to the task and add it to the team.
- **Str-1:** Select which candidate agents belong on the team before collaboration starts.
- **Str-2:** Choose which agents participate at each step, dynamically, using outputs from prior steps.
- **Str-3:** Route communication—decide which agent's output feeds into which other agent.

![The five optimization dimensions split into two functional axes (node capabilities) and three structural axes (graph topology)]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

Prior work covered slices of this space. DyLAN optimizes team composition (roughly Str-1) but uses unsupervised importance scores rather than task-grounded supervision. ADAS evolves agent prompts (Fun-1) but locks the collaboration structure. AFlow searches for workflow graphs with MCTS and supervised signals but doesn't touch agent prompts.

Each method optimizes one axis and freezes the rest. OMAC opens all five.

## Two Actors, One Loop

The single-dimension optimizer runs on two LLM-powered actors.

The **Semantic Initializer** takes the task description and the dimension being optimized, then generates a small, diverse collection of prompts—three candidate agents or controllers. Diversity comes from instructing the LLM to vary focal emphasis and implementation details.

Each candidate gets evaluated end-to-end. Plug it into the full multi-agent pipeline, run the collaboration on the training set, record the score.

Then comes the **Contrastive Comparator**. From the collection, sample a positive-negative pair—one high-performing, one low-performing. Feed both to an LLM and ask it to reason about what made the difference. The comparator produces a refined prompt designed to amplify the good factors and suppress the bad ones.

![The OMAC optimization pipeline: initialize candidates, evaluate, sample positive-negative pairs, contrastive reasoning, refine, repeat]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The cycle repeats: evaluate the refined candidate, sample a new pair, contrast, refine. The paper uses three iterations per dimension in its experiments.

The intuition is simple but powerful. The performance gap between a good run and a bad one is a supervised signal. Contrastive reasoning extracts the lesson.

### A Worked Example

Imagine optimizing a "Programmer" agent on HumanEval (Fun-1). The Semantic Initializer generates two prompts. The second one asks the agent to include comments throughout its code.

Both get scored. The comparator sees that the commenting prompt leads to higher Pass@1, reasons that inline comments improve logical coherence, and generates a refined prompt that not only requires comments but elaborates on their purpose and proper usage.

## From One Dimension to Many

Single-dimension gains are real but modest—the paper reports average relative gains of 3.6%, 2.8%, and 4.9% per dimension across the three benchmarks. The compound effect is where it gets interesting.

OMAC iterates across dimensions one at a time: optimize Fun-1, freeze the result, optimize Str-1, freeze the result, repeat. Why not optimize everything jointly? Because contrastive reasoning needs controlled variation. If you change prompts *and* routing *and* team selection simultaneously, the comparator can't isolate what's helping.

The catch is cost. Two dimensions over two iterations means four times the compute of one dimension. OMAC's pragmatic heuristic: run each dimension alone first, then pick the top improvers for joint iteration. It works well in practice, though the paper acknowledges exponential growth in dimension combinations without a theoretical fix.

## What They Found

On HumanEval (code generation, gpt-3.5-turbo-1106 across all methods):

- OMAC's best single-dimension result: **89.25 Pass@1**
- Best baselines: DyLAN at 85.74, AFlow at 85.63
- ADAS: 75.61

![Bar chart comparing OMAC against baselines on HumanEval code generation]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_016_05.png" | relative_url }})

On MMLU (general reasoning):

- OMAC: **74.22** accuracy
- AFlow: 70.06
- DyLAN: 69.42

On MATH (arithmetic reasoning):

- OMAC: **35.17%** accuracy
- AFlow: 32.49%
- DyLAN: 32.35%

The ablation tells the story of the Contrastive Comparator. Remove it—keep only the Semantic Initializer and pick the best candidate by score (OMAC-C)—and you still beat every baseline. But on the MATH task, contrastive reasoning adds roughly 0.7–1.2 percentage points on top, with similar trends on other benchmarks. The reasoning loop is the secret sauce, not just the search.

Joint multi-dimension optimization compounds the gains sharply. On arithmetic reasoning, single-dimension improvement goes from 2.9% to **9.6%** when iterating across the best two dimensions. Small per-dimension wins stack.

There's also a practical bonus. Optimized controllers dynamically select fewer agents and prune communication edges, so inference-time cost drops relative to baselines.

## Honest Limits

All experiments run on gpt-3.5-turbo-1106. The paper reports results with other base LLMs in an appendix, but whether the contrastive reasoning loop retains its edge on smaller, open-source models remains an open question—a weaker base model may not deliver the analytical depth the comparator demands.

The dimension-selection heuristic (pick the top single-dimension improvers) is pragmatic but theoretically thin. And multi-dimension optimization scales exponentially, which the paper flags without resolving.

Finally, the "optimization" in OMAC means improvement, not guarantee. The paper is explicit about this—there is no convergence proof. The framework works well in the regimes tested, but the boundary conditions remain unmapped.

## What This Means

OMAC gives the community a shared vocabulary. Think of your MAS as an information-flow graph with five optimizable axes, then iterate contrastive reasoning over supervised signals.

That's a recipe, not just a result. Swap in any LLM, and the loop structure stays the same.

From hand-wired teams to self-improving graphs—that's the shift OMAC carries.
