*TL;DR: Christoph Riedl gives multi-agent LLM systems their first falsifiable test for genuine synergy—and finds that adding a "think about what others might do" prompt transforms a chaotic swarm into a stable, goal-aligned collective, even though win rates look identical across conditions.*

## The Claim Nobody Could Prove

Everyone says multi-agent LLM teams are greater than the sum of their parts. Nobody could prove it.

Christoph Riedl's ICLR 2026 paper introduces the first falsifiable test for genuine cross-agent coordination. He adapts partial information decomposition—math that splits mutual information into redundancy, uniqueness, and synergy—and points it at multi-agent LLM systems. The result is a diagnostic that detects real coupling, localizes where it lives, and separates it from spurious correlation.

## A Game Designed to Punish Sameness

The experimental vehicle is group binary search, originally designed by Goldstone et al. (2024) for human subjects. Each agent privately guesses a number. The numbers must sum to a hidden target. The group receives only global feedback—"too high" or "too low"—and no agent can see anyone else's guess.

![Schematic of the group binary search task: three agents submit private guesses that sum to a group total, compared against a hidden target; agents only receive shared "too high" or "too low" feedback with no direct communication]({{ "/assets/figures/45d0b1ee0f1f/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

Identical strategies are self-defeating here. If everyone guesses the same number, the group overshoots and undershoots in lockstep, trapped in permanent oscillation. Success requires complementary strategies: some agents guess high, others guess low, settling into differentiated roles without ever talking.

That makes it a perfect microscope slide for studying whether emergence is real or cosmetic.

## Three Interventions, One Flat Win Rate

Groups of 10 GPT-4.1 agents play this game 200 times per condition (600 experiments total) under three prompt conditions:

- **Plain** — bare instructions, same as the human experiment.
- **Persona** — each agent gets a name, age, occupation, personality traits, and personal values.
- **ToM** — personas plus an instruction to "think about what other agents might do."

Here is the punchline: overall success rates do not differ significantly across the three conditions. Same win rates.

But peek inside the black box and the three conditions look nothing alike.

## The Diagnostic Toolkit

Before the results make sense, you need the three tests Riedl builds from partial information decomposition (PID). PID carves the mutual information that a set of sources carries about a target into four atoms: unique information from each source, redundant information they all share, and synergistic information that only exists in the joint.

The framework targets time-delayed mutual information—how agents' current states predict their future joint state. Three criteria extract different signals:

**Emergence capacity** asks whether pairs of agents carry joint predictive information that neither carries alone. For agents $i$ and $j$ at time $t$, with future joint state $T_{ij,t+\ell}$:

$$I(\{X_{i,t}, X_{j,t}\}; T_{ij,t+\ell}) = UI_i + UI_j + \text{Red}_{ij} + \text{Syn}_{ij}$$

A positive $\text{Syn}_{ij}$ means the pair predicts the future better together than either can alone. The median across all pairs gives the group-level score.

**Practical criterion** asks whether the macro state (group error) predicts itself better than the sum of its parts can individually:

$$S_{\text{macro}}(\ell) = I(V_t; V_{t+\ell}) - \sum_{k=1}^{n} I(X_{k,t}; V_{t+\ell})$$

Positive means the whole is literally more predictable than the sum of its parts.

**Coalition test** checks whether triplets carry predictive information beyond the best pair:

$$G_3 = I_3 - \max(I_{2\{1,2\}}, I_{2\{1,3\}}, I_{2\{2,3\}})$$

Each criterion ships with two falsification surrogates: row-shuffles (break identity-locked structure) and column-shuffles (break dynamic alignment while preserving individual dynamics). If the real data doesn't beat the shuffled nulls, the signal isn't real.

## From Gas to Crystal

All three conditions show significantly positive emergence capacity and practical emergence (Fisher joint p-values below $10^{-16}$). Multi-agent LLM systems *can* host synergy. That answers RQ1.

But the dynamical story diverges sharply. In the Plain condition, total stability—normalized $I_3$ serving as a proxy for Lyapunov stability—is indistinguishable from zero ($p = 0.976$). Groups float in a "gaseous" state, never settling into an attractor.

Persona doesn't fix this either ($p = 0.858$).

Only ToM produces a sharp jump in total stability ($p = 2.9 \times 10^{-14}$). The system falls into a deep basin of attraction. Agents converge on a shared schematic—a Schelling point—and the collective becomes robust.

![Multi-panel comparison of Plain, Persona, and ToM conditions across synergy, redundancy, and stability metrics: ToM produces significantly higher total stability and triplet mutual information while Plain and Persona remain near zero]({{ "/assets/figures/45d0b1ee0f1f/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

This mirrors what DeChurch and Mesmer-Magnus (2010) found in their meta-analysis of human teams: effective groups need both complementarity (distinct contributions) and redundancy (shared goal alignment).

The Plain condition has neither. Persona builds differentiation but no alignment. ToM builds both.

## Why Pairwise Is Enough

One surprise: the triadic information gain $G_3$ sits near zero in the Persona and ToM conditions. Stability is maintained by pairwise alignment, not irreducible three-body interactions.

In dynamical-systems terms, the ToM prompt creates Mean Field coupling. Because agents receive only global feedback and cannot see individual peers, they couple to the aggregate signal. Attempting complex higher-order synergy in that environment would be fragile. Instead, the system converges on the efficient solution: dense pairwise alignment to the Mean Field.

This is not a bug. It is the signature of a system that has found a stable regime.

## Synergy Needs Redundancy (and Vice Versa)

On their own, neither higher synergy nor higher redundancy predicts success. But when both are present, performance improves significantly—their interaction term is $\beta = 0.24$ ($p = 0.014$).

In marginal-effect terms: redundancy amplifies the benefit of synergy on the log-odds scale by 27%, and vice versa. The two amplify each other. Systems benefit when redundant pathways create goal alignment while synergistic interactions extract novel, non-overlapping information.

Causal mediation analysis backs this directionally: the ToM treatment indirectly increases performance by increasing synergy (ACME = 0.034, 95% CI: $-0.000$–$0.07$, $p = 0.053$). Marginal, but consistent with the story.

## The Model That Broke

Not every model plays along. LLAMA-3.1-8B "largely failed to break oscillatory cycles and develop goal-directed cross-agent synergy due to its lower ToM reasoning capacity"—the smaller model simply cannot model what others might do. The pattern holds across LLAMA-3.1-70B, Gemini 2.0 Flash, and QWEN3 with stronger emergent synergy under ToM, but LLAMA-3.1-8B stalls out.

And then there is QWEN3, the reasoning model. It exhibits something the paper memorably calls *paralysis under coordination ambiguity*. Faced with reconciling a local binary-search strategy with noisy group feedback, QWEN3 enters infinite chain-of-thought loops. The very reasoning capability that makes it strong in solo tasks becomes a liability when it must integrate conflicting social signals.

This is a frontier problem. Reasoning models in multi-agent settings face a qualitatively different failure mode than non-reasoning models.

## Where the Framework Hits Its Limits

The results rest on a single minimal task—group binary search—that deliberately isolates complementarity. Whether these patterns generalize to richer, real-world multi-agent tasks remains unproven.

The information-theoretic measures are limited to order $k = 2$ due to small-sample constraints, so higher-order synergy could be hiding in the data. And the causal link from synergy to performance rides on a marginally significant mediation ($p = 0.053$).

These are honest bounds, not dealbreakers. The framework itself is the contribution: a falsifiable, data-driven way to ask "is this group actually a group?" and get an answer that doesn't depend on win rates.

## Identical Outside, Different Inside

You can have identical win rates and completely different internals.

A collection of agents and a coordinated collective are not the same thing—and partial information decomposition can prove it. The cheapest lever for crossing that gap is a prompt that says "think about what others might do."
