*TL;DR: TMAS organizes test-time compute as a team of five specialized agents sharing two hierarchical memory banks—one for low-level experience, one for high-level strategies—and a hybrid RL reward that trains smaller models to close roughly 60% of the gap to models 7–8× their size on hard math benchmarks.*

## The Dead-End Problem

You have run eight parallel trajectories on a hard math problem. Three of them hit the same wrong lemma. Two more stall at a calculation that was already disproven three trajectories ago. The remaining three found the right answer—but they can't tell the others, because there's no shared memory.

That's the frustration facing current test-time scaling methods. They throw more compute at the problem—more rollouts, more iterations—but without a mechanism for trajectories to learn from each other's failures, they keep rediscovering the same dead ends.

TMAS asks: what if each rollout could read a cumulative log of what worked, what failed, and which strategic approaches are already exhausted?

## Two Memory Banks, Not One Dump

Not all shared information is created equal. Low-level findings—verified intermediate steps, specific error patterns, concrete computational shortcuts—serve a different purpose than high-level knowledge that "approach X has already been tried."

TMAS splits shared memory into two banks:

- **Experience bank**: low-level, trajectory-specific signals. Verified intermediate conclusions. Error patterns to avoid. Concrete skills that worked on subproblems.
- **Guideline bank**: high-level strategic memory. Which solution architectures have been explored. Which directions are exhausted. Think of it as a meta-record that says "don't try induction here again, we already did."

The ablation makes the split matter. Remove the guideline bank and early iterations suffer most—agents wander into already-explored strategies. Remove the experience bank and late iterations stagnate—agents can't reuse the granular progress they've already verified. Remove both and you get the steepest decline of all.

## Five Agents, One Loop

TMAS runs an iterative loop. Each iteration:

1. A **solution agent** generates $N{=}8$ candidate trajectories in parallel.
2. A **verification agent** runs $M{=}8$ independent checks per trajectory.
3. A **summary agent** condenses the verification feedback per trajectory.
4. An **experience agent** updates the experience bank from all rollouts.
5. A **guideline agent** updates the guideline bank from all rollouts.

![Architecture diagram showing the five specialized agents, the experience and guideline memory banks, and the exploration–exploitation branching within each iteration]({{ "/assets/figures/49f72749df87/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

The ε-greedy trick lives in step 1. With probability $1 - \epsilon$ (where $\epsilon = 0.2$), each solution trajectory conditions on the experience bank and previous rollouts—exploiting what's known. With probability $\epsilon$, it conditions only on the guideline bank—exploring directions the guidelines say haven't been tried yet.

$$c_{t,i} \sim \begin{cases} A_{\text{sol}}(Q, R_{t-1}, E_{t-1}) & \text{with probability } 1-\epsilon \\ A_{\text{sol}}(Q, G_{t-1}) & \text{with probability } \epsilon \end{cases}$$

The guideline bank doesn't tell the explorer what to do—it tells it what *not* to do. That small inversion keeps later rollouts from re-treading the same strategic ground.

## The Reward That Teaches Collaboration

Training a model to play well inside TMAS requires more than "get the right answer." A vanilla correctness reward leaves the model indifferent to the memory banks it's supposed to exploit.

TMAS introduces a hybrid reward with three components:

**Standard correctness.** $\tilde{r}_i = +1$ for correct, $-1$ for wrong. This keeps the baseline reasoning intact.

**Experience utilization.** The model needs to actually *use* the experience bank. Rollouts are split into a base group (no bank access) and a bank group (with bank access). The bank group gets an accuracy bonus that scales with problem difficulty:

$$\tilde{r}_i = r_i + \beta(1 - p_{\text{base}}), \quad i \in B_{\text{bank}},\; r_i = 1$$

where $p_{\text{base}}$ is the base group's accuracy without the bank. Harder problems—where $p_{\text{base}}$ is low—give bigger bonuses for solving them with the bank. This trains the model to lean on shared experience precisely when it matters most.

**Novel strategy exploration.** A four-way reward schedule based on two binary signals—correctness and guideline novelty:

| | Novel guideline | Known guideline |
|---|---|---|
| **Correct** | $+1.0$ | $+0.2$ |
| **Wrong** | $-0.5$ | $-1.0$ |

Correctness stays primary, but finding a *new* path to a correct answer earns a premium.

All three share the same GRPO training infrastructure—same clipped objective, same group-normalized advantage. Only the reward signal changes.

## What the Numbers Show

On IMO-AnswerBench-50 with Qwen3-30B, TMAS reaches 40.50% Pass@1 at iteration 19. The closest competitors—RSE and Verify-Refine—stall at 38.00% and 31.06% respectively. The gap widens with more iterations because TMAS keeps improving while others plateau.

![Line plot showing TMAS outperforming all baselines on IMO-AnswerBench-50, reaching 40.50% Pass@1 at iteration 19 while others plateau]({{ "/assets/figures/49f72749df87/fig_010_02.png" | relative_url }})

The hybrid reward RL results on Qwen3-4B are where it gets striking.

Without RL, TMAS on the 4B model hits 17.06% at iteration 19 on IMO-AnswerBench-50. With hybrid RL, it jumps to 30.88%. That narrows the gap to the 30B model from 23.44 points down to 9.62 points—a 59.0% relative reduction. On HLE-Math-100, the gap shrinks from 17.97 to 7.22 (59.8% reduction).

![Line plot comparing TMAS with hybrid RL, vanilla RL, and no RL on Qwen3-4B, showing hybrid RL narrowing the gap to the 30B model while vanilla RL degrades at late iterations]({{ "/assets/figures/49f72749df87/fig_020_05.png" | relative_url }})

And there's a subtler finding: vanilla RL (correctness-only reward) actually *degrades* performance at late iterations. Hybrid RL doesn't just boost the ceiling—it prevents the model from collapsing under its own repetitive rollouts.

## The Honest Limits

TMAS hasn't been tested on frontier models. If you're running GPT-5.5-class systems, you don't yet know whether multi-agent synergy adds anything on top of that scale. The upper bound remains unknown.

The RL pipeline also depends on a capable external teacher—DeepSeek-V3.2 generates the cold-start trajectory and memory data that seeds training. That's a dependency that won't disappear easily; you need someone to build the first round of experience and guidelines before the model can learn to use them.

And the sweet spot is narrow. Ablation shows $\epsilon = 0.2$ works best; $\epsilon = 0$ (pure exploitation) and $\epsilon = 1.0$ (pure exploration) both underperform. Verification count peaks at 8; 16 adds noise. More than 8 parallel solutions yields diminishing, sometimes unstable returns.

This is not "add more agents and it gets better." It's a balanced system that degrades when any component is over- or under-dimensioned.

## The Pattern Beyond Math

The experience/guideline split is a general organizational principle, not a math-specific trick. Any iterative multi-agent setting—code generation, scientific discovery, planning—has the same problem: low-level progress gets lost, and high-level strategies get re-explored. Two memory banks, one for *what works* and one for *what's been tried*, gives future work a clean abstraction to inherit.

The deeper lesson: test-time scaling doesn't have to mean more rollouts doing the same thing in parallel. It can mean rollouts that accumulate *structured shared memory*—and a reward signal that trains the model to write to that memory and read from it. The agent that knows what its siblings already tried doesn't waste compute on dead ends.

Dead ends, it turns out, are only repeated when no one writes them down.
