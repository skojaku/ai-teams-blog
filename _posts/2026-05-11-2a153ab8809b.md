*TL;DR: Picking training tasks at random for LLM multi-agent communication-structure optimization produces wildly unstable results — active learning via ensemble Kalman inversion restores reliability and boosts worst-case accuracy by up to 4 points, all under the same token budget.*

## A Graph Without a Compass

Run the same multi-agent LLM system twice with different random training tasks and you get very different communication graphs. The worst 25% of random-seed runs on MMLU average just 77.26% — barely above the 77.23% you'd get with no training at all. The graph you get depends heavily on which tasks you happened to pick.

Throwing more tasks at the problem barely helps. Scaling up random selection yields limited accuracy gains while burning proportionally more tokens. Yang, Dong, Negrut, and Wu from UW–Madison identify this instability as a first-class problem and propose a fix: stop treating task selection as an afterthought and start treating it as active learning.

## What Makes a Task Informative

Not all tasks are equally useful for learning *who should talk to whom*. A task that barely nudges the communication-graph parameters tells you little. A task that causes a big posterior shift tells you a lot. So we should pick the tasks that shift the posterior the most.

Formally, the utility of a task set $S$ is the KL divergence between the prior and posterior over graph parameters:

$$U(S) = I(S) = D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p(\mathbf{z} \mid S) \| p(\mathbf{z}))$$

A bigger divergence means the task set moves the parameters more — exactly what you want when your budget permits only a handful of training tasks.

## Estimating Information Gain Without Gradients

Here is the hard part. The forward map from graph parameters to MAS output is a black-box LLM pipeline — stochastic, non-differentiable, and nested three levels deep (task → multi-agent rollout → graph optimization → parameter update). You cannot backprop through it.

The authors sidestep gradients entirely with **ensemble Kalman inversion** (EKI), originally developed by Iglesias, Law, and Stuart (2013) and Kovachki and Stuart (2019) for derivative-free Bayesian inversion. The procedure:

1. Start with an ensemble of $J$ particles $\{\mathbf{z}^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^J$, each a set of mask logits for the communication graph.

2. Run every particle through the MAS on a candidate task $q$ to get scalar performance scores $l^{(j)} = \phi(\mathcal{G}(q; \mathbf{z}^{(j)}, \omega), q, y)$.

3. Compute empirical cross-covariances $\mathbf{C}_{\mathbf{z}l}$ and $\mathbf{C}_{ll}$ from the ensemble.

4. Update each particle: $\mathbf{z}^{(j)}_{\mathrm{post}} = \mathbf{z}^{(j)} + \mathbf{C}_{\mathbf{z}l}(\mathbf{C}_{ll} + \mathbf{\Gamma})^{-1}(l^* - l^{(j)})$.

5. Approximate information gain as the KL between the prior and posterior ensembles, fitting a Gaussian to each.

One EKI step is enough. The authors show that a single update reliably distinguishes informative tasks from uninformative ones. The connection to score-based posterior sampling — where the EKI update acts as a preconditioned likelihood score — explains why it works despite its simplicity.

## Building a Practical Pipeline

Evaluating every candidate task through EKI is still expensive. The authors build a two-stage selection pipeline:

**Stage 1 — Representative selection.** Embed all tasks with a sentence transformer, then greedily pick farthest-point samples from the embedding manifold. This shrinks a 1,000-task pool to roughly 50 diverse candidates — no MAS rollouts needed.

**Stage 2 — Informative selection.** Run EKI-based utility estimation on the compact pool. To avoid exhausting even this smaller set, fit a surrogate model $\hat{\psi}: \mathbf{v}(q) \mapsto U(q)$ from a few evaluated pairs, predict scores on unevaluated tasks, and use batch Thompson sampling to pick the next batch. Evaluate those, update the surrogate, repeat.

Ablation confirms that Stage 2 — the informativeness ranking — accounts for the bulk of the performance gain. Stage 1 is mainly a computational shortcut that shrinks the candidate pool without changing the final answer much.

## The Benign Setting: Tightening the Distribution

The experiments run six GPT-4.1-nano agents on MMLU and GSM8K, both with and without adversarial agents (three of six agents deliberately give wrong answers).

In the benign setting, active learning tightens the accuracy distribution rather than dramatically raising the mean.

| Metric (MMLU) | Random | Active |
|---|---|---|
| Mean accuracy | 79.69 ± 2.19 | **80.38 ± 1.35** |
| Worst-25% | 77.26 | **78.56 (+1.30)** |

On GSM8K, worst-25% improves by +0.71 points and the standard deviation drops from ±0.54 to ±0.28.

The primary effect is robustness: fewer cratered runs, more stable outcomes. The mean gain over random training is modest — but comparable to the gain random training provides over no training at all. Active learning extracts that gain reliably instead of leaving it to chance.

## Under Attack: Where Active Learning Shines

With three adversarial agents in the system, active learning improves both the mean and the tail:

| Metric (MMLU, attack) | Random | Active |
|---|---|---|
| Mean accuracy | 76.87 ± 3.72 | **78.32 ± 1.80** |
| Worst-25% | 72.00 | **76.25 (+4.25)** |

![Accuracy distributions under adversarial attack on MMLU: active learning collapses the left tail and raises the floor compared to random selection]({{ "/assets/figures/2a153ab8809b/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

The worst runs no longer crater. The standard deviation drops from ±3.72 to ±1.80 — the difference between a system you can ship and one you cannot.

On GSM8K under attack, mean accuracy improves by +0.93 points and worst-25% by +1.24 points.

These gains come under a matched total token budget. Active learning spends roughly seven units of budget on selecting the right tasks and one unit on training — and still outperforms spending all eight units on training with randomly chosen tasks. The selection overhead pays for itself.

## What This Builds On

The primary baseline is **Cut the Crap** (Zhang et al., 2025), which introduced the REINFORCE-based graph-parameter optimization that this paper's active-learning framework wraps around. That earlier work randomly sampled training tasks — exactly the source of instability this paper targets.

An alternative structural-search paradigm is **Assemble Your Crew** (Li et al., 2026), which generates topologies autoregressively rather than optimizing a mask. That approach searches a different design space but does not address task selection at all.

The EKI approximation itself comes from the Bayesian inversion literature (Iglesias, Law, and Stuart, 2013; Kovachki and Stuart, 2019). The paper extends it from a general mathematical tool into a practical, derivative-free active-learning estimator for multi-agent systems — a domain where gradient-based information-gain methods struggle with high variance from the black-box rollout.

## Honest Limits

The framework assumes labeled task–answer pairs are available. Extending it to unlabeled or generated tasks requires an outer expectation over possible labels — standard BALD territory, but not yet implemented.

EKI reliability with very small ensembles relative to parameter dimension remains unproven beyond the budgets tested. The authors explicitly flag this as future work.

Evaluation covers only two benchmarks and a single six-agent GPT-4.1-nano setup. Larger systems, different LLM backbones, and richer task distributions are open questions.

## Why It Matters

This is the first paper to show that *which tasks you train on* matters as much as *how* you train the graph — and that you can do something principled about it without touching a gradient. For anyone deploying multi-agent LLM systems where reliability trumps average-case performance, the +4.25-point worst-25% improvement alone is a reason to care. The derivative-free, ensemble-based approach also means the method ports to any black-box MAS, not just the one tested here.

In multi-agent communication design, task selection is not a preprocessing step — it is part of the optimization.
