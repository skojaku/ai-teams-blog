*TL;DR: Even frontier language models hit a wall when asked to plan silently, internally, without writing out their reasoning—and that wall is far lower than you might expect. Scaling up model size adds only two steps to the maximum depth of latent planning that can be discovered under final-answer supervision alone.*

## The Problem

Ask a language model to solve a problem step by step and it will.

Ask it to solve that same problem *silently*, keeping the steps internal, and performance collapses.

This isn't a trick. It's a fundamental limitation that Xu, Jettkant, and Ruis just quantified.

The question matters more than it might seem. Chain-of-thought (CoT) monitoring is a cornerstone of AI safety oversight. Overseers read the reasoning trace to check what models are actually doing. But that strategy only works if models *need* to externalize complex reasoning, not just prefer to. If a model could do deep multi-step planning internally, "thinking silently" would bypass the monitor entirely.

So: how much computation can an LLM actually perform inside a single forward pass, without writing anything down?

## The Setup

The authors use star graph path-finding—a clean lab test for planning depth.

![Architecture diagram showing the three-stage transformer pipeline]({{ "/assets/figures/6fd3b66192a9/fig_022_02.png" | relative_url }})

A star graph has one central hub and several equal-length branches. Given a source node and a target node somewhere deep in the graph, the model must identify the correct first branch.

No local heuristics help here. Every branch looks identical. The only way to succeed is to propagate information backward from the target, step by step, through the graph's structure. This is latent planning: the computation happens inside the model's weights, not on the page.

Crucially, models are trained with standard next-token prediction, supervised only on the final answer. No hints about intermediate steps. No curriculum that exposes shallow depths alongside hard ones. Just a pass/fail signal on the complete strategy.

This is a harder ask than prior work. Saparov et al. (2025) showed transformers can execute deep latent planning—but their training curriculum *bootstraps* by showing easy problems first. Bachmann & Nagarajan (2024) reported complete failure in a similar setup. Which result holds when you isolate pure discovery, without scaffolding?

## The Discovery Ceiling

Small transformer, trained from scratch: three steps.

Fine-tuned GPT-4o and Qwen3-32B: five steps.

GPT-5.4 with few-shot prompting: seven steps.

That is the entire story.

Going from a 1.6M-parameter from-scratch model to the most capable frontier model available adds *two steps* to what can be discovered autonomously.

![Architecture diagram showing the three-stage transformer pipeline]({{ "/assets/figures/6fd3b66192a9/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The failure is abrupt. At the ceiling, models don't degrade gracefully—they drop from near-perfect accuracy to random guessing overnight.

A model trained on six-step problems "remain[s] in the first training stage and produce random guesses." One more step, and the gradient signal simply vanishes.

## The Generalization Twist

Here is where it gets strange. A model trained on five-step problems doesn't just solve five-step problems.

It generalizes to eight-step problems at test time.

The discovered strategy extends beyond its training horizon. The authors call this the *generalization ceiling*: two steps beyond the discovery ceiling. Once a model stumbles onto the right internal algorithm, it can execute it further than it could have learned it directly.

This points to a dissociation. Discovering a latent strategy under final-answer supervision is fundamentally harder than executing one once found. The bottleneck is not computation—it's the sparsity of the training signal that lets you find the algorithm in the first place.

## Why Scale Doesn't Help

You might expect that a bigger model, trained longer, on more data, would eventually break through. The data says no—or at least, not much.

Increasing model depth, hidden dimension, or attention heads doesn't help the small transformer. Scaling across 7B–32B open-weight models and GPT-4o yields only incremental gains. GPT-5.4, a significant leap over GPT-4o, adds one step in zero-shot and two in few-shot. The ceiling persists even at the frontier.

The authors' diagnosis: scaling improves *breadth* (handling more branches) but not *depth* (more planning steps). A model that can juggle ten branches still chokes at step six when depth is what the task demands.

## The Backtracking Strategy

Attention analysis reveals what the models are actually doing. Successful planners learn to backtrack: follow the path from the target back toward the source, step by step, until reaching the branch point.

The backtracking ratio—a measure of how much attention flows along the target-to-source path—jumps from near-random (roughly $1/k$) to well above chance when the model has learned the strategy. Failed configurations show no pattern: attention diffuses uniformly, consistent with blind search.

Error analysis confirms it. For Qwen 2.5-7B, 95% of errors are *on-path*: the model correctly identifies the right branch but fails to complete all backtracking steps. It knows the answer but can't carry the computation far enough.

## CoT as a Workaround

The paper includes a control condition that cuts through the noise: what if models are *allowed* to externalize reasoning?

They train with a backtracking strategy written out in the chain of thought. The result is stark.

Graphs requiring twenty lookahead steps—four times the latent ceiling—converge in twenty training updates.

The task itself isn't hard. The bottleneck is *discovering* the latent strategy. Once you teach the strategy explicitly, even by a single demonstration in the CoT, models learn it rapidly and execute it deeply.

## The Limits

The study uses highly symmetric star graphs with no local heuristic cues. This is a lower bound on difficulty, not a ceiling on what's possible in messier domains. Real-world tasks with richer structure might partially bypass the bottleneck.

The authors cannot fine-tune GPT-5.4 or other closed-weight frontier models. The abrupt failure mode makes data scaling unlikely to close the gap—but this remains untested at the largest scale. The few-shot evaluation of GPT-5.4 serves as a proxy, not a final answer.

And the depth ceiling may not be specific to planning. Berglund et al. (2023) independently found LLMs struggle with implicit multi-step inference. If this generalizes, the implication is clear: complex reasoning may force externalization, not as a preference but as a necessity.

## The Closer

If these results hold beyond star graphs, they lend empirical credence to chain-of-thought monitoring as a viable oversight strategy.

The depth ceiling means models that want to think deeply have to think out loud.

And that, for now, is a window we can still look through.
