## Themes

**Consistency beats brilliance — again.** The coalition stability paper demonstrates that predictable team members outperform erratic ones; the temporal blindness paper shows that consistent time calibration matters more than sharp recall. Both argue from different angles that the binding constraint on agent systems is not peak capability but reliable, repeatable behavior.

**We are building agents faster than we can measure them.** OccuBench reveals that most professional domains have zero benchmarks. TicToc exposes a temporal blindness no existing eval caught. Does RL Expand the Capability Boundary of LLM Agents? proves the standard pass@k metric is structurally blind to compositional tool-use gains. Three independent gaps, one shared diagnosis: the evaluation apparatus lags the systems it is supposed to govern.

**RL is the post-training engine for everything that matters.** The capability boundary paper shows RL expands what agents can solve on compositional tasks. LLMOrbit documents RLHF as the dominant source of GPT-4's capability gains. Your LLM Agents are Temporally Blind fixes alignment with DPO. Three settings, one lever: after pre-training, the signal that reshapes agent behavior is gradient, not architecture.

**The scaling wall bends trajectories — it does not end them.** LLMOrbit's central claim is that data exhaustion forces every alternative paradigm into existence: test-time compute, sparse Mixture-of-Experts, small specialized models. OccuBench turns the same pattern inside out — the no-environments problem becomes a configuration problem when you let an LLM simulate the domain. Constraints create the alternatives.

## Editorial

Five papers, all tagged ai-teams, and the through-line is not "agents are getting better." It is "we are finally measuring where agents break."

The genuinely new results today are the 9:1 RL-over-SFT asymmetry in the capability boundary paper and the implicit-fault finding in OccuBench. The first isolates a causal mechanism — self-directed exploration under binary reward expands where expert imitation contracts — and it does so on matched training data, which is rare and convincing. The second flips the obvious intuition: silent data degradation hurts agents more than loud errors, because the agent never realizes it should retry. If you deploy agents in production, that result should make you nervous.

The coalition paper and the temporal blindness paper are incremental in the best sense: they formalize failures we already sensed and give them metrics. Nash stability at 41.8% and temporal alignment below 65% are numbers we did not have before. Now we do.

LLMOrbit is the outlier — a positioning survey rather than an experiment. Its data-exhaustion framing is grounded and useful, but the "70% of GPT-4's gains from RLHF" claim cites proprietary sources and the orbital metaphor organizes more than it explains. Worth skimming for the cost and energy tables; skip if you need depth on any single dimension.

The two papers that warrant a second look are OccuBench and the PASS@(k,T) analysis, and for the same reason: both expose a measurement problem we did not know we had. OccuBench's "strong agents make weak simulators" finding means your evaluation apparatus is not neutral — it is a variable. The capability boundary paper shows that the metric you choose determines whether RL looks like a reshuffle or a genuine expansion. In both cases, the evaluation is the claim.

Measure what you deploy, or you deploy what you measure.
