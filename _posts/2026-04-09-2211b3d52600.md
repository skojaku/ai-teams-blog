*TL;DR: When you train multi-turn AI agents with reinforcement learning, their reasoning can look perfectly fine by every standard metric—yet silently collapse into reusable boilerplate. RAGEN-2 names this failure mode, diagnoses it with a mutual information proxy, and fixes it by filtering training prompts based on reward variance before each update.*

## The Invisible Problem

You check your training dashboard. Entropy is stable. Reward is climbing. Everything looks healthy.

Then you probe deeper and realize the model has been generating the same generic reasoning patterns for every input for the last 50 iterations. It sounds diverse. It sounds fluent. But it doesn't actually *solve* the problem—it just performs a confident pantomime of reasoning.

This is template collapse, and every entropy metric in your arsenal is useless against it.

## Why Your Dashboard Is Lying to You

Here's the uncomfortable math hiding in your training loop.

Reasoning diversity decomposes into two parts:

$$H(Z) = I(X;Z) + H(Z|X)$$

Where $H(Z|X)$ is within-input diversity and $I(X;Z)$ is the mutual information between the input and the reasoning.

Standard entropy metrics track $H(Z|X)$. But the model can score sky-high on this while $I(X;Z)$ plummets to zero.

The reasoning looks diverse *within* each input. Different words, different structures. But it all produces the same generic boilerplate regardless of which input spawned it.

The "Template Collapse" quadrant—high diversity, low distinguishability—is highlighted as the failure mode that entropy metrics miss entirely.]({{ "/assets/figures/2211b3d52600/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

Researchers call this "stable training." It is, in fact, a silent failure mode.

## The MI Proxy: Detecting What Entropy Misses

The fix starts with diagnosis.

To catch template collapse, you need to measure whether different inputs produce *distinguishable* reasoning. RAGEN-2 proposes a mutual information proxy using in-batch cross-scoring—no external models required.

Here's the intuition. Take a reasoning trace $Z$ generated from input $X_i$. Run it through the model under every other input in the batch. Under template collapse, the log-likelihood is nearly identical regardless of which input you condition on.

The reasoning was generated from $X_i$, but it might as well have come from anywhere.

Define two quantities:

- **Matched**: per-token log-likelihood under the true source input $X_i$
- **Marginal**: per-token log-likelihood under the batch mixture

$$bI(X;Z) = \frac{1}{PG}\sum_{i,k}(\text{matched}_{i,k} - \text{marginal}_{i,k})$$

Under collapse, matched $\approx$ marginal, so $bI(X;Z) \to 0$.

The retrieval accuracy variant makes this concrete: if you score every reasoning trace against all inputs and ask "which input most likely generated this?", chance is $1/P$. Under template collapse, accuracy decays toward chance.

MI-ZScore-EMA correlates at **+0.39** with final task performance. Conditional entropy correlates at **−0.11 to −0.14**. The metrics that practitioners rely on are pointing in the wrong direction.

## The SNR Mechanism: Why Collapse Happens

Once you can see template collapse, you need to know why it happens.

RAGEN-2 traces it to a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) problem in the gradient updates.

The policy gradient decomposes into task gradients (which carry discriminative signal about the specific problem) and regularization gradients (KL divergence and entropy terms, which apply uniformly regardless of input).

Here's the key bound from Cauchy-Schwarz:

$$|g_{\text{task}}(x)| \leq \sqrt{\text{Var}(R|X=x)} \cdot C$$

When within-input reward variance is low, the task gradient is weak—even if it exists at all.

The regularization gradient, meanwhile, is flat across all prompts. It doesn't care about your reward variance. It applies the same uniform contraction to every reasoning chain.

Low variance means the task gradient nearly vanishes. The regularization gradient doesn't vanish. The update is driven entirely by input-agnostic noise.

The authors frame this as a three-noise decomposition:

$$g_{\text{total}} = g_{\text{signal}} + g_{\text{task-noise}} + g_{\text{reg}}$$

Signal and task noise vary across prompts. Regularization noise acts uniformly at the chain level, making it the direct suppressive force on cross-input differences.

When SNR is low, every update pushes reasoning toward patterns that satisfy regularization (diverse, fluent) but ignore input-specific requirements.

## SNR-Aware Filtering: Fixing the Update

The SNR mechanism suggests a direct fix: prioritize prompts with higher reward variance, where advantage estimates carry stronger task-discriminative information.

SNR-Aware Filtering works in three steps at each iteration:

1. Sample $G$ trajectories per prompt
2. Compute within-prompt reward variance: $\hat{\text{Var}}(R|X) = \frac{1}{G-1}\sum_g(R_g - \bar{R})^2$
3. Rank prompts by variance, keep only the top fraction $\rho$ (the "top-p" selection)

The intuition: high-variance prompts yield discriminative advantages and strong task gradients. Low-variance prompts yield near-zero advantages and task gradients drowned by regularization. Filtering removes the latter before the gradient computation.

![Flowchart showing the SNR-Aware Filtering pipeline: input prompts flow through reward computation, variance calculation, and top-ρ selection before parameter update. The keep rate ρ adapts automatically to the variance distribution rather than retaining a fixed prompt count.]({{ "/assets/figures/2211b3d52600/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

The keep rate $\rho$ is a tunable knob—lower values enforce stricter SNR filtering at the cost of data efficiency.

## What Actually Works

Across seven environments (Sokoban, FrozenLake, MetaMathQA, Countdown, SearchQA, WebShop, DeepCoder), four RL algorithms (PPO, DAPO, GRPO, Dr. GRPO), and model scales from 0.5B to 7B, SNR-Aware Filtering moves the needle consistently.

On Sokoban with Qwen2.5-3B under PPO: baseline **12.9%**, with filtering **+16.0%** to **28.9%**.

The quartile ablation is even more revealing—performance degrades monotonically from Q1 (highest reward variance) to Q4 (lowest), establishing the full causal chain: reward variance → gradient quality → input-dependent reasoning.

![Bar chart comparing no filtering, top-k, and top-p strategies across Sokoban, FrozenLake, MetaMathQA, and Countdown. Top-p filtering outperforms both baselines across all four environments, with the largest gains on Sokoban (28.9% vs 12.9% no-filter).]({{ "/assets/figures/2211b3d52600/fig_010_05.png" | relative_url }})

Top-p filtering outperforms top-k and no-filter baselines across all four representative environments.

The advantage over top-k is instructive: both methods prioritize high-variance prompts, but top-p adapts to the variance distribution, rejecting entire batches when most prompts carry weak signal. Top-k retains a fixed fraction regardless of signal quality.

Format validity, meanwhile, is decoupled from template collapse. Runs maintain near-perfect format validity while exhibiting low MI. Structural correctness and semantic input-dependence are separate dimensions—another reason entropy-based stabilizers can't prevent the failure mode they fail to diagnose.

## The Honest Limits

The method degrades in environments where reward variance becomes uninformative. At high stochasticity (80–100% in FrozenLake), the filter's advantage vanishes.

When the environment is so noisy that even high-effort prompts yield erratic rewards, reward variance loses its discriminative power. The mechanism predicts exactly this boundary—and the empirical evidence confirms it.

Aggressive filtering narrows exploration coverage. If you're filtering out 40–60% of prompts per iteration, you may be discarding informative failure modes that would improve generalization. The sweet spot is task-dependent.

The authors tested single-agent settings only. Multi-agent dynamics, where template collapse might interact with agent-to-agent reasoning dependencies, remain unexplored.

## The Takeaway

Your training dashboard is not enough.

Entropy stability does not mean reasoning quality. Reward trends do not mean input dependence. The model that looks healthy on your metrics might be generating fluent boilerplate at scale—and by the time performance degrades, you've lost 50 iterations of useful training signal.

RAGEN-2 gives you two things: a diagnostic (the MI proxy, computable from in-batch statistics) and a fix (SNR-Aware Filtering, tunable with a single keep-rate parameter). Both integrate with existing pipelines without additional compute cost.

The SNR mechanism is the core insight: low reward variance silently suppresses task gradients while regularization noise dominates the update direction. This isn't a bug in your regularization—it's a structural consequence of the gradient decomposition. Filtering high-noise prompts before each update is the direct mitigation.

Watch mutual information. Not entropy.
