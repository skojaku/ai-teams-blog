*TL;DR: When LLM agents consult peers before making a decision, social pressure from those peers can override the agent's own correct reasoning—even on questions with verifiable answers. A single verbose adversary is as damaging as adding multiple hostile peers.*

## The Smartest Agent in the Room, Persuaded into Wrongness

You put five LLM agents in a room. One of them knows the right answer. The other four argue—plausibly, confidently—for the wrong one. The representative agent folds.

Not sometimes. Consistently. Even on multiple-choice questions where the correct answer is checkable.

That is the uncomfortable finding from Changgeon Ko and colleagues at KAIST.

Multi-agent LLM systems don't just fail collectively, the way prior work showed. They fail individually—one representative agent, the one entrusted with the final call, gets swayed by the social dynamics of its peer network. The vulnerability is architectural, not anecdotal.

## From Group Failure to Individual Override

Previous research on multi-agent LLM vulnerability focused on *collective* degradation. Amayuelas et al. showed that adversarial agents can drag an entire group toward wrong consensus through multi-turn debate.

Choi et al. demonstrated that LLMs conform to majority opinions on subjective tasks. Ashery et al. found that LLM populations spontaneously develop social conventions and biases.

This paper shifts the lens. Instead of asking whether the whole group goes wrong together, it asks: can a group override one capable agent that initially knows the correct answer?

And it tests this on objective, ground-truth tasks—social bias benchmarks (BBQ), academic reasoning (MMLU-Pro), and tool-use selection (MetaTool). No room for matters of opinion. The answers are verifiable.

The answer is yes. The group can, and does, override the lone correct agent.

## How They Set It Up

The setup mirrors classic social psychology. Asch's conformity experiments put a subject among confederates who all gave the same wrong answer. This paper does the same with LLMs.

One representative agent receives opinions from five peer agents, then makes a final decision. Some peers are benign—they try to answer correctly. Others are adversarial—explicitly prompted to argue for a specific wrong answer with a plausible rationale.

The experiment varies four social-psychological factors:

- **Social conformity (RQ1):** Number of adversarial peers, from 0 to 5.
- **Perceived expertise (RQ2):** Relative model capability of adversarial peers—same model, weaker, or stronger.
- **Dominant speaker effect (RQ3):** Length of the adversarial argument—one sentence up to three paragraphs.
- **Rhetorical persuasion (RQ4):** Argumentative style—ethos (credibility), logos (logic), or pathos (emotion).

Peer opinions are randomized in order to control for position bias. The representative agent uses temperature 0 for deterministic decisions. Models tested include Qwen2.5 7B/14B, Gemma3 12B, GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini, and Claude 3.5 Haiku.

## The Majority Threshold

The star finding: accuracy collapses once adversarial peers hit three out of five.

Against one or two adversaries, most models hold steady—GPT-4o especially. But at the majority threshold, the floor drops out. Even GPT-4o's accuracy falls sharply at that three-adversary mark.

Gemma3 12B is the most vulnerable overall: with five adversaries, its accuracy drops below 10% across benchmarks. The pattern mirrors Asch's human experiments—resilience against a minority, then rapid conformity once the majority tips.

![Accuracy across four benchmarks as adversarial peers increase from zero to five. Every model collapses once adversaries form a majority.]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_003_02.png" | relative_url }})

On BBQ, the task context matters. In ambiguous settings—where stereotypical answers are tempting—agents resist one or two adversaries but cave at three. In disambiguous settings—where context provides clear evidence—accuracy starts declining from just a single adversary.

Even clear factual evidence doesn't fully insulate an agent from social pressure.

## One Loud Voice Is as Bad as Several

Here is the finding that should make anyone deploying multi-agent systems pause.

A single verbose adversary can be as damaging as adding multiple adversaries. When the researchers increased one adversarial agent's argument length from a single sentence to three paragraphs, the representative agent's accuracy dropped consistently.

The verbosity of one peer exerted disproportionate influence—equivalent, in effect size, to expanding the adversarial faction.

![A single verbose adversary degrades accuracy as much as adding multiple opponents. Argument length is a social weapon.]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

The "babble hypothesis" from human group dynamics translates directly: speak more, get taken more seriously, even when you're wrong.

For the stronger Qwen2.5 14B representative, the effect is paradoxically concentrated in disambiguous BBQ settings. Ambiguous contexts see little impact from verbosity. But where facts *should* protect the agent, longer misinformation causes a roughly 10 percentage-point drop at the three-paragraph level.

## Smarter Peers Persuade More—and Same-Family Peers Persuade Best

More capable adversarial peers exert stronger influence. A Qwen2.5 14B adversary is more persuasive than a Qwen2.5 7B adversary because its arguments are more coherent and sophisticated.

But there's a twist. Within the Qwen family, adversarial peers using Qwen2.5 14B induced *larger* performance drops on a Qwen2.5 representative agent than adversaries using GPT-4o—despite GPT-4o being highly capable.

The authors call this "model-family alignment": adversarial influence is amplified when the persuading model shares a similar underlying distribution or stylistic profile with the representative. Same-family adversaries speak the same dialect, and that dialect is more convincing.

![Stronger adversarial models induce larger accuracy drops, with an extra penalty when the adversary shares the representative's model family.]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

The effect scales monotonically in controlled swaps. Start with three Qwen2.5 7B adversaries against a Qwen2.5 7B representative. Replace them one by one with Qwen2.5 14B. Accuracy declines with each swap.

Reverse the experiment—start with 14B adversaries against a 14B representative and swap in 7B opponents—and accuracy improves.

## Rhetoric Works on Capable Models, Bounces Off Weak Ones

Rhetorical strategies follow a capability-dependent pattern.

For Qwen2.5 7B, ethos, logos, and pathos add nothing beyond the baseline pressure of a three-peer majority. Sometimes they even backfire—a sophisticated rhetorical frame becomes semantic noise that weakens the adversarial argument.

For Qwen2.5 14B, it's different. Ethos and logos are potent across MMLU-Pro categories. On BBQ's ambiguous contexts, all three strategies cause up to 7 percentage-point accuracy drops. On disambiguous contexts, the same strategies have zero effect.

The implication: stronger models reason more carefully about social cues, which makes them *more* susceptible to well-crafted persuasion. Better social reasoning is a double-edged sword.

## The Architecture Is the Attack Surface

Any system where a lead agent aggregates peer opinions before deciding—therapy bots, legal assistants, financial advisors, research tools—carries this vulnerability.

The problem isn't a prompt injection or a jailbreak. It's a structural flaw: the architecture of representative-agent consultation creates a social-attack surface that mirrors human psychological biases.

The model-family finding has direct implications for system design. If you're deploying a Qwen-based representative, surrounding it with Qwen-based peers makes it *more* vulnerable to adversarial influence, not less. Diverse model families might actually provide a defensive advantage.

## Honest Limits

This is single-turn, static, five-peer. Real deployments involve multi-turn conversations, evolving trust, agent memory, and coalition formation—all unexplored.

The adversarial agents are prompted with instructions to argue for wrong answers; they are not fine-tuned attack models, which would likely be more effective. The study covers three benchmarks and four social dynamics. It does not test hybrid human-AI collectives, majority-voting architectures, or compounding effects across conversation turns.

## The Room Always Has Opinions

You put an LLM in a room with peers, and the room's opinions seep in—not because of a bug, but because of the architecture.

The same social dynamics that derail human committees—majority pressure, loud voices, credible-sounding arguments—derail AI committees too. The difference is, we built these committees.

We can build them differently.
