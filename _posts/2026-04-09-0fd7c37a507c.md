*TL;DR: A new telecom-specific benchmark reveals that even the best instruction-tuned small language models struggle to follow structured troubleshooting workflows, exposing a critical gap between conversational fluency and operational reliability in domain-specific AI agents.*

## The Stakes

Imagine a network engineer hands an AI agent a ticket: "Downlink throughput is dropping on cell ABC123."

The agent responds confidently, generates a resolution summary, and logs the ticket.

Now imagine that same agent ran `coverage_map` before `oss_query`, recommended parameter changes without checking the KPI time series, and created a ticket about the wrong root cause entirely.

The agent sounded right. The process was wrong.

This is the gap TelcoAgent-Bench was built to measure.

## What Existing Benchmarks Miss

AgentBench, GAIA, and WebArena put LLM agents through their paces.

They test whether agents navigate websites, query databases, and finish tasks.

Telecom networks don't forgive process errors the way a web browser does.

In telecom troubleshooting, the *order* of actions *is* the workflow. You run `oss_query` before you optimize parameters. You check the KPI time series before you create a ticket. A tool called out of sequence isn't just inefficient—it can trigger unnecessary configuration changes or mask the real root cause.

Existing benchmarks measure task success, not process compliance.

They don't ask: did the agent follow the gold troubleshooting path?

TelcoAgent-Bench does.

## The Benchmark

Bariah, Mefgouda, Tavakkoli, Molero, Powell, and Debbah built a bilingual dataset around 15 telecom intents across 7 network management categories.

Each intent maps to a gold tool chain, like:

$$
\text{beam misalignment} \rightarrow \text{coverage\_map} \rightarrow \text{recommend\_param\_change} \rightarrow \text{create\_ticket}
$$

Each intent expands into 3–5 blueprints. Each blueprint generates 30 dialogue instances by sampling parameters within defined ranges.

The result: 49 blueprints, ~1,470 dialogues, all annotated with gold tool calls, flow alignment, and bilingual summaries.

The tool environment mixes core telecom tools (`oss_query`, `kpi_timeseries`, `recommend_param_change`, `create_ticket`, `coverage_map`, `neighbor_audit`, `push_config`) with distractor tools (`subscriber_insight`, `sla_policy_fetch`, `traffic_forecast`). Agents must choose wisely.

## The Metrics

Four dimensions, all grounded in the operational reality of telecom networks:

**Intent Recognition Accuracy (IRA)** measures whether the agent correctly identifies the troubleshooting intent from free-text problem descriptions. Embeddings are compared via cosine similarity:

$$
\text{IRA} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i=1}^{M} \frac{f(\tilde{y}^{(i)}) \cdot f(y^{(i)})}{\|f(\tilde{y}^{(i)})\|\|f(y^{(i)})\|}
$$

**Sequence Alignment Score (SAS)** evaluates tool execution order. It combines Mandatory Step Coverage (did the agent execute all gold steps in order?) with Extra Action Penalty (did the agent avoid distractors?):

$$
\text{MSC}(i) = \frac{\text{LCS}(\hat{A}^{(i)}, GP_b)}{m}
$$

$$
\text{EAP}(i) = 1 - \max(0, n-m)/n
$$

$$
\text{SAS}(i) = \text{MSC}(i) \cdot \text{EAP}(i)
$$

**Resolution Accuracy (RA)** measures semantic similarity between the agent-generated closing summary and the gold summary. High RA means the ticket reads correctly.

**Blueprint Reliability Score (BRS)** tests stability: does the agent follow the same path when given three different parameter variations of the same blueprint? It combines exact path consistency (GPC-0), tolerant consistency allowing one Levenshtein edit (GPC-1), and sequence dispersion (SD):

$$
\text{GPC-0}(b) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}[\hat{A}^{(i)} = GP_b]
$$

## What the Models Show

Eight small language models entered the benchmark: Llama-3-8B, Qwen-2.5-7B, Qwen-3-8B, Qwen-7B, Mistral-7B, Granite-3.1-MoE-3B, Granite-3.3-8B, and Gemma-3-4B.

**Intent recognition held up.** Gemma-3-4B hit 0.926 IRA in English with the intent list available. Drop the list—force the agent to infer intent from free text—and performance drops to 0.682.

**The sequence problem was brutal.** The best model, Granite-3.3-8B, achieved SAS of 0.644 in English and 0.623 in Arabic. The overall best SAS. Not a passing grade by any operational standard.

**Mandatory step coverage revealed a trade-off.** Qwen-7B hit 0.799 MSC in English—it executed the required steps—but only 0.367 EAP, meaning it fired off irrelevant tools constantly. Other models went the opposite direction: tight tool discipline, but missing half the required steps.

**Reliability scores were alarming.** Most models scored near-zero on exact path consistency (GPC-0). Granite-3.3-8B managed 0.303 in English—the best in the field. For everyone else, the same blueprint with slightly different parameters produced wildly different tool sequences.

**Resolution summaries looked decent.** RA scores ranged from 0.82 to 0.89 in English. The models could write a coherent closing paragraph even when they'd called the wrong tools in the wrong order.

**Arabic lagged English.** The gap was consistent across metrics, reflecting limited multilingual technical capabilities in current models, particularly for telecom-specific terminology.

## Why It Matters

The telecom industry is moving toward autonomous networks. Agents will triage tickets, diagnose anomalies, and recommend parameter changes.

An agent that misdiagnoses a beam misalignment could trigger a cascade of unnecessary configuration changes. The stakes are different from a chatbot that hallucinates a recipe.

Operators need quantified reliability guarantees before deployment. "The model generated a good answer" isn't enough.

"The model followed the gold troubleshooting path in 90% of variations"—that's the standard.

TelcoAgent-Bench provides the measurement framework we didn't have.

## The Limits

The benchmark evaluates open-loop reasoning. Agents receive an initial problem statement and generate a tool sequence and resolution. They don't interpret tool outputs, apply configuration changes, or observe network behavior before closing the ticket.

Closed-loop operational reasoning—where agents read the KPI time series, observe the result, and adapt—is the next frontier.

The authors flag this honestly.

## The Bottom Line

Current small language models can understand telecom problems reasonably well. They struggle to follow the troubleshooting steps reliably, and they collapse under scenario variations that a human engineer would handle consistently.

The fluency is there. The operational discipline is not.

Granite-3.3-8B emerged as the most balanced performer—best SAS, best BRS, decent IRA. But "best in class" at 0.644 SAS and 0.303 GPC-0 is a long way from production-ready for live networks.

TelcoAgent-Bench gives operators a yardstick. Now the model builders have something concrete to optimize toward: not just better answers, but better process.
