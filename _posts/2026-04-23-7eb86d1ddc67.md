*TL;DR: A single tool-equipped agent outperforms both bare models and multi-agent teams across 27 small language models on 20 financial tasks—proving that system design, not model scale, is the lever that matters when you can't afford a cloud LLM.*

## One Agent, Tools, and the Numbers That Changed My Mind

A regional bank under GDPR can't send customer data to GPT-4. A boutique fund can't spin up a private LLM cluster. These aren't edge cases—they're the deployment reality. And that reality runs on models smaller than 10 billion parameters.

Wang and Brorsson asked the question nobody was asking: if you're locked to small models, does wrapping them in agent architectures actually help, or does it just add complexity?

They tested 27 open-source SLMs across three paradigms—bare prompting, a single agent with tools, and a four-agent team—and measured not just accuracy but latency, energy, completion rate, and token cost.

The answer is sharp: one agent with tools wins. More agents mostly costs more and breaks more often.

## The Scaling Trap

The prevailing assumption says bigger models do better. Kaplan et al.'s scaling laws gave us that intuition, and it holds—when you can freely scale.

But under a 10B-parameter budget, the authors find a "U-turn." Among base models, the 8B–10B variants move toward *lower* effectiveness while energy per token stays high. Raw capacity, without architectural scaffolding, hits diminishing returns and then negative ones.

This matters because benchmarks like HELM and the Open LLM Leaderboard measure accuracy in a vacuum. They tell you which model scores highest, not which one actually ships under latency, energy, and reliability constraints.

## Three Paradigms, Head to Head

The experiment design is refreshingly clean. Every model runs under all three architectures:

- **Base SLM** — prompt in, answer out. No tools, no agents.
- **Single-Agent System (SAS)** — one ReAct agent with a calculator, wiki search, and web search, looping through think–act–observe cycles.
- **Multi-Agent System (MAS)** — one supervisor plus three specialists (FinancialKnowledgeAgent, FinancialNLPAgent, FinancialQuantAgent), each with restricted tool access.

All 27 models—spanning Qwen, Llama, Gemma, Phi, DeepSeek, Mistral, and Solar families—run the same 20 financial datasets across eight task categories: sentiment, classification, NER, QA, stock prediction, credit scoring, summarization, and bankruptcy prediction. Temperature is zero. ReAct turns are capped at five. The only variable is the architecture.

## The Numbers That Matter

SAS achieves a Normalized Response Quality (NRQ) of **4.85**—since NRQ measures improvement over the base model, SAS delivers nearly five times the proportional gain. MAS clocks in at just **0.36**, barely above the baseline. The multi-agent setup doesn't stack on top of the single agent. It barely outperforms doing nothing at all.

![NRQ comparison across the three paradigms, showing SAS far ahead at 4.85 while MAS barely exceeds the baseline at 0.36]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

Reliability collapses as you add complexity. Completion rate drops from **99.67%** (base) to **79.92%** (SAS) to **72.01%** (MAS). Nearly three in ten MAS runs fail to produce a valid answer.

Energy per token does improve—**1.83 → 1.02 → 0.52 mJ**, a 71% reduction from base to MAS—because models generate shorter per-step outputs. But total tokens per sample explode: **2,356 → 8,040 → 14,618**.

Average latency roughly doubles, from **12.39 seconds** (base) to **23.27 seconds** (SAS) and **22.76 seconds** (MAS). You pay in time and tokens for every layer of coordination you add.

The Pareto frontier in the energy-vs-effectiveness plot belongs to SAS. You get most of the quality gain at moderate cost. MAS sits off the frontier—more variance, more energy, more failure.

![Energy-effectiveness scatter plot showing SAS occupying the Pareto frontier while MAS trades effectiveness for lower per-token energy]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

## Where Each Paradigm Shines

No single architecture wins everywhere.

**SAS** dominates QA, summarization, and credit scoring. On smaller models like Qwen2.5-0.5B and Llama-2-7B, SAS improves over alternatives by more than 150%.

**MAS** edges ahead on bankruptcy prediction, where multiple perspectives reduce risk—but the margin is often small.

**Base SLM** stays best for classification, NER, and sentiment—tasks where agentic overhead dilutes precision on token-level extraction.

The tiniest models tell the most brutal story. On Gemma-3-270M, the agent systems simply fail to run on most tasks. The base model, limited as it is, still completes nearly everything.

## How the Systems Break

Failure analysis reveals two distinct pathologies.

In SAS, the dominant failures are **Context Length Exceeded** and **No Delegation**—the agent iterates until it exhausts the context window, or decides not to use tools at all.

In MAS, the problem shifts to **Delegation Failure** and **No Delegation** between agents. The supervisor hands off to a specialist that can't handle it, or never hands off in the first place.

Inter-agent dynamics inject a new kind of failure—the system gets stuck in hand-off loops rather than solving the task.

![Failure mode distribution showing Context Length Exceeded and No Delegation dominating SAS, while Delegation Failure and No Delegation dominate MAS]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

The bottleneck isn't raw generation capacity. It's context management and instruction adherence.

## The Honest Bounds

These results come with fixed choices: ReAct prompting only, a five-turn cap, simple static delegation. Adaptive routing, dynamic tool selection, or more sophisticated control strategies could shift the trade-off.

The evaluation is limited to English financial NLP tasks. Multilingual settings, real user traffic, and streaming deployments may tell a different story.

And the "U-turn" pattern—where larger base models get worse—deserves deeper investigation. The authors flag it but don't fully explain it. It might reflect training corpora, architecture mismatches, or something else entirely.

## What This Means

For anyone deploying under resource or privacy constraints, the takeaway is a decision framework, not a ranking:

- Complex reasoning tasks? Use SAS.
- Simple extraction and classification? Stay with base.
- High-stakes tasks that benefit from multiple perspectives? MAS—but accept the cost and the failure rate.

The deeper message: architectural design can compensate for—or negate—model capacity. Shen et al. showed that small models struggle with tools and proposed multi-model collaboration. Wang and Brorsson show that the coordination tax from that collaboration outweighs the benefit. FinGPT built domain-specific financial LLMs on cloud infrastructure; this work asks what you do when the cloud isn't an option.

Scale isn't the only dial. Sometimes the best move is to stop reaching for more parameters and start designing the system around the model you have.
