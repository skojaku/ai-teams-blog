*TL;DR: Three eigenvalues of a successor-representation matrix computed from the communication graph alone can predict how a multi-agent LLM topology will fail—drift, divergence, or brittleness—before you generate a single token.*

## The Stability Paradox

The chain topology is the most spectrally stable way to wire up a team of LLM agents.

And yet empirically, it accumulates roughly twice the error of every alternative.

That is the central surprise in Parks and Alharthi's paper. The chain—where each agent whispers to the next, no loops, no feedback—looks like the safest design on paper. Its spectral radius is 1, its spectral gap is 0, and its condition number is the smallest among the three topologies they study.

Every linear-algebraic instinct says this thing should not amplify noise.

But it does. Each agent's small, systematic bias gets handed forward like a rumor that nobody corrects. There is no averaging step to soak up the drift, so it compounds monotonically across all twelve steps.

They call this the *stability paradox*: linear spectral stability and stochastic-drift stability are two different notions. A topology can be maximally stable in the first sense while being maximally fragile in the second.

## Predicting Failure Before Inference

The paper's larger claim is that you do not need to run a pipeline to see these failures coming.

Given a communication graph—chain, star, mesh, or something richer—you row-normalize its adjacency into a stochastic operator $P$ and form the successor representation:

$$M = (I - \gamma P)^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k P^k$$

This is the same object Dayan introduced in 1993 to compress expected future state occupancy in reinforcement learning. Stachenfeld et al. later showed that the hippocampus appears to maintain something like it—a predictive map of the environment.

Parks and Alharthi transport it to a new setting: the "environment" is the communication graph, and $M$ compresses every multi-step influence pathway among agents into one linear operator.

From $M$ they pull three scalars:

- **Spectral radius** $\rho(M)$ — bounds the geometric growth of deterministic perturbations.
- **Spectral gap** $\Delta(M)$ — governs mixing time, hence convergence to consensus.
- **Condition number** $\kappa(M)$ — measures sensitivity of the output to perturbations of the input.

Each scalar targets a distinct failure mode. And each one is computable in milliseconds from the adjacency matrix, before you ever call a language model.

## Three Topologies, Three Spectra

The paper derives closed-form spectra for three canonical topologies under row-stochastic normalization, at $\gamma = 0.9$.

![The three communication topologies studied: a 12-agent chain, a four-leaf star with judge aggregation, and a four-agent mesh with peer deliberation and majority vote]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

For the **chain**, the adjacency is a 12×12 upper shift matrix. It is nilpotent—information flows one way and never comes back—so $M$ is a finite sum with diagonal entries equal to 1. This gives $\rho = 1$, $\Delta = 0$, $\kappa \approx 9.95$.

For the **star** (four leaves, one judge), the transition matrix has eigenvalues $\{1, 0, \ldots, 0, -1\}$, yielding $\rho = 10$, $\Delta = 9$, $\kappa \approx 28.61$.

For the **mesh** (four agents with peer deliberation and majority vote), the complete-graph eigenvalues $\{1, -\frac{1}{3}, -\frac{1}{3}, -\frac{1}{3}\}$ map to $\rho = 10$, $\Delta \approx 9.23$, $\kappa \approx 13.00$.

The chain is the odd one out: its nilpotency collapses the spectral radius and kills the gap entirely.

## What They Found

The empirical study runs 100 trials of a 12-step structured state-tracking task with Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. Each step updates a JSON state with three fields—a floating-point Value, a binary Parity, and a bounded integer Level—through order-dependent rules that couple arithmetic, branching, and bounded updates.

Three metrics, one per spectral quantity.

### Condition number → perturbation robustness

The ordering $\kappa_{\text{chain}} < \kappa_{\text{mesh}} < \kappa_{\text{star}}$ matches the empirical perturbation-sensitivity ordering exactly ($r_s = 1.0$). The chain is the hardest to knock off course with a single adversarial nudge; the star, with its judge bottleneck, is the easiest.

If you compute only one number before choosing a topology, make it $\kappa$.

### Spectral gap → consensus dynamics

The chain ($\Delta = 0$) has no path to consensus, as predicted. But the star and mesh are inverted relative to the raw gap's prediction: the star's judge-aggregation bottleneck accelerates consensus beyond what the graph-level gap captures. The rank correlation is $r_s = 0.5$—partial, not perfect.

The gap sees mixing on the graph but not the effect of explicit aggregation operators inserted into the pipeline.

### Spectral radius → cumulative error (inverted)

Here is the paradox again, backed by numbers. The chain has the smallest spectral radius ($\rho = 1$) but the largest cumulative error ($2094 \pm 843$). The star ($1184 \pm 952$) and mesh ($1241 \pm 950$) both have $\rho = 10$ but roughly half the error.

The rank correlation is $-1.0$—perfectly inverted.

![Empirical cumulative error across 100 trials: the chain shows the highest error despite the smallest spectral radius, illustrating the stability paradox]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_008_04.png" | relative_url }})

## The Drift-Corrected Gain

The inversion is not a bug in the framework. It is a signal that the homogeneous linear spectrum cannot see inhomogeneous stochastic drift.

The paper models this explicitly. Each agent produces a state with iid zero-mean noise of variance $\sigma^2$. For a topology that averages $k$ agents per step, the effective per-step noise variance drops to $\sigma^2 / k$.

Assuming the transition rule is approximately Lipschitz with constant near unity, iterating for $T$ steps gives:

$$E[E_{\text{ceg}}] \approx c \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{k}} \cdot T^{3/2}$$

The chain has $k = 1$; the star and mesh both have $k = 4$. So the predicted chain-to-aggregated ratio is $\sqrt{4} = 2$.

The empirical ratios are 1.77 (chain/star) and 1.69 (chain/mesh)—within sampling variance.

They summarize this with a drift-corrected gain:

$$\tilde{\rho}(M; k) = \rho(M) \cdot \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i}\frac{1}{k_i}}$$

where $k_i$ is the number of inputs aggregated at agent $i$. On the chain, the aggregation factor is 1 throughout; on the star and mesh, it contracts $\tilde{\rho}$ by $\approx 0.5$ relative to a chain of equivalent depth.

The rank order of $\tilde{\rho}$ across all three topologies now matches the empirical error ranking.

![Predicted vs. empirical error ratios showing the drift-corrected gain recovers the correct rank ordering: the affine-noise model predicts a ratio of 2, while observed chain-to-star and chain-to-mesh ratios are 1.77 and 1.69]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_019_06.png" | relative_url }})

The correction is lightweight: you need only the row-stochastic adjacency and the in-degree profile of aggregation nodes.

## What This Changes

Right now, if you are choosing between a chain, a star, and a mesh for your multi-agent LLM pipeline, you run each one and see what breaks. HELM and AgentBench can tell you *post hoc* which topology worked on a specific task. Du et al.'s multi-agent debate work showed that debate topologies improve factuality—but offered no structural predictor of when they will amplify drift instead.

This paper opens a different door. Compute $\kappa$, $\Delta$, and $\tilde{\rho}$ from the adjacency matrix, rank your candidates, and decide before generating a single token.

That is the move from "try topologies and see what breaks" to "read three scalars and predict which one breaks, and how."

## The Honest Limits

Three topologies, one model family (Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct), one task family, 100 trials per condition. A Spearman coefficient over three ranks carries almost no statistical power by construction.

The affine-noise model assumes iid noise across agents. In practice, agents sharing a base model likely share systematic biases—correlated noise that would erode aggregation's variance-reduction benefit. The paper sketches a correlation-aware extension: with inter-agent correlation $\rho_c$, the chain-to-aggregated ratio collapses from $\sqrt{k}$ to $\sqrt{k / [1 + (k-1)\rho_c]}$, which reaches unity as $\rho_c \to 1$.

The Lipschitz-near-unity assumption can also break for tasks with sharp conditional branching. And claims about model scale, closed-weight systems, or topologies beyond the chain–star–mesh triple are predictions of the framework, not empirical findings.

The paper is explicit about all of this. It offers a framework with derivable predictions and a first controlled instantiation—not a universal law.

The spectral radius said the chain was safe. The chain was the one that drifted.
