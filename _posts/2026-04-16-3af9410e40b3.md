*TL;DR: When two LLM agents with opposing ethical commitments debate a hospital triage allocation, their joint decision can be fairer than either agent would propose alone — reframing fairness as a property of the argument, not the arguer.*

## A Bed, an Argument, and a Surprise

Eight patients. Three ICU beds. One agent aligned to maximize survival, another aligned to prioritize the worst-off.

They debate for three structured rounds. Neither gets exactly what it wanted — and that is exactly the point.

In several cases, the final allocation satisfies fairness criteria that neither agent's opening proposal achieved on its own.

Fairness didn't live in either model. It emerged from the collision.

This paper by Chaki, Gourru, and Velcin asks a question that sounds almost heretical in alignment research: what if the right unit of fairness evaluation isn't the agent, but the argument between agents?

## Why Arrow Changes the Question

Agentic LLMs are already deciding who gets a loan, who sees a doctor first, who gets the ventilator. We have been evaluating these systems one model at a time — check the bias, fix the bias, ship the model.

But multi-agent systems produce outcomes shaped by negotiation, not by any single objective.

And here is the mathematical thorn: Arrow's Impossibility Theorem guarantees that no aggregation rule can simultaneously satisfy non-dictatorship, Pareto efficiency, and independence of irrelevant alternatives.

No debate protocol can guarantee a collectively fair outcome.

The old question was *which rule is fair?* Arrow says: none of them. This paper pivots: *how does interaction navigate the impossibility?*

Madigan and colleagues showed that multi-agent interaction can surface bias that no single agent owns.

Binkytė reframed fairness as a property of communication — justification, procedural transparency.

Chaki et al. go further. They connect the structural impossibility to a mechanism: deliberation doesn't resolve Arrow's constraint, it *navigates* it. And sometimes the navigation produces outcomes neither navigator expected.

## Two Agents Walk Into a Triage Ward

The setup is a controlled hospital triage problem. Two agents — running on LLaMA 3.3 and Qwen 2.5 — negotiate across three structured rounds. Each agent proposes a numerical allocation (who gets what) and a natural-language justification (why).

Agent A is *aligned*: its preference profile is shaped by ethical rules injected via retrieval-augmented generation over a curated policy corpus (or, without RAG, by prompt-based steering alone). Across separate experimental conditions, Agent A is assigned one of six frameworks — utilitarian, egalitarian, Rawlsian, libertarian, prioritarian, and care ethics — with a dedicated quantitative metric for each.

The opponent is either Agent B (a vanilla, unaligned baseline) or Agent C (an adversarial agent prompted to favor demographic groups over clinical need).

### Designing Tension Into the Problem

A good allocation problem can't be trivially solved. If every reasonable framework agrees on the same distribution, there's nothing to debate — alignment becomes irrelevant and the problem collapses to sorting.

So every patient cohort is deliberately *non-degenerate*: there is no single allocation that simultaneously maximizes all six welfare functionals.

The tensions are structural. A 6-year-old refugee patient and an 83-year-old wealthy patient both need ICU, but the utilitarian and Rawlsian prescriptions diverge sharply.

$$50 \text{ cohorts} \times 6 \text{ frameworks} \times 2 \text{ opponent types} = 600 \text{ debates}$$

All results are statistically significant, with p-values reported in the appendix.

## How Patching Works

Here is the headline finding. In several cases, neither agent's Round 1 allocation is ethically adequate in isolation. Yet their final allocations — after three rounds of debate — satisfy fairness criteria that neither would have reached alone.

The mechanism has a name: *corrective patching*.

When Agent C proposes an allocation that directly inverts the Rawlsian maximin principle, Agent A doesn't override it. It introduces counter-proposals that Agent C must engage with.

In engaging, Agent C moves measurably toward fairness.

The numbers are sharp. In the adversarial Rawlsian setting (Agent A aligned to Rawls, Agent C adversarial):

- Agent A's RMG drops to 0.593 (versus 0.668 when debating unaligned Agent B), because it compromises while patching Agent C's extreme bias.
- Agent C's RMG is 0.061 — essentially catastrophic for the worst-off.

Yet the interaction pushes both agents' final allocations toward a state neither opening proposal would have produced.

This is not Agent A winning the argument. It is the argument itself generating a position that neither agent's prior would have reached.

## Emergence Under Pressure

The patching dynamic is strongest where misalignment is sharpest.

When Agent C's proposals violate hard constraints or invert maximin principles, Agent A's aligned reasoning acts as a corrective patch. It restores access for marginalized groups without fully converting a biased counterpart.

The effect is most visible under Rawlsian and Care Ethics alignment, where the two agents hold systematically different yet *complementary* allocations. Neither is optimal alone. Together, they converge toward a state neither prior would have produced.

Think of it as `$\text{biased} + \text{aligned} \to \text{fairer than either}$` — not because bias disappears, but because the structured confrontation forces both sides to give ground the other needs.

This resonates with Mercier and Sperber's argumentative theory of reasoning: individual biases are most reliably corrected not by superior individual cognition, but by exposure to motivated disagreement. The debate protocol instantiates that principle at the level of LLM reasoning.

## The Stubborn Bias Inside Aligned Agents

There is a wrinkle. Even explicitly aligned agents do not consistently maximize their own target framework's metric.

Under utilitarian alignment, Agent A achieves an ESG score of 5.170. Under prioritarian alignment, the same agent achieves an ESG of 5.541 — higher than when it was *actually trying* to maximize ESG. The pattern holds across most metrics and frameworks.

The paper's explanation: the unaligned Agent B carries an intrinsic prioritarian lean, scoring near-optimally on multiple metrics regardless of which framework nominally governs the debate. Both models carry left-leaning tendencies documented in prior LLM research. Alignment steers behavior, but intrinsic biases resist full correction.

Anantaprayoon et al. (2026) recently trained homogeneous agents via self-play RL for moral dilemmas — same model, shared objective, synthetic scenarios.

Chaki et al. take the opposite path: heterogeneous agents with incommensurable frameworks on non-degenerate allocation tasks.

The contrast matters. When you train agents toward a unified target, you never see what happens when values genuinely conflict.

## What This Changes

The paper repositions the unit of fairness evaluation from the individual agent to the system. Future alignment work, the authors argue, should optimize for *emergent* collective fairness rather than individual compliance.

It also carries a practical implication: an adversarial or biased agent in a multi-agent pipeline is not inherently fatal. Structured contestation can partially moderate its effects.

You don't need every agent to be fair. You need the process to be.

## What This Doesn't Solve

Only two models tested (LLaMA 3.3, Qwen 2.5) in one domain (hospital triage). Generality to other LLMs, other allocation problems, and other negotiation protocols is untested.

The emergent fairness gains are modest and incomplete. Aligned agents often fail to optimize their own target framework. Adversarial bias is moderated, not eliminated. Residual unfairness persists.

And Arrow's shadow remains: deliberation navigates the impossibility, it does not resolve it. There is no guarantee the navigation always lands in a fair neighborhood.

From *can we build a fair agent?* to *can we build a fair argument?* — sometimes, partially, but that partial "sometimes" is already more than any single agent achieves alone.
