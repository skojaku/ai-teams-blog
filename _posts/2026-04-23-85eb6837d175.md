*TL;DR: Giving LLM agents an explicit warmth–competence rating of their teammates—borrowed straight from social psychology—cuts payoff loss by up to 77% in economic games and lifts real multi-agent benchmarks by 3–29%, all with no fine-tuning.*

## Your Agent Team Has a People Problem

Three agents are building a web app. One keeps shipping syntax errors. Another quietly works toward a different goal.

A third is competent and cooperative—but no one notices, because nobody is keeping score on *who* their teammates actually are.

Multi-agent LLM systems promise to distribute complex work across specialized collaborators. In practice they stumble over coordination failures—goal drift, error cascades, misaligned actions—that grow sharper as teams scale.

Role assignment and chain-of-thought prompting improve individual task performance, but they don't give agents a principled way to reason about *who they are working with* and *how to adapt*.

That gap is exactly where Explicit Trait Inference (ETI) steps in.

## Stealing from Social Psychology

Since at least Fiske et al.'s 2007 work, social cognition research has converged on a strikingly compact model: when humans size each other up, they do it along two dimensions—**warmth** (cooperativeness, trustworthiness, goal alignment) and **competence** (skill, reliability, efficiency).

These aren't polite abstractions. They directly predict whom we trust, whom we delegate to, and whether coordination succeeds.

ETI operationalizes that exact taxonomy for LLM agents. After each interaction round, the agent rates every partner on eight behaviorally anchored traits—four under warmth, four under competence—using a 1–7 Likert scale, with a short evidence justification for each rating.

Those trait profiles are then injected into the agent's context for all future planning and execution.

Low warmth signals prompt the agent to clarify intentions, discount unreliable input, or guard against adversarial behavior. Low competence signals prompt it to reassign work, add verification steps, or adjust task difficulty.

`warmth → cooperation decisions` and `competence → task-allocation decisions`—a clean, symmetric split.

No fine-tuning. No extra data. Just a structured prompt appended to the agent's context.

## How ETI Works in Practice

The procedure is deliberately minimal:

1. **Observe.** The agent receives a structured summary of task goals, partner actions, communication, and outcomes after each round.

2. **Infer.** It produces Likert ratings (1–7) for each of the eight traits, with brief evidence. A partner who repeatedly ships broken code? Competence: ability = 2/7, reliability = 1/7, with evidence citing the specific failures.

3. **Act.** The trait profile is appended to the agent's context. All subsequent planning and execution can now use it—deciding whom to trust with a critical subtask, whose code to verify, or whether to double-check a teammate's claims.

![Schematic diagram of the ETI framework: agents exchange messages, a trait inference module outputs warmth and competence ratings on 1–7 Likert scales, and the profiles feed back into the agents' context for subsequent planning and execution.]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Crucially, the warmth–competence separation is enforced in the trait definitions. *Ability* is defined solely in terms of successful execution, independent of intentions.

This prevents the common conflation where "uncooperative" bleeds into "incompetent," keeping the two signals distinct and informative.

Profiles can be updated sparsely—once calibrated, they transfer across tasks. The overhead is tiny: trait profiles are short and updates can be throttled or truncated.

## Walking Through the Games

The authors evaluate ETI in two stages: controlled economic games with ground-truth opponent traits, then realistic multi-agent tasks on MultiAgentBench.

In the economic games—Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and Iterated Stag Hunt—the opponent is a scripted bot parameterized by warmth (probability of choosing the cooperative action) and competence (probability of successfully executing it).

This gives clean ground truth: you know exactly what the partner's traits *are*, so you can measure whether the agent correctly infers them.

The results are sharp.

ETI reduces payoff loss by **77%** in Stag Hunt (down from 65% deviation to 15%) and by **42%** in Prisoner's Dilemma (135% to 78%).

Trait inference accuracy lifts dramatically over the chain-of-thought baseline. In noisy Prisoner's Dilemma—where opponent behavior is inconsistent and signals are weak—competence F1 rises from **0.06** to **0.71**, and cooperation F1 from **0.02** to **0.49**.

The baseline basically collapses under noise. ETI stays robust.

And it's fast: by roughly round 5, ETI agents converge to near-optimal play, while baseline agents continue to deviate substantially from the payoff-optimal strategy.

Theory-of-mind prompting, as in Guo et al.'s Suspicion-Agent, models partners' transient beliefs and intentions. ETI targets something different—the *stable* behavioral dispositions that Akata et al. (2025) documented in LLM game play: persistent cooperation biases, suboptimal social strategies.

A partner who is *consistently* uncooperative is not someone you reason into cooperating; you reassign work or add verification.

## Scaling Up: MultiAgentBench

The second evaluation moves from toy games to realistic tasks—coding, research, bargaining, and social deduction (Werewolf)—on Zhu et al.'s MultiAgentBench.

Across all four scenarios, ETI improves task performance by **3–29%** and coordination quality by **6–42%**. The strongest gains appear where adaptive task allocation or adversarial reasoning matter most: Coding–Tree (QWEN 45.75 → 52.98), Research (QWEN agents using GPT-generated profiles: 62.84 → 75.99), and Werewolf (GPT agents with QWEN-generated profiles: 25.26 → 36.46).

![Bar chart comparing ETI against a CoT baseline across MultiAgentBench scenarios—ETI bars consistently exceed baseline, with the largest gaps in coding and research where adaptive task allocation matters most.]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

In Werewolf, trait profiles directly separate villagers from werewolves. Villagers score higher on trustworthiness ($\Delta = +0.75$) and lower on maliciousness ($\Delta = -0.73$).

Logistic regressions confirm causality: higher maliciousness ratings increase the chance of an agent being voted out ($\beta = +2.74$), while higher trustworthiness reduces it ($\beta = -5.25$).

Agents aren't just collecting trait data—they're *acting* on it.

Sub-score analyses reinforce the mechanism. ETI agents produce more concrete plans (Coding–Graph planning +9.1), exchange more relevant messages (communication +15.2), and reach shared milestones more often.

## The Profile Quality Question

Here is where the story gets genuinely interesting—and where a naïve implementation would fail.

The authors ran cross-model experiments: QWEN agents using GPT-generated profiles, and GPT agents using QWEN-generated profiles.

The results are asymmetric. A GPT agent given QWEN-generated profiles achieves large gains (Coding–Graph coordination 57.01 → 74.38).

GPT-generated profiles often provide *little or no benefit*, and can even reduce performance (Bargaining task for QWEN agents: 59.11 → 55.74).

The difference? QWEN3-8B produces discriminative, high-variance trait profiles that span the full 1–7 scale. GPT-4o-mini—with its positivity bias linked to RLHF training—generates generic, uniformly positive ratings that carry almost no information.

![Correlation between trait ratings on warmth and competence dimensions and coordination outcomes, showing that higher warmth and competence ratings predict better coordination success across agent types and scenarios.]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

This matters: ETI's gains come from *informative* profiles, not from merely adding structured context or nudging agents to think about each other.

The content of the profile is the mechanism. Swap in a useless profile, and the gains vanish.

## The Honest Limits

ETI was evaluated on two small general-purpose models—QWEN3-8B and GPT-4o-mini. How it behaves with larger, heterogeneous, or specialized models is unknown.

The GPT-4o-mini results already show that model-specific calibration matters; a model that cannot discriminate among partners will produce profiles indistinguishable from noise.

The fixed eight-trait schema does not adapt to domain-specific needs. A medical triage team might need different traits than a code review squad.

Relatedly, ETI's emphasis on *stable* patterns can slow adaptation to sudden partner behavior shifts—until enough new evidence accumulates, the profile lags behind reality.

And like any partner-modeling approach, trait inference can reflect and amplify biases present in the underlying model. In deployments with demographic or social cues, practitioners must consider safeguards.

## Why It Matters

ETI is the first framework to show systematically that LLM agents can both *reliably infer* others' traits from interaction histories and *leverage* that structured awareness for coordination.

It doubles as a diagnostic tool: generating trait profiles surfaces model biases (positivity, sycophancy) before you deploy the model in a team setting.

The profiles transfer across tasks. They can be updated sparsely. The method is prompting-based, with no fine-tuning, and adds minimal overhead.

For anyone running multi-agent pipelines, ETI is a near-free coordination upgrade—but only if your model can generate sharp, honest ratings.

Your agent team's people problem turns out to have a people-problem solution: just let them rate each other.
