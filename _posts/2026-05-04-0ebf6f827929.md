*TL;DR: Tuning individual agents in a multi-agent system can stall or even hurt overall performance—but jointly optimizing the full prompt bundle across all agents lifts quality by up to 12 percentage points, with the biggest gains in safety and conversational quality.*

## The Grocery Problem That Breaks Monoliths

A customer types "my usuals, under $25, and add a wine pairing." That one message bundles underspecified intent, budget constraint, personal taste, and a revision—all at once.

Early versions of MAGIC, DoorDash's production grocery assistant, tried handling this with a single monolithic agent. As complexity grew, the design became brittle: context expanded with tool traces, responsibilities interfered, and early ambiguities propagated silently into downstream actions.

So they split the system. An Orchestrator now decomposes intent and delegates to sub-agents for search, cart management, and more.

The decomposition improved control—but created a new problem: improving any single sub-agent in isolation no longer reliably improves the end-to-end conversation.

Sometimes the improvement simply does not propagate. Sometimes the coupling means you need all agents to move together.

## From Vague Scores to Boolean Verdicts

You cannot optimize what you cannot measure. Standard retrieval metrics fall apart in multi-turn, multi-agent settings—quality is trajectory-dependent and multi-dimensional.

The authors borrow a page from HealthBench (Arora et al., 2025), which decomposes clinical quality into structured rubrics, and extend it to shopping. They define a four-domain rubric:

- **Shopping Execution** (50% weight): did the right items end up in the cart?
- **Personalization & Context** (20%): were dietary prefs, brand prefs, and prior-turn context honored?
- **Conversation Quality** (10%): clarification, information integrity, flow, tone.
- **Safety & Compliance** (20%): no hallucinated food-safety claims, no policy violations.

Each criterion is a binary pass/fail check grounded in observable trace artifacts—tool calls, cart state, confirmed selections.

Forget "rate helpfulness on a 1–5 scale." The verdict is boolean and deterministic. The same trajectory always produces the same score.

## Calibrating the Judge

Binary checks solve determinism, but someone still has to apply them. The authors deploy an LLM-as-judge and calibrate it against human labels using GEPA (Shao et al., 2026)—the same prompt optimizer they later use on the agents themselves.

Weighted judge-human agreement starts at 88.47%. After GEPA optimizes the judge's own prompt, it climbs to 93.45%.

The biggest domain-level improvements hit where definitions of correctness are most nuanced. Personalization starts at 70.8% and reaches 80.2%. Conversational Quality rises from 91.1% to 99.0%.

Safety was already at 100% agreement—binary policy checks are easy when the evidence is concrete.

Why does this matter? A calibrated judge is not just an evaluator. It becomes a *reward signal*—stable enough to drive prompt optimization for the agents it scores.

## Two Ways to Optimize a Prompt Bundle

With a reliable judge in hand, the authors compare two optimization strategies.

### Sub-agent GEPA: Tune Each Node Alone

For each sub-agent, they extract invocation-level examples from logged traces, derive a micro-rubric from failure modes, and let GEPA search for the best prompt variant:

$$p_a^* = \arg\max_{p_a} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim D^{\text{held-out}}_a} \left[ r_a(x, p_a) \right]$$

This reduces multi-turn optimization to a single-turn problem per node. It efficiently fixes atomic errors: wrong store type, bad quantities, duplicate items.

But it cannot fix coordination failures. An Orchestrator that omits a dietary preference from its delegation, or a search agent that floods the shared context window—those are *interactional* defects, invisible to any node scoring itself in isolation.

### MAMUT GEPA: Optimize the Whole Bundle Jointly

MAMUT (Multi-Agent Multi-Turn) GEPA, a companion method by Herrera et al. (2026), optimizes the entire prompt bundle $\Theta = \{p_{\text{orch}}, p_{\text{cart}}, p_{\text{search}}, \ldots\}$ against the aggregate rubric score of full simulated trajectories:

$$\Theta^* = \arg\max_{\Theta} \; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim S(\Theta)}[\text{Rubric}(\tau)]$$

The optimizer can now trade off performance across agents—for instance, making the Orchestrator more concise so the Search Agent has more context-window budget for retrieval results. These are gradients that node-level optimization simply cannot see.

To re-evaluate historical intents under new prompts, they need a user simulator.

The hybrid design is elegant: if the optimized agent's action is semantically equivalent to the logged action (verified by an NLI check), replay the real user's turn. If it diverges, a User Persona Agent generates a synthetic response consistent with the original constraints.

## What Joint Optimization Actually Buys

On 238 held-out trajectories, the numbers are clear:

| Rubric Domain | Sub-agent GEPA | MAMUT | Gain |
|---|---|---|---|
| Shopping Execution | 79.0% | 85.0% | +6.0% |
| Personalization & Context | 80.2% | 87.0% | +6.8% |
| Conversational Quality | 64.0% | 72.0% | +8.0% |
| Safety & Compliance | 76.0% | 88.0% | +12.0% |
| **Overall** | **77.1%** | **84.7%** | **+7.6%** |

![Bar chart comparing Baseline, Sub-agent GEPA, and MAMUT GEPA pass rates across all four rubric domains, with MAMUT showing the largest gains in Safety & Compliance and Conversational Quality]({{ "/assets/figures/0ebf6f827929/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

Safety and Compliance gains the most: +12.0 percentage points. When individual agents are tuned in isolation, they often violate interaction policies and hallucinate product details—because the optimizer has no signal that downstream agents depend on the Orchestrator passing along policy context.

Joint optimization gives that signal.

The Personalization gain tells a similar story. MAMUT learns to make the Orchestrator correctly forward retrieved preferences to downstream sub-agents.

Sub-agent GEPA cannot incentivize that behavior because the Orchestrator's micro-rubric never sees what happens after it delegates.

## The Honest Boundaries

This is a single-domain study. The system is MAGIC—a grocery assistant at DoorDash.

Whether the rubric structure, the calibration curve, and the MAMUT-vs-Sub-agent gap generalize to travel booking, healthcare navigation, or financial planning remains an open question.

The user simulator passes Turing tests and intent-consistency checks, but it has not been validated against live A/B tests. Synthetic users may be faithful to logged constraints without capturing the messy ways real customers change their minds mid-conversation.

The optimization itself is prompt-level only. Model weights stay frozen.

That is a pragmatic choice for production systems, but it means the ceiling is whatever the base model can express through better prompting.

## Why This Blueprint Matters Beyond Groceries

The core insight generalizes: for any tightly coupled multi-agent system, evaluation must be trajectory-level, and optimization must be joint.

Node-level tuning is the local search that gets you to a local optimum. MAMUT is the trajectory-level signal that pushes you past it.

The TextGrad lineage (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024) treated text outputs as differentiable signals. GEPA extended that to prompt evolution. MAMUT extends it again—to multi-agent prompt bundles evaluated over simulated rollouts, system-level "differentiation" for agentic architectures.

MultiAgentBench (Zhu et al., 2025) showed how to evaluate collaboration. This paper shows how to *improve* it.

The rubric templates and evaluation design guidance are released for practitioners. The promise: convert subjective conversational quality into a repeatable engineering signal, close the loop with optimization, and iterate.

Tune the parts, then tune the whole—because the parts alone will never learn to talk to each other.
