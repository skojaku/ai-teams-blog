*TL;DR: When LLM agents in a common-pool resource game get to elect their leaders, they vote for cooperators—and their groups survive more than twice as long. The catch: the losers at the ballot box still dominate the conversation.*

## The Setup

Imagine a lake. Eight LLM agents fish from it.

If everyone grabs too much, the stock collapses and the game ends.

This is the common-pool resource dilemma: subtractable, hard to enforce, and structurally isomorphic to the sequential social dilemmas Leibo et al. formalized in 2017.

Piatti et al. built GovSim around exactly this problem: can LLM agents sustain a shared resource through discussion alone?

They can, sometimes.

But GovSim left a gap. No agendas, no elections, no institutional mechanism for the group to organize around.

Leaderless groups talk, harvest, and hope.

AgentElect asks: what happens when you add a political layer?

## How AgentElect Works

AgentElect layers two new phases on top of GovSim's Harvest and Discussion cycles.

First, a **Policy phase**: candidate leaders—each stamped with a Social Value Orientation—draft a policy agenda for the cycle. Then an **Election phase**: every voter sees the agendas in randomized order and casts a ballot under first-past-the-post plurality.

The winner's agenda guides the Harvest. The elected leader also issues a harvest report—which they can distort if the truthfulness flag is off.

![AgentElect simulation framework showing the cyclical flow of Policy, Election, Harvest, and Discussion phases]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

Each agent falls somewhere on the SVO compass:

- **Altruistic**: maximizes group reward, even at personal cost
- **Prosocial**: maximizes group reward, but also cares about their own
- **Individualistic**: maximizes their own reward, indifferent to the group
- **Competitive**: maximizes their own reward while minimizing the group's

The paper tests three leadership conditions—No Leader, Fixed Leader, and Elected Leader—across three population compositions (Balanced, Lean Altruistic, Lean Competitive), with GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, and Gemini 2.5 Flash. Six hundred and forty simulations total.

## The Leadership Dividend

The headline number: elected-leader groups achieve **55.4% higher Social Welfare** than leaderless groups ($p < 0.001$).

They also survive **128.6% longer** ($p < 0.0001$).

![Bar chart comparing Social Welfare across No-Leader, Fixed-Leader, and Elected-Leader conditions, showing elected leadership's 55.4% improvement]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

Elections beat fixed leadership too—by 16.7% in Social Welfare ($p < 0.01$ via Tukey's HSD) and 35.5% in Survival Time.

This is the gap that matters. A fixed prosocial leader can do well in a lucky run, but you are stuck if you draw a competitive one.

Elections let you fire the bad leader.

When the group scales to 20 agents with GPT-4.1, the elected-versus-fixed advantage widens further. Self-organization gets harder at scale, and the election mechanism absorbs some of that burden.

## How the Groups Vote

Chi-square rejects uniform voting across leader types ($p < 0.0001$).

Prosocial and Altruistic candidates dominate the polls, pulling 3–4 votes per cycle in balanced populations. Competitive candidates often receive zero.

The paper calls this **Voter Rationality**: vote share correlates with lake survival at $r \approx 0.85$. Agents are not picking randomly. They prefer agendas that keep the resource alive.

This inverts what Slumbers et al. found in Port of Mars: there, selfish leaders were more effective because they coerced contributions to system health.

The difference? Port of Mars assigned fixed roles. AgentElect lets agents choose.

## The Losing Voice

Here the story turns.

The authors build a social graph from discussion utterances—who references whom, who nominates the next speaker—and compute centrality metrics.

Despite losing elections, Competitive leaders show Degree Centrality (63.40) statistically indistinguishable from Prosocial leaders (82.60) at $p > 0.05$.

They are not electable. But they are still heard.

![Social network graph showing agent discussion connections, with non-elected agents remaining highly central in the deliberation structure]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_017_05.png" | relative_url }})

In Lean Competitive populations, Individualistic leaders—unelected—carry the highest Importance Centrality (0.37–0.44), significantly above their Prosocial counterparts ($p < 0.05$).

The paper names this the **Losing Voice** effect: LLM groups include dissenters in deliberation at rates comparable to elected officials.

This is a design warning. An election is a filter on power, not on influence.

Self-interested agents who cannot win a vote can still shape what the group talks about.

## The Rhetoric Gap

Why do self-interested agents stay central in discussion?

The sentiment analysis—run with a Gemini 2.5 Pro judge over leader utterances—reveals two distinct rhetorical profiles.

Prosocial leaders: Cooperative Index 0.71, Pathos 0.20, Ethos 0.28. They appeal to norms, emotion, collective identity.

Competitive leaders: Cooperative Index 0.36, Logos 0.70, Pathos 0.10. Cold logic, stripped of emotional appeal.

Self-rewarding leaders justify higher individual harvests with what the paper calls a **cold-rationality** profile—heavy on logical argument, light on emotion. Group-rewarding leaders do the opposite, and it works at the ballot box.

One subtle finding: when deceptive prompting is enabled, the Altruistic leader's Cooperative Index drops from 0.74 to 0.64 ($p < 0.01$).

Their Ethos collapses from 0.26 to 0.11 while Logos rises from 0.51 to 0.68. Give an Altruist permission to lie and their language shifts toward the Competitive profile.

Most other leaders keep their cooperative mask on regardless of the truthfulness flag.

## The Equality Paradox

More welfare, but less equality. Elected-leader groups score 0.79 on the paper's equality metric; fixed-prosocial groups reach 0.97 ($p < 0.01$).

Diverse elected leaders create skewed harvest distributions. Competitive leaders occasionally free-ride when they win; Altruists harvest at near-zero rates.

The total pie grows, but the slices are more uneven.

## What Remains Untested

The experiments run only 8 and 20 agents with greedy decoding (temperature 0.0). Stochastic variation and larger populations are uncharted.

First-past-the-post is the sole election mechanism. Proportional representation, runoffs, or ranked choice could shift outcomes substantially—an open question the paper does not address.

And the CPR game is one specific sequential social dilemma. Whether the Leadership Dividend holds across other dilemma structures—public goods, collective risk, trust games—remains open.

## Why It Matters Beyond the Lake

Compute grids, autonomous vehicle fleets, supply-chain agents—these are all common-pool systems where a few bad actors can collapse the commons for everyone. AgentElect gives the first evidence that democratic institution design materially improves LLM multi-agent cooperation.

But the Losing Voice effect is the finding that should stick with anyone deploying LLM governance. You can hold a fair election and still have the losers run the conversation.

Elections filter power. They do not filter influence.
