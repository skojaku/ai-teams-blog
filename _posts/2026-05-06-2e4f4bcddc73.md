*TL;DR: A taxonomy paper that reframes LLM multi-agent RL around the orchestration trace—an event graph of orchestrator decisions, spawns, messages, tool calls, and aggregations—revealing that credit assignment at the message level is nearly empty, nobody trains the stopping decision, and dense rewards with strong credit decomposition are just two sides of the same ledger.*

## The Scene

Kimi K2.6 spawns 300 sub-agents that make 4,000 coordinated steps in a single task.

OpenAI Codex manages parallel software-engineering agents from a command center.

Anthropic ran sixteen parallel Claudes to jointly build a C compiler.

These are not demos—they are shipping production systems.

The real question isn't whether multi-agent teams work. It's how to train them when you can't tell which decision deserved the credit.

Chenchen Zhang's survey argues that the missing unit of analysis is the *orchestration trace*: a temporal event graph whose vertices are orchestrator decisions, spawns, messages, tool calls, returns, and aggregations.

Once you see the problem through the trace, three axes snap into focus—reward design, credit assignment, and orchestration learning—and you start noticing what's missing.

## Why the Old Framework Breaks

Classical MARL gives us Dec-POMDPs, centralized training with decentralized execution, and credit-assignment tools like COMA's counterfactual baselines and QMIX's value decomposition.

Those tools assume a fixed number of agents, small discrete action spaces, and short episodes.

LLM multi-agent systems violate every one of those assumptions.

Action space is natural language. Agent counts are dynamic—you learn to spawn and despawn. Episodes run thousands of steps with asynchronous parallel execution. And the credit-bearing units aren't just agents anymore; they include messages, tool calls, roles, and orchestrator decisions.

QMIX's monotonic value decomposition was built for fixed agent counts—it doesn't extend to variable populations. COMA's counterfactual baseline was designed for fixed-agent settings, not for an orchestrator deciding whether to spawn a planner or a coder.

The classical toolkit gives the vocabulary. It does not give the algorithms.

## The Orchestration Trace

Zhang extends the Dec-POMDP into what he calls the *dynamic-Dec-POMDP* ($M^+$):

$$M^+ = (I_t, S, A(\cdot), A_{\text{spawn}}, P, \Omega, r, \gamma)$$

The key addition is $I_t$: a time-indexed agent set that can grow or shrink.

The orchestrator's action space $A_{\text{spawn}}$ contains `spawn(role, context)` and `despawn(i)` operations.

The rollout produces an orchestration trace—a rooted, edge-labelled, vertex-labelled temporal graph:

$$G = (V, E, \ell_V, \ell_E)$$

where $V = V_{\text{orch}} \cup V_{\text{spawn}} \cup V_{\text{msg}} \cup V_{\text{tool}} \cup V_{\text{ret}} \cup V_{\text{agg}}$.

Events, not agents, are the carriers of credit.

![Orchestration trace as a temporal event graph with color-coded nodes for spawns, messages, tool calls, returns, and aggregations, connected by directed edges showing temporal progression and branching parallel paths]({{ "/assets/figures/2e4f4bcddc73/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

Value functions also need to change shape. Under $M^+$, the value is conditioned on the trace prefix:

$$V^\pi(G_{\leq t}) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{\tau \geq t} \gamma^{\tau - t} r_\tau \Big| G_{\leq t}\right]$$

$G_{\leq t+1}$ may contain more vertices than $G_{\leq t}$ when a spawn fires. The expectation runs over graph-growing transitions—not a fixed joint state space.

Zhang is explicit: this is a bookkeeping abstraction for taxonomy, not a new MARL theory with convergence proofs.

## Why Credit Leaks and Counterfactuals Vanish

Two informal observations organize the rest of the survey.

**Credit diffusion.** Share a terminal team reward uniformly across $n$ credit-bearing units on a long trace, with no structure-specific baseline.

The effective per-unit signal becomes less distinguishable as $n$ grows. This is the same pathology that motivated COMA's counterfactual baselines in classical MARL, and Dr. MAS documents the resulting training instability when GRPO is applied naively to multi-agent rollouts.

**Counterfactual ambiguity.** When an orchestrator decides `spawn` at time $t$, the counterfactual—what if it had said `no-spawn`?—produces a structurally different trace with no generated sub-graph.

On-policy rollouts give you zero realizations of that branch. Estimating the same-prefix contribution requires an explicit off-policy mechanism or strong structural assumptions.

These two observations lead to a critical insight: *dense rewards and strong credit decomposition are duals*.

A dense orchestration reward that lights up directly at the orchestrator-decision event means credit decomposition has less work to do. A sparse terminal reward shifts all the burden onto counterfactual baselines, role-level critics, and Shapley-style attribution.

Two apparently opposite design choices can both work because they distribute the same total work across different sides of the reward–credit ledger.

## Eight Reward Families, Eight Credit Units

The survey organizes reward design into eight families: shared, hybrid, orchestration, individual, debate, process, role, and verifier.

Orchestration rewards are the most distinctive—they target system-level properties like parallelism speedup, split correctness, and aggregation quality.

Credit-bearing units span eight levels from token to team. Zhang tallies the curated pool of 84 entries: agent-level credit dominates with 23 entries, role-level has 10, orchestrator-level has 8, turn-level has 5, and message-level has just 2.

Of those two, only one—C3—explicitly estimates counterfactual message-level credit.

Message-level credit is nearly empty. Yet message-level events are where collaboration actually happens—where one agent's critique changes another agent's answer.

This is the sharpest gap in the landscape.

## Orchestration as Five Decisions

Orchestration learning decomposes into five sub-decisions: when to spawn, whom to delegate to, how to communicate, how to aggregate, and when to stop.

Zhang's curated pool, covering all 84 entries through May 4, 2026, found *no explicit RL training method for the stopping decision*.

Agents learn to start; they don't learn when to quit.

The other four decisions have nascent coverage. Kimi PARL trains an orchestrator to spawn sub-agents. M-GRPO decomposes GRPO hierarchically by role, directly instantiating role-level credit. Puppeteer and HALO train orchestrator-level policies.

But the methods are concentrated in an 18-month window from Q4 2024 to Q2 2026, and no single entry addresses all five dimensions.

## Where the Benchmarks Fail

Current benchmarks fail to measure the properties that multi-agent RL is supposed to optimize—parallelism efficiency, collaboration quality, error amplification.

SWE-Bench and WebArena test individual agent competence. MultiAgentBench adds multi-agent scenarios but doesn't audit parallelism efficiency, collaboration quality, or error amplification.

Zhang proposes four dimensions for a MAS-native evaluation. No existing benchmark covers all four.

There is also a scale gap. Industrial systems like Kimi K2.5 (100 sub-agents, 1,500 steps) and K2.6 (300 sub-agents, 4,000 steps) operate at deployment envelopes that academic evaluation regimes cannot reach.

Kimi is the clearest public trained-orchestrator anchor; Codex and Claude Code document deployment shape but disclose no RL training recipes.

## What's Honest About the Limits

This is a taxonomy paper, not an experimental study. It proposes no new algorithm and proves no convergence result.

The formalism is explicitly a working abstraction, not a new MARL theory.

The curated pool of 84 entries is not exhaustive. Tags were assigned by a single author without formal inter-annotator agreement.

The exclusion log is transparently presented as a screening-decision log, not a PRISMA record.

And the coverage map itself reveals the problem: reward, credit, orchestration, evaluation, and safety are coupled but unevenly supported dimensions.

No single entry addresses all five directly.

## What This Gives You

Despite those limits, the paper delivers a reusable substrate.

An 84-entry tagged pool with an 18-column schema. A machine-readable JSON trace schema. Scripted corpus statistics so you can reproduce the sparsity claims yourself. Fifteen open problems spanning algorithms, rewards, systems, safety, and evaluation.

The message-level credit gap alone is worth the read.

If you're training multi-agent LLM teams and you're not instrumenting your traces at message granularity, you're flying blind on your most important credit decisions.

The trace is the thing. Not the trajectory, not the agent—the event graph that records who said what to whom and why it mattered.
