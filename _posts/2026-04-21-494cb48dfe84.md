*TL;DR: MHGPO ditches the expensive critic network that multi-agent LLM training usually requires and replaces it with heterogeneous group comparisons — and the simpler method more than doubles task performance while cutting GPU memory by a third.*

## The Cost of Cooperation

You want three LLM agents — a Rewriter, a Reranker, and an Answerer — to cooperate on a multi-hop search question. The standard playbook says: train them with MAPPO, the multi-agent extension of PPO. That means running a big critic network alongside your policy model, one that has to estimate joint action values across all three agents at once.

The critic gobbles memory, wobbles during training, and scales poorly as you add more specialized agents. The whole setup feels like overpaying for a bad supervisor.

This paper asks: what if you just fire the critic?

## No Critic, No Problem

Multi-Agent Heterogeneous Group Policy Optimization (MHGPO) does exactly that. Instead of a value network, it estimates advantages from relative rewards across groups of rollouts — some of which come from different agent roles entirely.

The core insight is a shift in perspective. Rather than asking "how good was this action locally?", MHGPO asks "how did this rollout compare to other rollouts in its group, even when those rollouts belong to different agents?"

This builds on GRPO, the group-relative method DeepSeekMath introduced for single-agent reasoning. GRPO compares responses that share the same prompt.

But in a multi-agent pipeline, agents don't share the same prompt — the Reranker sees the Rewriter's output, the Answerer sees the Reranker's. MHGPO generalizes GRPO by allowing the comparison group to contain rollouts from *different agent roles and different trajectories*, so long as they trace back to the same root question.

The authors prove that under an idealized context-sufficiency assumption, MHGPO's advantage estimate reduces exactly to GRPO's — making it a proper generalization, not a hack.

## How the Pipeline Works

MHGPO runs on a single shared LLM backbone (Llama3.1-8B-Instruct in all experiments). All three agents are just different prompts fed into the same model, so parameter sharing collapses the multi-agent problem into multi-task learning.

![Architecture diagram showing the three-agent MASS pipeline (Rewriter, Reranker, Answerer) and the MHGPO training loop with backward reward propagation from terminal F1 scores]({{ "/assets/figures/494cb48dfe84/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The pipeline has three moving parts.

### Backward Reward Propagation

The Answerer's final F1 score becomes the terminal reward. That reward propagates *backward* through the pipeline — averaged from Answerer to Reranker to Rewriter.

No direct gradient path connects the Rewriter to the Answerer. But the propagated reward exposes the dependency: if the Rewriter wrote bad sub-queries, the downstream F1 suffers, and that suffering flows upstream.

### Heterogeneous Group Advantage

For each agent's rollout, MHGPO computes advantage by normalizing the rollout's reward against every other rollout in its group — including rollouts from other agents. Concretely:

$$\hat{A}_{k,i} = \frac{R_{k,i} - \text{mean}(\{R_{l,j} \mid m_{l,j} = m_{k,i}\})}{\text{std}(\{R_{l,j} \mid m_{l,j} = m_{k,i}\})}$$

This is the formula that kills the critic. The group identifier $m$ determines which rollouts get compared.

When a group contains rollouts from different agents conditioned on different upstream outputs, the advantage signal captures *global* trajectory quality — not just local optimality under a fixed prefix.

### Rollout Sampling Strategies

How you form those groups matters. The paper explores three strategies:

- **Independent Sampling (IS):** Each agent gets its own homogeneous group. Every rollout in a group shares the same input to that agent. This is the GRPO baseline — it's stable but ignores inter-agent coupling.

- **Fork-on-first (FoF):** Branch only at the first agent (Rewriter). The Rewriter sees a homogeneous group; every downstream agent sees a heterogeneous group of G different upstream inputs. Captures interactions without redundant computation.

- **Round-robin (RR):** Stochastically choose which agent to fork at. Every sample forks at agent $i$ with probability $p_i$. Agents before the fork point form homogeneous groups; agents after see heterogeneous groups.

RR balances global coordination signals with local stability.

There's also FoF(os) — Fork-on-first with Over-sampling at the terminal agent. It generates G rollouts at the Answerer instead of one, yielding a denser reward estimate. FoF(os) is the top performer on task metrics, at the cost of extra compute.

## What They Found

The numbers are sharp. On HotpotQA with Llama3.1-8B:

- **MHGPO-FoF(os)** hits 50.86 F1, more than doubling the unoptimized MAS baseline of 21.45 F1.

- **MHGPO-RR** beats MAPPO on HotpotQA (49.72 vs 46.40 F1) while using 51.5% GPU memory to MAPPO's 82.2% — with no critic at all and substantially lower wall-clock overhead.

![Bar chart comparing F1 scores on HotpotQA across MHGPO variants, MAPPO, unoptimized baselines, and single-context RL-RAG methods]({{ "/assets/figures/494cb48dfe84/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

All MHGPO variants beat strong single-context RL-RAG baselines. Search-o1 scores 25.70 F1 and R1-Searcher-GRPO scores 45.39 F1 on HotpotQA, both well below MHGPO's range.

![Efficiency comparison showing GPU memory usage and per-step training time for MHGPO variants vs MAPPO, demonstrating the memory and compute savings from eliminating the critic network]({{ "/assets/figures/494cb48dfe84/fig_005_04.png" | relative_url }})

Out-of-domain, the story holds. On 2WikiMultihopQA, MHGPO-FoF(os) reaches 40.36 F1; on MuSiQue, 22.68 F1. The MHGPO family delivers the most consistent gains across datasets.

### The ablation is just as telling

Training any single agent alone crashes performance — Reranker-only drops the average from 42.0 to 24.7. The gains come from joint optimization, not individual tuning.

Strip the agent-specific format penalties from rewards and keep only terminal F1, and MHGPO-RR still holds up. MAPPO collapses: F1 falls to 35.0, the Rewriter hallucinates more than 10 sub-queries, and invalid selections spike to 5.3%. Group-relative advantage estimation handles sparse delayed rewards better than a critic.

## Honest Limits

All experiments run on a fixed three-agent linear pipeline (Rewriter → Reranker → Answerer) for multi-hop QA. No dynamic topologies, no branching agent graphs, no tool-use-heavy settings.

The backbone is always Llama3.1-8B — scaling behavior with larger models is untested.

And the formal analysis connects MHGPO to GRPO only under an idealized context-sufficiency assumption that real MAS pipelines — with their summarization and filtering — deliberately violate.

These are real boundaries. The method works for a straight-line three-agent team on QA. Whether it generalizes to a ten-agent web with branching and backtracking is an open question.

## Why This Matters

MAPPO's critic problem isn't niche — it's the tax every actor-critic MARL method pays. MHGPO shows you can pay that tax exactly zero times by comparing rollouts across heterogeneous groups instead of estimating joint action values.

The memory savings alone — from 82.2% down to roughly 50% GPU utilization across MHGPO variants — open multi-agent RL training to labs that couldn't afford a critic in the first place.

The theoretical bridge matters too. MHGPO doesn't just empirically work; it reduces to GRPO under clean assumptions, giving a formal lineage from single-agent group-based optimization to the multi-agent, multi-context world. That's a foundation others can build on.

Critic-free, critic-better.
