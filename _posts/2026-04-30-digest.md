## Themes

**The sparsity of what matters.** Most of what we pour into multi-agent LLM systems is noise. Verified Critical Step Optimization trains on 16% of trajectory steps and matches GPT-4.1. The stance detection comparison finds that a single verified example in one API call nearly matches the best multi-shot method. Emergent Coordination in Multi-Agent Language Models shows a one-line "think about what others might do" prompt does more for collective behavior than elaborate persona engineering. The signal was always sparse—we just kept drowning it in orchestration.

**Convergence as failure mode.** When there is no ground truth, agreement is a bug. Preserving Disagreement shows that same-model agents assigned opposing personas still converge on the same answer—a 70.9% first-choice concentration that drops to 46.1% when you swap in architectural heterogeneity. Emergent Coordination finds the inverse: identical win rates across conditions mask radically different internal dynamics. One produces a stable collective; the other, a gas. In normative domains, convergence is not evidence of correctness—it is evidence of collapsed diversity.

**Architecture over prompting.** Persona engineering cannot overcome shared inductive bias. The disagreement paper proves this directly: divergent system prompts on the same model produce artificial consensus, while different models per perspective shatter it. The MSC paper formalizes the same insight at the protocol layer—owned control flow is an architectural constraint, not a prompt suggestion. The stance detection paper confirms it from the other direction: no amount of role-playing debate architecture recovers the ground that a clean architectural choice (model + method) gives you for free.

**Verification by outcome, not proxy.** CSO drops six points the moment you replace outcome verification with PRM scores. The MSC paper eliminates deadlocks by construction rather than by testing every interleaving. Emergent Coordination builds falsifiable surrogates—row-shuffles and column-shuffles—that break specific signals, making every claim about synergy directly testable. Three different papers, one principle: trust the result, not the proxy.

## Editorial

Today's batch has a single pulse: stop adding machinery and start subtracting noise.

Five papers, all on multi-agent LLM systems, and the through-line is a quiet revolt against complexity. CSO throws away 84% of training steps and wins. The stance detection study shows twelve-agent debates losing to one clean prompt. The disagreement paper reveals that seven persona-diverse agents on the same model are just seven copies of the same bias in costume. Even the MSC paper's contribution is a structural guarantee that removes the need to check for deadlocks at all.

The genuinely new thing in today's stack is the falsifiable synergy test in the emergent coordination paper. Everything else in that paper—the PID decomposition, the stability metrics—has been floating around information theory for years. But nobody had pointed it at multi-agent LLM output and built surrogate nulls that let you reject fake emergence. That is the kind of tool this field needs more of: a ruler, not a framework.

What warrants a second look is the fidelity–diversity tradeoff in the disagreement paper. Quality-weighting agent votes can amplify the majority instead of protecting minority perspectives. That is not an edge case—it is a structural warning for anyone building deliberation systems. If your "quality" signal correlates with your dominant perspective, weighting by quality just entrenches the consensus you were trying to escape.

The incremental work today is the stance detection comparison. Useful as a benchmark, clear in its findings, but the result—simpler is better—is what most practitioners already suspected. The value is the systematic evidence, not the surprise.

The sparsity was already there. We just kept adding agents to hide it.
