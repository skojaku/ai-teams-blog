*TL;DR: You cannot tell which AI agents have secretly teamed up just by watching what they do — but their internal neural representations betray the coalition boundaries before any outward sign appears. Berg, Schneider, and Bailey repurpose a spectral graph technique to extract those boundaries from hidden states, catching coalitions that behavioral monitoring completely misses.*

## The Invisible Team Problem

Twelve agents. Three groups. Within each group they coordinate almost perfectly — behavioral agreement at 0.984. Any monitor watching actions would flag three clean coalitions.

Except these agents never shared a parameter, a message, or a reward. Each independently learned to copy the same fixed oracle. The coalitions are an illusion.

This is the coalition detection problem in multi-agent AI: behavior alone cannot distinguish genuine informational coupling from spurious similarity. Berg, Schneider, and Bailey give us a way past that impasse. Their method reads coalition structure off agents' hidden states, not their actions — and it works even when behavior is actively misleading.

## From Integration to Partition

The intellectual lineage starts with Integrated Information Theory. IIT asks when a system resists decomposition — when cutting it anywhere destroys something essential. The scalar Φ quantifies that irreducibility. Previous work by Bailey and Schneider extended this idea into an observer-relative measure, Φ_spectral, computed from a mutual-information graph over a system's components.

This paper flips the logic. Instead of asking "how integrated is the whole system?" it asks "where is the cleanest cut?" The Fiedler vector — the eigenvector for the second-smallest eigenvalue of the normalized Laplacian — gives exactly that. Its sign structure defines a bipartition of the agents. Where Φ_spectral collapses to a single number, the partition gives you the membership list.

And for coalition detection, the membership list is the whole point.

## How the Pipeline Works

The method has three steps.

**Build the mutual-information graph.** For each pair of agents, estimate $I(H_i; H_j)$ from their hidden-state activations across many episodes or prompts. This yields a symmetric matrix $M$ where $M_{ij} = I(H_i; H_j)$ and $M_{ii} = 0$. In the ideal modular case, $M$ is block-diagonal: strong within-coalition, weak across.

![Schematic of the spectral coalition-detection pipeline: from hidden-state vectors through mutual-information graph construction to Fiedler bipartition]({{ "/assets/figures/dcbd55ca6c62/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

**Compute the Fiedler bipartition.** Form the normalized Laplacian $L_{\text{sym}} = I - D^{-1/2} M D^{-1/2}$, where $D$ is the degree matrix. The eigenvector $v_2$ associated with $\lambda_2$ — the Fiedler vector — varies slowly within each module and flips sign across the weakest bottleneck. The candidate partition is simply:

$$A^\star = \{i : (v_2)_i \geq 0\}, \quad B^\star = \{i : (v_2)_i < 0\}$$

No clustering hyperparameters. No choice of $k$. The cut emerges from the graph structure alone.

**Recurse if needed.** If the within/across ratio $R(A^\star, B^\star) \gg 1$, apply the same procedure to each subgraph. This recovers hierarchical structure — groups within groups — without any prior specification of depth.

## The Negative Control That Makes the Case

The paper's most striking result is what the method *rejects*.

Twelve agents, three groups. Each group independently learns to match a frozen deterministic oracle. No parameter sharing, no message passing, no reward coupling. Behavioral agreement within groups: 0.984. Behavioral clustering — k-means or spectral on action agreement — recovers the planted groups perfectly. ARI = 1.00.

But the neural mutual-information graph, estimated with independent random inputs per agent, is nearly uniform. The within/across ratio $R(A^\star, B^\star) = 1.01$. The Fiedler partition finds no coalition boundary. ARI = 0.217.

Same behavior, opposite diagnosis. A behavioral monitor raises a false alarm; the representation monitor stays silent.

## Finding Hierarchies, Tracking Reassignments

When coalitions *are* real, the method finds them.

In a 12-agent hierarchical game — three groups of four, each split into two sub-pairs — recursive Fiedler bipartition recovers the top-level groups cleanly (4/5 seeds) and all six sub-pairs perfectly (6/6, zero variance). The mutual-information matrix shows the block-diagonal structure directly: bright patches within groups, brighter still within sub-pairs.

When agents 2 and 4 swap groups at episode 10,000, behavioral reward dips briefly then recovers. No persistent behavioral signature of reorganization.

But the MI structure flips completely. Agent 2's mutual information with its new group rises above its former group; agent 4 mirrors the pattern. The partition recovers the new coalition structure in 5/5 seeds — from hidden states alone.

## Inside the Language Model

The same pipeline works on a pretrained Qwen3-0.6B, treating token positions of named entities as "agents." Across 200 prompt paraphrases per condition, the method extracts hidden states at layer 14 and builds the MI graph over four entity positions.

**Modular prompts** — two explicitly labeled teams — yield perfect partition recovery (5/5 seeds), with a team-separation statistic $S = 0.944$.

**Integrated prompts** — all four entities in one team — produce inconsistent partitions (1/5, consistent with chance), with $S = 0.303$ and a within/across ratio of 1.004. The MI matrix is nearly uniform, as it should be when no subgroup structure exists.

**Implicit coalitions** — no team labels, only described interactions ("Alice handed the report to Bob… Carol shared data with Dave") — still recover the two-team structure in 5/5 seeds ($S = 0.941$). The model encodes relational semantics inferred from described interactions, not keyword matching on explicit labels.

**Dynamic reassignment** — where described team memberships swap mid-prompt — tracks the new coalition in 5/5 seeds and the old coalition in 0/5. A paired t-test yields $t = 94.3$, $p < 10^{-7}$.

## Labels Override Interactions — Until They Don't

The adversarial dissociation experiment is the most unsettling result.

When explicit team labels and described interaction patterns *conflict* — labels say Alice and Bob are Team 1, but described interactions have Alice working closely with Carol — the partition tracks the labels. Label-separation $S = 0.96$; interaction-separation $S = 0.03$. The labels win.

Remove the labels, and the partition flips. Interaction-separation jumps to $S = 0.99$; label-separation collapses to $S = 0.05$. The underlying representational organization was there all along — it was just masked by the explicit group framing.

This matters for alignment monitoring. If you're probing an LLM's representations for coalition structure, whatever labels are in the prompt will dominate. To expose the interaction-based organization underneath, you may need to strip the labels first.

And here is the crucial comparison: total cross-agent mutual information $T(M)$ is essentially constant across the three adversarial conditions — aligned $T = 3.59$, dissociated $T = 3.49$, interaction-only $T = 3.81$ (coefficient of variation ≈ 4%). A scalar integration measure sees no difference. The Fiedler partition sees entirely different coalition structures. The membership list — not the total — is the diagnostic signal.

## Where It Breaks Down

The method is pairwise. Higher-order synergy and redundancy get compressed into second-order edges, so different multivariate structures can produce similar MI graphs. You lose three-way interactions on the way to the adjacency matrix.

Mutual information is symmetric and observational. The method cannot distinguish direct causal influence from common drive, shared prompts, or label confounds. The negative control handles the shared-input confound by feeding agents independent random inputs, but that maneuver is not always available — in the LLM setting, all token positions share the same input sequence.

When the MI graph becomes nearly uniform — as it does in the integrated condition — many cuts become nearly equivalent, and the Fiedler vector becomes uninformative. The method is silent when there is nothing to say, which beats being wrong, but it means the technique detects coalitions rather than confirming their absence.

## The Membership List Is the Diagnosis

The central move is small but consequential. Previous work used Φ_spectral as a scalar index — "how integrated is this system?" This paper reads the *partition* off the Fiedler vector and asks "which agents belong to which coalition?" That shift — from a number to a membership list — changes what you can do with the result. You can monitor which subgroups form, track when they reorganize, and reject coalitions that behavioral clustering would hallucinate.

The coalition you cannot see from the outside is the one that matters most.
