*TL;DR: A 4B model enhanced with multi-agent test-time compute and a custom RL scheme reaches 73.5% on IMO-AnswerBench — closing most of the gap to a 685B frontier model. The key insight: organize what each parallel trajectory learns so that knowledge actually flows forward instead of drowning in noise.*

## A 4B Model Walks Into a Math Olympiad

A Qwen3-4B model — tiny by frontier standards — scores 73.5% on the full 400-problem IMO-AnswerBench.

That score closes in on models roughly 170× its size. The paper that makes it happen is TMAS, and its claim is sharp: the bottleneck in test-time scaling isn't compute alone. It's that parallel reasoning trajectories run in isolation, then dump their findings into an undifferentiated heap. When you structure what gets kept and what gets shared, each additional inference iteration actually pays off.

## Why More Iterations Plateau

Test-time scaling has an obvious appeal — just think longer. But "think longer" has a shape. You can run more parallel attempts (breadth), more refinement rounds (depth), or both.

PaCoRe aggregates historical trajectories to guide the next round, but it doesn't selectively decide what survives. RSE distills trajectories into positive and negative experience signals, but doesn't separate what's worth reusing from what's only worth avoiding.

The result is predictable. Early iterations help. Later ones plateau or degrade. More compute hits diminishing returns because the model is either recycling the same mistakes or drowning in context that should have been pruned.

MarsRL showed that multi-agent reasoning benefits from targeted RL training — a solver–verifier–corrector pipeline with role-specific rewards. TMAS takes this further: it restructures the inference architecture itself, then trains the model to exploit that structure.

## Five Agents, Two Memory Banks

TMAS assigns every step in the iterative reasoning loop to a specialized agent.

For each problem, the **solution agent** generates $N=8$ parallel candidate solutions. The **verification agent** checks each candidate $M=8$ times. The **summary agent** consolidates verification feedback into a rollout-level summary — what worked, what failed, what's still open.

Then the architectural novelty kicks in. Two memory agents split what was previously a single undifferentiated history:

- The **experience bank** stores low-level, trajectory-specific signals: verified intermediate conclusions, concrete problem-solving skills, verifier-identified errors and pitfalls.
- The **guideline bank** stores high-level strategic memory: which solution approaches were tried, what structural insights emerged, which reasoning patterns have already been explored.

The experience bank *feeds forward* — reuse what worked. The guideline bank *steers away* — avoid what's already been tried.

### How Each Rollout Decides

At iteration $t$, the solution agent generates each candidate via an $\epsilon$-greedy split:

$$c_{t,i} \sim \begin{cases} A_{\text{sol}}(Q, R_{t-1}, E_{t-1}) & \text{with probability } 1 - \epsilon \\ A_{\text{sol}}(Q, G_{t-1}) & \text{with probability } \epsilon \end{cases}$$

With $\epsilon = 0.2$, roughly four out of five candidates exploit past rollouts plus the experience bank. One in five explores new directions, guided by the guideline bank — not randomly, but deliberately *away* from what's already been tried.

This is the structural shift. Where PaCoRe and RSE pass forward information but don't curate it, TMAS separates "here's what to reuse" from "here's what to avoid," then samples from both.

## Training the Model to Use Its Own Memory

Untrained models don't naturally exploit a hierarchical memory system. So TMAS introduces a hybrid reward scheme built on GRPO, with three training objectives mixed together:

**Standard correctness reward.** $\tilde{r}_i = +1$ if the answer is right, $-1$ otherwise. Preserves base reasoning capability.

**Experience utilization reward.** This one is clever. Rollouts are split into a Base group (conditioned on problem + past trajectories only) and a Bank group (conditioned on problem + past trajectories + experience bank). Define base accuracy $p_{\text{base}}$ as the Base group's success rate.

If a Bank-group rollout gets the right answer, it earns a bonus proportional to $(1 - p_{\text{base}})$: the harder the problem is *without* the experience bank, the more the model is rewarded for using it.

$$\tilde{r}_i = \begin{cases} r_i + \beta(1 - p_{\text{base}}) & i \in B_{\text{bank}},\ r_i = 1 \\ r_i & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

**Novel strategy exploration reward.** Condition on historical guidelines. A correct rollout with a *novel* guideline earns $+1.0$. A correct rollout following an existing guideline earns $+0.2$. A wrong rollout with a novel guideline earns $-0.5$. A wrong rollout following an old guideline earns $-1.0$.

The message: be right, but be right in a *new way*.

MarsRL demonstrated that multi-agent systems need role-specific training. TMAS extends that principle: don't just train for correctness — train the model to *read its own memory banks* and *push past explored strategies*.

## The Numbers

On IMO-AnswerBench-50 at iteration 19, TMAS with a 30B backbone reaches 40.50% Pass@1, ahead of RSE (38.00%) and PaCoRe (30.31%). On HLE-Math-100, TMAS hits 35.38%, versus PaCoRe at 32.78%.

The headline numbers are on the full 400-problem IMO-AnswerBench:

- Qwen3-30B-A3B: 64.9% → 77.2% (+12.3 points)
- Qwen3-4B with Hybrid-RL: 53.5% → 73.5% (+20.0 points)

The 30B backbone at 77.2% surpasses Opus-4.6 Max and approaches DeepSeek-V3.2 (685B). The 4B backbone at 73.5% narrows the gap to those frontier models to single digits — roughly 170× fewer parameters, within striking distance.

![Pass@1 scores on the full IMO-AnswerBench showing TMAS-enhanced backbones approaching and surpassing frontier models]({{ "/assets/figures/49f72749df87/fig_022_07.png" | relative_url }})

### Why Not Just Vanilla RL?

TMAS + Vanilla-RL (standard correctness reward only) starts strong but degrades in later iterations. TMAS + Hybrid-RL keeps improving. The experience-utilization and exploration rewards stabilize scaling — the model actually *uses* the memory banks it's been given instead of treating them as passive context.

![Hybrid-RL sustains improvement across iterations while Vanilla-RL plateaus and degrades]({{ "/assets/figures/49f72749df87/fig_021_06.png" | relative_url }})

On IMO-AnswerBench-50, Hybrid-RL narrows the 30B-vs-4B gap by 59% (from 23.44 to 9.62 points). The 4B model doesn't just catch up a little — it catches up *most of the way*.

### Which Memory Matters When?

The ablation tells a clean story. Remove the guideline bank and performance suffers in early iterations — guidelines help the model rapidly steer toward promising paths. Remove the experience bank and gains flatten in later iterations — experience sustains effective refinement. Both banks are essential, and they serve different phases of the search.

## The Honest Limits

TMAS hasn't been tested on frontier models like GPT-5.5. The multi-agent synergy gains might look very different when the base model is already a strong reasoner — or they might compound further. We don't know yet.

The RL pipeline depends on an external teacher model (DeepSeek-V3.2) to pre-construct cold-start trajectories and memory-based training data. That's an expensive dependency, and it means the trained model's effectiveness is partly bounded by the teacher's coverage.

Eight parallel solutions, eight verification passes each, up to 20 iterations, 128K token outputs — this isn't cheap inference. TMAS trades compute for capability, and the question of whether these gains survive stricter compute budgets remains open.

## From Bigger Models to Better Scaffolding

The real claim here is architectural, not numerical. TMAS shows that the dominant bottleneck isn't model size — it's inference organization. Hierarchical memory (experience for reuse, guidelines for avoidance) and targeted RL (correctness plus utilization plus exploration) turn a 4B model into something that rivals models two orders of magnitude larger.

Undifferentiated history → curated, stratified memory. And the compute that was already there starts to actually compound.
