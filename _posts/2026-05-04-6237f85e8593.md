*TL;DR: A new six-tier benchmark shows that a 26B open-weight model matches GPT-5 on structured tool-use tasks while costing 15× less — but the frontier still owns long-horizon planning, and neither side is reliable there.*

## The routing question nobody answered

Your production agent pipeline makes a dozen model calls per user request.

Most of them are routine — a search, a lookup, a single submission. Yet the default is to send every single one to the most expensive frontier model you have.

That habit is expensive. It is also unnecessary.

AgentFloor, a new benchmark from Karmakar and Chatterjee, maps exactly where small open-weight models can replace frontier calls without losing accuracy — and where they definitively cannot.

## The capability ladder

AgentFloor is not another aggregate-score benchmark. It is a six-tier ladder, where each rung adds exactly one cognitive demand.

- **A0** — instruction following, no tools
- **A** — call one tool
- **B** — chain two tools sequentially (the first output feeds the second)
- **C** — branch on an intermediate result
- **D** — synthesize from multiple sources and recover from conflicts
- **E** — long-horizon planning under persistent constraints, 8–12 tool calls

Five tasks per tier, thirty total.

The same eight abstract tools — `search_records`, `lookup_record`, `get_attribute`, `list_options`, `check_constraint`, `compare_records`, `compute_value`, `submit_decision` — cover every task.

No filesystem, no live APIs, no web, no timestamps. The environment is deterministic by design.

That determinism eliminates the API drift that gutted ToolBench (44.4% API success at rerun) and the flawed test cases that undercut SWE-Bench Verified (59.4% of audited problems, per OpenAI's 2025 reanalysis).

This is a deliberate narrowing. Rather than asking whether agents succeed in messy real environments, AgentFloor asks: once you strip away the noise, which cognitive demands actually require scale?

## How they tested

Sixteen open-weight models from 0.27B to 32B parameters, plus GPT-5.

Native tool calling only — no prompt-based emulation, no JSON extraction from text. A single Python runner, no model-specific prompt tricks, temperature zero.

The result: 16,542 runs, scored binary pass/fail by four checker families. Bootstrap confidence intervals at 95% with 10,000 resamples.

Equivalence tested with a pre-registered two-one-sided-test (TOST) at a ±10 percentage-point margin, pairing on `(task, variant, run_idx)` tuples.

This statistical discipline matters. Prior tool-use benchmarks reported aggregate pass rates without confidence intervals.

FrugalGPT introduced model routing by predicted difficulty. It needed exactly the kind of static capability-and-cost prior that AgentFloor now provides.

## Where small models win

The headline number: gemma4:26b is equivalent to GPT-5 at the pre-registered ±10 pp margin in aggregate across all 30 tasks.

$$\Delta = +0.4 \text{ pp},\quad 90\%\ \text{CI}\ [-4.0,\ +5.1]$$

Same overall task completion rate. Fifteen times cheaper.

On A0 — plain instruction following, no tools — gemma4:26b strictly outperforms GPT-5: 100% vs 80%.

All nine of GPT-5's A0 failures land on a single task where it paraphrases with vocabulary the keyword matcher does not recognize, then declines to revise. The smaller model just does the work.

On tier A — single tool call — the two are formally equivalent: $\Delta = +2.2$ pp, 95% CI $[0,\ +6.7]$.

The deployment recipe is short and sweet.

For instruction-following and single-tool work, a sub-5B model clears the 80% bar.

A0 clears at 4B (nemotron-3-nano:4b, 92%). A clears at 3B (ministral-3:3b, 88%).

Cost per passed task: $0.00007 to $0.0010 on a Mac.

## The cliff between chaining and branching

The steepest drop in the entire heatmap is the B→C transition.

Tier B — sequential two-tool chains — admits a 2B model at the 70% reliability bar (qwen3.5:2b clears it with a point estimate of 80%). But no model in the corpus clears even 60% on C, D, or E without intervention. Zero.

This is not an artifact of the 80% convention. The qualitative break survives any reasonable threshold between 60% and 90%.

The cognitive demand of branching on an intermediate result is where the floor currently sits for every model tested — frontier included.

On B, C, and D, point estimates between gemma4:26b and GPT-5 are within 9 pp on all three tiers.

But per-tier paired samples are too small ($n = 45$) to certify formal equivalence at the ±10 pp margin. The data are simply not informative enough to call these ties with statistical certainty.

## The long-horizon wall

Tier E is where the frontier advantage shows up — and it is real but small in absolute terms.

GPT-5 passes 10% on E. The best open-weight cell is ministral-3:8b at 16%. Neither number is anywhere near deployment-grade reliability.

In the Frame A comparison (gemma4:26b vs GPT-5), the frontier is strictly superior on E ($\Delta = -10.2$ pp, 95% CI $[-18.4,\ -2.0]$). Both sides are failing.

This matters for routing.

The τ-bench work measured end-to-end pass rates on real-world agent tasks and found frontier reliability wanting. AgentFloor points to a likely culprit: the bottleneck is sustained constraint tracking over 8–12 steps, and that cognitive demand is hard for every model.

## Interventions are model-specific, not universal

The authors ran four ablations on the E-tier gap. What they found is a caution against one-size-fits-all fixes.

**Explicit-submission prompt.** Adding "you MUST call `submit_decision`" to the system prompt lifts ministral-3:8b on task E1 from 0% to 100%.

The model already computes the correct answer — it just emits it as text instead of calling the submission tool. The prompt converts that latent capability into action.

Null on four other models.

**Step-budget ×2.** Doubling GPT-5's max steps moves only E1: from 1/9 to 4/9, a +33 pp effect on one task. Trace inspection confirms GPT-5 never touches the extra capacity on any other E task — those failures are early resignation, not step exhaustion.

**Reasoning mode toggle.** Disabling reasoning on Qwen3 helps the 32B model on tier B (+12 pp) but hurts the 8B and 14B variants. Reasoning tokens help a small model compose a chain it cannot "just know"; at 32B the chain is already known, and the reasoning tokens burn output budget, triggering more resignations.

**Structured prompts.** The obvious candidate — a plan/execute/submit phase decomposition — hurt every single model tested. Ministral-3:8b dropped −33 pp overall.

The model obeys the "do not call tools yet" instruction in the PLAN phase, then emits a prose answer without ever entering EXECUTE.

The intervention designed to reduce early resignation produced more of it.

There is no universal lever here. Each effective intervention addresses a specific failure mechanism in one model on one task.

## Same score, different failures

The aggregate parity between gemma4:26b and GPT-5 hides categorically different failure signatures on tier E.

GPT-5 on E: 39% early resignation (F5), 24% plans-without-executing (F5b), 18% partial completion, 8% step-budget exhaustion, 0% hallucinated tools. The frontier engages, plans, sometimes partially executes — then quits.

Gemma4:26b on E: 41% step-budget exhaustion (F4), 36% hallucinated tools (F1), 23% early resignation, 0% plans-without-executing. The smaller model does not quit — it runs out of room and invents tools that do not exist.

Same overall TCR. Different bottlenecks.

That is why a step-budget increase helps GPT-5 (it has more room to finish its plan) but would be useless for gemma4:26b (it would just invent more fake tools for longer). The right fix depends on the failure mode, not the score.

## The honest limits

Five tasks per tier is enough for a controlled capability study, not for exhaustive coverage.

GPT-5 is the only frontier model at paper-grade paired sample size. So per-tier equivalence on B, C, and D cannot be formally certified.

The deterministic abstract-tool environment excludes real-API drift, GUI grounding, and web interaction. This map applies to structured tool-use routing, not to open-ended agentic deployment.

## The routing recipe

The practitioner takeaway is concrete.

Tiers A0, A, and B can be routed to a sub-5B open-weight model at negligible accuracy cost. That covers the vast majority of routine calls in production agent pipelines.

Reserve the frontier for the narrow class of long-horizon planning tasks where it still holds an advantage. Even there, reliability is below deployment grade for every model tested.

The cost gap compounds: gemma4:26b on a Mac Studio reaches $0.0022 per passed task against GPT-5 at $0.0327.

Wall-clock latency tracks the same direction — 16 seconds versus 41 seconds per passed task.

The Pareto frontier of cost-per-passed-task is occupied by open-weight models at every reliability point in this corpus.

Small models won't plan your long-horizon campaign. But they can handle the floor.
