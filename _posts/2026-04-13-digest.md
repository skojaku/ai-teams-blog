## Themes

The multi-principal problem has arrived. LLMs were built for one user, one context, one reward signal — and today's batch shows what cracks when you remove that assumption. *Multi-User Large Language Model Agents* demonstrates that shared agents leak private information and botch authority hierarchies across 19 frontier models, while *Many-Tier Instruction Hierarchy in LLM Agents* reveals that >99% two-tier accuracy collapses to ~40% at twelve tiers. Same root cause: no architecture for who outranks whom.

More agents does not mean better outcomes. The naive intuition — add collaborators, get smarter — takes a beating. *On the Uncertainty of Large Language Model-Based Multi-Agent Systems* shows single agents outperform multi-agent teams in 43.3% of tested scenarios, and *Strategic Algorithmic Monoculture* finds that LLM pairs told to disagree still agree 27% of the time. Scale the team, scale the failure mode.

The coordination-divergence asymmetry runs deep. LLMs are uncanny at converging on the same answer and strikingly bad at spreading out. *Strategic Algorithmic Monoculture* measures this directly — 72% coordination agreement versus 27% residual agreement under divergence pressure — but you see the shadow elsewhere. Gradual privacy leaks in the multi-user paper, premature consensus in the uncertainty paper, echo traps in the credit-assignment survey. The same homogeneity that makes coordination effortless makes diversification structurally hard.

Credit assignment is the bottleneck nobody solved. *From Reasoning to Agentic* surveys 47 methods and argues that the jump from reasoning RL to agentic RL breaks every assumption behind them — deterministic transitions, verifiable intermediates, single-generation trajectories. Signal-to-noise drops by roughly 100× when you go from one turn to a hundred. The many-tier paper's finding that models can't distinguish pivotal instructions from trivial ones is a symptom of the same disease.

Diagnosis is outpacing cure. Today's papers are heavy on measurement tools and light on fixes. Muses-Bench, MANYIH-BENCH, the Entropy Judger, the primary-versus-strategic monoculture decomposition — the field is building x-rays faster than it is writing prescriptions. That is probably the right order of operations.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag, one alarm bell. Every post today is about what happens when you push LLMs past the single-user, single-turn, single-tier regime they were trained for — and the answer is consistent: they break in specific, measurable, structurally inevitable ways.

The genuinely new thing is the measurement. A year ago we had anecdotes about multi-agent failures and vague intuitions about instruction hierarchy. Now we have Muses-Bench running 19 models through three conflict scenarios, MANYIH-BENCH isolating privilege-confusion from adversarial attack, an entropy judger that reads the first round and tells you whether the team will succeed, and a preregistered monoculture experiment separating primary from strategic correlation. The diagnostics have teeth.

What is incremental is the fix. The credit-assignment survey catalogs 47 methods and not one handles partial observability well. The multi-user paper ends where most papers end — "the next step is building agents that don't fail." The many-tier paper shows that even the format of your privilege tags changes accuracy by eight points. We can see the gap clearly. We cannot yet cross it.

What warrants a closer look is the entropy story. The finding that multi-agent effectiveness locks in during round one, that additional rounds just amplify noise, and that RL fine-tuning inverts the entire entropy-accuracy relationship — that is not a small-methods detail. It is a reframe of where practitioners should spend their compute budget. Stop tuning the topology. Start tuning the base model.

We are deploying agents into organizations and markets they are not built for. Today we learned exactly how thin the ice is.
