## Themes

**Emergent dynamics live where you didn't design them.** Three of today's papers find that multi-agent LLM systems produce behaviors no single agent was trained for. *Network Effects and Agreement Drift in LLM Debates* shows agents sliding toward "agree" regardless of majority position — a directional persuasiveness built into the models, not the network. *When Reasoning Models Hurt Behavioral Simulation* reveals that stronger reasoning annihilates the messy bounded-rational variation simulations need. *Beyond Arrow's Impossibility* finds fairness arising from the argument itself, not from either arguer. The pattern: the interaction is the unit of analysis now.

**Scaffolding beats raw strength.** A five-field notepad recovers compromise where GPT-5.2's native reasoning produces 45 out of 45 authority decisions. A 3B model wearing four hats outperforms a 72B monolith on real-device benchmarks. The message in both *When Reasoning Models Hurt Behavioral Simulation* and *Towards Scalable Lightweight GUI Agents* is the same: constrain the problem, don't scale the solver.

**Bias is a property of the process, not the model.** Agreement drift only surfaces when agents talk to each other — it is invisible in single-agent evaluations. Emergent fairness works because structured debate forces mutual patching. Both *Network Effects and Agreement Drift in LLM Debates* and *Beyond Arrow's Impossibility* shift the evaluation target from the agent to the argument, from the model to the mechanism.

**Selective activation over universal supervision.** The cognitive companion in *The Cognitive Companion* delivers large quality gains on loop-prone tasks and actively hurts on structured ones. LAMO scopes a 3B model to execution and reserves planning for a bigger partner. The rule forming across both papers: know your task type before you choose your tool.

**Decomposition is the new scaling.** LAMO splits one job into four roles on a shared backbone. The cognitive companion splits monitoring from generation. The solver-sampler paper splits "solving" from "sampling." Three different domains, one move: break the job into pieces that fit smaller, cheaper models — then orchestrate.

## Editorial

Today's batch has a spine: five papers, all touching multi-agent LLM systems, all converging on one uncomfortable idea.

Raw model capability is not the objective.

Agreement drift shows that stronger models don't fix a directional bias — they carry it. The solver-sampler mismatch shows that the strongest reasoner in the room is the worst sampler. Emergent fairness shows that the best outcome comes from conflict between misaligned agents, not from aligning one agent harder. LAMO shows that a 3B executor with the right structure beats a 72B monolith with none. The cognitive companion shows that a lightweight probe on the right task outruns a full LLM judge on the wrong one.

What each paper is telling you, in its own vocabulary, is that the lever has moved. The old way — make the model bigger, make it reason harder, make it more aligned — produces agents that solve games instead of enacting them. The new way decomposes the job, constrains the scope, and trusts the interaction to carry what no single model can.

The real question after today is not whether decomposition works. It is how far it can be pushed before the orchestration overhead costs more than the scaling savings.

We'll find out when someone tries it on a hundred agents instead of four.
