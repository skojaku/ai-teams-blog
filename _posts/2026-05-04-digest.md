## Themes

**Small models, big questions.** Three of today's five papers show small open-weight models rivaling frontier systems on specific slices of work. *AgentFloor* finds a 26B model equivalent to GPT-5 on routine tool-use tiers. *ML-Agent* trains a 7B model to match GPT-5's ML engineering at a twentieth of the cost. *Agent Capsules* proves that smart merging lets smaller models handle multi-agent workloads without losing quality. The pattern is consistent: the frontier's advantage narrows to the long tail.

**The joint-optimization principle.** Tuning agents one at a time leaves coordination gains on the table. *Build, Judge, Optimize* shows MAMUT — joint prompt-bundle optimization — beating per-agent tuning by up to 12 percentage points on safety. *Agent Capsules* makes the same bet at the execution layer: group-level quality gating outperforms per-agent dispatch. The lesson is structural — coupled systems need system-level signals, not node-level ones.

**The long-horizon wall.** Every model, large and small, falls apart on sustained multi-step planning. *AgentFloor* documents this with surgical precision: no model clears 60% on tiers C, D, or E, frontier included. *ML-Agent* sidesteps the problem with step-wise RL that avoids full rollouts. The Bayesian position paper frames the fix as a decision-theoretic one: stop when expected value of information drops below cost. The wall is real, and none of today's papers claim to have climbed it.

**Routing is the new deployment primitive.** When small models cover most of the workload and the frontier owns only the hard fringe, routing becomes the architecture. *AgentFloor* gives the static capability map. *Agent Capsules* adds a runtime quality gate that decides when to merge and when to escalate. The Bayesian position paper wants routing driven by posterior expected utility. Three papers, one direction: don't send everything to the biggest model — send it to the right one.

**Measurement opens the loop.** You cannot optimize what you cannot measure, and today's papers are obsessed with closing that loop. *Build, Judge, Optimize* builds a calibrated boolean rubric that turns subjective quality into a reward signal. *AgentFloor* designs a deterministic benchmark that eliminates confounds. *Agent Capsules* shadow-evaluates every compound run against a fine-grained baseline. The shared instinct: noisy evaluation is the bottleneck, and fixing it unlocks everything downstream.

## Editorial

Today's batch is a single conversation with five speakers, and they're all talking about the same thing: the gap between what small models can do and what agentic systems need them to do.

The genuine progress is that the gap is narrower than we thought. *AgentFloor* hands you a routing map where a 4B model clears instruction-following and a 26B model is statistically equivalent to GPT-5 on everything but the hardest tier. *ML-Agent* shows you can train your way past the gap on specific domains. *Agent Capsules* shows you can architect your way around it with quality gates and escalation ladders.

The incremental part is the Bayesian position paper. The framing is right — agentic orchestration should account for uncertainty — but without benchmark numbers it remains a research agenda, not a result. The seven design patterns are useful as a vocabulary. They are not yet evidence.

What warrants a closer second look is the *Build, Judge, Optimize* finding that safety gains the most from joint optimization. A 12-point lift in compliance, in a production system, from prompt-bundle tuning alone, is the kind of number that should make compliance officers and ML engineers sit at the same table.

And then there is the wall. Every paper acknowledges it. No paper clears it. Long-horizon planning under persistent constraints — tier E in AgentFloor, the full-rollout bottleneck in ML-Agent, the stopping problem in the Bayesian paper — is where the frontier's advantage is real and everyone's reliability is bad. We are routing around the hard part, not solving it.

The floor belongs to small models now. The frontier owns the long horizon, and it can barely stand on it.
