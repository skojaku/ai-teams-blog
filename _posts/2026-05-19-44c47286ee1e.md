*TL;DR: LatentUMM squeezes the reasoning gains of multi-agent debate into a single model's weights through hierarchical distillation—achieving 4.8% average improvement across benchmarks while running one forward pass instead of orchestrating five agents across multiple rounds.*

## The Debate Tax

Five agents arguing in rounds, catching each other's mistakes, converging on better answers—multi-agent debate works. Du et al. showed that back in 2023.

But you pay for every round.

Inference cost scales with agent count. Error propagation can amplify hallucinations instead of correcting them. The whole thing is too slow for real-time use.

What if you could skip the debate and just keep the wisdom?

LatentUMM asks exactly that: can you bake the critique-and-revision loop of a multi-agent system into a single model's weights, so one forward pass gets you what used to require five agents arguing over $K$ rounds?

## Why the Agents Don't Matter

Recent work by Kim et al. revealed something unexpected: if you strip away or perturb the explicit agent structure in a multi-agent system, performance barely drops.

The gains come from the *reasoning dynamics* debate induces—self-checking, error correction, step decomposition—not from the topology of who talks to whom.

Ke et al.'s MAS-Orchestra study confirmed the same pattern: the argument *process* is what matters, not the agent wiring.

LatentUMM takes this seriously. Instead of distilling who-said-what-to-whom, it distills the *process* of conflict and refinement.

The agent graph disappears. The reasoning behavior stays.

## How to Distill the Debate

The framework stacks three strategies, each one reaching deeper into the debate traces.

### Reasoning-Enhanced SFT

The first layer is straightforward: train the student on the final consensus traces that the agent group agreed upon, reasoning steps included.

Given an input $x$ and a successful trace $r = (r_1, \ldots, r_n)$ leading to answer $y^*$, the loss is:

$$L_{\text{SFT}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x,r,y^*) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \sum_{t=1}^{|r|} \log p_\theta(r_t \mid r_{<t}, x) + \log p_\theta(y^* \mid r, x) \right]$$

Two components: a reasoning loss that teaches the model to generate coherent intermediate steps, and an answer loss that grounds the final prediction in those steps.

This is baseline distillation. It works, but inconsistently.

### Data Augmentation via Diverse Extraction

The second layer exploits a property of multi-agent debate: different agents solve the same problem in different ways.

A high-capacity teacher LLM extracts $k$ diverse correct trajectories from the debate logs—same answer, different logic. The student trains on all of them:

$$L_{\text{Aug}}(\theta) = -\frac{1}{k} \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sum_{t=1}^{T} \log p_\theta(y_t \mid y_{<t}, r_i, x)$$

More paths to the same solution means more robust reasoning. But gains here are still spotty—DA helps on some benchmarks and flatlines on others.

### Process-Aware Distillation

This is the hero method.

PAD treats distillation as reinforcement learning. A Process Reward Model (PRM) $R_\phi$ scores each reasoning step for correctness—not just "is this step right?" but "is this step more consistent with the debate consensus than the alternative?" The contrastive objective drives that distinction.

The PRM training follows a two-stage curriculum.

Stage I: freeze the backbone, train only the reward head. This preserves pre-trained features while the head learns to map representations to correctness labels.

Stage II: unfreeze everything for end-to-end specialization on logical fallacy detection.

Then the student policy $\pi_\theta$ is fine-tuned with Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO). Given $G$ sampled outputs, GRPO compares rewards within the group—no separate value function needed:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{x, \{o_i\}} \left[ \frac{1}{G} \sum_{i=1}^{G} L_i(\theta) - \beta D_{\text{KL}}(\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\text{ref}}) \right]$$

where advantages $\hat{A}_i = (R_\phi(o_i) - \mu_R) / \sigma_R$ normalize PRM scores across the group.

The whole pipeline—debate, filter, train—shifts computation from inference to training. Five agents at test time becomes one.

## What Worked

Across 120 experiments spanning Qwen3, Gemma3, and Llama3 model families, three core results stand out.

First, LatentUMM improves single-agent accuracy by **4.8% on average** across all benchmarks, approaching vanilla multi-agent performance at a fraction of the inference cost. In-domain improvements peak at 30%. Out-of-domain gains are more modest at 1–3%.

Second, PAD dominates. It is the only method that consistently improves performance across every model, dataset, and evaluation setting.

RSFT and DA fluctuate—sometimes helpful, sometimes not.

PAD's reasoning coherence score hits 3.96 against a baseline of 1.88. On TruthfulQA, PAD achieves BLEU 0.66 versus baseline 0.60.

![Radar chart comparing reasoning quality metrics—step decomposition, intermediate verification, error localization, and reasoning coherence—between baseline, RSFT, DA, and PAD, with PAD showing the highest scores across all dimensions]({{ "/assets/figures/44c47286ee1e/fig_009_06.png" | relative_url }})

Third, PRM capacity matters more than student size. A strong PRM lifts even a 0.6B model noticeably, while a weak PRM limits gains regardless of how big the student is.

Scaling teacher agents beyond five hurts the smallest students (0.6B) but helps larger ones (8B). The student's representational capacity sets the ceiling.

## What Doesn't Work

Simply adding more training data does not help. For RSFT and DA, performance plateaus or degrades with data scaling.

PAD is the exception: it stays stable across data scales because PRM-guided supervision filters for high-signal reasoning rather than dumping volume on the model.

Reasoning quality outweighs quantity. More trajectories don't fix a small model, but better step-level signals do.

Out-of-domain transfer is real but thin. Models trained only on GSM8K math problems do transfer to HotpotQA, QASPER, and QMSum—but the paper reports modest gains of 1–3%, suggesting distillation sharpens trained reasoning patterns more than it builds fully general reasoning skills.

The experiments also explicitly exclude tool use and memory management tasks. Those are harder, messier, and still open.

## The Payoff

The core claim is clean: the reasoning benefits of multi-agent debate can be internalized by a single model. You don't need the debate at test time if you trained on what the debate *produced*.

The framework is algorithm-agnostic too. LatentUMM works with debate, self-consistency, and AgentVerse protocols—the distillation targets reasoning dynamics, not agent structure.

It also transfers to heterogeneous agent settings and multimodal LLMs, though gains there are preliminary.

The practical upside is straightforward: deploy one model, get multi-agent-quality reasoning, skip the multi-agent cost.

## The Ceiling on Small Models

There is an honest limit. A 0.6B student simply cannot absorb what a 20-agent teacher ensemble produces.

More agents beyond five hurt the smallest models—the signal becomes noise.

Scaling teachers is effective only when matched to the student's capacity.

And out-of-domain gains of 1–3% mean distillation strengthens what you already train on more than it unlocks new reasoning from scratch.

![Line plot showing that increasing teacher agents beyond five causes accuracy to decline for a 0.6B student model while continuing to improve for an 8B student]({{ "/assets/figures/44c47286ee1e/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

These aren't footnotes. They define where the method stops working and where the next research question begins.

The debate doesn't vanish—it just moves offline.
