*TL;DR: Post-hoc margin refinement can reshape the geometric boundaries between tokens in a converged 4B-parameter LLM, but the gains concentrate in punctuation and high-frequency tokens — and a single test on a fine-tuned model shows entity-like words going net negative.*

You talk to an LLM through discrete tokens. Inside, everything is vectors.

That gap has a shape. Marshall Brett calls it the Voronoi tessellation: the hidden states of a transformer live on a smooth manifold, and the unembedding matrix carves that manifold into territories, one per vocabulary token. Each hidden state lands in some territory and the model speaks the token on whose turf it sits.

The trouble is that some territories don't quite reach. Some positions in semantic space sit in an expressibility gap — too far from any token's territory to be assigned confidently. Mabrok (2026) proved this gap obeys a linear scaling law. Brett's question: can you fix it after the model has already converged?

## The Setup

The subject is Qwen3.5-4B-Base: 4.21B parameters, 248,320-token vocabulary. Brett uses WikiText-103 as the evaluation corpus, extracting 256,577 token positions.

The key metric is the Voronoi margin — the gap between the top-1 and top-2 logit at each position. Bigger margin means the model is more confident. The baseline median margin is 1.030, and roughly 30% of positions have margins below 0.5.

Here's the first wrinkle: bfloat16 inference destroys the measurement. The model's native precision quantizes 256K margin values down to just 223 unique values. The fix is surgical — extract the final hidden state, upcast to float32, and recompute the lm_head projection at full precision.

This recovers 238,399 unique margin values and pushes the log-log scaling regression to R² = 0.9997 — the strongest confirmation of Theorem 10.5 to date.

## Two Ways to Push Back the Boundaries

Brett tests two margin refinement procedures (MRP) over 200 gradient steps.

**Margin maximization** pushes the top-1 logit up and the top-2 logit down. It's cheap — just a top-2 sort of the logit vector. The loss is:

$$L_{\text{margin}} = -\mathbb{E}[m(h) \mid m(h) < \tau]$$

**Fisher information distance maximization** moves representations along the direction of maximum information divergence in the model's own geometry. The squared Fisher distance between two token embeddings is:

$$d_{\text{Fisher}}^2(w_i, w_j; h) = (\tilde{w}_i - \tilde{w}_j)^\top G(h)(\tilde{w}_i - \tilde{w}_j)$$

where $\tilde{w}_t = w_t / \|w_t\|$ are L2-normalized embeddings and $G(h) = W_k^\top \Sigma_k W_k$ is the Fisher metric induced by the top-k softmax covariance. The loss:

$$L_{\text{Fisher}} = -\mathbb{E}_h\left[\sum_{i \neq j \in \text{top-k}} p(t_i|h) \cdot p(t_j|h) \cdot d_{\text{Fisher}}(\tilde{w}_i, \tilde{w}_j; h)\right]$$

The geometric intuition: margin maximization pushes along a fixed axis (top-1 minus top-2 direction), which can distort representations when those two tokens aren't actually confusable. Fisher maximization rotates toward a more separable alternative — one the representation manifold can accommodate at greater distance.

## The Dose-Response Curve

Both methods improve margins monotonically with the strength parameter $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}$. But they differ critically in collateral damage.

Margin maximization damage escalates super-linearly. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.1$, the flip ratio (wrong-to-right vs right-to-wrong) is 2.9x. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.3$, it drops to 2.1x. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=1.0$, it collapses to 0.4x — more positions get worse than better.

Fisher MRP behaves differently. R→W damage stays flat at ~5,300 positions across $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.15$ through 0.6. The flip ratio holds near 3.0x. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.6$, median margin improves +28% with zero downstream benchmark degradation — six standard tasks (ARC-C, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA, LAMBADA, TruthfulQA) stay in the 0.631–0.636 band.

The corrections ceiling is revealing. Both methods find roughly the same ~16,300 correctable positions — only about 6.4 percentage points of the 256K positions are close enough to a better boundary that geometry alone can fix them. The rest are wrong for reasons that have nothing to do with token boundaries.

Fisher's runner-up rotation analysis explains why damage stays flat. About 25% of positions retain their top-1 prediction but swap the runner-up token. Fisher rotates to a different, more naturally separable alternative — one the representation manifold can accommodate at greater distance. The top prediction doesn't change. The second-place option becomes one the geometry supports cleanly.

## Where the Gains Actually Land

Here's the catch that flat benchmark means hide.

The 100+ frequency bucket — tokens appearing more than 100 times in the WikiText validation corpus — accounts for 84.0% of Fisher's net corrections at $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.6$. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=2.0$, that concentration rises to 92.1%.

Brett classifies tokens by coarse type: structural (punctuation, symbols, whitespace), numeric, function words, entity-like, content words, and fragments. At $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=0.6$, structural tokens account for 65.9% of net corrections. Content words contribute 15.6%, entity-like 3.2%. By $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}=2.0$, structural share rises to 75.0%, content words fall to 9.4%, and entity-like collapses to 0.9%.

The dominant positive corrections at every strength level are commas, periods, spaces, quotes, hyphens, @, and =. The persistent negative-net regressions at higher $\lambda_{\text{MRP}}$ are ordinary words: *and, he, his, with, which, it, they, and their*.

So Fisher is not a punctuation optimizer. But its gains are heavily concentrated in head tokens, and its higher-strength regressions cluster in medium-frequency content-bearing words.

## The Instruct Model Red Flag

One fine-tuned follow-up probe on Qwen3.5-4B instruct-style shows the same concentration after alignment training, with a sharper failure mode. Entity-like tokens go net negative even though overall token accuracy rises +2.63% and benchmark means stay flat.

Base-model gains do not transfer cleanly through fine-tuning.

## Why It Matters

This work validates Mabrok's latent semantic manifold framework at 4B scale with the highest reported R² on the expressibility gap scaling law. It demonstrates that post-hoc geometric intervention is feasible — the Voronoi tessellation of a converged model is plastic, even if it was assumed to be fixed.

Fisher information distance MRP is the better mechanism. It reshapes margins without creating geometric distortion, and its damage profile is bounded and predictable within the validated range.

The honest limits are sharp. Flat benchmark means do not prove uniform token-level utility. The corrections ceiling means most incorrect positions are wrong because of missing knowledge, not boundary proximity. And the frequency-concentration result — amplified at higher strength, surviving fine-tuning, concentrating in structural tokens while medium-frequency content words regress — means Fisher's practical ceiling is determined less by aggregate damage and more by whether concentrated structural-token gains justify the growing token-value inequality.

Whether a frequency-aware or token-value-aware Fisher weighting can preserve the head-token gains without sacrificing content words and entities remains an open question. The geometry is reshapable. What shape you should push it into depends on what you actually care about.
