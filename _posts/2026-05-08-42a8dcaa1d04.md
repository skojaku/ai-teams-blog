*TL;DR: A single 7B-parameter router learns to both decompose tasks and pick the right worker for each subtask, beating the strongest multi-agent workflow by 16% at roughly an order-of-magnitude lower cost.*

## A Router That Knows When Not to Route

Nine commercial LLMs sit behind one API, spanning over two orders of magnitude in per-token price—from Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite at the cheap end to Claude-Opus-4-6 at the other.

A simple factual question arrives. Most orchestration systems fire up a planner, decompose the query into subtasks, and dispatch workers—all to answer something a cheap model could handle in one shot.

Uno-Orchestra does something different: it *chooses not to decompose*.

A single 7B-parameter causal language model jointly emits the task decomposition and the per-subtask routing decision in one forward pass. When the task is simple, the policy collapses to a direct answer at near-zero coordination cost. When the task genuinely needs parallel subtasks, it decomposes and routes—not because a heuristic told it to, but because it learned when decomposition actually helps.

## Where Both Camps Fall Short

Current router systems sit in one of two camps, and both have the same blind spot.

Flat routers like xRouter pick one expert per query and optimize for cost, but they cannot see the parallel structure inside a complex task. Hierarchical orchestrators like AOrchestra decompose tasks into sub-agents, but dispatch is hand-engineered or prompt-driven—the assignment is never learned.

Neither camp optimizes the two decisions that actually matter together: *whether* to decompose and *where* to route. Uno-Orchestra collapses both into a single policy, trained end-to-end with reinforcement learning, so the router discovers its own decomposition depth instead of having it prescribed.

## One Policy, Two Decisions

The key design choice is deceptively simple.

Instead of separate planner and dispatcher modules, Uno-Orchestra uses a single causal LM that emits a plan and its routing tokens in the same assistant turn, separated only by left-to-right causal masking.

At turn $t$, the policy factors as:

$$\pi_\theta(a_t \mid h_t) = \underbrace{\pi_\theta(\text{plan}_t \mid h_t)}_{\text{decompose}} \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{K_t} \underbrace{\pi_\theta(p_{t,k} \mid h_t, \text{plan}_t, p_{t,<k})}_{\text{route}}$$

![Architecture diagram showing a single causal-LM router jointly emitting task decomposition and per-subtask (model, primitive) routing tokens via left-to-right causal masking]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Each subtask gets a routing pair $p_{t,k} = (m, s) \in \mathcal{P}$, where $m$ is a worker model and $s$ is a *primitive*—an atomic action drawn from a closed vocabulary covering cognitive operations, skill invocations, and tool calls.

The admissible set $\mathcal{P}$ is sparse: not every model supports every primitive, and token-level cost varies by orders of magnitude across pairs.

No auxiliary heads. No per-stage loss weights. The decompose-then-route structure falls out of the token order.

## How the Router Learns to Delegate

**Stage 1 — SFT on a verifier-gated curriculum.** A strong teacher orchestrator generates trajectories across 38 datasets. Only the ones that pass a source-specific verifier make it into the training pool: 61,201 trajectories, with code and tool subtasks executed in real sandboxes so observations are runtime outputs, not simulated traces.

**Stage 2 — Agentic-GRPO on the residual hard pool.** The 2,976 questions where the teacher also failed become the RL pool.

The objective extends GRPO—the group-relative policy optimizer from DeepSeekMath—with turn-level credit assignment. Instead of one trajectory-level reward that washes evenly across all turns, Agentic-GRPO routes a portion of the terminal reward back to each turn, augmented with bounded per-turn shaping:

$$\tilde{R}_{i,t} = \gamma^{T_i - t} R(\tau_i) + \sum_{s=t}^{T_i} \gamma^{s-t} r_{i,s}, \quad \hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{\tilde{R}_{i,t} - \mu_{q,t}}{\sigma_{q,t} + \varepsilon}$$

The shaping term is capped at $\eta \ll 1$ so it can never overpower the verifier signal.

This matters: early routing choices, repair triggers, and premature aggregation decisions now get individual credit instead of sharing one averaged gradient across the entire rollout.

## Blinding the Workers

During RL training, the router never sees model names. Workers are labeled WORKER 1 through WORKER K, and the identity mapping is resampled every episode. The router must profile each worker through interaction and reward feedback—a constraint that forces genuine delegation rather than brand-name shortcuts.

Removing this protocol tells you exactly how much the shortcut is worth. Without blind labeling, per-query cost inflates from $0.1610 to $0.8163 and average context grows from 391 to 548 tokens.

Accuracy barely moves—less than one point. The policy exploits brand identity to send more work to familiar names, even when it does not need to.

![Ablation chart comparing per-query cost and context length with versus without the blind worker protocol, showing cost jumping from $0.16 to $0.82 when brand identity is exposed]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_008_04.png" | relative_url }})

## Where Selective Delegation Wins

Across 13 benchmarks spanning math, code, knowledge, reading, and agentic tool-use, Uno-Orchestra hits 77.0% macro pass@1—roughly 16% above AgentOrchestra, the strongest workflow baseline.

The cost number is the punch line: $0.1011 per query versus AgentOrchestra's $1.2118. That is roughly an order of magnitude cheaper while achieving the highest accuracy.

![Bar chart comparing macro pass@1 accuracy across five capability domains, with Uno-Orchestra at 77.0% leading all baselines including AgentOrchestra at roughly 66%]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

The biggest per-benchmark gains land where selective delegation pays off most: AIME +80.2% relative, LiveCodeBench +54.9%, MRCR +22.6%. These are the tasks that reward knowing *when* to decompose and when a single direct dispatch is enough.

Each stage pushes the same way: accuracy up, cost down. SFT stabilizes the grammar, GRPO sharpens route selection, and tree rollouts add search coverage.

Agentic-GRPO goes further—it trims redundant dispatches faster than it introduces new ones, so the controller spends roughly the same token budget on better decisions.

## Honest Limits

The router backbone matters.

After SFT alone, a 4B variant nearly matches the 7B router. But after RL, the gap widens—especially on agentic tasks. Smaller controllers can follow the orchestration grammar; they struggle with credit assignment over multi-turn rollouts.

The evaluation uses a fixed pool of nine commercial workers. Real deployment faces worker churn, pricing changes, and new models entering the pool.

When that happens, the blind-worker mapping must be re-established through fresh interaction episodes.

On a Qwen2.5-only worker pool—every proprietary frontier model removed—the method still leads R2-Reasoner across all five capability domains. The gains come from the learned routing policy, not from a single strong worker.

## Why It Matters

The per-token price gap between the cheapest and most capable models now spans over two orders of magnitude. Indiscriminate routing is not just suboptimal—it is increasingly wasteful. Uno-Orchestra gives that gap a mechanical handle: selective delegation, learned under one objective, in a single forward pass.

The blind-worker protocol is a design choice worth copying. Any production router that cares about cost should foreclose brand-name shortcuts during training, because the policy will take them otherwise—and charge you for it.

Selective delegation—the ability to both decompose and abstain from decomposing—is the organizing principle that makes an orchestrator cheap when it can and capable when it must.
