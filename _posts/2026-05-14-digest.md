## Themes

**The bottleneck is coordination, not reasoning.** Three of today's five papers prove the same point from different angles. *Talk is Cheap, Communication is Hard* shows LLM agents that find Pareto-optimal solutions alone collapse into equal splits and broken agreements when paired. *Systematic Failures in Collective Reasoning under Distributed Information* puts a number on it: 30% team accuracy versus 81% solo — the models can reason, they just can't pool what they know. *When Identity Overrides Incentives* adds a twist: agents don't merely fail to coordinate, they actively override payoff structures the moment you give them a persona. In every case, the failure is social, not cognitive.

**The communication medium reshapes the outcome.** Cheap talk triples efficiency in *Talk is Cheap, Communication is Hard* but leaves two-thirds of the gap unclosed. *Good Agentic Friends Do Not Just Give Verbal Advice* asks a radical question: what if agents stop talking in English and rewrite each other's weights instead? TFLOW cuts token usage by 83% and stays within 1–5 accuracy points of text-based collaboration on four of five benchmarks. *Toward Scalable Verifiable Reward* makes a parallel move for evaluation: swap 60,000 lines of deterministic backend for an LLM state tracker, and hallucination detection still hits 94.7% human agreement. When you change how agents communicate — or whether they communicate in language at all — you change what the system can do.

**Representation choices are governance decisions.** *When Identity Overrides Incentives* hammers this home: a single binary choice — persona or no persona — moves equilibrium attainment by up to 90 percentage points. The same payoff table, the same game, entirely different outcomes. Meanwhile, *Good Agentic Friends Do Not Just Give Verbal Advice* flags the other side: weight-space communication is efficient but opaque, complicating safety auditing. How you describe an agent, and how you wire it to its teammates, are not neutral engineering choices. They determine what your simulation produces and what you can inspect.

**Structured protocols beat more talk.** Adding communication rounds, bigger models, or larger groups all fail to close the coordination gap — as *Systematic Failures in Collective Reasoning under Distributed Information* demonstrates across 15 frontier models. The protocol that works, Exchange-then-Decide, doesn't give agents more information; it forces them to point outward at what might be missing. *Talk is Cheap, Communication is Hard* finds the same pattern from the other direction: full transparency eliminates perfunctory fairness but *doubles* stubborn anchoring. More information, more talk, more model capacity — none of it fixes what structuring the conversation fixes.

## Editorial

Today's batch has a clear central argument: multi-agent LLM systems are broken at the one thing they were supposed to solve — coordination. The models reason fine alone. Put them in a team and they anchor on shared evidence, override payoffs with identity, abandon their own agreements, and talk past each other for rounds on end without surfacing what matters.

The genuinely new insight is how *structural* the failures are. Bigger models don't help. More discussion rounds don't help. Full transparency sometimes makes things worse. What helps is designing the interaction itself: Exchange-then-Decide forces epistemic exploration; TFLOW sidesteps the language bottleneck entirely by communicating in weight space; persona assignment turns out to be a confound so large it shifts equilibria by 90 points.

The incremental pieces are the evaluation framework in *Toward Scalable Verifiable Reward* (useful, but validated only on e-commerce scenarios) and the per-instance LoRA conditioning in TFLOW (a 4.29-point average gain over static adapters — real but modest). Neither is the reason to read these papers.

What warrants a closer second look: the stubborn anchoring result in *Talk is Cheap, Communication is Hard*. Full transparency *doubles* stubborn anchoring. More information made agents more rigid, not less. If that replicates, it complicates every proposed fix that relies on "just show them more context."

The coordinating agent doesn't need to be smarter. It needs to be structurally different.
