*TL;DR: When information is split across agents, multi-agent LLM teams get only 30% accuracy—barely better than guessing—while a single agent with all the facts hits 81%. The bottleneck isn't reasoning; it's that agents never think to ask what their teammates might know.*

## A Team That Can't Pool

Four AI agents are monitoring a flood. One sees the river level. Another knows the dam upstream is releasing water.

A third knows a supply truck is heading to the tunnel. The fourth sees a fire blocking that truck.

Each agent holds a piece of the puzzle. The right call—walk up the hill—requires combining all four clues. Instead, the agents lock onto what they all share and pick the wrong answer.

That's not a hypothetical. It's the core finding of this paper.

Across 15 frontier LLMs and 65 carefully constructed tasks, multi-agent teams under distributed information scored just 30.1% accuracy. A single agent with all the facts: 80.7%. The gap is enormous, and it doesn't close with more rounds of chat, cleverer prompts, or bigger models.

The diagnosis is precise. They don't fail at reasoning over information. They fail at *surfacing* it.

Agents converge on shared evidence and never think to probe for what others might know but haven't said yet—a failure mode so classic that social psychologists named it 40 years ago.

## The Hidden Profile Trap

The Hidden Profile paradigm, introduced by Stasser and Titus in 1985, works like this. A group faces a decision. Some facts are known to everyone; some are known to exactly one person.

The shared facts point toward a tempting-but-wrong answer. The unshared facts, pooled together, reveal the correct one. No individual can get there alone.

Human groups reliably fail at this. They talk about what they already share, nod in agreement, and pick the wrong option. Yuxuan Li, Aoi Naito, and Hirokazu Shirado asked: do LLM agents do the same thing?

To find out, they built HIDDENBENCH: 65 tasks where each agent gets shared information plus one unique clue. The shared facts point the wrong way. Only by pooling the hidden pieces can the group find the right answer.

Every task is solvable by a single agent given full information—verified by confirming at least 80% solo accuracy under the Full Profile condition. If a team fails, it's not because the reasoning is hard. It's because the coordination broke.

## The Numbers, Plain

Under the Hidden Profile condition, multi-agent accuracy across all 15 models averaged 30.1%. Under the Full Profile condition—same tasks, same models, just all information given to each agent upfront—the average was 80.7%.

![Accuracy gap between single-agent Full Profile and multi-agent Hidden Profile conditions across 15 frontier LLMs, showing consistent failure under distributed information]({{ "/assets/figures/72061bc48b55/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Communication helps—sometimes dramatically. Gemini-2.5-Pro reaches 67.1% after discussion. But even the best post-discussion accuracy falls well short of what a single informed agent can do.

Then the authors ran the most revealing intervention: force every agent to disclose all their facts in the first round. GPT-4.1 jumps from 3.7% to 92.6%. Gemini-2.5-Flash leaps from 17.3% to 98.2%.

That single result pins the bottleneck. The agents can reason just fine once they have the information. What they can't do is *recognize that they're missing something* and go looking for it.

## Why Everything Else Fails

The paper methodically tests every obvious fix. None works.

**More rounds of discussion?** Performance peaks at 15 rounds and declines by round 20. Extended talk reinforces the wrong consensus rather than surfacing hidden facts.

**Better prompts?** Cooperative, conflictual, chain-of-thought, explicit "Share All Information" instructions—the best prompts reach 46.7% accuracy. That halves the gap, not closes it. Telling agents to share everything helps, but they still don't know *which* facts matter or what others might be holding.

**Bigger groups?** This is the cruelest finding. Adding agents makes things worse. The improvement from communication drops from +0.348 with 3 agents to +0.006 with 7 agents.

![Communication improvement declines steadily as group size grows from 3 to 7 agents, dropping from 0.35 to near zero]({{ "/assets/figures/72061bc48b55/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

The whole premise of multi-agent systems—more diverse perspectives, broader information—backfires without coordination mechanisms.

**Bigger models?** GPT-5 variants fail to outperform smaller models like GPT-4.1-Mini in multi-agent settings. Individual reasoning ability simply does not predict collective performance. Scaling up the model scales the reasoning, not the coordination.

Prior multi-agent debate work, like Du et al.'s 2024 study showing debate improves factuality, assumed information symmetry—everyone starts with the same facts. Remove that assumption, and the premise collapses.

Cemri et al.'s 2025 catalog of multi-agent LLM failures identified many symptoms but never isolated information asymmetry as the cause. This paper names it.

## A Lightweight Fix That Works

If the bottleneck is surfacing, not reasoning, then a protocol that forces surfacing should help—even without revealing what's hidden.

The authors test Exchange-then-Decide: a two-stage protocol where agents first share 1–2 decision-relevant facts each and state one reason the current front-runner might be wrong (2 rounds), then summarize the strongest evidence and remaining uncertainties before voting (1 pass).

No agent is told what others know. No one is forced to disclose everything. The protocol just structures the conversation around *pointing outward* rather than *agreeing inward*.

| Model | Baseline | Exchange-then-Decide |
|---|---|---|
| GPT-4.1 | 3.7% | 80.0% |
| Gemini-2.5-Flash | 17.3% | 72.7% |
| Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite | 4.3% | 74.3% |

![Baseline versus Exchange-then-Decide protocol accuracy across three model families, showing dramatic improvement from structured communication]({{ "/assets/figures/72061bc48b55/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

That's roughly a 75% reduction in the gap, across three model families, without disclosing anyone's hidden information. The protocol works because it targets the right bottleneck: epistemic exploration—actively probing for what might be missing—rather than just running more rounds of unstructured chat.

## The Honest Limits

HIDDENBENCH is a controlled diagnostic, not a deployment test. Most evaluations use 4-agent groups; the scaling data only goes up to N=7.

The Exchange-then-Decide protocol is manually designed and evaluated on only 18 of 65 tasks. Whether it holds up in open-ended, multi-turn, real-world settings—incident response, clinical diagnosis, intelligence analysis—remains an open question.

The benchmark also uses discrete decision tasks with clean ground truth. Real distributed information problems are messier: facts are ambiguous, options are fuzzy, and sometimes no one knows the answer even with full information.

But as a diagnostic framework, HIDDENBENCH does something the field has lacked. It cleanly separates "can this model reason?" from "can this team coordinate?"

The answer to the first question, across 15 frontier models, is mostly yes. The answer to the second: not without help.

## What This Changes

The promise of multi-agent LLM systems—that diverse agents with distributed knowledge will outperform any single agent—assumes a coordination capability that current models simply don't have. They don't look for what's missing. They agree on what's shared and stop.

The fix isn't bigger models. It's interaction design that makes epistemic exploration the default, not the exception.

Exchange-then-Decide is a proof of concept that such design is tractable. The broader challenge—building multi-agent systems that actively seek out what their teammates might know—is the next frontier.

Forty years ago, Stasser and Titus showed that human groups talk about shared information and neglect what's hidden. Now we know LLM agents do exactly the same thing, for exactly the same reason. The hidden profile hides itself.
