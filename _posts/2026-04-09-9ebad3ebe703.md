*TL;DR: Multi-agent LLM systems don't always beat single agents—in 43.3% of cases, the solo player wins. The paper shows that MAS effectiveness is sealed in the first round of interaction, and entropy dynamics are the hidden governor of success or failure.*

## The Setup

You built a multi-agent system.
Three agents, two rounds of back-and-forth, a complex problem split across roles. The architecture looks sophisticated. The coordination graphs are elegant.

Then it fails.

You assume the agents are the problem—maybe you need better prompting, a bigger model, more agents.

You might be looking in the wrong place.

This paper argues that the culprit is *uncertainty itself*, and specifically how it compounds across interactions. The authors—Yuxuan Zhao, Sijia Chen, and Ningxin Su—don't just identify failure modes. They show that the entire trajectory of a multi-agent system is often determined before the first agent finishes its first sentence.

## The Surprise

Conventional wisdom says more agents improve performance. Previous work from Kim et al. (2025) had already complicated this, showing that coordination overhead eats into scaling gains. But the new twist here is how early the damage is done.

Across 5 open-source LLMs (LLaMA-3.1-8B, LLaMA-3.2-3B, Qwen3-0.6B, Qwen3-4B, Qwen3-8B) and 6 benchmarks (GSM8K, MATH500, AIME2024, AIME2025, HumanEval, MMLU), single-agent systems achieved the highest accuracy in 13 out of 30 cases—43.3%.

In those 13 wins, the single agent outperformed the *average* multi-agent configuration by 6.28%.

This isn't cherry-picking. SAS matches or exceeds at least one MAS architecture in 26 of 30 scenarios.

Gao et al. (2025) had hinted at this: single-agent systems with rich skill libraries can match or surpass MAS in both accuracy and efficiency. But why? The entropy lens provides the mechanism.

## Reading the Uncertainty

The authors log per-token probability distributions from each model at every step. From these, they compute entropy: $H(s) = -\sum_{v \in V} \pi_\theta(v|s) \log \pi_\theta(v|s)$.

High entropy means the model is genuinely uncertain—many tokens look plausible. Low entropy means the model is confident.

They extract 254 entropy features spanning three levels, of which 245 are trainable:

- Token-level: per-decoding-step uncertainty
- Trajectory-level: how entropy evolves through a single agent's reasoning
- Round-level: how entropy shifts when agents interact

Four MAS topologies get compared: Sequential (agents pass outputs down a chain), Centralized (an orchestrator aggregates), Debate (agents see each other's outputs before answering), and Hybrid (orchestrator + peer feedback).

The entropy landscape of each topology tells a different story about failure.

## The First Round Is Everything

SHAP analysis reveals a brutal pattern: round-1 maximum agent entropy ranks as the top or near-top predictor of correctness across every task, architecture, and round configuration tested.

When agents disagree wildly in the opening round, they enter incompatible reasoning trajectories. Early divergence doesn't self-correct—it compounds.

![Feature importance bar plots from the XGBoost/LightGBM ensemble showing round-1 maximum agent entropy as the top predictor of correctness across all models and datasets]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

For Qwen models, entropy variance across agents is the dominant failure predictor (Pearson correlation $\rho \approx -0.92$). If one agent is confident and another is flailing in round 1, the system is already doomed.

For LLaMA models, the failure signature is different: verbose and uncertain final answers. LLaMA tends to reuse others' outputs without verification, which amplifies error propagation.

Both families share one trait: higher base model entropy consistently reduces MAS accuracy ($\rho \approx -0.73$ for LLaMA, $\rho \approx -0.64$ for Qwen).

### Why Different Models Fail Differently

The paper isolates a key difference in reasoning style.

Qwen tends to verify and refine its answers before finalizing. This generates self-correcting trajectories that suppress error propagation in MAS, even at higher entropy ranges. The model treats uncertainty as something to resolve.

LLaMA treats uncertainty differently—it gravitates toward reusing existing answers, which at low entropy can be confidently wrong. The entropy-accuracy relationship is more monotonic.

This is why RL-trained models change the picture. When the authors test Qwen2.5-7B-SimpleRL-Zoo (trained with zero-shot RL on MATH problems), the entropy-accuracy curve reshapes: accuracy peaks at near-zero entropy, dips, then recovers at moderate entropy. The recovery reflects productive exploration—longer reasoning chains with structured uncertainty.

With this RL-trained base model, SAS never surpasses MAS across the benchmarks tested. The base model matters more than the architecture.

## Three Principles

The paper distills findings into three governing principles:

**Certainty Preference.** Reducing uncertainty at any stage for any agent correlates with correctness. This is universal across topologies and tasks.

**Base Uncertainty.** Lower initial entropy in the base model directly benefits MAS performance. You can't build reliable coordination on an unreliable foundation.

**Task Awareness.** Optimal entropy profiles vary by task difficulty. Simple problems (GSM8K) demand rapid convergence to low, stable entropy. Medium problems (MATH500) benefit from sustained moderate exploration before converging. Hard problems (AIME) require substantial early effort with controlled uncertainty—excessive early divergence kills performance, but moderate average entropy enables olympiad-level reasoning.

The harder the task, the more critical round-1 entropy control becomes. On AIME2025 (25% average accuracy), round-1 max entropy shows dramatically higher importance and dispersion compared to GSM8K (82% accuracy).

## When More Rounds Backfire

Extending deliberation from 2 to 5 rounds rarely helps and often hurts.

On AIME2025 and MATH500, most architectures show degradation. The only consistent gains occur in centralized systems, where strong orchestration enables effective aggregation over longer trajectories. Peer-based architectures (debate, hybrid) suffer from prolonged disagreement—repeated interactions without convergence amplify noise rather than refine reasoning.

Entropy dynamics explain why: key uncertainty metrics (max, mean, total entropy) drop sharply from round 1 to round 2, then remain nearly flat from round 2 to round 5. Agents largely stabilize after the second round. Adding more rounds just burns more tokens without changing the outcome.

MAS effectiveness is largely determined in the first round. Additional deliberation cannot reliably recover from initial misalignment.

## The Entropy Judger

The paper's practical contribution: a simple ensemble classifier (XGBoost + LightGBM) trained on entropy features alone.

It predicts per-sample correctness from MAS traces—without ever seeing the ground-truth answer.

Using only MAS-derived entropy features, the Entropy Judger achieves 72.6–79.1% cross-validation accuracy. Adding base model features raises this to 81.2–91.6%.

This enables label-free pass@k selection: given $k$ candidate outputs from a multi-agent run, pick the one with the highest predicted correctness. The paper reports consistent accuracy improvements across all MAS configurations and tasks.

The model doesn't need to understand the problem. It just needs to read the room—the room being entropy.

## The Honest Limits

The findings are based on open-source LLMs (LLaMA, Qwen series). Proprietary models may exhibit different uncertainty profiles, especially those with proprietary calibration or RLHF.

The Entropy Judger requires logging per-token probabilities. Not all deployed systems expose these.

Only four MAS topologies were evaluated. Other coordination structures may behave differently.

The 43.3% SAS win rate may not generalize beyond the six benchmarks studied. Harder problems requiring genuine task decomposition may still favor MAS.

## What This Changes

If you're building multi-agent systems today, one takeaway is blunt: more agents is not automatically better, and two rounds is probably enough.

The deeper shift is diagnostic. Entropy gives you a window into MAS behavior that aggregate metrics (accuracy, latency, cost) cannot provide. When your system fails, the answer isn't always "add another agent." Sometimes it's "read the room."
