*TL;DR: TFLOW shows that multi-agent LLM teams don't need to talk to each other in English — they can communicate by directly rewriting each other's weights, cutting token usage by up to 83% while staying competitive on accuracy.*

## When Your Agents Should Stop Talking

Picture three LLM agents collaborating on a math problem.

The strategist writes a paragraph of reasoning. The extractor writes another paragraph of domain knowledge.

Both paragraphs get stuffed into the executor's context window, which now has to prefill, cache, and re-encode information the models already computed internally.

That encode–decode–re-encode cycle is the bottleneck hiding in every text-based multi-agent system. TFLOW asks a simple question: what if the agents just rewired each other instead?

## The Token Bottleneck

Text is a terrible inter-agent protocol.

It forces a sender's rich internal state through a narrow token stream, then makes the receiver pay the full cost of reprocessing it — prefill latency, KV-cache memory, and generation tokens.

As you add more agents or communication rounds, the repeated read-write cycle dominates system cost.

Prior work tried to fix this by skipping tokens. CIPHER (Pham et al., 2024) lets agents swap probability-weighted output embeddings, cutting token overhead but still injecting activations into the receiver's cache. Thought Communication (Zheng et al., 2025) transmits compressed hidden states between agents — but requires representation-space compatibility between sender and receiver.

TFLOW takes a different route entirely: instead of sending information *for the receiver to read*, it sends information that *changes how the receiver computes*.

## Rewriting the Receiver

The core idea borrows from LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), which adapts a frozen model by injecting low-rank $A$ and $B$ matrices into its linear layers.

TFLOW extends LoRA from static task adaptation to dynamic, per-instance inter-agent signaling.

You start with three frozen Qwen3-4B agents sharing one backbone, each distinguished only by a role-specific system prompt: a strategist, an extractor, and an executor. The first two are senders. The third is the receiver.

Each sender runs a single forward pass on the prompted input. Its hidden states across layers are aggregated with learnable softmax weights, producing a conditioning signal $C_i$:

$$C_i = \sum_{l=0}^{L} \rho_l H_i^{(l)}$$

A parameter generator — a lightweight multi-axis Transformer with cross-attention — maps each sender's $C_i$ into a full set of LoRA factors $\{A_{i,m}^{(l)}, B_{i,m}^{(l)}\}$ targeting the receiver's attention and MLP modules.

### Fuse Perturbations, Not Factors

When multiple senders are active, their perturbations are fused with a softmax-gated mechanism before injection:

$$\Delta W_m^{(l)} = \frac{\alpha}{r} \sum_{i=1}^{N-1} \text{softmax}_i(g_w(C_i)) \; B_{i,m}^{(l)} A_{i,m}^{(l)}$$

This matters: fusing at the perturbation level (the product $BA$) rather than the factor level avoids cross-sender contamination.

The fused $\Delta W$ is injected by temporarily patching the receiver's forward pass.

After generation, the patch is discarded. The backbone stays frozen.

No context is appended. No tokens are exchanged.

## What the Numbers Say

On GSM8K, TFLOW cuts total processed tokens from 5,381 (TextMAS) to 900 — an 83% reduction — while dropping only 1.66 accuracy points (92.12% vs 93.78%).

Across all five benchmarks, token reductions range from 71% to 83%, and wall-clock inference is 2.3–4.6× faster than text-based collaboration.

TFLOW also consistently beats the single-agent baseline: +7.13 points on GSM8K (92.12% vs 84.99%), +7.98 on MATH, +7.98 on MMLU-Redux, +7.41 on MBPP+, and +8.53 on HumanEval+.

And on GSM8K, it uses fewer tokens than even a single agent working alone (900 vs 1,337).

### Why Per-Instance Conditioning Matters

A static LoRA adapter — the same perturbation for every input — also improves over the single-agent baseline.

But TFLOW outperforms that static adapter by an average of 4.29 points across the five benchmarks.

That gap is the proof that per-instance conditioning matters, not just added trainable capacity.

The LoRA fingerprints confirm this. Within-dataset cosine similarity ranges from 0.264 on MBPP+ (diverse coding problems) to 0.933 on MINERVA-MATH (templated competition problems). The perturbations are genuinely input-dependent — they vary more where instances vary more.

## The Honest Limits

The weight-space channel is much less interpretable than natural language. You cannot read a LoRA perturbation and understand what the strategist contributed.

That complicates debugging and safety auditing.

The HumanEval+ result exposes a deeper tradeoff. TFLOW lags TextMAS by 9.76 points (65.24% vs 75.00%).

The paper attributes this not to worse communication but to a hidden benefit of text: concatenated context implicitly gives the receiver more generation budget for iterative self-debugging.

TFLOW transfers collaborative knowledge through weights but does not extend the output distribution's natural length. For code synthesis, that extra reasoning room matters.

## Where This Heads

TFLOW demonstrates that model weights can serve as an executable communication medium — not a sentence to parse, not an activation to interpret, but a temporary rewiring of the receiver's computation path.

The perturbations are structurally compatible with the receiver by construction, sidestepping the representation-alignment problem that latent-space methods inherit.

The obvious next question is hybrid: can you combine short textual messages with transient weight perturbations to get both interpretability and efficiency?

Bao et al. point to this direction themselves. The weight-space channel does not have to replace text entirely. It just shows that most of what agents currently say to each other in English could be said in a language their parameters already speak.

Good friends don't just give verbal advice — they change how you think.
