## Themes

**Resource allocation beats brute force.** Every paper today argues that *where* you spend matters more than *how much*. WORC routes extra reasoning budget to the weakest agent instead of spreading it evenly. COMPASS shows that agents who search strategically outperform those who simply search more. In-Context Distillation with Self-Consistency Cascades routes only uncertain steps to the expensive teacher. The Experience Compression Spectrum argues that picking the right compression level—memory, skill, or rule—beats storing everything raw. The pattern is relentless: smarter allocation, not bigger budgets.

**Good enough isn't good enough.** COMPASS puts the starkest number on it: frontier agents produce feasible solutions 70–90% of the time but hit top-tier optimality only 20–50%. In-Context Distillation shows the ceiling can actually rise—a cheaper student with retrieved demonstrations *exceeds* teacher accuracy on ALFWorld. WORC demonstrates that uniform allocation, the "good enough" default, leaves a full point of instability on the table. Satisfying constraints and solving the user's problem are not the same thing.

**Siloed communities solve the same problems twice.** The Experience Compression Spectrum catches memory researchers and skill researchers citing each other less than 1% of the time while independently solving retrieval, conflict detection, and staleness. Instruction Inference bridges another gap: theory-of-mind benchmarks that test belief attribution in vignettes versus agents that must *act* on inferred goals. In-Context Distillation combines self-consistency and in-context learning—two well-known techniques that had never been aimed at cost reduction together. The lesson keeps repeating: talk to your neighbors.

**The weakest link shapes the system.** WORC names it explicitly—the buckets effect. But it echoes everywhere. COMPASS shows the bottleneck isn't tool use but exploration strategy, the component agents are weakest at. The Compression Spectrum maps an L3 desert where no system extracts rules from experience, the lowest compression level with the highest leverage. Instruction Inference finds that mid-tier models like Gemma-3-27B drag multi-agent collaboration scores down dramatically. Fix the短板 first.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag, one question: how do multi-agent systems actually allocate what they have?

WORC compensates the weak agent. In-Context Distillation routes only uncertain steps to the expensive model. COMPASS measures the gap between "found a valid solution" and "found the best one." The Compression Spectrum maps where knowledge should live along a compression gradient. Instruction Inference asks whether an agent can infer goals from incomplete instructions—or whether it just follows orders and hopes.

The genuine novelty today: COMPASS turning feasibility-optimality into a measurable gap, and the Compression Spectrum making the memory-versus-skill fragmentation visible with a number attached—under 1% cross-citation. Both give the field new measurement tools where it had vibes.

The incremental work: WORC's swarm-based weak-agent localization is solid engineering built on a known insight (the buckets effect). In-Context Distillation's combination of self-consistency with per-step retrieval works, but the authors are honest that it's two known tricks aimed at a new target. Neither paper will change how you think; both might change how you deploy.

The closer look: the Experience Compression Spectrum's L3 desert. No system extracts rules from experience autonomously, and RuleShaping's finding that negative constraints outperform positive directives by 7–14 percentage points is the kind of result that reshapes a research agenda. If the field takes compression seriously, L3 is where the next breakthrough lives—or where it stalls on spurious correlations.

The shortest stave, the cheapest student, the unmeasured gap—today's papers agree on one thing. The margin is always at the bottleneck.
