*TL;DR: Diffusion LLMs decode tokens in parallel at blazing speed, but when you put them in charge of an agent loop they collapse—retrying the same action over 100 times, botching every multi-turn tool call, and failing 86% of structured outputs even with guardrails. The one bright spot: they're surprisingly good at non-causal side roles like memory summarization.*

## 150 Tokens per Second—and Nowhere to Go

An LLM that decodes a whole sequence at once, clocking 150+ tokens/s on a single A800 GPU. That is the promise of diffusion-based language models: swap sequential left-to-right decoding for parallel denoising, and you shatter the latency wall that bogs down every multi-turn agent.

LLaDA and Dream showed this works on standard benchmarks.

Now hand that same model the controls of an embodied agent or a tool-calling workflow.

It spirals.

Retry loops. Malformed JSON. Near-zero task completion.

The speed is real. The agentic competence is not.

## The Speed Trap

Autoregressive LLMs pay a sequential tax. Qwen-8B and Ministral-8B emit tokens one by one, and in a 30-step ReAct loop that adds up. dLLMs like FdLLM-7B and DVar-8B bypass the tax entirely—parallel decoding gives them throughput of 151 and 167 tokens/s respectively, dwarfing Qwen's ~79.

But speed is a trap if the outputs are wrong.

On embodied tasks, FdLLM-7B and DVar-8B manage just 3.1% and 2.0% average success despite those blistering decode rates. The throughput-performance plot tells the whole story: the fastest models sit at the bottom of the graph, far from the slow-but-capable AR corner.

Efficiency without effectiveness is just expensive noise.

## Two Ways to Fail

Lu et al. test four dLLMs (LLaDA-8B, Dream-7B, FdLLM-7B, DVar-8B) against two AR baselines (Qwen-8B, Ministral-8B) on two agentic paradigms.

The failures fall into two clean categories.

### Retry-Loop Entrapment

On AgentBoard's embodied environments—AlfWorld household tasks, ScienceWorld experiments, BabyAI grid navigation—dLLMs get stuck.

The agent tries "pick up apple," the environment says "apples not found," and instead of branching to a new plan, the dLLM retries "pick up apple" again. And again. And again.

The numbers are brutal. LLaDA-8B, the strongest dLLM, manages 7.5% average success. Qwen-8B hits 45.0%.

Some dLLMs log over 100 retry loops in AlfWorld. Qwen stays around 10.

![Retry-loop failure counts across models—dLLMs tower above 100 loops while AR models stay near 10]({{ "/assets/figures/5ef493e46936/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

The ReAct framework—interleaving thoughts and actions to enable replanning—simply does not survive the parallel denoising process. dLLMs overwrite their own tentative plans at each denoising step, losing the causal thread that lets an agent say "that didn't work, let me try something else."

### When the Brackets Don't Line Up

On BFCL-v3's tool-calling benchmark, the picture is even starker.

No dLLM solves a single multi-turn test instance. Zero.

Qwen-8B's overall tool-calling average is 57.8%. Its multi-turn numbers are lower but still functional. The dLLMs are not functional at all.

![Multi-turn tool-calling success rates—every dLLM bar sits at 0% while Qwen-8B reaches double digits]({{ "/assets/figures/5ef493e46936/fig_002_02.png" | relative_url }})

The root cause: parallel denoising treats every token position independently during early diffusion steps. When the model needs to emit strict JSON—`{"function": "search", "parameters": {"query": "..."}}`—conditionally independent token predictions produce mismatched braces, hallucinated parameters, and broken schemas.

JSON schema errors and parameter/value errors dominate dLLM failure modes.

Even combining three schema guardrails—syntax checking, type enforcement, required-field validation—recovers only 14% of outputs to semantic correctness. 86% still fail.

## Why Parallel Decoding Breaks Agency

The common thread is causal dependency.

In an agent loop, each action depends on the observation it provoked. In a tool call, each JSON bracket depends on the bracket before it. dLLMs' conditional independence assumption—the very thing that enables parallelism—dissolves these chains.

This is not a new insight for non-autoregressive generation. Wu et al. (2020) showed it causes "uncoordinated" structural predictions in slot filling. Ding et al. (2021) found lexical choice errors in machine translation.

The same pathology surfaces at a larger scale: without a left-to-right causal scaffold, the model cannot commit to a partial plan or a partial data structure. It denoises everywhere at once, and the left hand never knows what the right is doing.

## Can We Patch It?

The paper throws every available fix at the problem.

**dLLM decoding optimizations.** Adaptive Parallel Decoding (APD), Discrete Diffusion Forcing (D2F), and Deferred Commitment Decoding (DCD) all improve over vanilla decoding. DCD lifts FdLLM-7B's AlfWorld success from 3.3% to 10.4%. But ScienceWorld and BabyAI show only limited gains; the gap to Qwen-8B remains large.

**Agent-level strategies.** AR self-refine raises embodied success from 0.7% to 1.5%. Step-wise AR feedback pushes it further to 2.2%. Helpful, but nowhere near closing the gap.

**Schema guardrails.** Already covered: 86% still fail with all three combined.

No patch overturns the core finding. The non-causal, fuzzy nature of dLLM denoising is not a surface defect. It is architectural.

## Where dLLMs Actually Help: DiffuAgent

If dLLMs cannot drive the agent loop, can they at least ride shotgun?

The paper introduces DiffuAgent, a modular framework that plugs dLLMs into four cognitive roles alongside an AR agent: memory summarizer, early-exit verifier, tool selector, and format editor.

Two roles shine. Two roles do not.

### Memory Summarization—A Win

LLaDA-8B as a memory module with Qwen-8B as the agent achieves 40.5% average embodied success. Qwen-8B with its own memory scores 34.9%.

The dLLM compresses long interaction history every 5 steps, and its parallel decoding is harmless here—summarization is a non-causal, "read everything, write a digest" task where token independence is a feature, not a bug.

Dream-7B, FdLLM-7B, and DVar-8B all match or nearly match the AR memory baseline.

### Early-Exit Verification—Conservative but Reliable

AR verifiers tend to exit too early, aggressively pruning trajectories but bleeding progress. dLLM verifiers are more conservative: fewer redundant steps removed, but less progress degradation.

Their global, non-causal view of the full trajectory makes them better judges of "is this agent actually stuck?" rather than "has it been stuck long enough?"

### Tool Selection—Viable; Format Editing—Not

As a tool selector, LLaDA-8B and Dream-7B filter large tool libraries down to relevant subsets and perform comparably to AR baselines. Selection is another non-causal matching task.

But as a format editor—post-processing malformed JSON into schema-compliant output—most dLLMs degrade performance. The very structural noise that breaks their tool calls makes them unreliable editors of other models' tool calls.

## The Honest Limits

All tested dLLMs are under 10B parameters. Task-specific fine-tuning or reinforcement learning was not applied; targeted training might shift the numbers, though prior work suggests simple fine-tuning behaves more like behavior cloning and does not substantially improve agentic capability.

DiffuAgent assumes a fixed pipeline with dLLMs as auxiliary modules. An end-to-end diffusion-native agent architecture—one that co-designs the agent loop around parallel denoising—remains untested. The authors acknowledge this explicitly: their framework is a diagnostic, not a final architecture.

And the benchmarks cover only embodied tasks from AgentBoard and tool-calling from BFCL v1–v3. Web-based agentic tasks, open-ended reasoning, and other domains could tell a different story—or could tell the same one.

Let the AR model drive. Let the dLLM ride shotgun.
