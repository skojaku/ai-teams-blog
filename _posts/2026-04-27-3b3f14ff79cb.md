*TL;DR: Making LLM agents pay to speak—through a VCG auction where bids equal predicted value density—sets new SOTA accuracy on seven reasoning benchmarks while cutting token costs 4–7×.*

## Pay to Speak

Put three LLM agents in a room and they will talk each other into the ground.

Progressive-Hint Prompting (PHP) burns 26 million tokens on GSM8K. DyLAN eats 14 million. The conversations are long, redundant, and mostly noise.

Nobody is deciding who actually has something worth saying.

DALA flips the premise. Communication bandwidth is a scarce resource, and agents should have to bid for it. The result is Dynamic Auction-based Language Agent—a framework that runs a centralized auction every round. Only the agents with the highest-value, most cost-efficient messages get to speak. Everyone else falls silent.

## How Much Is a Word Worth?

The key quantity is *value density*, $\rho$—marginal utility per token of length.

$$\rho_i(m, o_i^{(t)}) = \frac{v_i(m, o_i^{(t)}) - \bar{v}_t}{\sigma_{v_t} + \epsilon} \cdot \frac{1}{L(m)}$$

Here $v_i$ is a learned message-conditioned value function predicting the team's expected return if this message broadcasts. The $z$-score normalization keeps bids comparable across agents. Dividing by message length $L(m)$ punishes verbosity directly.

An agent's bid is simply $b_i = \max(0, \rho_i)$. Negative-value messages never enter the auction at all.

This is the Information Bottleneck principle in action: agents learn to compress their private knowledge into the smallest message that still maximizes team utility.

## From Full Text to Silence

Bid in hand, DALA doesn't just gate *who* speaks—it scales *how much* they say. A tiered content strategy maps value density to output granularity:

- $\rho \geq \tau_{\text{full}}$: full detailed answer
- $\tau_{\text{summary}} \leq \rho < \tau_{\text{full}}$: compressed summary
- $\tau_{\text{keywords}} \leq \rho < \tau_{\text{summary}}$: keywords only
- $\rho \leq 0$: strategic silence

When the budget is generous, most messages land in the full-text tier. When tokens are scarce, agents shift toward keywords and silence—compressing or withholding information that isn't worth the cost.

## How the Auction Runs

Each round is a combinatorial auction. Multiple agents can win speaking rights simultaneously, but their combined message lengths must stay under a hard per-round budget. The auctioneer solves a 0/1 knapsack problem to pick the winning set $W_t$:

$$W_t = \arg\max_{S \subseteq B_{\text{valid}}} \sum_{a_j \in S} b_j^{(t)} \quad \text{s.t.} \quad \sum_{a_j \in S} L(m_j^{(t)}) \leq B_{\max}^{(t)}$$

Winners pay via the VCG rule—each agent's payment equals the harm its presence causes to others' total value. This makes truthful bidding the dominant strategy. Agents that don't win pay nothing and stay silent.

The whole system—actor network, critic network, value function—is trained end-to-end with MAPPO. The reward blends task progress with the VCG payment penalty:

$$r_i^{(t)} = \alpha \cdot \Delta_{\text{task}}^{(t)} - \beta \cdot p_i^{(t)} \cdot \mathbb{1}[a_i \in W_t]$$

That payment term teaches agents not to overbid on low-value messages. Ablate it ($\beta = 0$) and token consumption nearly doubles, with accuracy dropping by up to 7.9 points.

## The Scoreboard

DALA sweeps seven benchmarks: 84.32% on MMLU, 96.18% on GSM8K, 97.87% on MultiArith, 92.33% on SVAMP, 80.92% on AQuA, 91.21% on HumanEval pass@1, and 83.42% on MATH-500. All new SOTA numbers.

The efficiency story is even sharper. On GSM8K, DALA uses roughly $6.2 \times 10^6$ tokens against PHP's $2.60 \times 10^7$—a 4.2× reduction. On MMLU, DALA consumes $3.80 \times 10^5$ tokens versus PHP's $2.60 \times 10^6$, a 6.8× saving.

The prior SOTA pruning method, AgentPrune-R, trims communication links based on random graph topology. It has no value-driven basis for deciding *who* should speak. DALA replaces that random pruning with principled economic allocation—and edges out AgentPrune-R by 0.35 points on GSM8K while using fewer tokens.

## Strategic Silence Emerges

Under a tight budget ($1 \times 10^5$ tokens), agents choose silence 33–40% of the time across domains. In STEM questions, keywords dominate at 48.9%. Full-text drops to as low as 2.1%.

Flip to a generous budget ($1 \times 10^6$ tokens) and the pattern reverses. Full-text surges to roughly 45–49% in three of four domains (STEM stays at 30.6%), and silence shrinks to roughly 3–6%.

Agents aren't following a fixed policy. They dynamically adapt how much they say to how much the market will bear.

![Bar chart showing communication strategy distributions under low versus high token budgets—silence and keywords dominate when tokens are scarce, while full-text prevails under generous budgets]({{ "/assets/figures/3b3f14ff79cb/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

The value network learns this fast. By epoch 160 of training, the predicted value for critical information has pulled sharply away from non-critical filler. That learned appraisal drives the market behavior.

Ablation confirms the architecture. Removing the learned value function—the core of intelligent bidding—drops GSM8K accuracy by 9.83 points and inflates token usage to $4.13 \times 10^6$.

Swapping value density for raw value (dropping the $1/L(m)$ term) costs 6.42 points. Every component earns its place.

## The Honest Limits

All experiments run on gpt-4-1106-preview across reasoning benchmarks—math, MMLU, code generation. Whether the auction scales to dozens of agents or open-ended tasks like software engineering is an open question. The VCG winner-determination problem is NP-hard in general, though dynamic programming handles moderate agent counts fine today.

Training the value network requires 2% of each benchmark as an optimization set—a task-specific warm-up phase. And the framework assumes information is distributed across agents so that no single agent can solve the problem alone; if your agents all see the same inputs, the auction has less to work with.

One note on the numbers: the paper's consolidated comparison table reports DALA's MMLU token consumption as $3.80 \times 10^5$, while the body text cites $1.81 \times 10^5$. The efficiency claims above use the table figures, which are the more conservative and systematic source.

## The Bigger Bet

The deeper claim is that *free communication is the bug, not the feature*. Every existing multi-agent LLM framework—DyLAN's dynamic networks, PHP's progressive hints, LLM-Debate's back-and-forth—assumes more dialogue is better. DALA shows the opposite: when you make bandwidth scarce and let a market allocate it, agents learn when to shut up, and the team gets smarter.

That "strategic silence" isn't hardcoded. It emerges from economic incentives. The auction doesn't just prune low-value messages—it teaches agents to *recognize* low-value messages before they generate them.

Making agents pay to speak really does make them say something worth hearing.
