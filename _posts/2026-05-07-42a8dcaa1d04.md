*TL;DR: Uno-Orchestra is a single 7B causal LM that learns both *whether* to decompose a task and *who* should handle each subtask—beating the strongest workflow baseline by 16% at an order-of-magnitude lower cost.*

Seven billion parameters, ten cents a query, 77% accuracy.

That is the Uno-Orchestra dot on the cost–accuracy scatter plot: higher and further left than 22 baselines, including systems that spend twelve times as much per query.

The key is a single causal LM that learns both *whether* to decompose a task and *who* handles each piece—two decisions every prior system optimizes in isolation.

## The Two Traps

Flat routers pick one model per query.

Fast and cheap, but they miss the parallel structure of genuinely complex tasks. A multi-hop reasoning question is not one call—it is several.

Hierarchical orchestrators decompose *everything*.

They spawn sub-agents and aggregate outputs, which is great for hard tasks but wasteful on easy ones. A simple factual lookup should cost one cheap call, not a planning ritual.

The two decisions that matter most—*how deeply to decompose* and *which worker to route each piece to*—have always been optimized separately. Uno-Orchestra collapses them into a single policy.

## One Policy, Two Decisions

The core idea: one causal language model emits both the decomposition plan and the routing assignment in the same forward pass.

![Architecture diagram showing the central router deciding between direct dispatch and task decomposition, with the causal-LM policy jointly emitting subtask plans and (model, primitive) routing pairs in one forward pass]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

At each turn the controller either answers directly—zero dispatch cost—or produces a plan over $K_t$ subtasks. Each subtask is tagged with a routing pair $(m, s) \in \mathcal{P}$, where $m$ is a worker model and $s$ is a *primitive*: a closed-vocabulary action spanning direct answers, chain-of-thought reasoning, multi-step skills, and tool calls.

The joint distribution factorises along the token order:

$$\pi_\theta(a_t \mid h_t) = \underbrace{\pi_\theta(\text{plan}_t \mid h_t)}_{\text{decompose}} \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{K_t} \underbrace{\pi_\theta(p_{t,k} \mid h_t, \text{plan}_t, p_{t,<k})}_{\text{route}}$$

No auxiliary heads. No per-stage loss weights.

The factorisation falls out of left-to-right causal masking.

Selectivity is structural: easy queries collapse to one direct-answer turn. Only genuinely compositional tasks pay for orchestration.

## Learning to Be Cheap

Training has two stages. Both are grounded in real worker interactions—not simulated traces.

![Training pipeline showing the verifier-gated curriculum splitting queries into an SFT pool of teacher-solvable residue and an RL pool of genuinely hard tail, followed by Agentic-GRPO refinement]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

**Stage 1 — SFT on a verifier-gated curriculum.** Starting from a cold-start router $\pi^{(0)}$ and a strong teacher $\pi^\star$, everything the cold-start already solves is discarded. What remains is split by teacher outcome:

$$\mathcal{D}_\text{SFT} = \{(q, \tau^\star(q)) : b^{(0)} = 0,\; b^\star = 1\}, \qquad \mathcal{D}_\text{RL} = \{q : b^{(0)} = 0,\; b^\star = 0\}$$

SFT clones the teacher on solvable residue. RL explores the genuinely hard tail.

61,201 trajectories from 38 datasets, with code and tool subtasks executed in real sandboxes.

**Stage 2 — Agentic-GRPO on $\mathcal{D}_\text{RL}$.** Vanilla GRPO folds an entire rollout into one trajectory-level return, leaving early routing choices unsupervised.

Agentic-GRPO routes terminal reward back to each turn, augmented with bounded per-turn shaping ($\eta \ll 1$) that cannot overpower the verifier signal.

The turn-level return and advantage:

$$\tilde{R}_{i,t} = \gamma^{T_i - t} R(\tau_i) + \sum_{s=t}^{T_i} \gamma^{s-t} r_{i,s}, \qquad \hat{A}_{i,t} = \frac{\tilde{R}_{i,t} - \mu_{q,t}}{\sigma_{q,t} + \varepsilon}$$

This is the credit-assignment fix that makes multi-turn orchestration learnable. Each decision—decompose, route, repair—gets its own gradient signal.

One more trick: during RL, worker identities are hidden. The policy sees only WORKER 1 … WORKER $K$, with the mapping resampled each episode.

Drop the blind-worker protocol and cost inflates fivefold ($0.16 → $0.82). The router exploits brand-name shortcuts instead of profiling capability.

## What They Found

Across 13 benchmarks spanning math, code, knowledge, long-context reading, and agentic tool use, Uno-Orchestra hits **77.0% macro pass@1** and 81.7% pass@2—roughly 16% above AgentOrchestra, the strongest workflow baseline.

The cost number is the punch line: **$0.10 per query**, compared to $1.21 for AgentOrchestra and $0.99 for AOrchestra.

![Scatter plot of macro pass@1 versus per-query billed cost across 22 baselines, with Uno-Orchestra occupying the favorable high-accuracy low-cost corner]({{ "/assets/figures/42a8dcaa1d04/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

An order-of-magnitude reduction, at higher accuracy.

The gains are lopsided in the best way.

On MATH-500, Uno-Orchestra reaches 91.9%—a +12.5% relative gain over the best baseline. On ToolBench it posts a +5.1% relative gain.

The orchestrator is not just cheaper. It is decisively better where compositionality matters most.

Each training stage tightens the loop: SFT stabilises the policy, GRPO sharpens routing, tree rollouts add search coverage, and Agentic-GRPO trims redundant dispatches via turn-level credit.

The last step *reduces* cost while raising accuracy—a sign that credit assignment removes lazy subtask invocations faster than it adds useful ones.

## Where It Strains

The worker pool is closed: nine hand-curated commercial models spanning two orders of magnitude in price. We do not know how the policy degrades when new workers join incrementally without retraining.

The router backbone is Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct. A 4B controller nearly matches after SFT but falls behind after RL, especially on agentic tasks.

Controller capacity is a credit-assignment bottleneck over long horizons, not merely an instruction-following one.

Evaluation covers broad capability axes, but the paper does not test cross-lingual generalization or distributional drift of the kind a production deployment would face.

## Why This Matters

Uno-Orchestra demonstrates that a 7B router can jointly learn decomposition depth and worker dispatch across heterogeneous commercial models—pushing the accuracy–efficiency frontier without a proprietary frontier planner.

The blind-worker protocol and verifier-gated curriculum are portable recipes. Plug them into any learned orchestrator.

The deeper lesson is that *selectivity* is the missing primitive. Current systems either always decompose or never decompose.

A policy that can decide "this one is easy—answer directly" and "that one needs three subtasks routed to three different workers" under a single objective captures the real shape of the cost–accuracy trade-off.

The router that knows when *not* to route is the router that saves you money.

Parsimony, it turns out, is a learned skill.
