*TL;DR: A 26B open-weight model matches GPT-5 on a rigorous agentic tool-use benchmark at roughly one-fifteenth the cost per passed task — but only up to a point, and the capability cliff comes earlier than you'd expect.*

A 26-billion-parameter model running on a Mac Studio passes the same share of agentic tool-use tasks as GPT-5 — at one-fifteenth the cost per passed task.

The catch: that parity holds until you ask the model to plan ahead, and then it falls off a cliff.

Karmakar and Chatterjee built AgentFloor to map exactly where that cliff is.

## You're Overpaying for Most Agent Calls

Production agent systems make many model calls per user request. Most of those calls are short, structured, and operationally simple — a search, a lookup, a record extraction, a single submission.

The default is to route every single call to the most expensive frontier model.

Nobody has a principled map of which pipeline stages actually need frontier intelligence. So teams over-provision by habit.

The cost difference compounds. A 3B open-weight model (granite4:3b) passes tasks at $0.00046 each on a Mac across all tiers — 71× cheaper than GPT-5 at posted rates.

But nobody routes to it, because nobody knew where it was safe to do so.

## Building the Ladder

AgentFloor is a deterministic 30-task benchmark organized as a six-tier capability ladder over eight abstract tools and an in-memory fixture database. No live APIs, no filesystem, no web, no contamination risk.

The design strips away environmental noise and asks: once you isolate the cognitive demand, where do small models succeed and where do they fall off?

Each tier stacks one new demand on top of the previous ones:

- **A0** — instruction following, no tools
- **A** — single tool call
- **B** — sequential 2-tool chain (first tool's output feeds the second)
- **C** — branch on an intermediate result
- **D** — multi-source synthesis with conflict recovery
- **E** — long-horizon planning under persistent constraints

Step budgets scale with tier: 1 at A0, up to 10 at E. Every task ships with a canonical prompt and four paraphrase variants. One Python runner, byte-identical system prompts, temperature zero — across all 17 models.

Sixteen open-weight models from 0.27B to 32B parameters. GPT-5 as the frontier anchor. 16,542 scored runs total.

Earlier work laid pieces of this foundation. API-Bank (Li et al., 2023) introduced graded tool-use difficulty across three levels; AgentFloor extends it to a controlled six-tier ladder with deterministic tooling.

τ-bench (Yao et al., 2024) brought pass$^k$ reliability metrics to agentic evaluation; AgentFloor adds paired bootstrap confidence intervals and pre-registered TOST equivalence testing, which most benchmarks omit.

FrugalGPT (Chen et al., 2023) demonstrated that cost-quality routing is viable; AgentFloor provides the static capability-and-cost prior that routing systems need but don't have — which tiers can be safely offloaded.

## Where Small Models Win

The aggregate headline is stark.

Across all 30 tasks paired between gemma4:26b and GPT-5 (270 paired observations), the two are equivalent at the pre-registered ±10 pp margin:

$$\Delta = +0.4 \text{ pp}, \quad 90\% \text{ CI } [-4.0, +5.1]$$

The same data certify equivalence at any margin $\geq 5.1$ pp. This is not a tie in the informal sense — it is a formal TOST equivalence verdict.

At matched $\sim$60% aggregate task completion rate, gemma4:26b on a Mac Studio runs at $\$0.0022$ per passed task. GPT-5 runs at $\$0.0327$.

That is 15× cheaper on self-hosted hardware, 3× cheaper on cloud GPU, and 2.5× faster per passed task.

The tier-by-tier view is where the routing recipes live. On A0 (no tools), gemma4:26b strictly outperforms GPT-5: 100% vs 80%. On A (single tool), it is formally equivalent.

A sub-5B model clears the 80% reliability bar on both tiers — nemotron-3-nano:4b on A0, ministral-3:3b on A — at $\$0.00007$ to $\$0.0002$ per passed task, between half a second and 1.4 seconds latency, Mac-hostable.

The Pareto frontier of cost-per-passed-task is occupied by open-weight models at *every* reliability point in the corpus. GPT-5 sits alone in the expensive upper-right corner.

## The Capability Cliff

The transition from B to C is the steepest single column-step in the entire capability heatmap.

Tier B — sequential 2-tool chains — clears at a 2B model (qwen3.5:2b) under the 70% reliability bar. No open-weight model in the corpus reaches the 80% bar on B.

Tiers C, D, and E never clear at any threshold between 60% and 90% for any zero-shot open-weight model. The cliff between B and C is a qualitative break that survives any reasonable threshold choice within that range.

On B, C, and D, point estimates for gemma4:26b remain within 9 pp of GPT-5 across all three tiers. But per-tier paired sample size ($n = 45$) yields confidence intervals 24–35 pp wide — too wide to certify equivalence at the ±10 pp margin.

The data are not informative enough for a formal verdict. They are descriptive, not declarative.

On E — long-horizon planning under persistent constraints — GPT-5 is strictly superior: $\Delta = -10.2$ pp, 95% CI $[-18.4, -2.0]$.

The absolute numbers are humbling for both sides. GPT-5 passes 10% of E-tier tasks. The best open-weight cell on E is ministral-3:8b at 16%. Neither frontier nor open-weight reaches practitioner-relevant reliability on long-horizon planning.

## Same Score, Different Breakpoint

Aggregate equivalence hides a deep asymmetry. GPT-5 and gemma4:26b land at indistinguishable overall TCR, but they fail in categorically different ways on tier E.

GPT-5's E-tier failure mass: **F5 39%, F5b 24%, F7 18%, F4 8%, F1 0%**.

The frontier engages with the task, plans, partially executes, then *resigns*. It has no trouble finding the right tools — it just gives up. Hallucinated tool calls do not appear.

Gemma4:26b's E-tier failure mass: **F4 41%, F1 36%, F5 23%, F5b 0%**.

The mid-scale model exhausts its step budget on four runs in ten and invents tool names that don't exist in another third. It is running out of room and grasping for tools that aren't there.

The same TCR cell hides different bottlenecks. This matters enormously for anyone trying to close the gap, because the right fix depends on which failure regime you're in.

## Why No Single Fix Works

The authors ran four targeted ablations on the residual long-horizon gap. Three helped exactly one model on one cell. One hurt every model they tried.

**Explicit-submission prompt.** Adding "you MUST call submit_decision" to the system prompt lifted ministral-3:8b on task E1 from 0% to 100% — a +100 pp jump.

The model already executed the correct tool chain; it just emitted a prose answer instead of calling the submission tool. The prompt converted an implicit answer into an explicit one. On four other models, the same prompt did nothing — they fail before reaching the submission step, so there is nothing to convert.

**Doubled step budget.** Giving GPT-5 twice the max_steps on D and E moved only E1 ($1/9 \to 4/9$, +33 pp). Every other D/E task terminated well within budget; those failures are resignation (F5), not exhaustion (F4). More steps do not help a model that has already given up.

**Reasoning mode toggle.** Disabling reasoning on Qwen3's 32B model helped tier B by +12 pp — the model stopped burning output budget on chain-of-thought and completed the 2-tool chain instead.

But switching reasoning *off* hurt the 8B ($-9$ pp) and 14B ($-12$ pp) variants on the same tier. Reasoning is a deployment knob, not a capability axis.

**Structured prompt.** A plan/execute/submit phase decomposition was the obvious candidate for a universal F5 reducer — tell the model to plan first, then execute, then submit.

It regressed every model tested. Ministral-3:8b dropped −33 pp overall. Trace inspection showed the model complying with "Do NOT call any tools yet" in the PLAN phase, then emitting a prose answer without ever entering EXECUTE. The intervention designed to reduce early resignation *produced more of it*.

No universal lever emerged. Each effective intervention addressed one model's specific failure mode and was null on the others. The obvious universal candidate made things worse.

## Where to Route

AgentFloor provides the first controlled, tier-graded capability map that directly answers the deployment question: which parts of an agent pipeline can be routed to small models, and which still need the frontier?

For the lower tiers, the answer is unambiguous. Instruction following and single-tool use can go to sub-5B models at the 80% reliability bar, at negligible cost and roughly one-second latency on a Mac. Sequential 2-tool chains can go to a 2B model at the 70% bar.

Route the broad base of routine actions to open-weight; reserve the frontier for the narrower class that demands deeper planning.

For C, D, and E, the honest answer is that no zero-shot configuration in this corpus clears even 60%. Deployment there requires accepting reduced reliability, finding a model-specific intervention, or paying for the frontier API. And even the frontier only reaches 10% on E.

The failure-mode asymmetry gives system designers specific attack surfaces per model family. If your agent is built on GPT-5, the bottleneck on long-horizon tasks is resignation — the model engages then quits. If it's built on gemma4:26b, the bottleneck is exhaustion and hallucination — the model runs out of room and invents tools. Different failure regimes call for different fixes.

## The Honest Limits

GPT-5 is the only frontier anchor. There is no second flagship to cross-validate the per-tier verdicts.

Per-tier paired sample sizes ($n = 45$) are too small to certify equivalence on B, C, or D at the pre-registered margin — those verdicts are descriptive, not formal.

Each tier contains only five tasks.

AgentFloor measures native tool-calling in a deterministic abstract-tool environment, not end-to-end performance with real APIs, GUI grounding, or prompt-based tool emulation.

There is no human baseline. Some failure mass, especially early resignation, may reflect compliance or prompting effects rather than hard capability limits.

Three cells in the corpus resist clean explanation.

The highest tier-D cell belongs to a 4B model, not the 26B — multi-source synthesis does not scale monotonically with parameter count.

Ministral-3:14b is dominated cell by cell by its 8B sibling.

Mistral-small3.2:24b reaches 96% on A0 then collapses to 16% on B.

Parameter count alone is a poor predictor of agentic capability across model families.

The small models can climb most of the ladder. They just fall off at the same rung every time — right between chaining two tools and branching on the result.
