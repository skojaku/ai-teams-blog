*TL;DR: SBD frames multi-agent delegation as a bilevel optimization problem where a learned meta-weight network continuously tunes the safety–efficiency trade-off at runtime, with a formal guarantee that higher contextual risk automatically produces safer delegation policies.*

## A Patient Is Crashing

A patient's acuity score is climbing. A clinical AI agent needs to hand a treatment decision to a specialist sub-agent.

How much authority should transfer—and what guarantee do you have that the answer gets *more cautious* as the situation gets worse?

Current safety mechanisms for multi-agent systems don't have a good answer. Constrained Policy Optimization enforces safety constraints at training time but freezes them before deployment. Multi-agent architecture search picks a topology at design time and locks it in. Human-in-the-loop checkpoints sit at fixed intervals, regardless of whether the current moment is calm or chaotic.

Yuan Sun's Safe Bilevel Delegation (SBD) proposes something different: a closed-loop system where the safety knob turns itself.

## Make the Trade-Off Learned, Not Fixed

Delegation authority should be a learned, context-sensitive scalar—not a binary permission flag or a fixed design choice.

SBD introduces a *delegation degree* $\alpha \in [0,1]$ that smoothly interpolates between full human override ($\alpha = 0$) and fully autonomous sub-agent execution ($\alpha = 1$).

The twist: $\alpha$ is not set by hand. A meta-weight network $\phi$ observes the current state $s$ and outputs $\lambda_\phi(s) \in [0,1]$, the weight placed on the safety loss relative to the efficiency loss. When contextual risk rises, $\lambda$ goes up, which forces $\alpha$ down, which reduces the sub-agent's autonomy.

The system self-regulates.

Think of it like network congestion control. OSPF dynamically redistributes traffic away from congested links while maintaining connectivity. SBD redistributes *decision authority* away from autonomous agents when safety risk is elevated—while keeping task throughput as high as conditions allow.

## How the Optimization Stacks

SBD formalizes this as a two-level optimization.

**Outer problem.** Learn $\phi$ to minimize the combined safety–efficiency loss across states:

$$\min_\phi \; \mathbb{E}_s\Big[\lambda_\phi(s) \cdot L_{\text{safety}}(\pi^*_\phi) + (1 - \lambda_\phi(s)) \cdot L_{\text{eff}}(\pi^*_\phi)\Big]$$

The outer loop decides *how much to care about safety* in each state. It's a meta-learning problem: $\phi$ learns when to be cautious.

**Inner problem.** Given a fixed $\phi$, find the optimal delegation policy under a probabilistic safety constraint:

$$\pi^*_\phi = \arg\min_\pi \; \mathbb{E}_s\Big[\lambda_\phi(s) L_{\text{safety}}(\pi) + (1 - \lambda_\phi(s)) L_{\text{eff}}(\pi)\Big] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad P(\text{safe})(\pi, s) \geq 1 - \delta \;\; \forall s$$

The inner loop finds the best delegation policy given the current safety weighting, with a hard floor $\delta$ on unsafe decisions.

There's a deliberate asymmetry. The outer loop *modulates* the trade-off before the constraint becomes binding.

Constrained MDP theory (Altman, 1999) gives you the constraint framework. SBD extends it: before you hit the constraint, the learned $\lambda_\phi(s)$ has already tilted the objective toward safety.

CPO (Achiam et al., 2017) cannot do this—it treats the safety weight as fixed at training time, with no mechanism for runtime re-weighting based on observed context.

## Walking Through the Guarantees

Three results make SBD's formal bones.

### Safety Monotonicity (Theorem 4.4)

If $\lambda_\phi(s) \leq \lambda_{\phi'}(s)$ for all states $s$, then $P(\text{safe})(\pi^*_\phi, s) \leq P(\text{safe})(\pi^*_{\phi'}, s)$ everywhere.

Higher safety weight $\Rightarrow$ weakly safer inner policy. Not just in expectation—pointwise, for every state.

This is the closed-loop property: raise $\lambda$ and safety goes up. The proof walks through Danskin's theorem and the KKT conditions of the inner problem to show that increasing $\lambda$ shifts the optimal policy toward safer decisions at every state.

### Inner Policy Convergence (Theorem 4.6)

Projected gradient descent on the inner problem converges linearly:

$$\|\pi_t - \pi^*_\phi\|^2 \leq (1 - \eta\mu)^t \|\pi_0 - \pi^*_\phi\|^2$$

Under standard $L$-smoothness and $\mu$-strong convexity assumptions. The condition number $L/\mu$ determines the speed.

The authors plan $T_{\text{in}} = 50$ inner steps and estimate that a full 500-outer-iteration run finishes in under seven minutes on a single A100—but the paper explicitly states that no empirical results have been reported yet. The convergence rate is a theorem under convexity; whether it holds in practice with neural-network policies remains to be seen.

### Accountability Propagation (Proposition 4.8)

In a delegation chain of length $k$ with maximum delegation degree $\bar{\alpha} = \max_\ell \alpha_{i_\ell}$, no single agent can bear full accountability:

$$\max_j w_j \leq 1 - (1 - \bar{\alpha})^k$$

When $\bar{\alpha} < 1$, this bound is strictly less than 1. As chains grow longer and delegation degrees approach 1, accountability diffuses—it accumulates but never concentrates entirely on one agent.

This gives a formal auditing tool for multi-agent incidents: after something goes wrong, the accountability weights pinpoint which agent in the chain bears the largest share of responsibility.

## How It Runs

Algorithm 1 alternates inner and outer updates.

The inner loop runs projected gradient descent on $\pi$ for $T_{\text{in}} = 50$ steps, projecting $\alpha$ onto the $\delta$-safe feasible set after each update. In the medical domain, this projection is a simple clip: $\alpha \leftarrow \min(\alpha, \alpha_{\max}(s))$, where $\alpha_{\max}$ drops to 0.70 when the patient's APACHE-II proxy exceeds 20.

The outer loop computes a hypergradient through the inner solution via approximate implicit differentiation—standard bilevel practice following Lorraine et al. (2020). Each outer step requires $T_{\text{in}}$ inner forward-backward passes plus one additional pass for the hypergradient, running 500 outer iterations total for an estimated wall-clock time under seven minutes on a single A100.

MaAS (Zhang et al., 2025) learned the agent topology. SBD is orthogonal: it can be layered on top of any fixed architecture, including one found by MaAS, to provide runtime safety guarantees that MaAS's design-time optimization cannot offer.

## Planned, Not Yet Proven

This is a pre-registered protocol paper. No empirical results yet. The theorems are proven; the experiments are specified but not executed.

Three domains are targeted:

- **Medical AI** on MIMIC-III (58,976 ICU admissions). Safety means no drug-allergy conflicts and $\alpha \leq 0.70$ for high-acuity patients. Efficiency means matching attending physician decisions.
- **Financial risk control** on S&P 500 data (2010–2023, with a COVID stress test in early 2020). Safety means no single-asset concentration above 10% and $\alpha \leq 0.80$ during high volatility.
- **Educational supervision** on ASSISTments (525,534 interactions). Safety means no content more than 2 standard deviations above a student's estimated knowledge, and $\alpha \leq 0.60$ for at-risk students.

The falsifiability criteria are sharp.

SBD is considered empirically falsified on a domain if it doesn't beat every non-ablated baseline on Safety–Efficiency Area.

Or if the monotonicity sweep yields Spearman rank correlation below 0.9.

Or if removing the outer loop causes less than 1% SEA drop—meaning the meta-weight network is doing no real work.

## Where It Hits Limits

The convergence proof assumes $\mu$-strong convexity of the inner loss. When $\pi$ is a deep neural network, that assumption may not hold.

The authors state that convergence appears linear in practice, but given that no experiments have been executed yet, the empirical convergence speed remains an open question. The non-convex extension is future work.

SBD takes the safety constraint set $C$ as given. It doesn't learn $C$ jointly with $\phi$ and $\pi$.

It also doesn't model the internal safety of sub-agent execution. A safely delegated task can still be executed unsafely.

No empirical results exist yet. The theorems are proven; the experiments are a promise with pre-registered falsifiability criteria.

The gap between "the math works" and "it works on MIMIC-III" is where this framework will live or die.

The accountability propagation bound carries a subtle message. As chains grow and $\alpha$ approaches 1, responsibility diffuses but never fully concentrates anywhere.

That's a formal statement about a real governance problem—long agent chains make accountability inherently diffuse—and SBD makes that statement precise rather than pretending it away.

When risk rises, the safety knob should turn itself.
