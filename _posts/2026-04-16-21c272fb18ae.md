*TL;DR: Andric shows that stronger native reasoning makes LLM agents worse at simulating boundedly rational negotiation — every run collapses into authority-imposed decisions — while a simple structured notepad recovers compromise trajectories across three domains and four model families.*

## 45 Out Of 45

GPT-5.2 with native reasoning ran 45 multi-agent negotiations across three different scenarios.

Every single one ended the same way: an authority-imposed decision. No compromises. No concessions that stuck.

Now run the same model with a five-field private ledger — a bounded notepad tracking concessions, opponent reads, and open issues — and compromise outcomes reappear in every environment. Not fully, especially in the trading-limits cases (5 of 15 and 7 of 15), but they surface where zero existed before. Same model. Same task. Fundamentally different behavioral distribution.

That gap is the paper. Sandro Andric calls it the *solver-sampler mismatch*: the capabilities that make a model a good strategic solver are precisely the ones that destroy the bounded-rational variation a simulation needs.

## The Wrong Question

LLM agents are now standard tools in synthetic societies, policy simulations, and economic games. Park et al.'s generative agents showed that language models can produce plausible social behavior at scale. Horton's work on LLM-as-economic-simulator extended that logic — if models can stand in for human decision-makers, whole economic worlds become simulable.

The implicit bet: stronger reasoning → more realistic behavior.

Andric shows that bet can lose. The question "which model is smartest?" is the wrong one for simulation. The right question is: which model best matches the behavioral target?

A good solver seeks dominance, consistency, and efficiency.

A good sampler preserves bounded-rational variation — it lets agents concede late, misread leverage, settle on suboptimal but plausible arrangements, or fail to converge at all.

When those messy trajectories disappear, your simulation isn't cleaner. It's less faithful.

## How the Mismatch Takes Hold

The paper studies three multi-agent negotiation environments. Two are trading-limits variants with different coalition structures — fragmented authority versus unified opposition. The third is a grid-curtailment case that replaces financial-market actors with electricity-system regulators under emergency load-shedding pressure.

All three share the same institutional grammar: multiple actors, ambiguous authority, fallback decree pressure, and terminal outcomes classified as compromise, consensus, or authority decision.

Three reflection conditions span the core experimental manipulation:

1. **No reflection** — just the main prompt context, no private thinking.
2. **Bounded reflection** — a constrained five-field private ledger tracking own concessions, other side's concessions, current state, opponent assessment, and open issues. Not open-ended chain-of-thought. A limited cognitive scaffold.
3. **Native reasoning** — the provider's built-in reasoning mode, enabled at medium or high effort depending on the model.

Two primary model families — Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite Preview and DeepSeek V3.2 — plus an OpenAI extension with GPT-4.1 and GPT-5.2 give four models across three provider families.

That's 495 completed runs: 270 in the primary matrix, plus 225 in the OpenAI extension.

## What to Measure

Rather than judging simulation quality by whether agents close deals, the paper proposes three trajectory-level metrics as *falsification tools* for sampler failure:

- **Action entropy** — Shannon entropy $H = -\sum_a p(a) \log_2 p(a)$ over the within-run action distribution. Low entropy means repetitive play; higher entropy means a richer mix of support, counter, concede, and oppose moves.

- **Concession arc rate** — fraction of runs where the same agent first rejects or counters, then later concedes or supports. Captures whether anyone actually softens during the negotiation.

- **Max-turn exhaustion rate** — how often interactions hit the turn cap rather than resolving earlier. High exhaustion means rigid, unadaptive play.

These are not completeness criteria. The paper is explicit: near-zero diversity, zero concession, and universal turn-budget exhaustion are *inconsistent* with any plausible account of boundedly rational negotiation. They falsify the sampler, not validate it.

## What the Runs Reveal

The result is strikingly consistent. Across all three experiments and both primary model families, bounded reflection produces substantially more diverse and compromise-oriented trajectories than either no reflection or native reasoning ($p \leq 0.0019$ for all primary contrasts).

Gemini under no reflection: action entropy 0.041, concession arc rate 0.000, max-turn exhaustion 1.000. Fifteen out of fifteen runs end in authority decision.

The same Gemini under bounded reflection: action entropy 1.040, concession arc rate 1.000. Fifteen out of fifteen runs end in compromise.

![Outcome distribution for Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite across three reflection conditions, showing bounded reflection recovering compromise while no reflection and native reasoning collapse to authority decisions]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

Native reasoning doesn't recover sampler quality. Gemini with native reasoning collapses back to authority decision in all 15 runs, and its parse success rate drops from 1.000 to 0.267 — behaviorally rigid *and* operationally fragile.

DeepSeek tells the same directional story under no reflection and bounded reflection. But DeepSeek native reasoning reveals something more unsettling.

## The Diversity-Without-Fidelity Trap

DeepSeek with native reasoning in the grid-curtailment experiment shows action entropy of 1.476 and a concession-arc rate of 0.933. By local diagnostics, the agents look lively.

They're countering, supporting, conceding. The traces are full of behavioral variation.

Every single run still ends in authority decision. Fifteen out of fifteen.

![Outcome distribution for DeepSeek V3.2 in grid curtailment, illustrating how native reasoning preserves intermediate variation but never reaches compromise]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

Andric names this the *diversity-without-fidelity* pattern: internal behavioral variation survives, but compromise-oriented terminal outcomes never appear. It's the sharpest finding in the paper because it rules out the lazy reading that native reasoning simply suppresses all variation. The problem isn't that agents look dead. The problem is that they look alive but can't close a deal.

Bounded reflection is the only condition that converts that local adaptation into negotiated resolution. For DeepSeek, bounded reflection drops max-turn exhaustion from 1.000 to 0.400 relative to native reasoning ($p = 0.0006$, Cliff's delta 0.600) and shifts terminal outcomes from 15 authority decisions to 12 compromises, 2 consensus, and 1 authority decision.

## Why Temperature Doesn't Fix It

A natural suspicion: maybe bounded reflection just adds noise. The paper addresses this directly with a temperature sweep on Gemini, running the same experiment at 0.3, 0.7, and 1.0.

No reflection remains 10 of 10 authority decisions at all three temperatures. Bounded remains 10 of 10 compromises. Native remains 10 of 10 authority decisions. Action entropy shifts within conditions, but the terminal distribution does not budge.

![Compromise rates across three temperature settings, showing no-reflection and native reasoning stay rigid regardless of sampling stochasticity]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_006_05.png" | relative_url }})

Higher stochasticity alone cannot rescue the rigid sampler conditions. The bounded-reflection scaffold is doing something structurally different from turning up randomness.

A bounded-ledger sensitivity check reinforces that reading. The paper tested three ledger variants with the same five state fields but different instruction tightness. Two — the current ledger and a more permissive variant — remain compromise-oriented. The third, a compact ledger that truncates the private state too aggressively, collapses toward authority decision: 9 of 10 runs.

The ledger needs to track concessions, opponent flexibility, and open issues. A too-sparse notepad is no better than no notepad at all.

## Simulation's Wrong Default

The practical lesson is direct: simulation pipelines should qualify models as *samplers*, not assume that stronger reasoning automatically improves fidelity. A model excellent for advising a policymaker may be the wrong model for simulating the policymakers.

The cross-provider evidence is hard to dismiss. GPT-5.2 with native reasoning: authority decision in 45 out of 45 runs. GPT-5.2 with bounded reflection: compromise outcomes surface in every environment, ranging from 5 of 15 in the first trading-limits case to 13 of 15 in grid curtailment.

The pattern spans Gemini, DeepSeek, and OpenAI. It survives domain transfer from financial trading limits to emergency grid curtailment. It survives changes in coalition structure. It is not a single-provider artifact.

## The Honest Limits

Three scenario types and four model families is solid but not exhaustive.

The bounded-ledger design explicitly tracks concessions and opponent assessment — it may implicitly privilege compromise-relevant state variables, a normative-bias concern the author acknowledges.

And there's no direct human behavioral baseline in these specific domains. We don't know exactly what "faithful" boundedly rational negotiation looks like here. We only know that zero-diversity authority-imposed outcomes cannot be it.

The paper also doesn't claim access to provider internals. The inference is behavioral: under the conditions studied, native reasoning changes the observable distribution of trajectories in ways that reduce simulation fidelity. We see the symptom clearly. The mechanism inside the model remains a black box.

## A Notepad, Not a Theorem Prover

Bounded reflection works because it operates closer to a cognitively limited private notebook than to an internal theorem prover.

It gives agents enough private state to track the conversation without pushing them into unrestricted strategic optimization. The old way — just turn on reasoning and hope — produces agents that solve the game instead of enacting it.

The solver-sampler mismatch is not a claim that reasoning is generally harmful. It's a methodological warning: model capability and simulation fidelity are different objectives.

If you're simulating boundedly rational negotiators, the strongest reasoner in the room may be the worst sampler.
