*TL;DR: Parks and Alharthi show that three eigenvalue quantities computed from a communication graph's successor representation can predict how a multi-agent LLM system will drift, converge, and break — and they discover that the spectral radius, the classical measure of stability, is perfectly inverted with respect to cumulative error.*

## The Most Stable Topology Drifts the Most

You have a multi-step reasoning task and a handful of LLM agents. Wire them in a line? Arrange them in a star around a judge? Let them debate as a mesh?

Today, you pick a topology, run the pipeline, watch it fail, and try again. Parks and Alharthi propose something that sounds almost too convenient: compute three numbers from the communication graph's adjacency matrix *before inference*, and they will rank-order your candidate topologies on drift, consensus, and adversarial fragility.

The punchline is not that the diagnostics work. It is that the most intuitive one — the spectral radius, the classical measure of linear stability — ranks the topologies in *exactly the wrong order*. The chain, which looks maximally stable to the spectrum, accumulates roughly double the error of the star and mesh.

## Borrowing a Map from Reinforcement Learning

The intellectual move is to repurpose the successor representation (SR), an operator Dayan introduced for temporal-difference learning in 1993.

In RL, the SR compresses expected future state occupancy into a single matrix. Stachenfeld, Botvinick, and Gershman later showed the hippocampus appears to maintain something like it — a predictive map of the environment.

Here, the "environment" is not a maze. It is the communication graph among agents. You row-normalize the adjacency into a stochastic transition operator $P$, then form:

$$M = (I - \gamma P)^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k P^k$$

Each entry $M_{ij}$ tells you how much influence from agent $j$ is expected to reach agent $i$, discounted across all pathways. The eigenspectrum of $M$ gives you three scalar summaries, and each one maps to a distinct failure mode.

Di Giovanni et al. (2023) used spectral bottleneck analysis to diagnose over-squashing in GNN layers. Parks and Alharthi borrow the same analytic posture — read the spectrum of a message-passing operator to predict information pathologies — but apply it to LLM agent graphs, not neural network layers.

## Three Readings, Three Failure Modes

From $M$, extract three quantities:

- **Spectral radius** $\rho(M)$ — bounds geometric growth of perturbations. The prediction: it should rank topologies by error amplification.
- **Spectral gap** $\Delta(M)$ — governs mixing time. The prediction: it should rank topologies by how fast they converge to consensus.
- **Condition number** $\kappa(M)$ — measures sensitivity of the output to perturbations of the input. The prediction: it should rank topologies by adversarial fragility.

The closed-form spectra are clean. For the chain (a 12-agent line), the nilpotent adjacency gives $\rho = 1$, $\Delta = 0$, $\kappa \approx 9.95$. For the star (four leaves plus a judge), $\rho = 10$, $\Delta = 9$, $\kappa \approx 28.61$. For the mesh (four agents deliberating with majority vote), $\rho = 10$, $\Delta \approx 9.23$, $\kappa \approx 13.00$.

![The three communication topologies: a sequential chain, a judge-and-leaves star, and a deliberating mesh]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

All three quantities were computed in milliseconds. Then they ran 100 trials per topology on Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct doing a structured state-tracking task.

## The Condition Number Is Perfect

$\kappa(M)$ perfectly rank-predicts perturbation robustness across all three topologies: chain (238) < mesh (247) < star (444).

The condition number is the cleanest diagnostic in the paper, and the one the authors suggest putting first in any pre-inference triage.

The spectral gap partially predicts consensus dynamics ($r_s = 0.5$). The chain fails to reduce disagreement, as expected. But the star converges faster than the mesh, which the raw gap does not predict. The explanation: the star's judge aggregation imposes a geometric bottleneck that accelerates convergence beyond what the graph's mixing time alone would suggest.

Then comes the surprise.

## The Stability Paradox

The spectral radius says the chain ($\rho = 1$) should be the most stable topology. Empirically, the chain's cumulative error (2094) is roughly double the mesh (1241) and the star (1184). The Spearman correlation between $\rho$ and cumulative error is $-1.0$. Perfectly inverted.

![Spectral radius across topologies — the chain has the smallest value yet the worst drift]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_008_03.png" | relative_url }})

This is not a numerical artifact. It is a structural finding about what kind of stability matters.

The spectral radius governs the geometric growth of *deterministic* perturbations to a homogeneous linear flow. But the dominant error source in LLM pipelines is stochastic per-agent deviation — each agent introduces a small random bias at every step. In the chain, those biases accumulate monotonically because each agent's output is handed to the next without any averaging. In the star and mesh, aggregation steps act as implicit averaging and suppress the drift.

![Cumulative error across 100 trials — the chain accumulates roughly double the error of the star and mesh despite having the smallest spectral radius]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_018_05.png" | relative_url }})

### Why Aggregation Saves You

Parks and Alharthi make this precise with an affine-noise model. Each agent produces:

$$\hat{x}_t^{(i)} = \tau(\hat{x}_{t-1}) + \eta_t^{(i)}, \quad \eta_t^{(i)} \sim \mathcal{D}(0, \sigma^2)$$

Topologies that average $k$ agents per step reduce noise variance to $\sigma^2/k$.

The cumulative error grows as:

$$E[E_{ceg}] \approx c \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{k}} \cdot T^{3/2}$$

For the chain ($k = 1$) versus star and mesh ($k = 4$), the predicted ratio is $\sqrt{4} = 2$. The empirical ratios — 1.77 and 1.69 — match within sampling variance.

The fix is a drift-corrected gain:

$$\tilde{\rho}(M; k) = \rho(M) \cdot \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_i \frac{1}{k_i}}$$

This recovers the correct rank ordering. Linear spectral stability and stochastic-drift stability are distinct notions, and LLM pipelines are governed by the latter.

## Honest Limits

Three topologies, one model family, one task, 100 trials per condition. A Spearman $r_s$ over three ranks carries essentially no statistical power — the authors say so directly.

The affine-noise model assumes iid per-agent noise and a Lipschitz-near-unity transition rule. When agents share a base model with correlated systematic biases, the iid assumption breaks. The paper derives a correlation-aware extension, $\tilde{\rho}_c$, but the empirical upper bound on bias correlation is $\rho_c \lesssim 0.06$ — so the iid model was already a good approximation here. On harder tasks with sharp branching, or with agents that share deeper inductive biases, the prediction may shift.

The framework also extends naturally to weighted, dynamically-learned transition operators parameterized by attention mechanics — Appendix A.5 sketches this — but none of it has been tested yet.

## What This Changes

Topology selection for multi-agent LLM systems has been trial and error. This paper turns it into three eigenvalue computations run in milliseconds before inference starts.

More broadly, the stability paradox reframes how we should think about robustness in LLM ensembles. Linear spectral stability — the kind spectral graph theory gives you for free — is not the stability that matters. What matters is drift stability, and it cuts the opposite direction. Aggregation, which the spectrum penalizes as a bottleneck, is exactly what saves you from monotone error accumulation.

The spectrum is not wrong. It is answering a different question than the one you need asked.
