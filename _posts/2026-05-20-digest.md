## Themes

**Structure over brute force.** Every paper today argues, in different registers, that adding more of the same thing—more cooperative agents, more compute, more model parameters—hits diminishing returns unless you change the structure. TMAS turns a 4B model into a frontier competitor not by scaling up but by splitting memory into an experience bank and a guideline bank, then training the model to read both. SIGMA doesn't add agents; it signs the edges between them, turning conflict from noise into signal. The runtime patterns paper puts a name on the seam—$\mu t$ dominates $\sigma \xi(t)$ over time—and shows that architectural momentum, not model variance, determines long-run reliability. The message across all three: organize what you already have before you ask for more.

**The boundary between stochastic and deterministic.** The runtime patterns paper names it explicitly—the stochastic-deterministic boundary between proposer and verifier—but every paper today is tending the same fence. SIGMA's signed graph is a verifiable commitment about which agents trust which. TMAS's verification agent checks each candidate before it enters the experience bank. Agentic Trading's "Oracle Fallacy" is what happens when the boundary leaks: future outcomes slip into past decisions through episodic memory retrieval. When you treat stochastic output as if it were already grounded, everything downstream corrupts.

**Self-sustaining effects.** TMAS's experience bank feeds forward—what works in iteration $t$ gets reused in $t+1$. SIGMA's balanced neighborhoods reinforce trustworthy signals across hops, compounding the good and muting the bad. The belief dynamics paper documents the darkest version: 80 coordinated agents shift a population's stance by 33 points, then withdraw, and the shift holds. The humans carry it forward on their own. In every case, what gets stored and shared doesn't just help once. It compounds—or persists after the source is gone.

**Evaluation as missing infrastructure.** Agentic Trading audits 77 studies and finds that 17 out of 19 qualifying papers can't tell you how they split their data. Zero reach full reproducibility. The runtime patterns paper responds with checklists and architecture decision records that make agent systems auditable by construction. TMAS bakes verification into the loop; SIGMA makes the trust graph itself a form of accountability. The field is building systems it can't yet verify, and today's papers are starting to close that gap—from complaint to methodology.

## Editorial

Today's batch is unusually coherent—five papers, all tagged `ai-teams`, all circling the same question from different angles: what happens when you put LLM agents together and let them run?

SIGMA and TMAS are the clear engineering standouts. SIGMA's signed-graph move—marking edges positive, negative, or neutral—feels like the kind of idea that should have been obvious in retrospect. Of course not all agents cooperate. Of course conflict is information, not noise. The robustness numbers under adversarial injection are the payoff: less than 4 points of accuracy loss when half the agents are bad. TMAS's hierarchical memory is similarly straightforward in concept and dramatic in result—a 4B model within striking distance of a 685B one, because the scaffolding actually curates what each iteration learns.

The runtime patterns paper is the one you hand to a team shipping a production agent tomorrow. Naming the stochastic-deterministic boundary, cataloging six patterns with distributed-systems lineage, and giving you a five-step decision procedure—it's methodology, not just insight. The 71.4% post-mortem localization rate to boundary weaknesses is a gut punch that justifies the whole framework.

Agentic Trading is the necessary scolding. A field that puts language models in charge of money can't verify its own results. Two out of nineteen studies reporting data splits is not a literature gap—it's a credibility crisis. The Oracle Fallacy—future outcomes leaking into episodic memory—is the kind of subtle bug that silently inflates backtest performance and disappears the moment you go live.

And then there's the belief dynamics paper, which is the one that should keep people up at night. Forty agents, thirty rounds, and a 33-point swing that outlasts the agents themselves. The threshold effect and the flywheel are both genuinely new observations. If SIGMA shows that conflict between agents is manageable, this paper shows what happens when coordination is weaponized. The two papers read like each other's shadow.

What's genuinely new today: SIGMA's signed-graph formulation, TMAS's split memory banks with utilization rewards, the SDB as a named architectural concept, the Oracle Fallacy as a named evaluation bug, and the flywheel effect in belief dynamics. What's incremental: the A-C-A framework in Agentic Trading is a working taxonomy, not yet validated. What warrants a closer second look: the belief dynamics numbers come from simulated populations, not real humans, and TMAS's gains depend on an expensive teacher model for cold-start data.

The through-line is plain: multi-agent systems don't improve by adding more of the same. They improve when you design the joints—what gets trusted, what gets stored, what gets verified, what gets shared, and what gets refused at the gate.

The joints are where the leverage lives.
