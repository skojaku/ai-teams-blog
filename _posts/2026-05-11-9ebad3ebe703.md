*TL;DR: A single LLM agent outperforms the whole multi-agent team in 43% of configurations—and the paper shows it's because peak entropy and early-round disagreement causally destroy collaboration, not just correlate with failure.*

## One Agent Versus the Whole Team

Run five LLM agents in a debate topology and let them argue their way to consensus on a math problem. You'd expect them to beat a single agent working alone.

Nearly half the time, they don't.

Across 30 model–dataset combinations, solo agents win in 13 of them—outscoring the average multi-agent system by 6.28 percentage points. The gap isn't random. It follows the entropy baked into the tokens those agents produce.

![SAS versus MAS accuracy across five models and six datasets, showing single agents outperform multi-agent teams in 13 out of 30 configurations]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_005_01.png" | relative_url }})

Yuxuan Zhao, Sijia Chen, and Ningxin Su trace this to actual Shannon entropy—measured at every token, for every agent, across every round of interaction. And the story those numbers tell is sharp: multi-agent systems fail when entropy spikes early and agents can't re-align.

## Reading the Entropy of a Team

The authors formalize a multi-agent system as $M = (A, G, R)$—a set of agents $A$, an interaction graph $G$, and $R$ rounds of deliberation. They test five architectures: single agent, sequential, centralized (with an orchestrator), debate, and hybrid.

For every sample, they harvest 254 raw features from the entropy traces—aggregating token-level entropy $H_t^{(i,a,r)}$ for each token $t$ produced by agent $a$ in round $r$ on sample $i$, up through agent-level, round-level, and sample-level statistics. After removing nine identifier columns, 245 trainable features remain. These include token costs, latency, and reasoning time alongside the entropy metrics.

Then they hand those features to an ensemble classifier—XGBoost plus LightGBM, averaged—called the **Entropy Judger**. One question: can entropy traces predict whether the team got the right answer?

They can. The Entropy Judger hits 72.6%–91.6% cross-validation accuracy, depending on the feature group and model family.

But prediction is correlation. The real contribution is establishing causation.

## How Entropy Governs Team Outcomes

### Certainty Preference: Peak Entropy Hurts, Stable Entropy Helps

The single strongest causal factor is **maximum answer-token entropy**—the peak uncertainty in the tokens forming the final answer. Its average treatment effect on correctness is $\text{ATE} \approx -0.31$ ($p < 10^{-28}$). When an agent's output distribution spikes sharply at a wrong answer, that spike poisons the team.

The flip side: stable, low entropy across the reasoning chain directly benefits correctness. Successful teams converge quickly to confident, consistent outputs. Struggling teams wander.

This pattern holds across all five architectures. What differs is *which* entropy matters most:

- Centralized and debate systems choke on inter-agent dispersion—the orchestrator or voting pool amplifies any erratic agent.
- Sequential systems are fragile to answer-level entropy—errors propagate through the chain with no cross-checking.
- Hybrid systems are the most robust, because dual feedback loops from peers and orchestrator provide recovery paths.

### Base Entropy: The Model's Floor Is the Team's Ceiling

The average per-token entropy of the base model, before any multi-agent interaction, causally limits team performance ($\text{ATE} \approx -0.12$, $p < 10^{-21}$). When base-model entropy exceeds roughly 100 nats, accuracy drops off a cliff.

This reframes a known finding. Kim et al. (2025) showed that adding agents yields diminishing or negative returns. Tran and Kiela (2026) showed that a single agent with enough thinking tokens can match multi-agent teams. This paper provides the mechanism: a confused base model makes confused copies, and confused copies compound confusion rather than canceling it.

The model families diverge in instructive ways. Qwen3 runs hotter—entropy ranging from 100 to 1,000 nats—but its self-verification habits suppress error propagation when agents share answers. LLaMA runs cooler, between 0 and 100 nats, yet reuses others' outputs without checking, so even modest disagreement cascades uncontrollably.

### Task Awareness: Hard Problems Need Goldilocks Entropy

Not all tasks want the same entropy profile. The authors sort their math benchmarks into easy (GSM8K, ~82% accuracy), medium (MATH500, ~49%), and hard (AIME 2024/2025, 25–32%).

**Easy tasks** demand rapid convergence. Low, stable round-1 entropy is highly predictive of success. Exploration is a waste.

**Medium tasks** reward sustained exploration with moderate entropy—longer reasoning time in round 1 correlates positively with correctness—but entropy *spikes* still kill you. The right profile is a right-skewed distribution: moderate average entropy, no extreme outliers.

**Hard tasks** need structured deliberation. Round-1 reasoning time is the top predictor. But uncontrolled early entropy and inter-agent variance in round 2 both predict failure.

You need long chains *and* tight alignment, simultaneously.

## The First Round Decides Everything

This is the paper's sharpest practical result.

Extending interaction from 2 rounds to 5 rarely helps and often hurts. The only consistent gains come in centralized systems with strong orchestration.

Why? Entropy metrics drop sharply from round 1 to round 2, then go nearly flat from round 2 to round 5. Agents settle after their first exchange. If they haven't aligned by then, more rounds just amplify noise.

![Entropy metrics across interaction rounds showing a sharp drop from round 1 to round 2, then stabilizing through round 5]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

Causal discovery agrees. Both the PC and FCI algorithms identify round-1 total entropy as a consensus direct cause of correctness ($p < 10^{-19}$). Mediation analysis shows that round-1 inter-agent entropy dispersion transmits 30–33% of its causal effect *through* round-2 entropy. Early misalignment compounds—it does not self-correct.

Cemri et al. (2025) catalogued multi-agent failure modes: communication breakdowns, insufficient verification, inter-agent misalignment. This paper adds the mechanism. Those failures aren't random sociological accidents. They're written in the entropy, and they're locked in during round one.

![Consensus causal DAG showing that base-model entropy, round-1 total entropy, and maximum answer-token entropy are direct causes of MAS correctness, with other entropy signals cascading through round-1 to round-2]({{ "/assets/figures/9ebad3ebe703/fig_008_05.png" | relative_url }})

## The Entropy Judger as a Practical Tool

Beyond diagnosis, the Entropy Judger gives practitioners something immediately useful: a label-free way to select better outputs from multi-sample runs.

Given $k$ candidate solutions, it picks the one whose entropy trace predicts the highest correctness probability. No ground-truth labels required. This consistently improves accuracy across all configurations and tasks—a rare result that works not by changing the system but by reading its internal telemetry more carefully.

## Honest Limits

The causal analysis relies on observational data, not randomized controlled interventions. The PC and FCI algorithms find structure in correlations, and propensity-score methods approximate causal effects—but true randomized experiments would be stronger evidence.

All experiments use open-source models (LLaMA 3.2-3B, LLaMA 3.1-8B, Qwen3 0.6B/4B/8B). Proprietary models like GPT-4 or Claude don't expose token-level probabilities, so the entropy framework can't be applied there without API changes.

The main analysis uses two interaction rounds, extending to five only on two benchmarks and two models. The round-1 dominance finding might shift in systems designed with longer, structured deliberation—but the data suggests it wouldn't.

## What This Changes

Multi-agent LLM systems ship on the assumption that more agents is better. This paper gives that assumption a proper postmortem.

When the base model is uncertain and early entropy spikes, adding agents doesn't distribute wisdom—it distributes confusion.

The actionable diagnostic: watch round-1 entropy. If agents disagree sharply at the start, more rounds and more agents won't save you. If they align early, the team can explore productively.

The entropy was always there. We just weren't reading it.
