*TL;DR: Today's agentic AI systems route tasks and decide when to stop using heuristics and prompting — this paper argues that the orchestration layer should instead maintain explicit Bayesian beliefs over task-level latent variables and make decisions by posterior expected utility, while leaving the LLMs themselves non-Bayesian.*

An agentic AI system decides which tool to call, which expert to consult, when to stop — and almost none of these decisions come with a principled accounting for uncertainty.

That's the gap this 30-author position paper names. The control layer orchestrating LLM agents should be Bayes-consistent, even if the LLMs underneath never are.

## The Wrong Kind of Uncertainty

Here's the crux: token-level uncertainty and task-level uncertainty are not the same thing.

A diffuse next-token distribution can coexist with high confidence about the semantic answer. A sharp token-level distribution can accompany genuine uncertainty about whether the code will pass its tests. Raw LLM probabilities are the wrong dashboard for control.

Falck et al. (2024) showed this empirically: Bayesian posterior predictive diagnostics — exchangeability, martingale constraints — are often violated by in-context predictions of pretrained LLMs.

The models aren't internally Bayesian, and pretending they are gives you a faulty instrument panel.

## Prediction Is Not Decision

The paper draws a clean architectural line: prediction is the LLM's job, decision-making is the orchestrator's job.

ReAct-style interleaving of reasoning and action (Yao et al., 2023) and Toolformer-style tool calling (Schick et al., 2023) are the dominant paradigms right now. They work.

The authors don't deny that. They call them what they are: practical engineering baselines that lack any principled accounting for uncertainty, cost, or stopping.

The shift matters as horizons lengthen and stakes grow. When you must decide whether to burn another $0.50 of API credit or escalate to a human, a prompting heuristic won't tell you whether the expected value of information exceeds its cost.

A belief state will.

## The Bayesian Controller

The proposal: a Bayesian controller sits above the LLM agents. It maintains a posterior over low-dimensional, task-relevant latent variables — a task outcome $Y$, a hypothesis $H$, an agent's competence — and updates beliefs using observation models learned from outcome-labeled data.

![Architecture diagram showing a Bayesian orchestrator maintaining posteriors over task-level latents while routing, stopping, or escalating, with LLMs remaining non-Bayesian predictors below]({{ "/assets/figures/be077b06322b/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

The belief update is the engine. At time $t$, after agent $i_t$ produces message $z_t$:

$$r_t(y) \propto r_{t-1}(y) \, p_{i_t}(z_t \mid y)^{\alpha_{i_t}}$$

The exponent $\alpha_{i_t}$ is a reliability weight that tempers each agent's likelihood contribution. A miscalibrated or noisy agent gets a low $\alpha$, damping its influence on the posterior; a well-calibrated agent earns a higher one.

This is a composite-likelihood (pseudo-likelihood) construction — approximate but defensible, and it prevents any single overconfident agent from hijacking the belief state.

The reliability weights update online too. If $L_i$ is the cumulative log-loss of agent $i$ on past tasks, the unnormalized weight is $w_i \propto \exp(-\beta L_i)$ for a sensitivity parameter $\beta > 0$.

Agents that performed well earn more trust. Agents that didn't get demoted.

It's an exponential-weights scheme straight out of the online learning playbook.

## Two Worked Examples

### Code generation with outcome tracking

Imagine a multi-agent code-writing system. Agents write code, retrieve examples, run static safety checks, execute unit tests.

The orchestrator tracks a single binary latent variable: $Y \in \{0, 1\}$ — will the candidate code pass all tests?

Each agent call produces a message $z_t$, and the observation model $p_{i_t}(z_t \mid y)$ maps that message to likelihood information about $Y$. The controller updates $r_t(y)$, decides whether another tool call's expected value of information justifies its cost, and stops or escalates when it doesn't.

The LLMs remain black-box predictors. The Bayesian structure lives entirely in the orchestration loop.

### Deliberation with hypothesis tracking

Now the latent variable is a hypothesis $H$ — say, which of three root causes explains a production outage: memory leak, race condition, or upstream API change.

Different agents inspect logs, review recent code changes, summarize operational context. Their messages are noisy observations about $H$.

The orchestrator updates $r_t(h)$ as the discussion unfolds, and recommends an action $a^\star_t = \arg\max_a \sum_h u(a, h) \, r_t(h)$ when posterior mass concentrates or value of information runs dry.

## Seven Design Patterns

The paper distills reusable patterns from these examples:

1. **Task-level belief factoring** — track latent variables that matter for decisions, not token-level noise
2. **Messages as observations** — every agent output enters through an observation model
3. **Reliability-weighted updates** — temper likelihoods by learned source reliability
4. **Dependence-aware evidence pooling** — repeated calls to the same model produce correlated evidence; pool conservatively
5. **Utility-based control** — routing, stopping, escalation, and budget allocation are posterior expected-utility decisions
6. **Cross-task posteriors for routing** — maintain competence profiles across tasks, adapt online
7. **Belief-state distillation** — compress interaction history into an approximately sufficient statistic so the controller stays feasible under context-window limits

## What's Missing

This is a position paper, not an empirical study. There are no benchmark numbers, no comparison of a Bayesian orchestrator against ReAct or Toolformer on a held-out task suite.

The examples are illustrative. Whether this actually outperforms heuristic baselines in practice remains open.

The observation models themselves can be misspecified. High-dimensional LLM messages fed into $q_i(y \mid z_t)$ may not faithfully capture the mapping from message to outcome.

Repeated tool calls yield correlated evidence — the paper acknowledges this and proposes tempering and conservative pooling, but doesn't demonstrate that these mitigations work at scale.

These aren't small caveats. A Bayesian controller is only as good as its observation model: garbage likelihood in, garbage posterior out.

## Why It Matters Anyway

Despite the missing experiments, the framing is a genuine shift. Right now, agentic AI engineering is ad-hoc prompting workflows stacked on top of each other.

This paper says: the orchestration layer should maintain a belief state, update it coherently, and make decisions that explicitly trade information value against cost. Stopping, escalating, and budget-aware behavior become principled expected-utility calculations, not prompting hacks.

The seven properties and seven design patterns give the "AI teams" community a concrete research agenda — one that treats uncertainty as a first-class design primitive.

The question isn't whether your LLM is Bayesian. It's whether your system behaves like a coherent decision maker.
