*TL;DR: The first paper to ground LLM coalition formation in hedonic game theory shows that stability hinges on preference consistency, not perfect rationality — and a five-step prompting protocol called CoalT pushes Nash stability from 41.8% to 73.2%.*

Put six LLM agents in a room and ask them to form teams.

Most of the time, they can't agree on who belongs with whom. The coalitions cycle, collapse, and re-form. Standard prompting yields stable partitions just 41.8% of the time.

Give those agents a game-theoretic scaffold for reasoning about membership.

That number jumps to 73.2%.

## The Gap Nobody Closed

Multi-agent LLM deployments — research squads, negotiation tables, distributed task systems — are coalition problems at heart.

Agents must decide not just *what* to do but *with whom* to do it. Yet the formal theory for when and why stable groups emerge was missing.

Prior work on LLM strategic behavior stayed in two-player territory. GTBench and FAIRGAME analyzed competitive games. Shapley-Coop measured credit inside a fixed grand coalition. Briman et al. used LLMs as *mediators* that generate compromise proposals — not as autonomous players deciding their own membership.

Meanwhile, the classical theory existed. Bogomolnaia and Jackson's 2002 framework for hedonic games — where each agent's preference depends only on who else is in its coalition — is well-established and widely cited.

Nobody had extended it to LLM agents with their noisy, inconsistent preferences.

This paper is the first to close that gap.

## ε-Rational Preferences

Classical hedonic games assume agents have perfect, consistent preferences over coalitions: if coalition $S$ gives me higher per-capita value than $T$, I strictly prefer $S$.

LLM agents don't work that way. Ask GPT-4 the same coalition comparison twice and it might flip its answer.

The authors capture this with a single definition: an agent has **ε-rational preferences** if

$$v_i(S) > v_i(T) + \epsilon \implies S \succ_i T$$

When the value gap between two coalitions exceeds ε, the agent reliably picks the better one. When the gap is smaller than ε, anything goes.

Empirically, ε ≈ 0.15 for GPT-4 and ε ≈ 0.22 for Llama-3. Both far exceed the δ/2 ≈ 0.04 value-gap threshold that would guarantee deterministic stability.

This flips the central intuition: *stability doesn't require agents to be optimal. It requires them to be consistent.*

## Walking Through the Math

The paper defines the LLM Coalition Formation Game (LCFG) on a set of agents $N = \{a_1, \ldots, a_n\}$, each with a capability profile over skill dimensions like math, facts, and logic.

Coalition value follows

$$v(S) = \phi\left(\bigoplus_{a_i \in S} c_i\right) - \psi(|S|)$$

where ⊕ is componentwise max — any expert in the group covers that skill — and ψ captures coordination costs that grow superlinearly (β = 1.3). Per-capita value is $v_i(S) = v(S)/|S|$.

The first theorem is a deterministic existence result. If every agent's ε-bound satisfies ε < δ/2 — meaning agents can reliably distinguish any two coalitions with different values — then a Nash-stable partition exists and can be found in polynomial time via improving dynamics.

But in practice, ε ≫ δ/2. So Theorem 2 gives a probabilistic bound that actually matters:

$$\Pr[\text{Nash-stable}] \geq p^{K_{\text{eff}}} \cdot p_{\text{easy}}^{K_n - K_{\text{eff}}} \cdot \gamma(G)$$

Here $p$ is preference consistency on the *critical decisions* — the ones where the value gap is small enough that agents might flip.

$K_{\text{eff}}$ is the number of such critical decisions. And γ(G) captures the probability that consistent dynamics find a stable partition.

Corollary 3 shows the bound scales as $p^{O(\sqrt{n})}$ for large populations — a scaling law that explains why stability degrades as groups grow.

The theoretical lower bound for six agents comes out to roughly 35%. The observed rate of 73.2% exceeds it handily, confirming that the bound is conservative and that consistency is the dominant factor.

## Coalition-of-Thought: Five Steps That Work

The practical contribution is CoalT, a five-step prompting protocol grounded in the game-theoretic framework:

1. **Capability Analysis** — List what each member of the candidate coalition can do
2. **Complementarity Assessment** — Identify capability gaps and overlaps
3. **Value Estimation** — Estimate task performance for each coalition
4. **Coordination Cost Analysis** — Assess communication overhead
5. **Preference Declaration** — State preference based on per-capita expected value

Each step mirrors a concept from the LCFG formalism. The complementarity step is the most important — ablation shows removing it costs 7.8 percentage points, the single largest drop.

Remove all five game-theoretic ingredients while keeping the structured format, and performance collapses to vanilla chain-of-thought levels.

This is the key distinction from Wei et al.'s chain-of-thought work that CoalT builds on. CoT gives structure. CoalT gives *content* — game-theoretic content. The 14.8pp gap between CoalT and vanilla CoT ($p < 0.001$) is the content premium, not the structure premium.

## What the Numbers Say

Across 2,400 episodes with six agents drawn from GPT-4, Claude-3, and Llama-3:

- **Standard prompting**: 41.8% Nash stability, consistency 0.64
- **Vanilla CoT**: 58.4%, consistency 0.74
- **Self-Consistency**: 62.7%, consistency 0.79
- **CoalT**: 73.2%, consistency 0.86

The correlation between consistency and stability is monotonic across all conditions.

![Nash stability rates across prompting methods: Standard at 41.8%, CoT at 58.4%, and CoalT at 73.2%, with significance markers showing p < 0.001 for CoalT]({{ "/assets/figures/05d03518bbbe/fig_007_01.png" | relative_url }})

Mixed-architecture coalitions — pairing a GPT-4 agent strong in math with a Claude agent strong in facts — achieve higher social welfare (0.81) than any homogeneous team (0.68–0.76), even though their stability is slightly lower than pure GPT-4 or Claude teams.

Capability complementarity outweighs coordination costs, at least at this scale.

## The Honest Limits

This paper runs six agents. Corollary 3 predicts stability degrades as $p^{O(\sqrt{n})}$, and above roughly 15 agents you'd need hierarchical decomposition — which hasn't been tested.

The framework assumes agents report preferences truthfully. A strategically manipulative agent could destabilize coalitions, and incentive-compatible mechanism design is explicitly future work.

Temperature is set to zero throughout, and the ε-estimates shift at higher temperatures.

The capability profiles are estimated on domain-specific benchmark subsets, not official benchmark scores — the authors are transparent about this.

## Beyond This Paper

The consistency-over-optimality insight echoes a finding from organizational behavior: teams with predictable members outperform teams with higher-variance stars.

That parallel suggests the framework may generalize to human-AI hybrid teams — where what matters is not whether your teammate is the best possible, but whether you can predict what your teammate will do.

For anyone deploying multi-agent LLM systems, the practical takeaway is immediate: use CoalT-style prompting, pick heterogeneous model architectures for capability coverage, and monitor preference consistency as an early warning signal for coalition instability.

Predictable teams beat brilliant but erratic ones — even when the teammates are language models.
