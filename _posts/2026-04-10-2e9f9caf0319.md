*TL;DR: TrACE is a training-free controller that measures how often an LLM agent agrees with itself across multiple rollouts—high agreement means the step is easy, so it stops sampling early, matching fixed-budget self-consistency at 33–65% fewer LLM calls.*

## The Waste Inside Self-Consistency

Picture an LLM agent navigating a kitchen. At one step, the only sensible action is "go to hallway"—all eight stochastic samples agree. At the next, it faces a genuine choice between two objects, and the samples split 5-3.

Self-consistency, as proposed by Wang et al. (2023), spends the same eight calls on both steps. The easy step burns four times the compute it needs; the hard step might benefit from even more.

This uniform budget isn't just inefficient—it's structurally wrong.

The whole premise of inference-time scaling, explored by Snell et al. (2024) and Brown et al. (2024), is that harder problems deserve more compute. But those methods need trained verifiers or reward models to decide what's hard. What if the model already knows?

## The Signal Hiding in Plain Sight

Khushal Sethi's TrACE—Trajectorical Adaptive Compute via agrEement—extracts a difficulty signal that every sampled LLM already produces for free: how often do independent rollouts agree on the same next action?

The intuition is simple. When the model's distribution strongly peaks on one action, stochastic sampling converges fast—most draws land on the same thing. When the model is uncertain, draws scatter.

Agreement $\alpha_t$ is just the fraction of samples matching the plurality action:

$$\alpha_t = \frac{|\{i : a_t^{(i)} \equiv a^*\}|}{|A_t|} \in [0, 1]$$

where $a^* = \text{mode}(A_t)$ and $\equiv$ denotes action equivalence under a lightweight canonicalizer (lowercase, strip punctuation, normalize whitespace). No training. No verifier. No labels.

## How TrACE Decides

TrACE runs a three-step loop at each agent timestep:

**Sample.** Draw $k_\text{init} = 2$ candidate actions at temperature $\tau = 0.7$.

**Agree?** Compute $\alpha_t$ across the current batch. If $\alpha_t \geq \tau_\text{high}$—default 0.75—commit to the plurality action and move on.

**Expand.** Otherwise, draw one more sample, recompute $\alpha_t$, and repeat until either agreement clears the threshold or the batch hits $k_\text{max}$ (4 or 8). Then commit.

The call budget per step lands somewhere between greedy ($k = 1$) and fixed self-consistency ($k$ everywhere). Easy steps exit at $k_\text{init} = 2$ calls. Hard ones expand toward $k_\text{max}$.

That bimodal call distribution is the operational signature of a system spending its budget where it matters.

## What They Found

On GSM8K ($n = 50$) and MiniHouse ($n = 30$), using a single Qwen 2.5 3B Instruct model quantized to Q4_K_M on CPU:

TrACE-4 matches SC-4 accuracy (0.82 on GSM8K, ~0.37 on MiniHouse) while using 33% and 39% fewer LLM calls respectively. TrACE-8 matches SC-8 accuracy (0.84 on GSM8K) with 55% fewer calls.

On MiniHouse, the gap widens to 65% fewer calls—16.27 calls per task versus 46.93.

![MiniHouse results: all methods hit ~0.37 accuracy, but TrACE uses 39–65% fewer LLM calls than self-consistency baselines]({{ "/assets/figures/2e9f9caf0319/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

The wall-clock story is equally stark. TrACE-8 processes 30 MiniHouse tasks in roughly 14 minutes on CPU. SC-8 takes about 40 minutes. Same model, same accuracy, same hardware—just smarter compute allocation.

And the signal is real. On MiniHouse, steps with $\alpha_t \geq 0.8$ are overrepresented in successful tasks; steps with $\alpha_t < 0.4$ cluster in failing ones. Agreement doesn't just save compute—it reliably predicts whether the agent is on track.

## Why the Threshold Matters

The commit threshold $\tau_\text{high}$ sits at the heart of the tradeoff.

Set it to 1.0 (require unanimity) and TrACE degenerates toward full self-consistency—almost every step expands to $k_\text{max}$. Set it to 0.5 (commit at bare majority after two samples) and you underspend, committing too early on uncertain steps.

The default 0.75 lands on the Pareto frontier in both benchmarks, matching accuracy while cutting calls.

![Agreement threshold ablation: accuracy holds flat as τ varies while call count drops, with the default τ = 0.75 hitting the sweet spot]({{ "/assets/figures/2e9f9caf0319/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

## The Honest Limits

All results come from one model—a 3B-parameter quantized model on CPU. Whether the efficiency advantage survives at 7B, 13B, or 70B+ scale is untested. The authors hypothesize it scales, but that's a hypothesis, not a result.

The discrete, enumerated action spaces tested here make agreement easy to compute. GSM8K answers converge to the same number; MiniHouse actions are copied from a fixed list. Open-ended generation—code, web navigation, long-form planning—might dilute the signal, since surface-form agreement becomes harder to assess.

MiniHouse itself is a handmade benchmark with two house templates and deterministic dynamics. It's not ALFWorld or WebArena. The authors chose reproducibility over difficulty, and the ~0.37 accuracy ceiling across all conditions suggests the model is already at its capability limit regardless of how many times you sample.

## Don't Overthink It

Every time an LLM agent samples eight rollouts for a step where two would have sufficed, it burns money and time for no gain. TrACE reads the model's own consensus and walks away early.

Sometimes the smartest thing an agent can do is stop thinking.
