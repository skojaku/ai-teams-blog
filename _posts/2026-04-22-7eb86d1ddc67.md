*TL;DR: Giving a small language model tools beats making it bigger—but adding more agents makes things worse. This is the first systematic study of 27 open-source SLMs across base, single-agent, and multi-agent setups, and it finds that tool-augmented single agents hit the Pareto sweet spot for deployment under real constraints.*

Give a 0.5B-parameter model a calculator and search tools, and the Pareto analysis suggests it can outperform a bare 10B model on financial tasks.

But only if you stop at one agent. Add a supervisor and three specialists, and completion rates crater, tokens balloon, and the gains evaporate. That's the uncomfortable finding from Wang and Brorsson's study of 27 small language models under three deployment paradigms. The lesson: architectural design can substitute for raw scale—but only up to a point, and the wrong architecture undoes the benefit entirely.

## The Deployment Problem Nobody Benchmarked

Financial institutions face a privacy trap. GDPR and PCI DSS restrict sending sensitive data to cloud LLMs. Regional banks, boutique funds, and individual investors—the ones who can't afford private LLM clusters—are stuck running models under 10 billion parameters locally.

These small models struggle with multi-step numerical reasoning and domain-specific knowledge. The standard playbook says: scale up parameters or fine-tune.

Kaplan et al.'s scaling laws told us performance is a monotonic function of parameter count and compute. But existing financial benchmarks from PIXIU and FinGPT measure task accuracy while overlooking deployment-oriented factors like energy consumption and latency.

Nobody measured what happens when you run these models under real deployment pressure—latency, energy, completion rate, system stability.

This paper fills that gap. Not by proposing a new model, but by asking: can *agent architectures* compensate for limited scale, and at what cost?

## Three Paradigms, Twenty Datasets, One Clear Winner

The authors evaluate 27 open-source SLMs—from Qwen, Llama, Gemma, Phi, DeepSeek, Mistral, and Solar families, spanning 0.27B to 10.7B parameters—across three paradigms:

1. **Base SLM**: bare model, direct prompting, no tools.
2. **Single-Agent System (SAS)**: one agent with a calculator, wiki search, and web search, running the ReAct think–act–observe loop, capped at 5 turns.
3. **Multi-Agent System (MAS)**: one supervisor routing tasks to three specialists (FinancialKnowledgeAgent, FinancialNLPAgent, FinancialQuantAgent), each with restricted tool access, also ReAct-based with a 5-turn cap.

All 27 models run on 20 financial datasets across 8 task categories: sentiment analysis, text classification, NER, question answering, stock prediction, credit scoring, summarization, and bankruptcy prediction. Fifty samples per dataset, same tools, same prompts, same H100 GPU—only the architecture changes.

The metrics go beyond accuracy.

Completion rate captures whether the system returns a valid answer at all.

Normalized Response Quality (NRQ) aggregates effectiveness across heterogeneous tasks, normalized against the base model. A composite Z-score enables cross-dataset comparison.

Energy per token, latency, and tokens per sample track deployment cost. These are the numbers that matter when you're running under constraints.

## The Sweet Spot

Single-agent systems dominate the Pareto frontier.

SAS achieves the highest NRQ (4.85), meaning tool-augmented single agents produce the biggest quality jump over bare models. MAS adds almost nothing on top—its NRQ gain is just 0.36. Reliability drops sharply as you add complexity: completion rate slides from 99.67% (Base) to 79.92% (SAS) to 72.01% (MAS).

![Grouped bar chart comparing Base, SAS, and MAS across completion rate, latency, energy per token, and token usage, showing the steep reliability drop and token inflation as complexity increases]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

The resource story is equally stark. Energy per token drops roughly 71% from Base to MAS—1.83 → 1.02 → 0.52 mJ/token—because the larger system amortizes work across more tokens.

But latency nearly doubles from 12.39 seconds (Base) to ~23 seconds for both agent paradigms. Token usage explodes: 2,356 tokens per sample for Base, 8,040 for SAS, 14,618 for MAS. The coordination tax is real and measurable.

### The Scaling Law U-Turn

Here's the most surprising result: bigger base models don't always help.

The Pareto analysis shows that 8B–10B bare models drift into the lower-right quadrant—high energy use, *lower* effectiveness (z < −0.5). Scaling parameter volume in isolation yields diminishing, then negative, returns.

The "scaling laws" curve bends backward without an agent paradigm.

![Pareto frontier scatter plot showing energy use versus effectiveness (Z-score), with SAS points clustering in the optimal upper-left zone and large base models drifting to high-energy, low-effectiveness territory]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

In contrast, SAS configurations cluster in the upper-left quadrant with z > 0.4 at relatively modest energy levels. Tool augmentation rescues mid-sized models. For Qwen2.5-0.5B and Llama-2-7B, SAS produces over 150% improvement over other setups.

## No Single Paradigm Wins Everywhere

The heatmap of task–architecture interactions reveals a nuanced picture.

MAS performs best on bankruptcy prediction—the one high-risk task where the statistical redundancy of multiple perspectives outweighs coordination costs.

SAS wins on QA, summarization, and credit risk, where structured tool use amplifies reasoning.

Base SLMs remain competitive for NER, sentiment analysis, and stock prediction—tasks where token-level precision matters more than multi-step reasoning.

And then there's the tiniest model in the study, Gemma-3-270M. It actually beats both agent paradigms on most tasks—because the agent paradigms for that model fail to run on most tasks. When your model can't follow the structured output protocol, wrapping it in an agent doesn't help.

## How Things Break

Failure modes shift fundamentally across paradigms.

Base SLMs mostly time out—straightforward failure. SAS shifts failures toward structural problems: context-length overflows from iterative reasoning, and "No Delegation" errors where the agent refuses to use available tools.

MAS introduces an entirely new category: delegation failures. Agents get stuck in hand-offs.

The supervisor delegates to a specialist, the specialist can't complete the task, and the system spirals through failed coordination rather than converging on an answer.

![Stacked bar chart showing failure mode distributions across Base, SAS, and MAS, with Base dominated by timeouts, SAS by context-length and no-delegation errors, and MAS by delegation failures]({{ "/assets/figures/7eb86d1ddc67/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

The bottleneck is no longer raw generative capability. It's context management and instruction adherence. Adding more agents introduces *new* vectors for systemic failure—infinite delegation loops, protocol violations, context exhaustion—rather than collective intelligence.

## The Honest Limits

This study uses a fixed set of 27 models, simple ReAct-based coordination, and a 5-turn interaction cap. More sophisticated orchestration—adaptive agents, dynamic routing, real-time user traffic—might change the picture.

The multi-agent design has one supervisor and three specialists. Richer coordination strategies, dynamic role assignment, and self-correcting delegation loops are all unexplored.

The evaluation is limited to the financial domain with 50-sample subsets per dataset. Results may not generalize.

And the 5-turn cap, while practical, means we never see whether longer agent chains could recover from early failures—or whether they'd just accumulate more errors.

## Why This Matters

Practitioners deploying SLMs under privacy and compute constraints finally have an empirical guide.

Don't reach for multi-agent collaboration by default.

Single-agent tool augmentation is the sweet spot for most financial tasks.

Reserve MAS for high-entropy domains like bankruptcy prediction where multiple perspectives genuinely add value. And for simple extraction tasks, just use the base model—the agent overhead dilutes precision.

The deeper challenge is to Kaplan et al.'s scaling-law orthodoxy. Performance is *not* a monotonic function of parameter count.

Architecture can substitute for scale, but only if you pick the right architecture. The wrong one is worse than none.

Scale is not the only dial. Turns out, it was never the most important one.
