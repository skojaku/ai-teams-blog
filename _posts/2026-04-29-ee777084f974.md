*TL;DR: Stateless LLM agents hit a hard wall — a 0.1% success cliff — when forced to bridge inter-dependent facts buried in long context. A two-stage "Architect / Executive" scaffold called SSRP pushes that boundary to 71.6%, and the real villain isn't incompetence but attention lock-in.*

## The 0.1% Cliff

Ask GPT-5.4 in vanilla ReAct mode to synthesize three logically dependent facts buried across 10K tokens of system logs, and it succeeds 0.1% of the time.

Not a typo. One in a thousand.

Then hand it a metacognitive scaffold — a self-synthesized reasoning protocol — and the same model hits 71.6%. The model was never dumb. It was latched.

## What Is the Attention Latch?

Shehata and Li formalize a failure mode they call the **Attention Latch**. In a multi-turn conversation, early constraints (turns 1–10) generate high-weight tokens that dominate the Transformer's attention heads. When a contradicting update arrives in turn 11, the model's output still prefers the old intent.

The latch condition is:

$$I(O; G_1) > I(O; G_2)$$

The mutual information between the output $O$ and the superseded goal $G_1$ exceeds the information about the new goal $G_2$. The model doesn't *forget* the update. It *weights* it below stale priors.

This extends what Liu et al. showed in *Lost in the Middle*: Transformer attention follows a U-shaped positional bias, privileging the start and end of a context window.

Shehata and Li demonstrate that in multi-turn dialogue, the bias compounds into something worse — metacognitive inertia where historical intent overrides explicit instructions, even when the model can *see* the update.

## Decoupling Latch from Position

The authors don't just show that models fail in the middle of long contexts. They prove the failure is about *what* the model weights, not *whether* it can retrieve the update.

In an equidistant stress test, they place the archived intent $G_1$ and the update $G_2$ at symmetric positions — identical distances from the context boundaries, at the 25% and 75% marks. If the failure were purely a retrieval problem, equidistant placement should make $G_1$ and $G_2$ equally hard to find.

Instead, GPT-5.4 achieves 90% APA on the equidistant control.

It can find and use the update just fine when positional bias isn't stacking the deck. The remaining 10% gap comes from safety-induced metacognitive refusal, not positional decay.

The Attention Latch — as Barbero et al. formalized for Transformers — is Information Over-squashing: structural bottlenecking of attention manifesting as behavioral lock-in. It's a weighting problem, not a retrieval one.

## SSRP: Architect Meets Executive

The proposed fix is architectural, not prompted. **SSRP** (Self-Synthesizing Reasoning Protocols) splits the agent into two roles:

- **The Architect** — a high-reasoning model that detects goal contradictions and synthesizes a compact Standard Operating Procedure (SOP). The SOP explicitly purges superseded intents and defines verification checkpoints.

- **The Executive** — a lower-latency model that follows the SOP verbatim, shielded from the noisy history by the Architect's scaffold.

The architecture is grounded in the Information Bottleneck principle. The Architect's SOP $P$ is a lossy compression of the conversation history $H$ that maximizes mutual information with the current goal $G$ while discarding noise:

$$\min_{P} \{I(P;H) - \beta \, I(P;G)\}$$

$\beta$ acts as a "sycophancy quotient" — high $\beta$ pushes the protocol toward instruction-following (pivot), low $\beta$ favors historical stability (latch).

The SOP replaces the decaying history as the primary driver of the Executive's attention pointer.

A granularity ablation nails down the sweet spot: a 3-step scaffold hits 92% APA. A 10-step protocol drops 8.7% — procedural overhead induces its own information loss.

Scaffold density has three phases: signal (too sparse), plateau (optimal), and decay (the protocol itself becomes noise).

## Stress-Testing the Boundary

The authors escalate through three tiers of pressure:

1. **Shallow Retrieval** — facts seeded at the recency boundary (2K tokens). Baselines perform decently; SSRP adds a modest lift (+26.41% for Gemini 3.1 Pro, +23.41% for GPT-5.4).

2. **High-Entropy SOP** — facts buried at the attention trough (10K tokens, centric seeding). This triggers Information Over-squashing. Claude Sonnet 4.6 jumps from 53.8% to 92.5% APA — a +71.93% lift — once the protocol bypasses safety-induced refusals via Administrative SOP framing.

3. **Semantic Hijacking** — three-hop logical dependencies with adversarial decoys across 10K tokens. This is where GPT-5.4's vanilla baseline collapses to the 0.1% Scientific Cliff.

Across 9K trajectories and four frontier model families, SSRP yields statistically significant resilience lifts at the high-entropy tiers for three of four families — Gemini, Claude, and DeepSeek all hit $p < 0.001$.

DeepSeek V3.2 — a sparse attention architecture — gains +58.06% in the high-entropy condition ($p = 9.8 \times 10^{-6}$). GPT-5.4's single-fact high-entropy tier proved a "Context Ceiling": its baseline was already 94.2%, leaving almost no room for the scaffold to help.

## Reflexion Proves It's Attentional, Not Reasoning

On the same 3-hop task, a Recursive Reflexion baseline hits 100% APA.

The model *can* reason. When it gets post-hoc corrective feedback, it bridges all three hops perfectly.

The 0.1% collapse is not a capability deficit — it's an attentional one.

Reflexion works because it corrects focus after the fact, but it remains a trial-and-error mechanism vulnerable to hallucination cascades in longer horizons. SSRP works pre-emptively, re-synthesizing the reasoning path before execution begins.

## The Grounding Paradox

A Procedural Integrity audit on 1,000 trajectories shows 98.8% adherence to the scaffold.

But the task success (APA) is only 71.6%.

That 27.2% gap is what the authors call the **Grounding Paradox**: the Executive follows the protocol perfectly, but the Transformer's attention trough still loses signal at retrieval time.

SSRP solves the *reasoning* gap — the metacognitive ability to pivot goals. The *retrieval* gap remains an orthogonal architectural limit of the Transformer's context window.

High-stability models — particularly Claude — exhibit an 18% metacognitive refusal rate. They fail by *refusing to hallucinate*, not by reasoning incorrectly.

Safety guardrails become indistinguishable from attentional lock-in when the model interprets re-scaffolding as a context-manipulation attack.

## Where It Holds, Where It Doesn't

The evaluation is thorough within its scope — 9K trajectories, four model families, temperature 0, LLM-as-a-Judge scoring validated against verbatim signal audits.

But it runs on a single dataset: MultiWOZ 2.2, a task-oriented dialogue benchmark. The authors themselves flag this, calling for validation on SWE-bench, GAIA, and other high-complexity distributions.

The 27.2% logic-action gap is a real limit. SSRP ensures the agent *thinks* correctly but cannot guarantee it *retrieves* correctly from the noisy context.

That gap is a Transformer-level problem — and no amount of prompt scaffolding fully closes it.

Single-model-tier pairing (Architect = strong model, Executive = fast model) is the only configuration tested. Whether the pattern generalizes to same-model role-switching or multi-agent ensembles remains open.

## The Boundary Is Real

The Attention Stability Boundary isn't a soft degradation curve. It's a cliff.

Stateless reasoning works fine for single-hop retrieval, then collapses when asked to synthesize across the trough. The fix isn't more parameters or longer context — it's architectural separation between planning and execution.

SSRP gives that separation a clean information-theoretic grounding and shows it works across model families without retraining. The 715× resilience lift over the 0.1% baseline is almost absurd — but the mechanism is straightforward: when you can't rewrite the context window, write a protocol that points past the noise.

The attention trough was always there. Now it has a name and a number.
