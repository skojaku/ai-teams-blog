*TL;DR: When you hold thinking tokens constant, a single LLM agent matches or outperforms every multi-agent architecture on multi-hop reasoning — and the Data Processing Inequality proves this is no accident: splitting context across agents is inherently lossy.*

## The Multi-Agent Illusion

Picture your favorite multi-agent LLM setup. A planner delegates, workers chew on subproblems, an aggregator stitches the pieces back together. It looks smart. It *sounds* smart. But when Dat Tran and Douwe Kiela gave both the solo agent and the team the exact same thinking-token budget, the solo agent won.

Across every model family, across both benchmarks, at every meaningful budget level. The multi-agent glow-up? Mostly just more compute in a trench coat.

## Why Equal Budgets Change the Answer

Previous multi-agent evaluations rarely controlled for total computation. A five-agent debate naturally burns five times the tokens, so of course it looks stronger — you are comparing five gallons of gas to one.

Wang et al. (2024) nailed this for prompting and search strategies: many fancy methods collapse under matched token budgets. Kim et al. (2025) showed that multi-agent gains concentrate in weaker models and evaporate as base capability rises, flagging coordination overhead as a first-order cost. Cemri et al. (2025) catalogued the specific failure modes — drift, information loss, evaluation artifacts — that orchestration introduces.

Tran and Kiela tighten the screws. They define *thinking tokens* as the total intermediate-reasoning tokens, excluding prompts and final answers. Then they cap that number for every architecture, from 100 up to 10,000, and race.

## The Theorem That Settles It

Here is the clean core of the paper.

Let $C$ be the full context a single agent sees — the question, the retrieved facts, every intermediate reasoning step. In a multi-agent system, agents communicate through messages $M = g(C)$, a compressed summary relayed between steps.

Because $M$ is a function of $C$, you get the Markov chain:

$$Y \leftarrow C \leftarrow M$$

The Data Processing Inequality then guarantees:

$$I(Y; C) \geq I(Y; M)$$

No estimator based only on $M$ can beat the best estimator based on $C$. The multi-agent pipeline *cannot* recover information that was already discarded when $C$ was compressed into $M$. Under perfect context utilization and a fixed token budget, single-agent reasoning is information-theoretically optimal.

This is not a vague heuristic. It is a theorem.

## When Context Degrades

The DPI guarantee assumes a single agent actually *uses* its full context well. In practice, LLMs don't. Long contexts suffer from attention dilution, lost-in-the-middle effects, and noise sensitivity.

Tran and Kiela model this by replacing $C$ with a degraded version $\tilde{C}_\alpha = T_\alpha(C)$, where $T_\alpha$ could delete tokens, mask them, substitute random ones, or inject distractors.

Now the Markov chain gains a link:

$$Y \leftarrow C \leftarrow \tilde{C}_{\alpha_1} \leftarrow \tilde{C}_{\alpha_2}$$

Heavier degradation means higher residual uncertainty: $H(Y \mid \tilde{C}_{\alpha_2}) \geq H(Y \mid \tilde{C}_{\alpha_1})$. The DPI still says you cannot beat $C$, but a well-structured multi-agent pipeline that filters and decomposes *might* beat a degraded $\tilde{C}_\alpha$.

Low degradation favors the single agent. High degradation levels the field — or tips it toward MAS.

## How They Tested It

The empirical sweep is thorough. Three model families — Qwen3-30B-A3B, DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B, and Gemini 2.5 (Flash and Pro). Two datasets — FRAMES and MuSiQue 4-hop. Five MAS architectures: Sequential, Subtask-parallel, Parallel-roles, Debate, and Ensemble. Plus two single-agent variants: SAS, and SAS-L, which adds a structured pre-answer scaffold that gently encourages more internal reasoning.

Six thinking-token budgets: 100, 500, 1k, 2k, 5k, 10k.

Sequential is the cleanest comparator. Both it and SAS reason serially over the same question with the same total budget. The only difference: Sequential externalizes intermediate states as messages between steps, while SAS keeps them latent in one continuous train of thought. Same gas tank, same route, different transmission.

## What They Found

At every budget above the degenerate 100-token floor, SAS matches or outperforms the best MAS architecture. The margins are not always large — SAS and Debate often overlap in confidence intervals — but SAS is the default winner, not the exception. It wins while spending *less* of its thinking budget, because it does not need to re-encode context into inter-agent messages.

![Accuracy of single-agent SAS versus five multi-agent architectures across thinking-token budgets on FRAMES and MuSiQue, showing SAS consistently competitive or superior]({{ "/assets/figures/1bab74f0d624/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

Debate is the strongest MAS variant. It appears most often in the top confidence band, especially on Gemini-2.5-Pro MuSiQue. The adversarial pressure to critique and defend seems to extract something useful from the extra tokens.

Performance saturates fast. Accuracy gains between 1,000 and 10,000 thinking tokens are marginal for most settings. Past the 1k–2k range, models burn tokens for diminishing returns, sometimes over-exploring rather than converging.

## Where Multi-Agent Tips the Scale

The context-degradation experiments confirm the DPI prediction precisely.

On MuSiQue with Qwen3 at a fixed 1,000-token budget, SAS leads at mild degradation ($\alpha = 0.3$). Systems roughly tie at moderate degradation. And at heavy degradation — 70% masking or substitution — Sequential MAS overtakes SAS.

Substitution, which both removes signal *and* injects misleading content, produces the cleanest crossover. SAS ahead at $\alpha = 0.3$, neck and neck at $\alpha = 0.5$, Sequential clearly better at $\alpha = 0.7$. This is exactly where a structured pipeline that decomposes and filters can rescue reasoning that a single degraded pass would derail.

![Accuracy of SAS versus Sequential MAS as context degradation increases, showing the crossover where MAS overtakes SAS under heavy corruption]({{ "/assets/figures/1bab74f0d624/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

Deletion and distractor injection show weaker versions of the same trend. Under deletion, systems overlap at moderate degradation, and SAS regains a small edge at the heaviest level. With distractors, both systems degrade but SAS stays ahead throughout, albeit by shrinking margins.

MAS helps most not when context is merely long, but when it becomes genuinely hard to separate signal from noise.

## Two Methodological Landmines

The paper surfaces two dirty secrets about how these comparisons are measured.

First: Gemini 2.5's API-level thinking-token accounting conflates latent internal computation with visible thought text. Under the same requested budget, MAS surfaces more visible tokens because multiple API calls each generate their own thinking trace, while SAS may plateau well below the cap. The SAS-L variant — which adds a structured pre-answer analysis scaffold — mostly helps Gemini models, precisely because it pushes their underutilized thinking channel to produce more. For open-source models, SAS-L is no consistent improvement.

Second: deep paraphrasing of benchmark questions *improves* accuracy for stronger models, while light paraphrasing *hurts* it. Where simple rewordings degrade performance but semantically equivalent deep rephrasings boost it, you are likely looking at memorization artifacts from pretraining. Original benchmark numbers may be inflated for the wrong reasons.

## Known Limits

This study covers text-only multi-hop reasoning. MAS advantages with tools, vision, safety constraints, or real-time interaction are explicitly out of scope.

The thinking-token cap is a ceiling, not a floor. Models can choose to use less, so the comparison controls an upper bound rather than actual compute. And the Gemini thinking-token accounting remains approximate.

## The Takeaway

If you are deploying an agentic system under real cost and latency constraints, the default should be a single agent with a generous thinking budget.

Multi-agent orchestration is not inherently superior — it is inherently lossy. Reach for MAS only when single-agent context utilization is genuinely degraded: noisy inputs, distractor-heavy retrieval, or contexts so long that models lose the thread. Anywhere else, you are paying for coordination overhead that information theory says you cannot recoup.

One agent, one context, full information. The math does not lie.
