*TL;DR: You can predict whether a multi-agent LLM topology will drift, converge, or collapse under attack — before generating a single token — by computing three spectral quantities of its successor representation.*

## A Graph Is Not a Guess

You have twelve language-model agents and a task. Do you wire them in a chain, route them through a judge, or let them debate as peers?

Right now the answer is: try all three, run the pipeline, see which one breaks least. That's expensive and slow.

Parks and Alharthi show that you don't need to run inference at all. The communication graph alone — its adjacency matrix, row-normalized into a stochastic operator — holds enough information to rank topologies on drift, consensus, and adversarial fragility. You just need the right representation of that graph.

That representation is the successor representation, an object Dayan introduced in 1993 for temporal-difference learning and that Stachenfeld, Botvinick, and Gershman later showed describes a predictive cognitive map in hippocampal circuits. Here, instead of encoding transitions between states in an environment, it encodes how influence propagates between agents.

Three spectral quantities drawn from it predict three failure modes with striking precision.

## The Stability Paradox

Here is the finding that stops you cold. The chain topology — twelve agents in a line, handoff after handoff — has the smallest spectral radius of the three topologies. By classical linear-stability theory, it should be the most stable. Empirically, it produces the worst cumulative error.

Mean cumulative error on a 12-step state-tracking task: chain at 2094, star at 1184, mesh at 1241. The chain produces roughly twice the error, despite being "the most stable" on paper.

This is not a bug. It is the paper's central insight, and the authors name it the stability paradox. The spectral radius governs how deterministic perturbations grow under repeated application of a linear operator. But the dominant error source in LLM pipelines is not deterministic amplification — it is stochastic per-agent drift, small random deviations that accumulate when no one averages them out.

The chain hands every agent's bias forward, unaveraged. The star and mesh both include aggregation steps — a judge or a majority vote — that implicitly average away independent noise.

Linear spectra are blind to this distinction. Maximally stable in the spectral sense, maximally fragile under real drift.

## How the Diagnostic Works

The construction is simple enough to fit on a napkin. Take your multi-agent system's directed graph and row-normalize the adjacency to get a stochastic transition matrix $P$. Then compute the successor representation:

$$M = (I - \gamma P)^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k P^k$$

with discount $\gamma = 0.9$. This $M$ compresses every multi-step influence pathway into a single linear operator. From its spectrum, extract three scalars:

- **Spectral radius** $\rho(M)$ — bounds geometric growth of perturbations. On the chain, $P$ is nilpotent and $\rho(M) = 1$; on ergodic topologies it saturates at $(1 - \gamma)^{-1} = 10$.
- **Spectral gap** $\Delta(M) = |\lambda_1| - |\lambda_2|$ — governs mixing time and, by analogy, how fast agents converge to consensus.
- **Condition number** $\kappa(M) = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ — classical perturbation sensitivity, predicting how badly a small input perturbation can be amplified.

Each scalar pairs with a falsifiable empirical metric: cumulative error growth, consensus decay rate, and perturbation sensitivity respectively.

The whole computation — eigendecomposition of $M$ on the adjacency of your graph — runs in milliseconds.

## Test the Predictions

The condition number is the cleanest result. Across chain ($\kappa \approx 9.95$), mesh ($\kappa \approx 13.00$), and star ($\kappa \approx 28.61$), the rank order matches empirical perturbation sensitivity exactly. Spearman $r_s = 1.0$.

If you want to know which topology resists an adversarial nudge, compute $\kappa$ and stop.

The spectral gap partially predicts consensus dynamics ($r_s = 0.5$). The chain fails to converge, as expected. But the star converges faster than the mesh, contrary to what the raw gap predicts. The explanation: the star's judge node is a many-to-one bottleneck that forces consensus geometrically, an effect the graph's spectral gap doesn't capture because it doesn't know about explicit aggregation operators.

The spectral radius, as we already saw, is perfectly anti-correlated with cumulative error ($r_s = -1.0$). This is the paradox made quantitative.

### Fixing the Prediction: Drift-Corrected Gain

The authors model per-agent noise as additive, iid, and zero-mean: $\hat{x}^{(i)}_t = \tau(\hat{x}_{t-1}) + \eta^{(i)}_t$. For a topology with $k$-fold aggregation, the effective per-step noise variance drops to $\sigma^2/k$. The chain has $k=1$. The star and mesh both have $k=4$.

This yields a closed-form prediction:

$$\mathbb{E}[E_{\text{ceg}}] \approx c \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{k}} \cdot T^{3/2}$$

Predicted chain-to-aggregated ratio: $\sqrt{k} \approx 2$ for $k = 4$. Empirical ratios: 1.77 (chain/star) and 1.69 (chain/mesh). Within sampling variance.

The fix is a drift-corrected gain $\tilde{\rho}(M; k)$ that divides the spectral radius by the square root of the average aggregation count. Its rank order — chain > star ≈ mesh — now matches the empirical error.

The spectrum wasn't wrong. It was answering the wrong question. Ask about drift, not about deterministic amplification, and the prediction recovers.

## The Honest Boundaries

Three topologies, one model family, one task family, 100 trials per condition. A Spearman correlation over three ranks carries essentially no statistical power — the authors say this plainly. The condition-number result is suspiciously clean, and we should want to see it survive a wider sweep.

The affine-noise model assumes iid per-agent noise and a Lipschitz-near-unity transition rule. Both are simplifications. Agents sharing a base model will have correlated biases, and tasks with sharp branching will violate the linearization. The appendix sketches a correlated-noise extension that interpolates between the iid and fully-correlated regimes, but it is untested.

Cemri et al. cataloged fourteen failure modes of multi-agent LLM systems in 2025 and argued that current evaluation cannot anticipate them. This paper offers the kind of pre-inference diagnostic they called for — but only for the three failure modes it studies, and only under controlled noise assumptions.

## Why This Matters Beyond One Paper

The practical pitch is blunt: stop choosing topologies by trial and error.

Compute $\kappa(M)$ first for adversarial robustness, $\Delta(M)$ for consensus speed (with the aggregation caveat), and $\tilde{\rho}(M; k)$ for drift. Three spectral computations on a matrix you already have. No inference, no GPU hours, no task-specific benchmarking.

The conceptual pitch is longer. The successor representation built a bridge between reinforcement learning and cognitive neuroscience three decades ago. Parks and Alharthi have now driven that same bridge into multi-agent AI system design.

The idea that a graph's predictive-map spectrum tells you how the agents on it will fail — before anyone writes a prompt — is a genuinely new diagnostic stance. It sits upstream of prompting strategies and downstream of classical spectral theory.

![Summary of the spectral diagnostic framework mapping three spectral quantities to three failure modes]({{ "/assets/figures/900d15d70bea/fig_019_06.png" | relative_url }})

If the ordering holds across models and tasks, topology selection becomes a closed-form linear-algebraic question rather than a post-hoc empirical one. That is the shift: from "run it and see" to "compute and know."

Three eigenvalues, before a single token, and you know whether your graph drifts, converges, or collapses.
