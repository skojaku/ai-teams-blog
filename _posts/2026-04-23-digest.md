## Themes

**Structure over scale.** Every paper today agrees on one point: how you organize multi-agent LLM systems matters more than how big the models are. *Rethinking Scale* shows a single agent with tools crushes multi-agent teams across 27 small models. *Diversity Collapse* demonstrates that wiring—authority hierarchies, debate topologies—determines the idea space, not model quality. *Evaluating Cooperation* proves that a simple election mechanism outperforms fixed leadership by double-digit margins. The lever is architectural, not parametric.

**Social science as engineering blueprint.** Three papers directly port human social-cognition constructs into agent design and they all work. *Explicit Trait Inference* borrows Fiske's warmth–competence taxonomy and cuts payoff loss by up to 77%. *Cooperative Profiles* uses behavioral economics games to forecast team performance for under 1,000 tokens. *Diversity Collapse* replicates the Ringelmann effect and production blocking in agents with no psychology—then cures them with Nominal Group Technique. The human playbook translates.

**The coordination tax.** Adding agents and communication channels often degrades rather than improves outcomes. *Rethinking Scale* reports that multi-agent teams fail to complete 28% of tasks and barely outperform the baseline. *Diversity Collapse* shows per-agent diversity utilization drops from 1.03 to 0.47 as group size grows from three to seven. *Evaluating Cooperation* finds that Competitive leaders lose elections but retain high centrality in discussion—a "Losing Voice" effect where rejected agendas still shape deliberation. More voices, more interference.

**Independence as a feature.** When agents are decoupled, they produce better and more diverse outcomes. *Diversity Collapse* shows blind writing and subgroup topologies sustain constructive conflict that round-robin debate kills. *Rethinking Scale* proves one well-equipped agent beats a team of four. *Evaluating Cooperation* reveals that elections work precisely because they let the group select cooperators without forcing consensus on a shared plan. The fix is less coupling, not more.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag, one message: multi-agent LLM design is a systems problem, not a model problem.

The genuinely new insight is that social-science constructs—elections, warmth–competence ratings, Nominal Group Technique—transfer into agent architectures and work on first contact. *Explicit Trait Inference* and *Cooperative Profiles* both deliver large gains from thin interventions, and *Evaluating Cooperation* shows democracy functioning as an honest engineering technique. That is not incremental; it is a proof that the human coordination playbook has untapped engineering value for AI.

What is incremental: the observation that adding agents hurts. We already knew delegation failures and communication overhead degrade multi-agent performance. *Rethinking Scale* and *Diversity Collapse* quantify the tax precisely, which is useful, but the direction was expected.

The finding that warrants a second look is *Diversity Collapse*'s Alignment-Topology Mismatch. Heavily aligned models like GPT-5.1 converge into narrow consensus regardless of structure—alignment priors act as a floor that scaffolds cannot breach. If that holds across domains, it means the most capable models may be structurally incapable of divergent group ideation. That is not a scaffold problem anymore. That is a model problem.

Structure is the lever. But the lever has limits.
