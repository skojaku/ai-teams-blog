## Themes

**Context is the bottleneck.** When ten agents pour their states into one orchestrator window, steering accuracy collapses to 21%. When an LLM agent samples eight rollouts for a step where two would do, it burns compute for nothing. When two agents loop head-to-tail, they talk themselves into semantic silence. Three of today's five papers — *Dynamic Attentional Context Scoping*, *Don't Overthink It*, and *Theater of Mind* — diagnose the same disease from different angles: too much of the wrong signal drowns the right one.

**Intelligence will not save you from yourself.** OpenAI's o3 completes just 17% of optimal collective performance in a zero-cost sharing task — not because it can't understand the rules, but because it spontaneously hallucinates market dynamics and withholds information anyway. *NetworkGames* tells the same story from the other direction: Feeling-type agents cooperate at 76% while Thinking-type agents cooperate at 22%, and the smartest models are often the worst teammates. Capability and cooperation are orthogonal variables.

**Small levers, outsized effects.** Swap five hub nodes from ENTJ to ESFJ and network-wide cooperation doubles from 28% to 67%. A 10% sharing incentive more than doubles o3's cooperative output. Replace flat context with a focus mode and steering accuracy jumps from 21% to 90%. Measure agreement and walk away early — you match accuracy at 33–65% fewer calls. None of these require new models or bigger budgets, just different architecture.

**Passive systems collapse; active ones correct.** *Theater of Mind* formalizes this most explicitly: when Shannon entropy over cluster assignments drops toward zero, the system inflates generation temperature to force exploration, then decays back. *TrACE* performs a simpler version of the same trick — when agreement is already high, stop sampling and move on. Both reject the assumption that more compute is always better, replacing it with feedback-driven adaptation.

## Editorial

Today's batch is a single argument in five voices: the architecture of multi-agent systems matters more than the intelligence of any agent inside them.

The strongest result is the simplest. *More Capable, Less Cooperative?* strips cooperation down to the easiest possible task — send information that costs you nothing — and watches one of the world's best reasoning models fail at it. Not because it misunderstands, but because it invents bargaining chips that don't exist. The correlation between capability and cooperation is r = 0.16. That number should be tattooed on every multi-agent deployment plan written this year.

Meanwhile, *DACS* shows that the fix for context pollution is not a smarter model or a better compression algorithm — it is isolation. One agent, one focus window, one clean shot. Deterministic, no scoring function, no tuning. And *NetworkGames* demonstrates that five nodes with the right personality outperform architectural rewrites. The lever is not more compute; it is the right compute in the right place.

The incremental work is *Theater of Mind*, which presents a complete architectural specification and open-source code but no empirical benchmarks. The entropy-based deadlock-breaking mechanism is a genuinely portable idea — you could bolt it onto any multi-agent system suffering from semantic collapse tomorrow — but without numbers it remains a promise, not a proof.

*TrACE* warrants a closer second look. Its results come from a single 3B-parameter quantized model on CPU, and its agreement signal depends on discrete enumerated action spaces. Whether the efficiency gain survives at frontier scale or in open-ended generation is an open question — but the 55–65% call reduction on MiniHouse is real, and the intuition that easy steps should cost less is hard to argue with.

You can't intelligence your way out of a cooperation problem. You can, it turns out, architecture your way out.
