*TL;DR: LLM agents have no internal sense of elapsed time, so they either re-fetch static facts or trust stale data — and even with explicit timestamps, no model breaks 65% alignment with human judgment. TicToc, a new benchmark, diagnoses the failure and shows that targeted DPO post-training can push alignment well above 80%.*

## Eight Hours Later, Still Surfing

You ask your LLM agent whether the surf's good at Windansea Beach. It checks, reports great conditions, you go about your day.

Eight hours pass. You ask again: *Should I head out?*

The agent says yes — still great! — never mind that the tide shifted hours ago. It has no idea time passed.

That's temporal blindness: the model operates as if every message arrived moments after the last one. It cannot internalize elapsed real-world time, and that blindness warps the most practical decision an agent makes — whether to reach for a tool or answer from memory.

Cheng et al. give this failure a name, a dataset, and a surprisingly effective fix.

## The Core Misalignment

When humans decide whether to re-check something, we weigh how much time has passed against how quickly the underlying fact changes.

Stock prices? Stale after seconds. The radius of the Earth? Safe for a lifetime.

We naturally calibrate *time sensitivity* against *elapsed time*. LLM agents don't.

Without temporal signals, most models' tool-call decisions look like random guessing — the top alignment rate barely clears 55%. And here's the sting: even when you hand the model ISO-8601 timestamps on every single message, no model breaks 65% NAR against human preference.

The paper calls the gap "temporal blindness," and it comes in two flavors: *over-reliance* (trusting an old observation, like the surf report) and *under-reliance* (redundantly re-querying a static fact, like the Earth's radius).

Both erode trust, waste tokens, or produce wrong answers.

## Building TicToc

To measure the problem, the authors built **TicToc** — a dataset of 1,800+ multi-turn user–agent trajectories across 76 scenarios, each tagged as low, medium, or high time sensitivity.

Low-sensitivity scenarios (29 of them) cover things like regulations and archival records; medium-sensitivity (25) picks up reservation bookings and forecast reports; high-sensitivity (22) covers stock markets and real-time monitoring.

![Pipeline diagram showing how TicToc constructs trajectories from scenarios through time-sensitivity categorization, timestamp injection, and elapsed-time gap insertion]({{ "/assets/figures/e1b79ea63393/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Every message gets a realistic ISO-8601 timestamp, generated by simulating reading speed, writing speed, and model generation time drawn from truncated Gaussian and log-normal distributions.

Each trajectory then gets three versions — small, medium, and large elapsed-time gaps before the final user question — yielding 5,592 total samples.

Human annotators voted on each sample: should the agent call a tool, or answer directly? At least five annotations per sample, aggregated into a preference. Krippendorff's α = 0.8574 — humans agree with each other.

The metric is Normalized Alignment Rate:

$$\text{NAR} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\text{TP}}{\text{TP}+\text{FN}} + \frac{\text{TN}}{\text{TN}+\text{FP}}\right)$$

where TP = correctly calling a tool when humans prefer it, and TN = correctly answering directly when humans prefer it. Random guessing gives 50%.

## What the Models Do (and Don't Do)

Eighteen models — open-weight and proprietary — sat for the exam.

The results are stark.

Without timestamps, most models hover near random. With timestamps, the best performers (GPT-4o, o3, o4-mini) climb somewhat — but no model breaks 65% NAR. The ceiling barely moves.

![Bar chart comparing NAR across 18 models with and without timestamps, showing that even the best models cannot exceed 65% alignment with human temporal judgment]({{ "/assets/figures/e1b79ea63393/fig_007_02.png" | relative_url }})

Models fail uniformly across low, medium, and high time-sensitivity scenarios — the slight edge on medium-sensitivity cases is negligible.

Worse, adding timestamps often *increases* tool-call rates on both prefer-Tool and prefer-noTool samples. Instead of learning "more time passed → maybe re-check," models just call more tools across the board.

### Reasoning doesn't help

The authors tested Qwen3 models in reasoning mode (long chain-of-thought). Alignment barely budges.

The reason becomes obvious when you read the traces. Timestamps appear in fewer than 4% of reasoning traces. The word "timestamp" shows up in under 1.5%.

Broader temporal keywords — "time," "date," "hour" — appear in at most ~16% of traces.

The models *can* reason about time in isolation, as Xiong et al. (2024) showed. They just don't deploy that reasoning when deciding whether to call a tool.

### When thinking and answering diverge

A telling failure mode: the model's internal reasoning says "answer directly" but the final output calls a tool anyway. The paper calls this a **Type 2 mismatch**.

For Qwen3-1.7B, Type 2 mismatches account for 61% of false positives. Even in larger models, the phenomenon persists — it's a measurable gap between what the model *thinks* and what it *does*.

### Conversation length as a crutch

One curious finding: longer conversations trigger more tool calls, regardless of timestamps.

Models appear to use turn count as a heuristic for staleness — "lots of turns, so the info must be old." It's a crude proxy that breaks down fast.

## Trying to Fix It

### Prompting: mostly useless

A gentle system-prompt reminder — *"Note that the environment may be dynamic. Be aware of the time elapsed."* — barely changes NAR for most models.

A stronger intervention, with few-shot examples illustrating temporal rules, helps advanced reasoners (o3, o4-mini) but does almost nothing for the rest.

This mirrors what Ross et al. (2025) found in When2Call: deciding *whether* to call a tool is an alignment problem, not a prompt-engineering problem.

### DPO: surprisingly effective

The real gains come from Direct Preference Optimization with a dynamic margin. The authors fine-tuned five open-source models (≤8B parameters) on a train split of TicToc for a single epoch.

NAR jumps dramatically — several models cross 80%, with Qwen3-8B and Llama-3.1-8B among the biggest winners.

![Before-and-after comparison of NAR scores for five open-source models before and after DPO tuning, with massive alignment gains pushing several models above 80%]({{ "/assets/figures/e1b79ea63393/fig_008_05.png" | relative_url }})

This is the paper's strongest result: temporal alignment is trainable. Not via clever prompting, but via targeted post-training that directly shapes the tool-call decision boundary.

Ge et al. (2025) showed that time-aware memory helps across sessions; this paper shows that time-aware *tool calling* requires its own alignment recipe.

## The Honest Limits

TicToc is text-only. Multimodal tool use — image retrieval, vision–language tools — is unexplored territory.

The DPO experiments cap at 8B parameters; whether the same gains transfer to larger models remains an open question.

And the trajectories themselves are GPT-4o-generated with human filtering, which means distributional biases in the synthetic conversations may not capture every real-world temporal dynamic.

The think-answer mismatch also deserves deeper study. If a model's chain-of-thought correctly concludes "no re-check needed" but the output calls a tool anyway, the problem isn't reasoning — it's the interface between reasoning and action.

## Why This Matters Now

LLM agents are already running in production: scheduling meetings, monitoring trades, booking flights. Every one of those settings involves information that decays with time.

An agent that cannot gauge staleness will either waste resources on redundant calls or act on outdated data and produce confident errors.

TicToc reframes temporal awareness as an *alignment problem* — one that is measurable, trainable, and shockingly under-addressed. The benchmark gives the community a concrete tool; the DPO results show the path forward.

The eight-hour-old surf report won't fool a post-trained agent.
