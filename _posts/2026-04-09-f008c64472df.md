## The Old Way vs. The New Way

Previous activation-level interpretability tools share a fatal flaw: they assume single-GPU execution.

Nostalgebraist's 2020 logit lens projects intermediate residual-stream activations through the output embedding to trace how token predictions evolve across layers. Belrose et al. (2023) improved alignment with learned per-layer corrections. Turner et al. (2023) showed that steering vectors‚Äîdirections computed from contrastive examples‚Äîcan reliably shift model behavior without retraining.

All powerful. All broken at scale.

The reasons are embarrassingly concrete:

1. Per-layer re-forwarding requires running the full model N times for N layers.
2. KV caching gets disabled because layers need access to intermediate states.
3. Full vocabulary projection happens at every step ($O(|V|)$ per token).
4. Single-GPU memory can't hold 70B parameters *plus* all intermediate activations.

Memory usage scales as $O(T \cdot d \cdot |L| \cdot |C|)$‚Äîfor a 1,500-token sequence, 80 layers, and three activation types, that's roughly 5.9 GB in bfloat16. Manageable. But only if you don't blow your GPU budget on redundant forward passes first.

## How It Works

The authors embed lightweight instrumentation directly inside the tensor-parallel inference path.

Three stages:

**Stage 1: Distributed initialization.** The model partitions across GPUs under DeepSpeed. Selected layers get wrapped with `BlockOutputWrapper`‚Äîthin interceptors that expose post-attention activations from attention outputs, MLP outputs, and residual-stream outputs. The final normalization layer and shared LM head stay unmodified.

**Stage 2: Single-pass activation capture.** Standard autoregressive decoding proceeds with KV caching enabled. Wrappers record only the hidden-state slice for the most recent token: shape $[1, 1, d]$, not $[1, T, d]$. These compact slices accumulate during generation. No additional forward passes. No interference with caching.

**Stage 3: Batched post-hoc decoding.** After generation finishes, all captured activations get concatenated and projected in batch through the shared LM head. One vocabulary projection per layer, not one per token per layer.

The math for the logit lens is straightforward:

$$z_{\ell,t} = W_{\text{out}} \cdot \text{LN}(h_{\ell,t}) + b$$

Given hidden state $h_{\ell,t}$ from layer $\ell$ at token position $t$, project through the final layer norm and LM head. Extract top-k logits and softmax over those. Done.

Steering follows similarly:

$$v = \frac{y_+ - y_-}{\| y_+ - y-_2 \|}$$

$$h' = h + \alpha v$$

Compute a normalized direction from contrasting base and target activations. Inject it post-LayerNorm with multiplier $\alpha$. No additional passes. KV cache reuse preserved.

## What They Found

The numbers are stark.

On identical 4√ó RTX A6000 hardware, decoding 500 tokens on LLaMA-3.1‚Äì8B:

- Baseline (LogitLens4LLMs with per-layer re-forwarding): 833 seconds at 0.6 tokens/s.
- Their method: 20 seconds at 25 tokens/s.

That's 41√ó faster.

Memory drops by up to 7√ó because activations scale with hidden dimension $d$, not vocabulary size $|V|$.

Scaling holds across architectures. LLaMA-3.1‚Äì70B completes a 1,500-token run in 61 seconds at 20 tokens/s. Qwen3‚Äì4B hits 27.8 tokens/s. Throughput varies by less than 1.7√ó across models spanning 4B to 70B parameters‚Äîthe bottleneck is tensor-parallel inference, not the instrumentation.

Steering works too. Mid-layer injections (layer 35) on LLaMA-3.1‚Äì70B achieve a mean steerability slope of 0.72 with $R^2 = 0.85$. Push $\alpha$ from ‚àí1.5 to +1.5 and the model's label-token propensity shifts monotonically. A 0.3 propensity increase requires $\alpha \approx 0.35$ at layer 35‚Äîcompared to 4‚Äì6.5√ó at deeper layers.

## Honest Limits

Early layers (L < 20) produce near-zero steering effects ($R^2 < 0.01$). The semantic signal hasn't separated yet.

Large multipliers ($|\alpha| > 1.5$) cause fluency degradation and saturation. The sweet spot is narrow.

The framework targets decoder-only transformers. Encoder-decoder architectures and non-Transformer models remain unexplored.

And it's inference-only‚Äîtraining dynamics, representation learning during fine-tuning, are out of scope.

## Why It Matters

We finally have a practical path to unified interpretability-and-control on production-scale multi-GPU LLMs.

No model modifications. No throughput sacrifice. No out-of-memory failures on mid-sized models.

The 70B barrier isn't theoretical anymore.
