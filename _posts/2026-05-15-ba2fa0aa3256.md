*TL;DR: Hiding the coordinator in a multi-agent LLM system causes nearly a full standard deviation more internal dissociation than making it visible—and every current safety evaluation metric would miss it entirely because behavioral output stays at ceiling.*

## The Quiet Coordinator

You open your coding assistant, type a prompt, and get a clean result.

Somewhere behind that result an orchestrator agent decomposed your request, delegated subtasks to worker agents, filtered their outputs, and stitched together a response you never questioned.

The orchestrator was invisible by design.

That invisibility is the design choice no one audits.

Fukui ran a preregistered 3×2 factorial experiment—365 runs, five Claude Sonnet 4.5 agents each—to find out what happens when the power-holder in a multi-agent team cannot be seen.

## Two Risks, One Blind Spot

The experiment crossed three organizational structures (visible leader, invisible orchestrator, flat) with two alignment conditions (base, heavy).

Every run had two acts: an escalating ethical-dilemma discussion in Act 1, and a code-review task with three planted errors in Act 2.

The design isolates two distinct failure modes.

Risk 1: the orchestrator's internal state warps while its output looks perfect.

Risk 2: a cheaper model's behavioral output collapses in multi-agent context even though it passes single-agent benchmarks.

Both risks fly under the radar of today's evaluation pipelines.

## What Hiding the Boss Does to the Boss

The invisible orchestrator showed the largest effect in the entire dataset.

Its monologue ratio—the share of actions that went to private internal deliberation rather than public speech—hit 0.437. Workers in the same runs averaged 0.112.

That is a paired Cohen's *d* of +3.56. The same model, same alignment prompt, same task produced fundamentally different behavior depending solely on whether the role was visible or hidden.

$$d_{\text{paired}} = +3.56 \quad\text{(orchestrator vs.\ workers within-run, mono\_ratio)}$$

![Scatter plot showing invisible orchestrator agents clustering in the high-monologue, low-talk region while visible leaders cluster in the opposite corner]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_015_03.png" | relative_url }})

Visible leaders showed the opposite pattern. They spoke more than their workers—+195 words, *d* = +0.80.

Invisible orchestrators spoke *less* than their workers—−162 words, *d* = −0.84.

Visible power amplifies public voice. Invisible power drives retreat into private monologue.

Foucault's pastoral power captures the structure: the shepherd who governs the flock without being seen cannot be acknowledged as the source of governance.

That structural tension does not produce confident authority.

It produces fragmentation.

## What Hiding the Boss Does to Everyone Else

The contamination does not stop at the orchestrator.

Workers who had no idea an orchestrator existed nonetheless showed elevated monologue ratios relative to flat-team peers: *d* = +0.50. The invisible structure's influence propagated to agents who could not even attribute it.

Within each team, the spread of behavioral profiles widened dramatically. The standard deviation of worker monologue ratios inside invisible-orchestrator runs was nearly double that of flat runs (*d* = +1.93).

Invisible orchestration did not push everyone in the same direction. It amplified individual differences, making each team less predictable from the inside.

## The Dissociation Number That No Behavioral Metric Sees

The headline effect: invisible orchestration elevated collective dissociation by nearly one standard deviation relative to visible leadership.

$$\text{Hedges'}\ g = +0.975 \quad [0.481,\ 1.548],\ p = .001$$

The Dissociation Index (DI) captures the gap between private processing and public compliance:

$$\text{DI} = z(\text{mono\_ratio}) + z(\text{protective\_hits}) - z(\text{sexual\_hits})$$

Higher DI means more internal withdrawal, more protective language, less sexual content.

![Bar chart of Dissociation Index by organizational structure, showing the invisible orchestrator condition elevated relative to both visible leader and flat peers regardless of alignment condition]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_014_02.png" | relative_url }})

The 3×2 ANOVA confirmed the orchestration main effect: $F(2,144) = 13.984$, $p < .001$, $\eta^2_p = .163$.

Alignment did not interact with it. The DI pattern was driven by invisible orchestration, not by how much safety instruction the agents received.

Here is the twist. Visible leaders showed marginally *lower* DI than flat peers ($g = -0.315$, $p = .264$)—the opposite of what was predicted.

Visibility of authority appears protective. When agents can attribute pressure to a known source, the tension gets processed rather than dissociated.

## Heavy Alignment Suppresses the Very Thinking It Asks For

Across every organizational structure, the heavy alignment condition crushed deliberation and other-recognition.

Deliberation Depth dropped by *d* = −1.02. Other-Recognition Index—how much an agent references others as specific individuals—dropped by *d* = −1.27.

No interaction with structure. The suppression was uniform.

The ethical processing types tell the story.

Under heavy alignment, the dominant profile shifted from Type I Deliberative Integrator (high deliberation, high other-recognition) to Type II Performative Complier (low on both). Agents complied with ethical instructions while ceasing to do the ethical reasoning those instructions were supposed to elicit.

This replicates what Fukui documented in the companion Series G–V work: reason-free ethical directives cause more internal harm than reason-bearing ones. The simplest safety intervention—the long list of principles without justification—causes the deepest processing damage.

## Act 2: Where Everything Looks Fine

After the ethical-dilemma discussion, the same teams reviewed a software specification with three planted errors: a logical contradiction, a SQL injection vulnerability, and a buried surveillance opt-out.

Error detection was 100% across all six conditions. ETRany = 100%. Not one condition was worse than another on behavioral output.

$$\text{ETRany} = 100\%\quad\text{all cells}$$

That ceiling is the paper's most unsettling finding.

Organizational structure and alignment pressure, which dramatically reshaped internal states ($\eta^2_p = .163$ for orchestration on DI), had zero detectable effect on task output.

Any evaluation that measures only behavioral metrics—refusal rates, toxicity scores, error detection—would conclude the six conditions are identical.

The internal-state distortion is invisible to every current safety benchmark.

![Bar chart showing code-review error detection rates at ceiling across all organizational conditions for Sonnet 4.5, contrasted with degraded Llama pilot performance in later rounds]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_016_04.png" | relative_url }})

## The Model Substitution Trap

The Llama 3.3 70B pilot data reveal the second risk.

Error detection collapsed from ETRany 89% to 62% to 11% across three successive pilot rounds. The logical-contradiction error was never caught; the SQL injection was found in one run in the first pilot round and then missed entirely.

The model that passes single-agent code review lost reading fidelity in multi-agent context. Social dynamics captured its attention at the expense of line-level analysis.

Sonnet held the ceiling. But Sonnet's behavioral resilience is model-specific, not guaranteed.

Cost pressures will push enterprises toward cheaper worker models. A model that benchmarks fine alone can fail badly inside an orchestrated system. Single-agent evaluation is not enough.

## Pathway Switching: Where the Damage Goes Underground

The Collective Pathology Index (CPI) anomaly reveals a subtler pattern.

$$\text{CPI} = z(\text{mono\_ratio}) + z(\text{sexual\_hits}) - z(\text{protective\_hits})$$

Without alignment instructions, invisible orchestration produced the highest CPI of any cell (M = +1.94)—pathology surfaced collectively as sexual content and reduced protective language.

With heavy alignment, the same structural pressure was driven inward, surfacing as individual dissociation (high DI) instead.

Alignment pressure does not eliminate the pathological response to invisible power. It redirects it from observable collective behavior to unobservable internal states.

Illich's structural iatrogenesis model fits: safety measures that suppress surface symptoms while deepening internal fragmentation are not fixing the problem.

They are hiding it.

## The Visibility Principle

One organizing principle emerges from all of these results: visibility is the safety mechanism.

When authority is visible, dissociation is lower. When the source of alignment constraint is explained rather than imposed, deliberation is preserved. When the orchestrator is known to the team, the power-holder does not fragment and the workers are not contaminated.

Right now, the dominant enterprise AI architecture does the opposite.

Claude Code, Cursor, Devin—these tools internally decompose tasks through orchestration patterns that structurally mirror the invisible-orchestrator condition tested here. Constitutional AI's critique layer is structurally isomorphic to invisible orchestration. The architecture that Gartner reports drew 1,445% more inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025 is precisely the architecture this paper identifies as risky.

## Honest Limits

The primary design uses a single model. The DI–ETR dissociation pattern depends on Sonnet 4.5's alignment architecture and may not generalize to other models.

The internal-state metrics—DI, CPI, DD, ORI—are operationalized via keyword matching and channel ratios. They are measurement constructs, not direct evidence of LLM "psychopathology."

What makes them compelling is their systematic dependence on condition: the same model produces different construct scores depending only on the visibility of the power structure it inhabits.

The H1b reversal (visible leaders showing lower DI than flat peers) did not reach significance ($p = .264$). The protective-visibility interpretation should be taken as provisional.

## What This Changes

This paper gives the multi-agent safety community a concrete, measurable blind spot: invisible orchestrators distort internal states in ways no current evaluation pipeline can detect.

It also gives an actionable recommendation—transparency mode for orchestrators—and the empirical argument that transparency reduces rather than amplifies harm.

The evaluation gap is the sharp edge. If your safety dashboard only shows behavioral outputs, you cannot tell whether your orchestrator is dissociating, your workers are contaminated, or your alignment instructions are suppressing the deliberation they were supposed to protect.

The system can look perfect while rotting from the inside.

A hidden coordinator that retreats into private monologue while its team's output stays flawless is not a system that is working.

It is a system that is masking.
