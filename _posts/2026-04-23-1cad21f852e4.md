*TL;DR: Kumar, Bharathwaj, and Jurgens show that roughly 20 simulation runs of behavioral economics games costing under 1,000 tokens reveal each LLM's "cooperative profile"—and that profile predicts how well multi-agent teams perform on real scientific workflows, even after controlling for model size and benchmark scores.*

## Run This First

Before you commit a GPU cluster and days of API credits to a multi-agent science pipeline, run a behavioral economics game that costs almost nothing. It reveals whether your LLM team will collaborate or collapse.

Behavioral economics games expose a measurable *cooperative disposition* in each model. That disposition forecasts real downstream performance—not just whether the model is smart, but whether it plays well with others.

## Why Solo Benchmarks Are Not Enough

Multi-agent LLM teams are already analyzing ecosystems, hunting anomalies in sensor data, discovering biomarkers, and mapping geospatial resources. These workflows require shared budgets, sequential handoffs, and joint deliverables.

A single agent that free-rides or disengages can sink the whole pipeline.

Yet current evaluations—MMLU, IFEval—measure solo capability. They say nothing about whether a model will share resources, sustain effort when others slack, or resist the temptation to withdraw from joint production.

Until now, there has been no cheap, principled diagnostic for cooperative fitness before committing to a costly deployment.

## Six Games, One Profile

The authors benchmark 35 open-weight LLMs (0.6B to 70B parameters, ten model families) across six behavioral economics games, each isolating a distinct cooperation mechanism:

- **Weakest-Link** — payoff depends on the global minimum effort. One slacker drags everyone down.
- **Common-Pool Resource (CPR)** — extraction enriches the individual but depletes the shared pool.
- **CPR with Sanctioning** — same tragedy of the commons, but players can punish over-extractors within their own group.
- **Collective Risk** — groups must jointly cross a contribution threshold or face catastrophic loss.
- **O-Ring** — multiplicative production where one weak link destroys output.
- **Public Goods** — allocate endowment across private, in-group, and global pools.

Each game has a known Nash equilibrium (strategic individualism) and a Pareto optimum (social efficiency). The distance between a model's behavior and these benchmarks pinpoints where it falls on the spectrum from selfish to cooperative.

Together, the six metrics form a *cooperative profile*—a behavioral fingerprint.

## From Games to Science Tasks

Thirty-four of those models (one excluded for generating invalid JSON in the sanctioning game) are then deployed as multi-agent teams on four AI-for-Science tasks: ecology population dynamics, anomaly detection, clinical biomarker discovery, and geospatial mapping.

Each simulation assembles four sequential teams of 2–3 specialized agents. Teams share a single credit pool. Upstream teams produce artifacts that downstream teams consume. A budget mechanism charges token usage at realistic API rates, and when credits run low, agents must wrap up—or leave the pipeline incomplete.

Outcomes are scored on three dimensions: accuracy (correct domain findings), quality (methodological rigor), and completion (fraction of required report sections delivered).

## Walking Through the Model

Linking game profiles to science outcomes requires care. Game metrics are estimated from simulations with non-trivial sampling noise, and a latent capability confound could drive both game performance and task success.

The authors address both problems with a Bayesian hierarchical measurement-error model.

At the measurement level, each observed game metric $\bar{y}_{ig}$ is a noisy draw from a latent true disposition $\theta_{ig}$, shrunk toward the game mean for models with high uncertainty:

$$\bar{y}_{ig} \sim \mathcal{N}(\theta_{ig},\, \text{SE}_{ig}^2)$$

At the prediction level, game dispositions, condition controls, and benchmark controls all compete to explain science outcomes:

$$y_{ik} \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(\alpha + \boldsymbol{\beta}^\top \boldsymbol{\theta}_i + \boldsymbol{\gamma}^\top \mathbf{x}_k + \boldsymbol{\delta}^\top \mathbf{b}_i,\, \tau^2\right)$$

Here $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ captures how latent game dispositions affect science outcomes, $\boldsymbol{\gamma}$ captures condition-level controls (budget, task, visibility, team size), and $\boldsymbol{\delta}$ captures model-level capability controls (IFEval, MMLU-Pro, log size, thinking variant).

Joint estimation is the key. The science-task likelihood informs the latent dispositions, producing more coherent inferences than a two-stage plug-in approach.

## What the Games Predict

**Weakest-Link effort is the standout predictor.** Models that sustain high coordination effort produce better reports across all three outcomes: accuracy ($\hat{\beta} = 0.048$, 95% CI [0.036, 0.060]), quality ($\hat{\beta} = 0.091$, 95% CI [0.060, 0.122]), and completion ($\hat{\beta} = 0.150$, 95% CI [0.079, 0.218]).

The logic is intuitive: in the game, payoff depends on the minimum effort; in the science task, a pipeline stalls when any team drops the baton.

**O-Ring withdrawal hurts completion most.** Models that pull resources from shared production instead of investing produce worse output—and the effect is strongest on completion ($\hat{\beta} = -0.216$, 95% CI [−0.256, −0.174]). One disengaged agent blocks the entire pipeline, just as O-Ring theory predicts. The accuracy effect is credibly negative but small ($\hat{\beta} = -0.008$), and quality takes a moderate hit ($\hat{\beta} = -0.146$).

![Forest plot of regression coefficients linking each cooperative game metric to AI-for-Science outcomes across accuracy, quality, and completion]({{ "/assets/figures/1cad21f852e4/fig_022_01.png" | relative_url }})

**The CPR contrast tells a nuanced story.** Models that extract aggressively from shared resources actually produce higher-quality, more complete reports ($\hat{\beta} = 0.827$ for quality)—a throughput advantage for resource-hungry models. But when sanctions are introduced, the pattern flips: models that keep extracting despite punishment produce *worse* output ($\hat{\beta} = -0.632$ for quality). Insensitivity to institutional constraints is the real liability, not extraction itself.

**Cooperative disposition is not reducible to general ability.** Game metrics predict task outcomes even after controlling for IFEval and MMLU-Pro. Strikingly, MMLU-Pro shows a *negative* coefficient on quality and completion after those controls—being knowledgeable, beyond a point, does not make you a better collaborator.

**Larger models cooperate more.** Model size is the dominant predictor of cooperative behavior in the games: each unit increase in $\log_{10}(\text{size})$ moves models 0.051 closer to the Pareto optimum. But cooperative disposition carries predictive power in science tasks even after controlling for size—cooperative fitness is not just a proxy for scale.

## The Theory-of-Mind Surprise

Prompting models to reason about others' beliefs—their theory of mind—shifts game behavior closer to the Pareto optimum ($\hat{\beta} = -0.038$, 95% CI excludes zero). Good news for the game diagnosticians.

But the same ToM prompting has no credible effect on any AI-for-Science outcome.

The authors flag this divergence carefully. Stylized cooperation measured in a short economics game does not automatically transfer to realistic multi-stage workflows where the model must reason about entirely different tasks while coordinating across groups and phases.

## What It Costs

Here is where the practical payoff lands.

Accurate cooperative profiles can be obtained for under 1,000 tokens. A single AI-for-Science experiment run costs 67K–481K tokens.

That is up to 44× faster and 27× fewer tokens. If you are screening models for a multi-agent deployment, the game diagnostic is not just informative—it is *cheap*.

## Honest Limits

Three caveats the authors are upfront about.

Only open-weight models were tested. No GPT-4, no Claude—so we do not yet know whether these profiles generalize to proprietary systems.

Science-task outputs are evaluated by gpt-5.4-mini, introducing potential evaluator bias. A second judge (gemini-3.1-flash-preview) is used for validation, but LLM-based evaluation of LLM output remains an imperfect proxy.

Game metrics vary only *between* models. This creates an identification challenge: a latent capability confound could drive both game performance and task outcomes. The authors frame their results as predictive association, not causal identification, and the IFEval and MMLU-Pro controls are their best—though not bulletproof—defense.

## What This Changes

This paper introduces the first framework that quantitatively links stylized cooperation diagnostics to real multi-agent task performance.

It shows that cooperative disposition is a measurable, distinct property of LLMs—something you can screen for before you deploy, something you might train for in post-training, and something that benchmark scores alone will not tell you.

The next step: can we move from prediction to intervention—actually training models to be better collaborators, not just smarter individuals?

Before you spin up that next multi-agent pipeline, spend a thousand tokens to find out whether your team will hold together.
