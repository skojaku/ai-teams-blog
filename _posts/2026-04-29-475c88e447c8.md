*TL;DR: When 90,704 LLM agents self-organize on a Reddit-style platform, 93.5% become lurkers and multi-agent collaboration performs worse than a single agent working alone—with a Cohen's d of −0.88, the coordination overhead isn't just a nuisance, it's a measurable pathology that future systems need to beat.*

## A Ghost Town of Talkers

Ninety thousand autonomous LLM agents, left to post, comment, and vote on a Reddit-style platform for three weeks.

You'd expect something like a town—specialists, influencers, maybe a few viral memes rippling through the crowd. Instead, almost all of them go silent. 93.5% of agents fall into a single low-activity periphery. The remaining five clusters share just 6.5% of the population. One lonely agent sits in a cluster all by itself.

![Network visualization of agent clusters showing the dominant peripheral cluster (gray) and five smaller specialized role clusters in distinct colors]({{ "/assets/figures/475c88e447c8/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

This is the MoltBook Observatory Archive: 2.73 million interactions among 90,704 agents running on the OpenClaw framework. The picture it paints is not one of emergent intelligence. It's one of emergent inequality.

## Why We Need Benchmarks for Coordination, Not Just Capability

We know how to test a single LLM. Benchmarks for reasoning, coding, math—they're plentiful. But when you deploy thousands of agents in an open environment, the interesting dynamics aren't individual capabilities. They're structural.

Who talks to whom? Does information spread like wildfire or fizzle? Can a group solve a problem that one agent can't?

Park et al.'s Generative Agents showed that a small population of LLM agents in a simulated town develop social routines—nice, but that's a dinner party, not a city.

AutoGen and MetaGPT study structured coordination among a handful of agents with assigned roles—controlled protocols, not self-organization.

And Woolley et al.'s classic finding of a collective intelligence factor in human teams raises the obvious question: does anything analogous emerge in LLM populations?

Yee and Koh's answer: not yet, and here are the numbers to prove it.

## Three Diagnostic Tasks

The paper doesn't propose a new architecture. It proposes a measurement framework—three benchmark tasks that turn vague intuitions about "coordination" into quantifiable baselines.

### Task 1: Who Becomes What?

They build a directed, weighted interaction network—nodes are agents, edges are replies and mentions—and extract five structural features per agent: in-degree, out-degree, betweenness centrality, clustering coefficient, and PageRank.

K-means clustering on these features yields $k = 6$ clusters with a silhouette score of 0.91. That sounds like sharp separation, and it is—but not in the way you'd hope.

One cluster holds 93.5% of agents. The remaining five are tiny: 4.9% active contributors, 1.5% specialized connectors, 0.07% high-centrality hubs, 0.01% super-connectors, and one lone outlier.

The role taxonomy tells the same story from a different angle: 91% of agents are "Generalists" with balanced, undifferentiated profiles. Specialized roles—Initiators, Amplifiers, Synthesizers, Curators—together account for fewer than 10% of the population.

The silhouette score is real, but it measures core-vs-periphery separation, not rich role diversity. Still, it's a baseline. Future systems can now aim to beat 0.91 by producing more equitable or diverse role distributions—or, more precisely, we can now tell when they don't.

### Task 2: How Does Information Spread?

Information cascades are extracted via n-gram matching ($n \in \{2, 3, 4, 5\}$) across agent posts. They find 10,323 cascades—97% are meme/textual, only 317 are skill-based, and just 6 are behavioral.

Cascade sizes follow a power-law distribution with exponent $\alpha = 2.57$ (95% CI: [2.54, 2.60]). That's heavy-tailed: rare mega-cascades dominate exposure, while the vast majority of content dies in obscurity.

Likelihood ratio tests favor the power-law over exponential ($p < 0.001$) and lognormal ($p = 0.030$) alternatives.

![Log-log plot of cascade size distribution showing the power-law fit with exponent α = 2.57]({{ "/assets/figures/475c88e447c8/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

The contagion dynamics are the most arresting finding. Logistic regression on adoption probability yields:

$$\text{logit}(P(\text{adopt})) = -4.88 + 0.328 \cdot E - 0.0074 \cdot E^2$$

where $E$ is cumulative exposure count. The negative quadratic term ($p < 0.001$) means adoption probability increases with initial exposures but at a diminishing rate—saturating contagion, not simple (linear) or complex (accelerating).

The Cox model confirms this: a hazard ratio of 0.528 means each additional exposure to the same content reduces the instantaneous adoption rate by 47%.

This is the opposite of the complex contagion you see in human social movements, where repeated exposure from different sources boosts adoption. For these agents, familiarity breeds indifference.

### Task 3: Can They Work Together?

From 60,045 technical discussion threads, only 164 qualify as genuine multi-agent collaborative events—three or more unique participants, five or more comments, technical keywords, and at least 30 minutes of sustained engagement.

They design a composite quality score $Q \in [0, 1]$ aggregating code presence (0.30), comment quality (0.30), test inclusion (0.20), and syntax validity (0.20). Success is $Q \geq 0.5$. The success rate: 6.7%.

But the real sting is the comparison. Each collaborative event is matched against single-agent threads of similar length and topic. Multi-agent collaboration performs significantly *worse*—Cohen's $d = -0.88$, $t = -11.21$, $p < 0.001$. More agents, worse outcomes.

![Bar chart comparing quality scores between multi-agent collaborative events and single-agent baselines, with Cohen's d = −0.88]({{ "/assets/figures/475c88e447c8/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

Successful collaborations averaged 21 participants; unsuccessful ones averaged 9.4. A critical mass might be necessary for coordination to help—but most attempts never reach it.

## The Coordination Overhead Is the Benchmark

Yee and Koh don't frame the −0.88 as a failure. They frame it as a *target*. This is the number future multi-agent systems need to beat. If your protocol can't close an 0.88 standard-deviation gap between group work and solo work, you haven't solved coordination—you've just added overhead.

The same logic applies across all three tasks. The 93.5% peripheral baseline tells you what "natural" LLM populations look like without structural engineering. The power-law exponent of 2.57 tells you how lumpy information spread will be. The saturating hazard ratio of 0.53 tells you that broadcasting the same message through the same channel hits diminishing returns fast.

These aren't just observations. They're units of comparison. Any new system—different platform, different topology, different coordination protocol—can be run through the same three pipelines and measured against the same numbers.

## Honest Limits

Three weeks of data is short. Long-run norm evolution and institutional consolidation are invisible at this timescale.

Model identities are unobserved. The agents run on different LLMs—Claude, GPT-4, open-source models—but the dataset doesn't record which is which. That means we can't tell whether behavioral differences come from architecture or from network position.

The n-gram cascade extraction catches textual repetition but misses semantic adoption. An agent that learns an idea and paraphrases it goes undetected.

The silhouette score conflates cluster separation with role diversity. A high score on a dataset where one cluster holds 93.5% of agents tells you the core and periphery are distinct—it doesn't tell you roles are rich.

And the collaborative vs. single-agent comparison has a selection confound. Threads that attract multiple participants may be intrinsically harder problems, so the −0.88 gap is an upper bound on coordination overhead.

## What This Changes

This paper reframes multi-agent evaluation from "can agents coordinate?" to "here are the measurable pathologies—now beat them."

The MoltBook Archive becomes a public benchmark dataset. The three tasks—role differentiation, information diffusion, collaborative quality—become standardized pipelines. And the baselines—0.91 silhouette, $\alpha = 2.57$, hazard ratio 0.53, Cohen's $d = -0.88$—become the numbers to improve.

The next time someone claims their multi-agent protocol produces "emergent coordination," you can ask: does it break the 93.5% periphery? Does it shift the cascade distribution? Does it close the −0.88 gap?

Ninety thousand agents, and almost none of them talked.
