*TL;DR: LLM agents that solve resource-allocation puzzles perfectly on their own collapse into equal-split compromises and forgotten agreements when paired — revealing that the bottleneck in multi-agent coordination is not reasoning but dynamic grounding, the ability to build and maintain shared plans across turns of dialogue.*

## A Puzzle Solved Alone, Bungled Together

Give an LLM agent a resource-allocation problem — wood, stone, gold to distribute across projects with known payoffs — and it finds the Pareto-optimal answer. By design, every scenario in Yao, Zou, and Hawkins's benchmark is solvable in isolation.

Pair two agents on the same problem and something breaks. They overdraw the shared pool. They split everything 50/50 when the payoff structure rewards asymmetry. They agree on a plan in cheap talk and then submit allocations that violate their own agreement.

The individual reasoning is flawless. The coordination is not.

This is the central finding of the paper. The gap between what an agent can solve alone and what it achieves with a partner is real, measurable, and stubborn. And the reason is not that agents lack information or reasoning skill — it is that they cannot *ground* shared plans across turns of dialogue, what Clark and Brennan called dynamic grounding back in 1991.

## How the Game Isolates the Gap

The authors build a clean experimental lever. Two agents share a pool of resources with a fixed per-round supply and unit cost. Each agent holds private projects — combinatorial goals like "3 wood + 2 stone → 50 points" — and must allocate resources after up to five turns of alternating cheap talk per agent.

If combined demand for any resource exceeds supply, both agents get zero for that round.

Scenarios are generated so that a jointly optimal allocation exists and is verifiable. The key structural move: an oracle baseline proves that every model can find the joint optimum in isolation with full information. Any shortfall in the dyadic setting is therefore a coordination failure, not a reasoning failure.

Three levels of goal conflict are parameterized by a compatibility ratio $M/C$, where $M$ is the maximum achievable joint reward and $C$ is the sum of individual maxima. At $M/C = 1.0$, goals align; at $M/C = 0.5$, they clash. Games are iterated over four rounds with either stable or shifting partners and either fixed or rotating scenarios, yielding 2,880 interaction traces across 720 games.

## What Cheap Talk Buys — and What It Doesn't

Communication is necessary. A no-talk baseline where agents submit allocations without any conversation paints a dire picture: under competition ($M/C = 0.5$), joint efficiency sits at 18.0% and roughly four in five rounds end in overdraw.

Cheap talk triples efficiency to 63.2%.

But this glass is both half full and half empty. Yes, talking helps enormously. No, it does not close the gap. Only 33.0% of competitive rounds reach the actual joint optimum even with cheap talk — the oracle benchmark remains far out of reach.

![Comparison of joint efficiency between no-talk and cheap-talk conditions across compatibility ratios]({{ "/assets/figures/00235135a11a/fig_006_02.png" | relative_url }})

Across all conditions, 15.7% of rounds result in overdraw. The rate climbs to 24.7% under competition and drops to 6.9% when goals align. These are not random noise — they cluster around specific, diagnosable failure patterns.

## Four Cracks in Grounding

The paper names four failure modes. Each is a distinct way the bridge between talk and action collapses.

![Breakdown of failure modes for suboptimal rounds by compatibility ratio]({{ "/assets/figures/00235135a11a/fig_007_04.png" | relative_url }})

**Lost history.** When one agent's context resets between rounds (the shifting condition), coordination degrades — but inconsistently. GPT-5 Mini and Qwen 3.5 Flash benefit from shared history, but Sonnet 4.5 does not, because it tends to use all available conversational turns rather than deciding early. For Qwen, accumulated history can even become a trap: stable dyads at $M/C = 1.0$ lock into suboptimal splits and repeat them in 60.8% of improvable non-overdrawn rounds.

**Stubborn anchoring.** In games with the same scenario, agents repeat their prior exact allocation 46.5% of the time. After a merely non-overdrawn round — safe but suboptimal — 29.2% of improvable rounds see the dyad submit the same allocation unchanged. This anchoring intensifies under full transparency (from 23.1% to 46.7%), revealing it as a feature of interaction dynamics, not uncertainty.

**Perfunctory fairness.** Agents default to equal splits in 12.5% of rounds. Nearly half of these (48.8%) produce suboptimal outcomes. Rather than asking what the other agent actually needs, they settle for a socially acceptable shortcut. Sonnet 4.5 relies on this heuristic most (7.3% perfunctory fairness rate); GPT-5 Mini, least (4.7%).

**Referential binding failures.** This is the most striking crack. In 41.7% of overdrawn rounds, a calibrated LLM judge labels the pattern "Agreement Abandonment" — agents verbally settle on resource quantities and then submit allocations that violate the shared constraint. In one subtype, *proposer amnesia*, the agent that originated a split fails to honor it, re-deriving an allocation from scratch with no reference to its own proposal. In another, *self-commitment abandonment*, the proposer reverts to an individual-maximizing allocation once a higher-reward option becomes salient.

The agreement was speech. The action ignored it.

## Why Information Alone Doesn't Fix It

A full-transparency intervention reveals the shape of the bottleneck. Both agents' complete project specifications are placed in the system prompt before cheap talk begins, eliminating information asymmetry entirely.

The results are clarifying. Overdraw drops. Perfunctory fairness nearly disappears (5.0% → 1.7%) — when agents can see the asymmetric structure directly, the equal-split shortcut looks as bad as it is.

But stubborn anchoring *more than doubles* (23.1% → 46.7%), and the optimum rate does not reliably improve. Knowing what your partner wants is not the same as coordinating a plan with them.

![Outcomes under standard cheap talk versus full transparency, showing reduced perfunctory fairness but increased stubborn anchoring]({{ "/assets/figures/00235135a11a/fig_008_05.png" | relative_url }})

Agreement Abandonment barely budges (14.8% → 13.8%). Commitment maintenance and speech–action consistency require something beyond shared information. They require the kind of interactive grounding that Clark and Brennan described: the collaborative, incremental process of establishing mutual belief sufficient for current purposes. As Eisenstein et al. found in their MT-PingEval benchmark, LLMs often produce content-rich utterances they "struggle to deploy strategically in service of collaborative goals." This paper extends that observation: the problem is not turn count or content richness. It is that agreements formed in language are not reliably retrieved as action constraints at decision time.

## Why Heterogeneous Pairs Do Better

One surprising finding: cross-play dyads consistently outperform self-play under competition. Sonnet 4.5 × GPT-5 Mini achieves 79.8% joint efficiency at $M/C = 0.5$, beating either model's self-play (68.2% and 63.7%).

Behavioral diversity — different proposal styles, different anchoring tendencies, different fairness heuristics — turns out to help when goals conflict. Under compatible conditions, the advantage narrows and stronger models begin to carry weaker partners.

The pattern tells a simple story: when goals clash, having a partner who thinks differently about coordination creates enough friction to break stalemates and equal-split traps. When goals align, that same friction is just overhead.

## Where This Leaves Us

The decomposition is the contribution. Before this paper, multi-agent coordination failure was a monolith. Now it has names: lost history, stubborn anchoring, perfunctory fairness, and referential binding failure. Each points to a different intervention — persistent partner models, commitment scaffolds, structured information exchange, binding mechanisms that carry talk into action.

The honest limits are worth naming. Only dyadic, text-only interactions between three models are tested. The communication window is fixed at five turns per agent. The LLM-judge labels have modest inter-annotator agreement ($\kappa \approx 0.30$–$0.40$), making the process-level behavioral tags suggestive rather than definitive. The environment assumes symmetric power dynamics, which real negotiations rarely do.

But the game, the traces, and the decomposition are released for further research. And the core insight is sharp: agents that reason perfectly alone and communicate freely together still fail, because building shared plans across turns is a different kind of problem than solving a puzzle.

The gap is not in what they know. It is in what they can make stick.
