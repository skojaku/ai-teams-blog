*TL;DR: LATTE gives LLM agent teams a shared, mutable task graph they maintain together—cutting token costs roughly in half while outperforming every baseline, from MetaGPT to decentralized debate.*

Imagine five LLM agents assigned to debug a codebase. One rewrites a function another just fixed. Two agents edit the same file simultaneously. A third spins its wheels for ten minutes on a subtask that depends on something no one has done yet.

This is what unstructured multi-agent collaboration looks like: tangled, wasteful, somehow less than the sum of its parts.

LATTE (Language Agent Teams for Task Evolution) resolves the mess by giving agent teams something distributed systems have had for decades—an explicit coordination structure that evolves during execution.

## Where Teams Fall Apart

Current LLM team designs fall into one of two traps.

On one side, fixed pipelines like MetaGPT and ChatDev assign roles and task orders *before execution begins*. The pipeline runs, and if reality deviates from the plan—if a bug turns out to be three bugs, if a dataset has surprises—there is no mechanism to adapt.

On the other side, fully decentralized teams (think multi-agent debate from Du et al.) let agents self-organize freely. Without shared scaffolding, they over-communicate, overwrite each other's work, and sometimes can't outperform a single expert model.

Structure prevents chaos, but over-specification kills the flexibility that makes LLMs powerful.

LATTE's insight: let the team *build and revise its own structure* as it works.

## The Coordination Graph

At LATTE's heart is a dynamic coordination graph—a directed acyclic graph where each node is a subtask with a status and an assigned agent, and each edge is a completion dependency. If node $v$ depends on node $u$, then $v$ cannot start until $u$ is done.

$$G_t = (V_t, E_t, \lambda_t)$$

Here $V_t$ is the set of subtask nodes, $E_t$ the dependency edges, and $\lambda_t$ maps each node to an agent and a status from $\{$`pending`, `assigned`, `in_progress`, `done`, `verified`$\}$.

The key property is the *frontier* $F_t$: the set of pending nodes whose dependencies are all satisfied. These are the tasks ready to go right now, and their count equals the maximum number of workers who can proceed in parallel without violating any constraint.

$$F_t = \{v \in V_t \mid \text{status}(v) = \text{pending} \land \forall(u,v) \in E_t, \text{status}(u) = \text{done}\}$$

No valid protocol can dispatch more workers than $|F_t|$ at round $t$.

The graph encodes the maximum parallelism that dependency structure allows.

## Seven Operators

LATTE gives the team seven graph mutation operators, each with explicit preconditions and postconditions that preserve the DAG invariant:

| Operator | Who calls it | What it does |
|----------|-------------|---------------|
| `DISCOVER` | Lead or Worker | Add a new subtask node |
| `ASSIGN` | Lead | Assign a pending node to a Worker |
| `CLAIM` | Worker | Worker self-assigns a frontier node |
| `COMPLETE` | Worker | Mark assigned node as done |
| `RELEASE` | Lead | Return a stalled node to pending |
| `CLOSE` | Lead | Force-complete a node |
| `VERIFY` | Lead | Spawn a verification subtask |

The division of labor is deliberate.

Workers have local visibility—they know their own subtask and can propose new subtasks they discover while working (`DISCOVER`) and certify completion (`COMPLETE`). The Lead has global visibility and controls operators with graph-wide consequences: assigning work, releasing stragglers, and triggering selective verification.

`CLAIM` is where LATTE borrows from work-stealing in distributed computing. Instead of the Lead micromanaging every assignment, idle Workers grab frontier tasks directly. Fast agents pick up work without waiting. The Lead's bottleneck dissolves.

## Fault Tolerance Emerges

Two of the seven operators exist specifically for when things go wrong.

A heartbeat mechanism monitors each Worker, and if one stalls, the Lead can `RELEASE` that node back to the pool—making it available for another agent to claim. The Lead can also `VERIFY` a subtask it finds uncertain, spawning an explicit review without blanket-checking everything.

Across 300 trials, `RELEASE` fired in 36% of trials and `VERIFY` in 19%.

The system uses these recovery tools when it needs them and skips them when it doesn't—emergent fault tolerance without hard-coding every failure mode.

## The Numbers

Across three tasks (exploratory data analysis, debugging, library extension) and two base models (Claude Sonnet 4-6, GPT-5.2), LATTE Pareto-dominates every baseline on accuracy vs. token cost and matches or beats every baseline on wall-clock time.

**Accuracy.** LATTE hits 79.7% overall, beating Leader-Worker (70.1%), decentralized teams (73.9%), static graphs (57.6%), and MetaGPT (33.9%). The gap with decentralized teams is not statistically significant at conventional thresholds—the mean is higher, but the evidence is suggestive rather than decisive.

![Pareto frontier showing LATTE achieves the highest accuracy at the lowest token cost, dominating all baselines on the accuracy-vs-efficiency tradeoff]({{ "/assets/figures/4f17f72392e2/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

**Token cost.** LATTE averages 47.5% normalized token cost—roughly half the next-best method (static graph at 86.9%).

Every other baseline is more than double: Leader-Worker at 104.2%, decentralized at 120.9%, MetaGPT at 228.7%.

**Coordination quality.** Overwrites drop from 22.8× per trial (Leader-Worker) and 35.4× (decentralized) to just 4.3× under LATTE—a 5.3× to 8.2× reduction.

![Bar chart comparing overwrite rates across methods, with LATTE at 4.3 overwrites per trial versus 22.8 for Leader-Worker and 35.4 for decentralized]({{ "/assets/figures/4f17f72392e2/fig_009_02.png" | relative_url }})

Wasted characters fall from 45,436 and 78,062 down to 5,236.

**Idle computation.** LATTE agents are active only 48.7% of rounds, versus 80% for Leader-Worker and 100% for decentralized.

They spend half the time idle *by design*—there's no point burning tokens when the frontier is empty.

**Straggler tail latency.** Node completion time at the 95th percentile: 130 seconds for LATTE versus 294 seconds for static teams.

A 2.3× speedup at the tail.

These coordination metrics—overwrite rate, concurrent conflicts, wasted characters, idle rounds, straggler tail latency—fill a gap. Most multi-agent LLM evaluations measure only task accuracy. LATTE makes the *cost of coordination* visible and measurable.

## Why Adaptive Beats Static

The static graph ablation is the most revealing baseline. Same graph initialization, same team size—but once planning ends, the graph freezes. No `DISCOVER`, no `CLAIM`, no `RELEASE`.

It scores 57.6% accuracy versus LATTE's 79.7%, and it burns nearly twice the tokens.

The gap tells you what the dynamic operators are worth.

When a Worker hits an unexpected subtask—say, a data column that needs cleaning before it can be analyzed—`DISCOVER` lets the team grow the graph mid-execution. When a Worker finishes early, `CLAIM` prevents idle time. When someone stalls, `RELEASE` recovers the work.

Static graphs can't do any of this, and the performance shows it.

Interestingly, the static ablation achieves comparable accuracy on Task 3 (library extension), where the required subtasks are known in advance and the initial graph is well-specified.

LATTE's advantage comes from *not knowing what you'll need*—which is most of the time.

## The Limits

LATTE isn't free. The graph initialization phase adds planning overhead that may not pay off on short, simple tasks where a single agent would suffice.

The evaluation is limited to coding-oriented domains with natural subtask boundaries. It's an open question how well the operator set extends to less structured, open-ended reasoning.

Team size is fixed at five agents throughout—matching MetaGPT's default—so scalability to larger teams remains untested.

The verification mechanism is emergent and encouraging: Leads trigger `VERIFY` more often in difficult trials (18.1 average rounds) than easy ones (8.1 rounds, with zero verification events). But it's still heuristic, not learned. Understanding how to strengthen it is a clear open question.

## What This Changes

LATTE flips an assumption that has held since the first multi-agent LLM frameworks: that coordination structure must be imposed by the system designer before execution starts.

Instead, the agents maintain their own coordination structure online—adding nodes when they discover new subtasks, claiming frontier work when they're idle, releasing stalled tasks when teammates freeze.

Token costs halve. Overwrites drop by an order of magnitude. Accuracy goes up.

The coordination graph itself becomes an audit trail—you can read off exactly why a team did what it did, which is more than you can say for five agents chatting in an unstructured channel.

When agents build the scaffolding themselves, the scaffolding actually fits.
