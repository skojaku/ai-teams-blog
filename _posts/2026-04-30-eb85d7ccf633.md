*TL;DR: When you give seven LLM agents different stakeholder perspectives on a policy question, they still converge on the same answer — but assigning each perspective to a different model cuts that concentration roughly in half, with one catch: quality-weighting those votes can backfire depending on the structure of the question.*

## The Consensus Nobody Asked For

You assign one agent the Security Focus perspective, another Risk Tolerance, a third Pragmatism. You give them different system prompts, different value frameworks, different lenses on a policy choice.

And still—They. All. Agree.

This is the failure mode Ariel Sela calls *artificial consensus*, and it is specific to normative deliberation. In a math problem, convergence is the point. In a policy simulation, convergence is a bug. It hides the very trade-offs the system was built to surface.

Park et al. measured convergence scores above 0.88 between agents assigned opposing personas. Pitre et al. showed that sycophantic conformity is a core challenge in multi-agent debate—agents systematically converge toward shared positions. The problem is documented. The fix, it turns out, depends on whether the question has a right answer at all.

## The Old Way and the New Way

The old way: prompt one model seven ways. Assign different system prompts, different values, different stakeholder frames. Hope that persona engineering alone will produce genuine disagreement.

It doesn't, because every prompt runs on the same inductive biases—the same training data, the same RLHF preferences, the same implicit value hierarchy baked into the weights.

The new way: give each perspective its own model. Assign one evaluator to DeepSeek-R1-8B, another to Mistral-7B, a third to Qwen3-8B. Now the disagreement is architectural, not just rhetorical.

This is the core intervention in Sela's AI Council framework, and it works. First-choice concentration drops from 70.9% to 46.1% ($r = 0.58$) in the child welfare scenario and from 46.0% to 22.9% ($r = 0.50$) in the housing scenario. Those are large effect sizes by any standard.

## How the AI Council Works

The AI Council runs in three phases.

### Structured Debate

Three champion agents each advocate for one policy option across three rounds—position papers, critiques, and defenses. Champions critique competing options through their own value lens, not from a neutral stance. A Security-focused champion attacks alternatives on safety grounds.

### Independent Evaluation

Seven evaluator agents, each primed with a distinct value perspective, read the full debate corpus and rank all three options. They never see each other's output.

This isolation matters. Sela's failed Delphi experiments showed that 8B models have no "consider and reject" gear—expose them to a counter-argument and they either hold or capitulate entirely. Peer exposure amplified artificial consensus rather than correcting it.

The seven models run locally on consumer hardware via Ollama: Qwen3-8B, Mistral-NeMo, Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3, Qwen2.5-Coder-7B, Dolphin3-8B, DeepSeek-R1-8B, and Gemma2-9B. Each is matched to its evaluator role through a keyword-frequency profiling battery. Coherence validation is the only step that calls a frontier model.

### Coherence Validation

A frontier model (Claude Sonnet 4) scores each evaluator's reasoning for alignment with its assigned value perspective. The judge receives only the perspective name, its definition, and the evaluator's reasoning text—no votes, no policy option names, no other evaluator's output.

These coherence scores weight the Borda count. A low-coherence evaluator still votes, but at reduced weight. The Voice Authenticity Rate (fraction of evaluators scoring $\geq 0.6$) provides a quality diagnostic. Cross-model validation with GPT-5.2 confirms the rankings are robust (Pearson $r = 0.74$–$0.88$), even if absolute levels differ.

## Where the Agreement Breaks

Heterogeneity is the headline.

In the child welfare scenario—one option clearly better-resourced—heterogeneity drops first-choice concentration from 70.9% to 46.1%. The dominant option still wins 32 of 35 runs, but minority voices appear.

In the urban housing scenario—three genuinely competitive options—the shift is dramatic. The homogeneous baseline produces a single-option dictatorship (Public Housing wins 23 of 25). Heterogeneity produces three-way competition: Rent Control 15, Public Housing 12, Deregulate 3, Tie 5.

$$\text{FCC} = \frac{\max(v_1, v_2, v_3) - \lfloor N/K \rfloor}{N - \lfloor N/K \rfloor}$$

The pattern flips prior expectations. Fang et al.'s A-HMAD found that heterogeneous model pools do *not* reduce convergence in accuracy-oriented debate. Chen et al.'s ReConcile showed diversity helps you converge on the *right* answer. Those results make sense: when a ground truth anchors the outcome, all models are pulled toward the same attractor regardless of their architecture.

Remove the ground truth—enter normative territory—and shared inductive bias becomes an anchor toward spurious agreement. Different models, different anchors, less spurious agreement.

Roughly half of theoretical value tensions are trustworthy: 55.2% in child welfare, 44.8% in housing. The rest are either suspect agreement (both sides agree but at least one isn't reasoning faithfully) or partial (one side's reasoning is too muddled to interpret). This is a baseline diagnostic for 7–9B deliberation capability, not a ceiling.

## The Fidelity–Diversity Tradeoff

Here is where coherence validation gets complicated.

In the child welfare scenario, coherence weighting *helps*. It drops concentration from 46.1% to 40.8% by downweighting low-coherence evaluators who happen to agree with the majority. Pragmatism (0.59) and Simplicity Preference (0.61) vote for the same dominant option as everyone else, but with shakier reasoning. Downweighting them amplifies what little dissent exists.

In the housing scenario, coherence weighting *hurts*. It increases concentration from 22.9% to 26.6%. The two highest-coherence perspectives—Performance Focus (0.95, DeepSeek-R1-8B) and Risk Tolerance (0.86, Mistral-NeMo)—both cluster on Deregulate. The two lowest-coherence perspectives—Pragmatism (0.63, Mistral-7B) and Simplicity Preference (0.61, Dolphin3-8B)—are spread across Rent Control and Public Housing. Amplify the coherent voices and you amplify the Deregulate coalition.

This is not a bug. The coherence validator is identifying faithful reasoning correctly. The problem is that faithful reasoning from a stronger model does not produce more diverse viewpoints—it produces more coherent versions of the same viewpoint.

When model quality varies across perspectives, fidelity and diversity become competing objectives.

The right move: always report both weighted and unweighted results. When they diverge, the divergence itself is informative. It tells you which perspectives are being modeled less faithfully, and it warns you that the disagreement surface needs interpretation.

## No Middle Gear

Three Delphi designs failed before the current external validation approach succeeded.

**Delphi v6.0** showed evaluators all their peers' critiques. The 8B models were overwhelmed and fled to whichever option hadn't been criticized—a third option nobody originally endorsed.

**Delphi v6.1** filtered to only tension-partner critiques. A single relevant counter-argument still destabilized the models. Two rounds compounded sycophantic flips.

**Delphi v7** asked evaluators to self-reflect on whether their reasoning was consistent with their assigned values. Across 10 test runs, zero vote changes. The models simply re-read their own reasoning and reiterated it.

The pattern: 8B models have a binary response to pushback. Hold entirely or capitulate entirely. No gradient. This constraint motivated the move to external validation—don't modify the deliberation, just score it afterward.

## Honest Limits

Three of seven evaluators share the Security Focus perspective due to a trait-resolution bug (tied parameters defaulting via Python dictionary ordering). This leaves five effectively independent perspectives rather than six, and structurally overweights Security Focus concerns.

Model–role assignments came from keyword-frequency profiling, not randomization. This confounds model capability with perspective fit. The correlation between profiling alignment and deliberation coherence is negative ($r = -0.83$ to $-0.94$)—the model that best echoed a perspective's characteristic vocabulary (Mistral-7B on Pragmatism: 0.667) produced the lowest coherence in actual debate (0.59–0.63).

Meanwhile, the model with the worst profiling alignment (DeepSeek-R1-8B on Performance Focus: 0.375) produced the highest coherence (0.91–0.95). Keyword frequency does not capture what matters under debate pressure.

Only two scenarios were tested. The fidelity–diversity tradeoff may be general, but confirmation across additional domains is needed.

## What to Take Forward

Architectural heterogeneity is a practical, low-cost intervention. Use different models per perspective. The effect sizes are too large to ignore: a 25 percentage point reduction in one scenario, 23 in another. This should be the default for any multi-agent normative deliberation system.

The fidelity–diversity tradeoff is a generalizable warning. Any system that weights agent contributions by quality will face the same tension.

Report both weighted and unweighted results. Treat the gap as data.

And the binary response pattern in small models is a hard constraint for system design. At the 7–9B scale, agents cannot absorb and selectively reject counter-arguments. Isolation during evaluation isn't an optimization—it's a necessity.

Convergence is the point when there's a right answer. Convergence is the bug when there isn't.
