*TL;DR: A 3B-parameter model trained to play four roles inside one shared backbone matches or beats specialized GUI agents up to 24× its size on grounding—and when paired with a big planner, it surpasses 72B monoliths on real-device benchmarks.*

## The Cost–Scalability Trap

Three heavyweight models—UI-TARS-72B-DPO, Tesseract OCR, UNO—just to tap a button on a real phone.

That is Agent-S2's bill for a single grounded action, and it is brutal. You can pay for a 72B model and get a system that reasons over long horizons, delegates to specialist executors, and actually finishes tasks on real devices. But every component is expensive.

Or you can go small. Sub-7B agents trained end-to-end look great on static step-wise benchmarks.

Then you let them loose in an app with more than a few screens, and they collapse. The reasoning gets tangled. The action hallucinations stack up. Context windows fill with history the model cannot manage.

Cheap and dumb, or smart and expensive. LAMO asks whether that tradeoff is actually necessary.

## One Backbone, Four Hats

The core move is deceptively simple: instead of training a 3B model to do everything end-to-end, train it to do one thing at a time—then orchestrate those skills at inference via context engineering.

The framework—Lightweight Agent Multi-role Orchestration—decomposes GUI automation into five skill categories: Action–Tool Alignment (mapping instructions to PyAutoGUI calls), Logic-Consistent Chain-of-Thought (step-wise reasoning grounded in history), Screen Understanding (describing what is on screen), Goal Planning (breaking goals into subtasks), and Screen Grounding (locating UI elements by coordinate).

Teacher models distill skill-specific data for each: Qwen-2.5-VL-72B-Instruct for alignment and grounding, Gemini-2.5-Pro for understanding, planning, and reasoning.

At inference, the same 3B backbone puts on four hats—Observer, Planner, Allocator, Executor—via different prompt contexts.

No extra parameters. No separate models. Just the same weights with different jobs, each seeing only the context it needs.

![Architecture diagram showing LAMO's 3B backbone instantiated as four roles via multi-role orchestration, plus the plug-and-play policy executor mode with an external planner]({{ "/assets/figures/516cfe28f44d/fig_015_01.png" | relative_url }})

That is the MAS mode. But LAMO also offers a policy-executor mode: hand the high-level planning to a big external model (Gemini-2.5-Pro, GPT-5) and let the 3B agent handle nothing but precise, low-level screen interaction.

The planner improves over time; the executor stays sharp. The performance ceiling rises with the planner.

## Making Coordinates Count

Standard SFT on GUI data has a quiet failure mode: it learns text just fine, but coordinate predictions drift.

The authors noticed that coordinate tokens consistently carry higher perplexity than text tokens during training—numerical spatial prediction is inherently uncertain, and the loss treats every token equally.

Their fix is Perplexity-Weighted Cross-Entropy (PWCE):

$$L_{\text{PWCE}} = L_{\text{CE}} + \lambda \cdot L_{\text{PW}}$$

where the perplexity-weighted term upweights tokens with higher generation uncertainty. The per-token weight:

$$w_i = \frac{1 + \alpha \frac{\text{PPL}_i}{\overline{\text{PPL}} + \epsilon}}{\frac{1}{|M|} \sum_{j \in M} \left(1 + \alpha \frac{\text{PPL}_j}{\overline{\text{PPL}} + \epsilon}\right)}$$

The effect: numerical coordinate tokens—the ones that decide whether a click lands on the button or the void—get more gradient signal.

Ablation shows that dropping PWCE causes a 38.3% degradation on ScreenSpot-pro and a 26.9% drop on MiniWob++.

The second visual trick is Intricate-Layout Grounding (ILG) data. Real screens are cluttered; training data often is not.

ILG synthesizes clutter by overlaying foreground UI targets onto busy background screens with random scaling.

Removing ILG data causes a 34.7% drop on ScreenSpot-pro. Both PWCE and ILG target the same bottleneck: the model has to see *where* things are, not just *what* they are.

## Where LAMO Wins

On MiniWob++ (web), LAMO-3B end-to-end scores 50.0%—already beating OS-Atlas-7B by 42%.

In the MAS mode (same backbone, four roles), that jumps to 60.9%.

In policy-executor mode with Gemini-2.5-Pro as planner: 77.2%. That is a 54.4% lift over end-to-end, and it beats the 72B monolithic Aguvis by roughly 17% relative—11 percentage points outright.

On AndroidWorld (mobile), LAMO-3B paired with GPT-5 hits 77.6%—surpassing Mobile-Agent-V3 (73.3%), UI-Venus-Navi-72B (65.9%), and UI-Ins-7B with the same GPT-5 planner (74.1%).

A 3B model out-executing a 7B model that shares its planner.

![Benchmark comparison showing LAMO-3B's success rates across MiniWob++, AndroidWorld, OSWorld, and ScreenSpot-pro against larger baselines]({{ "/assets/figures/516cfe28f44d/fig_028_02.png" | relative_url }})

On OSWorld (desktop, up to 50 steps), LAMO-3B as executor reaches 38.5%—beating UI-TARS-1.5-7B (28.2%) by 36.5% and Qwen3-VL-4B (33.3%) by 15.6%, with roughly ten times fewer parameters than the 32B top scorer.

On ScreenSpot-pro (grounding), LAMO-3B averages 36.1% across domains, matching or exceeding 7B GUI-specific models—particularly strong on CAD scenarios, traditionally the hardest category.

## The Honest Limits

Desktop is still hard. Spreadsheets, software with deep domain priors, high-density layouts—LAMO-3B's mobile-grounded training does not fully transfer.

Long horizons need external help. Past roughly ten steps, a 3B model's reasoning depth bottoms out.

The authors are explicit: if you want reliable performance on complex workflows, you still pair the executor with a big planner.

LAMO does not eliminate the scaling law. It sidesteps it by making the small model's job smaller.

But that sidestep is the point. You do not need a 72B model to click the right button. You need a 72B model to decide *which* button and *why*—and once you separate those concerns, a 3B agent can carry the execution burden at a fraction of the cost.

As planners improve, the executor rides along for free.

One backbone, four hats, and a ceiling that moves with the planner—without retraining a thing.
