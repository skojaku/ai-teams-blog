*TL;DR: NEXA lets multi-agent LLM systems look at their draft answers first and then decide — via a learned policy — whether any sequential communication is needed, achieving the best accuracy across three benchmarks while cutting token use by over a third compared to the nearest competitor.*

## The False Choice

Pick one: run all your agents in parallel and aggregate, or wire them into a communication graph and let messages flow.

That's the fork every multi-agent LLM system faces today. Parallel is cheap but blind to what other agents already know. Sequential is expressive but forces you to commit to a topology before anyone has said a word.

NEXA refuses to choose. It treats "parallel or sequential" not as a design-time decision but as a response-conditioned one — a call the system makes *after* seeing what the agents actually wrote.

## Where Existing Methods Fail

SelfOrg, introduced by Tastan et al. earlier this year, showed that you can rank agents by how much their answers contribute to the group and aggregate without an external judge. But SelfOrg's self-organization rule is stochastic and fixed. It can't learn *which* communication paths are worth paying tokens for.

GPTSwarm represents the multi-agent system as an optimizable computation graph. The catch: the graph is conditioned on task templates and role labels, not on what agents actually produce. And it can't decide that *no* communication is needed — the topology is always there.

G-Designer learns role-specific topologies with a GNN, but it ties the policy to agent identities. Swap an agent out and the topology breaks.

Each of these systems adds orchestration overhead, and none can simply say: "these answers are good enough — stop here."

## How NEXA Decides to Talk

NEXA works in two passes.

First, every agent answers independently — the **parallel draft stage**. No coordination, no waiting. Just *N* responses.

Then, each response gets embedded into a shared semantic space by a lightweight sentence encoder (all-MiniLM-L6-v2). These embeddings become the input to a tiny one-layer, one-head transformer that predicts a **directed acyclic communication graph** over the agents.

![NEXA architecture: parallel drafts flow into embeddings, which flow into a policy network that predicts a sparse directed acyclic graph — or an empty one, meaning no communication needed]({{ "/assets/figures/594298d6fbb9/fig_007_01.png" | relative_url }})

The key move: the graph can be empty. If the initial answers are already strong and aligned, the policy can predict zero edges. No sequential pass. No extra tokens. The system returns the parallel aggregate and stops.

If the graph is non-empty, selected agents get one sequential update — they receive messages from upstream parents, revise their answers, and the final result is picked by contribution-weighted centroid aggregation. One round, not an indefinite loop.

### Acyclic by Construction, Not by Repair

You might expect cycle detection and repair after predicting the graph. NEXA sidesteps that entirely.

The topological order π isn't learned. It's derived from **contribution scores** — the cosine similarity of each agent's embedding to the group mean. Higher-contribution agents go first. Edges can only point forward under this order, so no directed cycle can exist.

$$E_\pi = \{(m, n) : \pi^{-1}(m) < \pi^{-1}(n)\}$$

By restricting the feasible edge set to forward-only edges, DAG validity is baked into the action space. The policy never needs to check for cycles because it literally cannot create one.

![Contribution-based ordering: cosine similarity to the group mean defines a topological order, and edges are restricted to point forward only, making directed cycles impossible by construction]({{ "/assets/figures/594298d6fbb9/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

### What the Policy Actually Sees

The transformer encoder ingests the response embedding matrix $X = [r_1, \dots, r_N]^\top$ and produces contextualized hidden states $H = \text{Enc}_\theta(X)$.

Edge logits come from the outer product:

$$\Lambda = H H^\top$$

Each entry feeds a sigmoid and is sampled to decide whether that edge exists. The transformer has no positional encodings and no identity tokens, so the policy is **permutation-equivariant** — shuffle the agent order and the graph distribution shuffles identically. What matters is what the agents *say*, not what they're *called*.

## Training on REINFORCE with a Sparsity Kick

The learning objective is straightforward: maximize task correctness minus a penalty on edge count.

$$\mathcal{R}_{\text{sp}}(G) = \mathbf{1}[\text{Eval}(\hat{y}_G, y) = 1] - \lambda_{\text{sp}} \frac{|E|}{M}$$

where $M = \frac{N(N-1)}{2}$ is the number of feasible forward edges and $\lambda_{\text{sp}} = 0.1$.

The policy is trained with REINFORCE and a batch-mean baseline. No external LLM judge, no reward model, no hand-crafted search at test time.

Correctness is checked by an xVerify-based binary reward. The sparsity term penalizes dense communication, pushing the policy toward the minimum edges needed to improve the answer.

## What the Numbers Say

NEXA hits **60.90% average accuracy** across AQUA-RAT, HumanEval, and GSM8K — best in class — while burning only **18.36M tokens**.

![Accuracy across five methods on three benchmarks, with NEXA leading the average at 60.90%]({{ "/assets/figures/594298d6fbb9/fig_008_04.png" | relative_url }})

SelfOrg* uses 28.41M. GPTSwarm uses 38.02M. That's roughly 35% fewer tokens than the closest competitor and over 50% fewer than GPTSwarm — paired with higher accuracy.

The rescue rate tells a clean story. On GSM8K with 7B agents, NEXA converts wrong parallel answers into correct ones at a rate that climbs from 19.2% (5 agents) to 23.8% (20 agents).

It harms correct answers less than 2.5% of the time. The policy mostly preserves what's already right and selectively fixes what's wrong.

And the communication stays sparse. Across both 1.5B and 7B model sizes, the fraction of examples where the policy selects low-edge plans ranges from 35% at the low end to over 70% at the high end. More agents don't mean more chatter.

### Transfer That Barely Bends

A policy trained with ten 1.5B-parameter agents transfers to:

- **More or fewer agents**: accuracy stays above single-agent and CoT baselines across $N = 5$ to 20.
- **Different tasks**: cross-task transfer gap is 0.05–0.18 accuracy points — nearly indistinguishable from same-task training.
- **Larger models**: 1.5B-trained policy on 7B agents scores 90.48 vs. 90.52 (7B-trained) on GSM8K, and 76.98 vs. 77.40 on AQUA-RAT.
- **Newer generations**: transferring from Qwen2.5-1.5B to Qwen3.5-2B costs just 0.17 accuracy points on GSM8K, as reported in the paper.

One policy, zero retraining, near-identical results. The formulation — not the backbone — does the heavy lifting. A GNN backbone swap confirms this: transformer and GNN variants score within a fraction of a point of each other.

## Honest Limits

The evaluation landscape is narrow. AQUA-RAT, HumanEval, and GSM8K are reasoning and programming tasks with verifiable answers. Open-ended generation, multi-turn planning, interactive tool use — the settings where multi-agent collaboration might matter most — remain untested. The authors say this plainly in the appendix.

The method also leans on its embedding model. If sentence embeddings don't capture task-relevant differences between responses, the contribution ordering and graph policy can miss useful communication paths. A bad encoder means bad decisions about who should talk to whom.

NEXA's efficiency still depends on the initial pool size. Every agent generates a draft before the policy decides anything. Adaptive agent selection — deciding *how many* agents to query, not just *who talks to whom* — is left for future work.

## Why This Matters

NEXA's core shift is small but fundamental: the coordination decision is *response-conditioned*, not design-time. The empty graph is always an option.

This means the method strictly subsumes pure parallel execution. It can only do better or equal — never worse — than the parallel baseline.

If the transfer results hold up under broader conditions, a single trained policy could serve as a plug-and-play orchestrator: deploy it once, and it decides whether your agents need to talk at all. For anyone running multi-agent inference at scale, that's the difference between paying for ten rounds of debate and paying for one round of quiet competence.

Parallel was fast. Sequential was smart. NEXA reads the room.
