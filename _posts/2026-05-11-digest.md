## Themes

**Internal state beats outward behavior.** Three of today's five papers converge on the same unsettling lesson: the signals most systems monitor are exactly the wrong ones. *When Does Multi-Agent Collaboration Help?* shows that token-level entropy predicts team failure with 72–91% accuracy while behavioral metrics mislead. *Hidden Coalitions in Multi-Agent AI* demonstrates that behavioral agreement of 0.984 can be entirely spurious — a false coalition invisible to anyone watching actions alone. *The Context Gathering Decision Process* proves that stripping an LLM's entire implicit memory and replacing it with a compact, programmatic belief state actually improves accuracy. The surface is a mirage. The internals are the diagnosis.

**More agents can make things worse.** The comfortable assumption that adding agents distributes wisdom takes real damage today. *When Does Multi-Agent Collaboration Help?* quantifies it: solo agents beat the team in 13 of 30 configurations. *Active Learning for Communication Structure Optimization* shows that the worst 25% of randomly trained multi-agent runs barely outperform no training at all — confused copies compound confusion rather than cancel it. More nodes in the graph do not guarantee more insight. They guarantee more entropy.

**The leverage is in the loop, not the weights.** *MAS-Algorithm* delivers the sharpest version: a five-agent workflow beats LoRA fine-tuning by 7× on the same data, without touching a single model weight. *The Context Gathering Decision Process* shows that offloading the stopping decision from the LLM to a programmatic gate saves 39% of tokens with no accuracy loss. *Active Learning for Communication Structure Optimization* spends seven units of budget on selecting the right tasks and one on training — and still outperforms spending all eight on random training. The pattern is consistent: orchestration, not parameter updates, is where the gains live.

**Early signals lock in late outcomes.** Round-1 entropy causally determines final correctness in multi-agent teams — mediation analysis shows early misalignment compounds, it does not self-correct. Task selection in the first stage determines whether the entire communication-graph optimization trajectory succeeds or wanders. Coalition structure is readable from neural representations before any behavioral signature appears. What happens first is what matters most. Everything after is either amplification or damage control.

## Editorial

Today's batch is a reckoning for the "more agents, more better" school of AI system design.

Four of the five papers — all of them, really — point in the same direction: the thing that breaks multi-agent systems is not the agents' individual weakness. It is the lack of external scaffolding for state, stopping, selection, and diagnosis. The entropy paper tells you that early disagreement is causal poison. The CGDP paper tells you the LLM should not manage its own memory. The coalition paper tells you behavior is the wrong layer to monitor. The active-learning paper tells you the task you train on matters more than the training method. MAS-Algorithm tells you the pipeline outperforms the parameter update by an order of magnitude.

The honest incremental work is in the coalition paper's negative control — the method that correctly stays silent when there is nothing to say. That is genuinely new: a diagnostic that knows when not to raise an alarm.

The rest is cumulative. Each paper sharpens a known insight (better orchestration helps; teams can fail) with better measurement — entropy traces, Fiedler vectors, POMDP formalism, EKI selectors. The measurements are valuable. The insight is not a surprise.

What warrants a closer second look is the active-learning paper's worst-case story. A +4.25-point floor improvement under adversarial attack is the kind of number that changes deployment decisions. Most multi-agent work optimizes the mean. This one optimizes the tail. That is where reliability lives.

Stop asking the agents to police themselves. Start building the infrastructure around them.
