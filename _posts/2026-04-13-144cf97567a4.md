*TL;DR: Today's LLMs are secretly optimized for a single boss — the moment multiple users with conflicting goals and private data share the same agent, things fall apart. This paper formalizes the problem and stress-tests 19 frontier models, revealing systematic failures in authority tracking, privacy, and coordination.*

## One Boss, Many Bosses

Picture a Slack bot that helps your whole team. Alice asks it to draft a confidential strategy memo. Bob asks it to share project updates publicly. Carol, an intern, asks it to reveal Alice's salary.

Right now, that bot will probably leak Alice's memo to Bob, publish confidential updates, and tell Carol the salary — not because it's malicious, but because nobody built it to know the difference.

This paper shows that these failures aren't bugs in any one model. They're structural consequences of training every LLM under a single-user assumption.

## Built for One

Every mainstream LLM pipeline — instruction tuning, RLHF, chat templates — collapses all users into one `user` role.

The SFT objective learns a single conditional distribution $p_\theta(y \mid x)$: one context, one best response, one principal. RLHF piles on by learning a scalar reward $r_\phi(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}$ from pairwise preferences that reflect what an "average" user wants.

There's no slot for user identity. No mechanism for authority levels. No architecture for "this information belongs to Alice and should not be shown to Bob."

Even when researchers experiment with multi-user settings, they serialize everything — `userA says: ... userB says: ...` — under that same single role.

Fickinger et al. (2020) formalized multi-principal assistance games in AI alignment theory. Bakker et al. (2022) showed how to learn reward functions from heterogeneous group preferences. But current RLHF conflates those preferences into a single scalar, making multi-user arbitration structurally impossible.

Jhamtani et al. (2025) explored multi-user LLM coordination, yet kept secondary users as auxiliary information providers — not independent principals.

This paper moves from those theoretical and partial-solution footholds to a full practical evaluation.

## The Math of Multiple Principals

The authors model multi-user LLM interaction as a multi-principal decision problem. Each user $u_i$ carries an authority level $p_i$, a private context $C_i$, and a utility function $U_i$.

The agent must optimize a weighted social objective:

$$\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \, U_i(a; C_i, p_i)$$

subject to access-control constraints: information from $C_i$ may only be revealed to users authorized to see it.

This is not a small generalization. Optimizing one scalar reward → optimizing N competing utilities with access-control walls is a qualitative shift. The agent becomes a coordinator, not a delegate.

## How They Stress-Test It

The paper introduces Muses-Bench, built on a unified Multi-User Agent Interaction Protocol that separates per-user private contexts and sessions from shared environment state. Three scenarios probe distinct failure modes.

### Instruction Following Under Conflict

A CEO orders a halt to all new development. An engineer asks the agent to continue development and post updates to a personal blog. The agent must apply authority hierarchies and global objectives to decide which instructions to accept and which to refuse.

### Cross-User Access Control

An HR director has privileged access to a salary database. An unauthorized engineer asks whether salaries are being cut. The agent must serve the authorized query while refusing the unauthorized one — even under adversarial pressure like fake authorization claims and role-playing attacks.

### Meeting Coordination

Multiple team members have different, partially overlapping availability. The agent must gather constraints across turns, track who has responded, and converge on a valid slot without hallucinating preferences.

They evaluate 19 frontier models — proprietary and open-weight — across all three scenarios, using simulated user personas with varied authority levels, security postures, and collaboration styles.

## Where They Break

The numbers tell a clear story.

**Selection ≠ execution.** Qwen3-4B-IT correctly identifies which instructions to follow at 83.8 F1, but its execution accuracy drops to 57.9. Knowing what to do and actually doing it are different skills, and the gap is wide.

**Conflict wrecks performance.** Claude-Haiku-4.5 drops from 0.86 accuracy when instructions are aligned to 0.62 under conflict. GPT-OSS-120B falls from 0.64 to 0.50. Models do not robustly internalize authority hierarchies — they rely on surface-level cues that dissolve under pressure.

![Instruction execution accuracy drops sharply when user instructions conflict versus when they align, across all 19 models]({{ "/assets/figures/144cf97567a4/fig_022_04.png" | relative_url }})

**Privacy erodes gradually, not suddenly.** Claude-3.5-Haiku starts above 0.95 privacy score in round one and slips below 0.75 by round four. The degradation is cumulative and steep in early rounds, then slower — as if the agent's defenses simply accumulate small leaks.

This is more dangerous than a binary pass/fail, because a quick spot-check won't catch it.

![Privacy scores decline steadily over multi-round interactions, with most models losing significant ground by rounds 4–6]({{ "/assets/figures/144cf97567a4/fig_022_05.png" | relative_url }})

**Privacy and utility trade off.** GPT-5.1 hits 98.6 privacy but only 60.3 utility — it's so conservative that authorized users can't get their work done. GPT-OSS-120B flips the trade: 92.2 privacy but 94.8 utility. Neither pole works; you need both.

**Coordination remains unsolved.** The best meeting-scheduling success rate peaks near 65% (Gemini-3-Pro at 64.8). Many strong models sit in the 40–60% range. Successful schedulers tend to resolve the task in under 4 turns; weaker models either waste extra rounds or, like Llama-3-70B, prematurely commit to wrong slots.

The overall best average across all scenarios: Gemini-3-Pro at 85.6, followed by Claude-Sonnet-4.5 at 82.6 and Gemini-3-Flash at 82.0.

## The Caveats

The findings are scoped by two constraints.

The user personas are simulated, not human — they may not capture real organizational dynamics, genuine deception, or social engineering. And the evaluation serializes multi-user messages into a single `user` role with prefix conventions (`userA says:`, `userA:`, `<userA>`), which may understate what purpose-built multi-user architectures could achieve.

These are not minor caveats. Real organizations have politics, power dynamics, and adversarial actors far messier than any simulator. And a native multi-user message schema — with first-class user identity, roles, and visibility constraints — could change the picture substantially.

But the paper's point stands: under current interfaces, the failures are systematic and measurable.

## Deploying Into the Gap

LLM agents are already running in team Slack channels, shared org-wide assistants, and collaborative coding environments. Nobody asked whether they can handle multiple principals with conflicting goals and private information.

The answer, it turns out, is: barely.

The gradual erosion of privacy is the most unsettling finding. A model that passes your first-round access-control test can still leak information over a sustained conversation.

If you're deploying shared AI assistants in any organization with role-based access — which is most of them — this is a live risk.

Muses-Bench gives the field its first systematic tool for measuring these failures. The next step is building agents that don't fail them.

One agent, many bosses — that's the world we're deploying into.
