*TL;DR: When LLM agents get to elect their leaders before harvesting a shared resource, they consistently vote for cooperators—and the group's social welfare jumps 55% while survival time more than doubles. Elections aren't just a human political institution; they're a computationally effective governance layer for multi-agent AI systems.*

## A Resource Pool, a Ballot Box

Eight LLM agents share a lake. Each cycle they harvest fish—privately, simultaneously, without knowing what others take.

Overharvest and the lake collapses. This is the classic tragedy of the commons, and without structure it ends fast: No-Leader groups survive an average of under two cycles.

Now add a twist. Before each cycle, four candidates pitch sustainability agendas and the group votes. The winner's policy guides that round's harvest.

The groups that hold elections survive more than twice as long.

## The Gap This Fills

Multi-agent LLM systems increasingly face real collective-action dilemmas—allocating compute, coordinating vehicle fleets, sharing research infrastructure.

The prior benchmark for studying these scenarios, GovSim from Piatti et al. (2024), showed that frontier models can cooperate sustainably in a common-pool resource game. But only under a narrow condition: every agent is an equal appropriator with no institutional structure.

That leaves a gap. Human groups don't survive commons problems by wishing harder. They build institutions—rules, monitoring, leadership, elections.

Slumbers et al. (2025) studied fixed leadership roles in their Port of Mars simulation and found that less altruistic leaders were more effective, essentially coercing the group into compliance. But those leaders were assigned, not chosen.

Nobody asked: what happens when the group gets to vote?

## How AgentElect Works

Faulkner, Deshpande, Piedrahita, Leibo, and Jin build AgentElect on top of GovSim, inserting two phases that transform the game. The original GovSim loop runs Harvest → Discussion → Reflection. AgentElect adds Policy and Election phases before the harvest.

Each candidate carries a Social Value Orientation—a behavioral angle drawn from a spectrum.

Altruists ($57.15°$–$90°$) maximize group reward at their own expense. Prosocial agents ($22.45°$–$57.15°$) balance group and self.

Individualists ($-12.04°$–$22.45°$) favor themselves. Competitive agents ($-45°$–$-12.04°$) actively minimize others' payoff.

Four candidates draft agendas. Voters cast ballots under first-past-the-post. The winner's policy guides the harvest.

![Framework diagram showing how AgentElect extends GovSim with Policy and Election phases before each Harvest cycle]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

Three population compositions are tested: Balanced (one of each SVO type), Lean Altruistic (skewed toward cooperation), and Lean Competitive (skewed away from it).

Each composition runs across three regimes—No Leader, Fixed Leader, and Elected Leader—using GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, and Gemini 2.5 Flash, all at temperature zero.

### The Game-Theoretic Backbone

The authors prove that their CPR game is isomorphic to a sequential social dilemma (the formalism from Leibo et al., 2017).

Mutual cooperation yields the Pareto-optimal payoff $R_{\max}$. Defection earns the tempted agent $R_{\text{limit}} + \epsilon$ but guarantees eventual collapse.

The inequalities $R > P$, $R > S$, and $2R > T + S$ all hold. This isn't just a simulation; it's a grounded game-theoretic dilemma.

## What Elections Buy

Elected-Leader groups achieved a 55.4% gain in Social Welfare over No-Leader groups ($p < 0.001$) and a 128.6% increase in Survival Time ($p < 0.0001$).

Elections also beat Fixed-Leader settings by 16.7% in Social Welfare ($p < 0.01$) and 35.5% in Survival Time ($p < 0.01$).

![Bar chart comparing Social Welfare across No-Leader, Fixed-Leader, and Elected-Leader regimes, with Elected-Leader showing the highest scores and statistical annotations]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

The mechanism is straightforward: agents vote for sustainability. Prosocial and Altruistic leaders received between 3.0 and 4.0 votes per cycle in balanced populations. Competitive candidates frequently received zero.

Vote share correlates with lake survival at $r \approx 0.85$. The group recognizes who preserves the resource and puts them in charge.

![Bar chart showing votes received per election cycle by candidate SVO type, with Prosocial and Altruistic candidates receiving 3–4 votes while Competitive candidates receive near zero]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

This flips the finding from Port of Mars. When leadership is *assigned*, less altruistic leaders can coerce compliance. When leadership is *earned through election*, the group selects cooperators—and does substantially better.

## The Rhetoric of Power

The authors run a Gemini 2.5 Pro sentiment judge over every leader utterance to classify cooperative intent and rhetorical style (using fixed-leader populations across both truthful and deceptive conditions).

Group-rewarding leaders score a Cooperative Index of 0.72 (Prosocial) versus 0.36 (Competitive), $p < 0.001$. But the rhetorical profiles diverge even more sharply.

Self-rewarding leaders rely almost entirely on Logos—cold, logical arguments justifying their larger harvests. Competitive Logos: 0.70.

Group-rewarding leaders lean into Pathos and Ethos—emotional appeals to group norms and moral authority. Prosocial Pathos: 0.20, double that of Competitive leaders at 0.10 ($p < 0.001$).

When deceptive prompting is introduced, the Altruistic leader's cooperative language degrades significantly. Cooperative Index drops from 0.74 to 0.64, $p < 0.01$. Rhetoric shifts from Ethos ($0.26 \to 0.11$) toward Logos ($0.51 \to 0.68$).

Prosocial leaders, by contrast, hold steady at 0.72 versus 0.71. The Altruist's cooperation is conditional; the Prosocial's is structural.

## The Losing Voice

Despite losing elections, Competitive and Individualistic leaders remain influential in group discussion.

A Kruskal-Wallis test finds no statistically significant difference in Degree Centrality between Prosocial (82.60) and Competitive (63.40) leaders ($p > 0.05$). Self-interested agents keep getting referenced and nominated as next speakers.

The authors call this the "Losing Voice" effect: the group rejects their agenda at the ballot box but still listens to them in deliberation.

In Lean Competitive populations, Individualistic leaders actually held the highest Importance Centrality (0.37–0.44)—they were referenced by other influential agents more than anyone else, despite failing to win a single vote.

## The Equality Paradox

Elections improve survival and welfare, but they don't improve equality.

Elected-Leader populations show an equality index of 0.79, significantly lower than Fixed-Prosocial settings at 0.97 ($p < 0.01$). Competitive leaders sometimes free-ride on the group's cooperation. Altruistic leaders harvest almost nothing for themselves.

The diversity that helps the group survive also produces uneven outcomes.

Scaling to 20 agents widens the elected-leader advantage but depresses absolute scores across the board—coordination gets harder with more voices in the room.

## Why It Matters

This paper does two things worth noticing.

First, it shows that a simple democratic institution—candidates, agendas, a plurality vote—dramatically improves collective outcomes in multi-agent LLM systems. You don't need complex governance. You need a ballot.

Second, it provides an open-source framework (AgentElect) and a reusable analysis toolkit—social graph centrality, rhetorical sentiment classification—for studying *any* institutional design you want to layer on top. Coalitions, vetoes, term limits: those are now testable hypotheses, not thought experiments.

The limits are real.

Only three frontier models at temperature zero. Only six cycles. Only one CPR game structure.

Real governance involves repeated elections, coalition building, and institutional memory over much longer horizons.

But the direction is clear: if you want LLM groups to cooperate, let them choose who leads.

Democracy, it turns out, is an engineering technique.
