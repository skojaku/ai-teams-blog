*TL;DR: The same governance problem that shaped the Qin Dynasty and Athenian democracy now governs teams of LLM agents — and swapping one institutional blueprint for another swings performance by over 57 percentage points on the same model, with no single topology winning everywhere.*

## The Same Old Problem

The Qin Dynasty organized power as a chain of command — a single line of delegation from emperor to county magistrate.

Athens built a voting chamber.

The Tang Dynasty inserted a gatekeeper who could reject proposals and send them back for revision.

Different civilizations, different answers to the same question: who proposes, who reviews, who executes?

Teams of LLM agents face that exact question today.

A single agent trying to plan, review, and act all at once hits a hard ceiling — limited context, no built-in error correction, brittle reasoning.

Multi-agent systems can distribute the labor, but only if someone decides the flow of decisions.

That "someone" is governance topology, and until now it has been an unstudied variable hiding in plain sight.

## Making Governance a Variable

Most multi-agent research has focused on prompting tricks and debate formats. MetaGPT showed that assigning specialized roles in a pipeline improves over a single-agent baseline — but it only evaluated one governance form.

GPTSwarm jointly optimized agent prompts and edge connectivity with gradient methods — but validated on a single model backend, leaving cross-model generality open.

GovSim demonstrated that LLM agents need explicit institutional mechanisms to cooperate in commons-dilemma settings — but never varied the institutional structure to isolate its causal effect.

Fei, Guo, and Xiao make governance topology an explicit, isolatable design variable. They build SOCIALSYSTEMARENA, a framework that formalizes each architecture as a specification:

$$G = (P, A, S, T, F)$$

where $P$ is the pattern (pipeline, gated pipeline, autonomous cluster, or consensus), $A$ is the set of agents, $S$ is the ordered sequence of stages, $T$ is the transition function, and $F$ is a set of pluggable feature modifiers like monitoring or shared state.

Same runtime code, same LLM adapter, same model backend — only $G$ changes.

## Seven Institutions, Four Patterns

The authors translate seven historical political institutions into executable multi-agent architectures:

- **Pipeline** — Qin-Han Commandery, Soviet Party-State, Mongol Empire. Linear delegation; one stage hands off to the next.
- **Gated Pipeline** — Tang Three Departments ($\rho = 0.17$, one gate) and US Federal ($\rho = 0.56$, five gates). Gates can reject and loop back for revision.
- **Autonomous Cluster** — Edo Bakuhan. An orchestrator dispatches to parallel subsystems, then aggregates.
- **Consensus** — Athenian Democracy. Seven citizen-voters with distinct ideological biases vote in parallel; majority rules.

![Four canonical governance patterns — pipeline, gated pipeline, autonomous cluster, and consensus — each shown as a node-and-arrow topology with agent roles and gate stages.]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

Plus a single-agent baseline with no governance scaffolding at all.

Gate density $\rho$ — gate stages divided by total stages — becomes a structural predictor: it measures how much of the topology is devoted to review rather than execution.

## The 57-Point Swing

On PinchBench (23 single-turn tasks), the results are dramatic. Within a single model, the gap between the best and worst institution exceeds 57 percentage points:

- **MiniMax M2.5**: Tang 88.2% vs. single-agent baseline 31.1% (57.1 pp gap)
- **Kimi K2.5**: Mongol 67.3% vs. Soviet 8.7% (58.6 pp gap)
- **Gemini 2.5 Flash**: Edo 87.7% vs. Tang 27.2% (60.5 pp gap)

![Grouped bar chart showing task success rate across seven institutions and a baseline for each of the three LLM models, with dramatic variation — Tang peaks on MiniMax but collapses on Gemini.]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

Governance topology is not a rounding error. It is a first-order determinant of performance.

And here is the kicker: Tang — the overall best on MiniMax at 88.2% — drops to last place on Gemini at 27.2%.

The single-agent baseline outperforms five multi-agent architectures on Gemini.

No single institution dominates across models and tasks.

## The Gate-Loop Trap

The gated-pipeline pattern reveals a paradox. When the LLM can satisfy the gate's criteria, revision loops converge quickly and boost quality. When it cannot, the loop becomes catastrophic.

On Kimi, Tang's Menxia gate rejects proposals up to 15 consecutive times per task. The average task runs 26.7 steps and burns 89K tokens — while scoring only 21.6%.

Eighteen of twenty-three tasks exhaust the step budget without the gate ever approving. Same topology, same runtime, different model: the gate flips from quality enabler to resource sink.

![Gate-loop failure metrics for Tang on Kimi — bar charts showing 26.7 average steps, 89K tokens, and up to 15 consecutive rejections, correlating with near-zero task scores.]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_007_04.png" | relative_url }})

US Federal avoids loop explosion because its five gates use reject → terminal semantics — outright rejection, not revision.

But that binary accept-or-die pathway yields consistently low scores across all models: 43.0%, 13.0%, 51.1%.

The gate saves you from loops but kills you from over-filtering.

Gate density $\rho$ captures the trade-off: too little review and errors propagate; too much and the system either loops or chokes.

## The Pareto Contender

Edo Bakuhan — the autonomous cluster — emerges as the Pareto-efficient institution.

On MiniMax, it hits 83.7% with only 3.0 steps and 48K tokens per task, and just 1 zero-score task. On Gemini, it reaches the overall best at 87.7% with 3.0 steps and zero zero-score tasks. Parallel execution, minimal coordination overhead, no gate loops. It is not always the highest scorer, but it consistently delivers strong performance at low cost.

On ClaweBench (104 multi-step tasks), the governance gap compresses roughly 80% — from 57–61 pp down to 8–12 pp. Task difficulty starts to dominate governance effects. Complex reasoning chains care more about the base model's planning capability than about review loops or parallel dispatch.

## No Universal Blueprint

The central finding echoes the historical record: no single political form prevailed across all societies and environments, and no single governance topology prevails across all models and tasks. The optimal institution shifts with model capability and task structure.

This is not a reason to give up on governance design. It is a reason to build meta-governance — a layer that dynamically selects or reconfigures topology based on real-time signals like gate approval rates, task complexity estimates, and step-count budgets. Historical institutions evolved under pressure; agent societies should too.

The honest limits: only three commercial LLM backends were tested, and all institutions are static specifications. The paper identifies adaptive governance as the key open direction but does not implement it yet.

Governance topology is not a detail. It is the architecture — and the architecture has to evolve with the agents that run through it.
