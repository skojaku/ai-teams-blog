*TL;DR: This survey breaks LLM reasoning scaling into four dimensions and shows that more compute can actively hurt — each lever has a distinct failure mode, and the right one depends entirely on what kind of task you are solving.*

You add more reasoning steps and the model gets worse.

You spin up more debate rounds and agents start reinforcing each other's mistakes.

You scale RL training compute and hit a hard ceiling that no amount of extra budget can push past.

This is the uncomfortable reality behind the "just scale it" narrative. Chen et al. map the full landscape of reasoning scaling and find that each of its four dimensions carries a characteristic failure mode — and a task profile where it actually helps.

## Where the Scaling Story Breaks

The scaling-laws story we know — bigger model, more data, better results — works cleanly for general capability.

But reasoning is not general capability.

Reasoning demands multi-step deduction, planning, and counterfactual thinking. The same compute that helps a model fluently complete a paragraph can actively degrade its chain of logic. Simply increasing reasoning steps, the paper warns, "may introduce redundancy, compounding errors, or even diminished accuracy."

Prior surveys organize reasoning methods by technique family — Zhang et al. on test-time scaling, Ke et al. on reasoning regimes and architectures. But grouping by technique — CoT here, RAG there, multi-agent over there — hides the question that matters: *what* are you scaling, and does that scaling help or hurt for *your* task?

This survey reorganizes the landscape by what is scaled, not by how it is scaled.

That reframing makes the failure modes visible and the cross-family trade-offs comparable for the first time.

## Four Levers, Four Failure Modes

The survey carves reasoning scaling into four dimensions. Each has a different mechanism, a different cost, and a failure mode specific enough to name.

![Taxonomy of four scaling dimensions for LLM reasoning: input sizes, reasoning steps, reasoning rounds, and model optimization, with representative methods in each quadrant]({{ "/assets/figures/8ddcd3d6b04f/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

**Input sizes** — more context, more demonstrations, more retrieved documents. Better grounding at the cost of retrieval overhead and long-context compute.

The failure mode is *lost-in-the-middle*: the model ignores relevant passages buried deep in a long context. Best for knowledge-intensive QA and long-context tasks.

**Reasoning steps** — deeper chains of thought, tree search, self-correction. Better decomposition and verification at the cost of token and search overhead.

The failure mode is *overthinking*: extra steps compound early mistakes instead of catching them. Best for math, logic, and planning.

**Reasoning rounds** — multi-agent debate, human-in-the-loop feedback, iterative refinement. Critique, diversity, and iterative polish at the cost of coordination and latency overhead.

The failure mode is *premature consensus*: agents converge on a shared bias rather than surfacing genuinely new perspectives. Best for open-ended reasoning and collaborative decision-making.

**Model optimization** — RL fine-tuning, latent-space reasoning. Stronger amortized reasoning across deployments at the cost of training compute and data.

The failure modes are *underthinking* (switching between shallow branches without sustained deliberation) and *overthinking* (excessive reasoning on simple instances degrading accuracy), plus a hard compute-performance ceiling. Best for high-value domains with reusable reasoning improvements.

## When More Input Helps

Input scaling is the simplest story of the four, and the one where "more is better" holds longest.

In many-shot in-context learning, going from a few demonstrations to hundreds yields substantial gains across discriminative and generative tasks. But the gains are not unbounded — performance plateaus and can decline once demonstrations become noisy or redundant.

RAG tells a similar story.

Shao et al. find that increasing datastore size "monotonically improves performance across various language modeling and downstream tasks without clear saturation." They scale to MASSIVEDS, a datastore containing trillions of tokens. More retrievable knowledge keeps helping even when more model parameters would not.

But input scaling hits a structural wall: the *lost-in-the-middle* bias.

LLMs assign less importance to passages in the middle of a long context. MOI counters this by aggregating inference calls over permuted retrieval orders, forcing balanced weighting across the context.

The lesson: input scaling solves the "missing knowledge" bottleneck.

It does not solve the "bad reasoning" bottleneck.

## The Overthinking Trap

Step scaling is where the "more is better" story breaks most visibly.

Chain-of-thought prompting, self-consistency, Best-of-N, and tree search all allocate more inference-time compute to decomposition and search. On math, logic, and planning, this helps.

But the mechanism is fragile.

A longer chain of thought can compound an early error into a confidently wrong answer. Tree search helps by exploring multiple paths, but only when the search is pruned well — randomly allocating compute to unpromising branches is inefficient and suboptimal.

Self-correction helps, but only when the critic is reliable. On tasks like summarization, generation-time correction is infeasible because quality can only be assessed after the full output exists.

The survey names this *overthinking*: the model spends compute on reasoning depth that neither catches errors nor finds better paths.

The fix is not more steps — it is better verification and smarter search.

## Three Rounds, Then Stop

Round scaling introduces external feedback and perspective diversity.

The most striking finding: for multi-agent debate, CIPHER shows that "increasing the number of debate rounds improves performance up to a threshold of three rounds, beyond which additional rounds provide diminishing returns."

Three rounds. Not ten, not twenty.

After three, agents increasingly "reinforce each other's biases rather than contribute genuinely new insights."

![Multi-agent debate performance plotted against number of debate rounds, showing gains up to roughly three rounds then diminishing returns]({{ "/assets/figures/8ddcd3d6b04f/fig_004_03.png" | relative_url }})

The same non-monotonic pattern appears in collaborative frameworks. Research finds that larger multi-agent systems can achieve greater accuracy on open-ended tasks — MetaGPT is one example — but beyond a certain scale, performance plateaus or declines from conflicting reasoning paths, redundancy, and growing coordination overhead.

LLM Harmony finds that scaling agents helps only when they play complementary roles. Adding homogeneous agents produces redundant reasoning patterns.

The debate setting has its own nuance.

For weaker models, more debate rounds introduce confusion rather than clarity. For consultancy-based interactions — one model persuading a judge — the judge's accuracy improves over successive rounds. The skill of the debater matters more than the number of rounds.

Round scaling is powerful for open-ended tasks. But its returns are bounded by communication quality and role complementarity, not by compute alone.

## Internalizing the Gains

The model optimization dimension is where the payoff shifts from inference time to training time.

RL-based fine-tuning after supervised pre-training has become the dominant recipe — ReFT, T1, and DeepSeek-R1 all follow this SFT-then-RL pattern. DeepSeek-R1-Zero exhibited an "aha-moment" during GRPO training: as response length increased, the model learned to rethink.

Similar aha-moments have since appeared in real-world software engineering, logical puzzles, and automated theorem proving when scaling training steps and response length.

But RL-trained reasoning has its own characteristic failures.

*Underthinking* — the model switches between shallow reasoning branches without sustained deliberation. *Overthinking* — excessive reasoning on simple instances degrades accuracy.

And the compute–performance curve is not open-ended.

ScaleRL shows that "RL reward improvements follow a predictable sigmoidal compute–performance curve" with early low-gain regions, a sharp transition, and a clear asymptotic limit. Most commonly tuned components — curriculum design, normalization, loss aggregation — affect compute efficiency. Only a small subset of choices, like loss formulation and off-policy setup, materially shifts the performance ceiling.

![Sigmoidal compute–performance curve for RL post-training, showing three phases: flat low-gain region, sharp transition, and asymptotic plateau]({{ "/assets/figures/8ddcd3d6b04f/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

Latent-space reasoning offers a distinct pathway within this dimension.

Methods like Coconut, ITT, and CoCoMix iterate over hidden representations rather than lengthening verbalized chains. The insight: natural language is not always the most efficient substrate for reasoning. Many tokens serve textual coherence, not thinking.

Coconut iteratively processes hidden states to enable parallel exploration of multiple reasoning paths in latent space without proportionally increasing sequence length.

This is a genuinely new scaling axis — scaling *thinking time* without scaling token count. Saunshi et al. show that model depth can effectively be scaled under a limited parameter budget through looping, introducing a paradigm based on iterative latent-space transformations rather than model enlargement.

## Where the Framework Falls Short

As a survey, it synthesizes existing findings. It does not run new controlled experiments.

The cross-dimensional effectiveness claims rest on heterogeneous benchmarks and experimental setups from different papers — the numbers are not directly comparable.

The "model optimization" dimension also conflates two mechanisms that deserve separate treatment. RL fine-tuning and latent-space reasoning are grouped together because both internalize computation at training time, but their applicability constraints are very different.

RL requires reward functions and stable training; latent methods require architectural access and retraining. Lumping them under one heading obscures those differences.

## Pick Your Lever, Then Stop Pulling

The core contribution of this survey is not a new method. It is a decision framework.

If your bottleneck is missing knowledge, scale input. If your bottleneck is shallow reasoning, scale steps — but watch for overthinking. If your bottleneck is monotonous perspective, scale rounds — but stop at three.

If your bottleneck is repeated deployment of the same reasoning patterns, scale model optimization — but know that the sigmoid curve has a ceiling.

Table 2 makes these failure modes legible in a way that technique-family surveys never could.

More compute is not the answer. The right kind of compute for the right kind of task is.
