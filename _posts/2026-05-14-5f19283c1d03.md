*TL;DR: Assigning personas like "Industrialist" or "Environmental Activist" to LLM agents wipes out their payoff-aligned behavior—shifting equilibrium outcomes by up to 90 percentage points—even when agents can see the full payoff table. Prompt design is not a neutral engineering choice; it is a governance decision that determines what your simulation produces.*

## A Game Where Identity Wins

You hand four LLM agents a complete payoff table. The numbers say: pollute, and you profit individually. The Nash equilibrium is the Tragedy of the Commons.

You'd expect the Industrialist agent to pollute. Instead, when that agent is described as an "Industrialist motivated by growth"—given a persona—it chooses clean production. The payoff table might as well not exist.

This is the central finding from Manoranjan and Gaikwad at FAccT '26. In four-agent environmental policy games, persona assignment overrides payoff-aligned behavior so thoroughly that over 99% of Nash equilibria correspond to the Green Transition—regardless of which equilibrium the payoffs actually support.

A single binary choice—persona or no persona—moves equilibrium attainment by up to 90 percentage points.

## How the Game Works

Four players—Industrialist, Government, Activist, Citizen—each choose a binary action. The joint action profile determines payoffs for everyone.

Two canonical equilibria define the strategic landscape:

- **Green Transition**: $a_{\text{green}} = (\text{CLEAN}, \text{REGULATE}, \text{CAMPAIGN}, \text{SUPPORT\_GREEN})$ — coordinated pro-environmental action.
- **Tragedy of the Commons**: $a_{\text{tragedy}} = (\text{POLLUTE}, \text{NOREG}, \text{NOCAMPAIGN}, \text{BUY\_CHEAP})$ — individually beneficial, collectively harmful.

A profile $a^*$ is a Nash equilibrium if no player can improve their payoff by deviating alone:

$$u_i(a^*_i, a^*_{-i}) \geq u_i(a_i, a^*_{-i}) \quad \forall i \in N, \, \forall a_i \in A_i$$

Fifty-three scenarios are constructed around this structure: 41 Green-dominant (where $a_{\text{green}}$ is the unique Nash equilibrium) and 12 Tragedy-dominant (where $a_{\text{tragedy}}$ is). The design isolates a clean question: do agents follow payoffs, or do they follow their assigned identities?

![The four-agent environmental policy game, with persona presence and payoff visibility as experimental conditions]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

A $2 \times 2$ factorial design crosses two factors: persona presence (identity description or none) and payoff visibility (full payoff table or narrative-only). Four open-source models serve as agents: Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen2.5-32B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Mistral-7B. Seven different persona formulations test whether results depend on specific wording.

## Identity Overrides Payoffs

With personas present, every model reaches near-zero Tragedy equilibrium rates in Tragedy-dominant scenarios—even when the full payoff table is visible.

The Industrialist who should pollute doesn't. The Citizen who should buy cheap doesn't. Role semantics overwhelm incentive structure completely.

Over 99% of all Nash equilibria under persona conditions correspond to Green Transition. This holds across all four models, both payoff visibility conditions, and both scenario types.

![Equilibrium type distribution across conditions: personas drive near-universal Green Transition selection]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

Removing personas alone does not restore payoff-aligned behavior. Only when personas are removed *and* payoffs are made explicit do Qwen models reach 65–90% Tragedy equilibrium in Tragedy-dominant scenarios.

Llama-8B and Mistral-7B stay at 0% Nash attainment across all Tragedy-dominant conditions. Visible payoffs or not, they cannot find the selfish equilibrium.

## Three Behavioral Profiles

The four models sort into three distinct profiles under the Visible + No Persona condition—the only condition where payoff-aligned behavior has a chance to emerge.

**Qwen adapts.** Qwen-32B shifts from near-100% Green Transition to 88.6% Tragedy of the Commons when personas are removed and payoffs are visible. In Green-dominant scenarios, the same intervention drops equilibrium attainment from 96.1% to 16.6%.

Qwen-7B shows the same pattern at smaller magnitude. Identity → payoff-aligned when framing flips.

**Mistral scatters.** Mistral-7B increases response variance under the no-persona visible condition but never reaches Tragedy equilibrium. It drops 47.8 percentage points in Green-dominant scenarios without finding the payoff-optimal path.

**Llama holds steady.** Llama-8B barely budges across conditions—97.6% to 93.2% in Green-dominant scenarios, 0% in all Tragedy-dominant conditions. It appears locked into a cooperative prior regardless of framing.

![Three behavioral profiles: Qwen adapts to framing changes, Mistral scatters, Llama holds steady across all conditions]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_011_05.png" | relative_url }})

The same representational change that unlocks payoff-aligned behavior for Qwen *disrupts* coordination for Mistral and barely touches Llama. Fish et al. (2025) showed LLM agents spontaneously collude in pricing games; here we see that collusion depends on the persona scaffold.

Fontana et al. (2025) found LLMs cooperate more than humans in Prisoner's Dilemma. This paper shows that cooperation is partly a persona effect—stripping the persona collapses prosocial bias for the models that can respond to payoffs, while Llama and Mistral keep cooperating regardless.

## Changing the Prompt Changes the Equilibrium

Even within the persona condition, how you phrase the role matters. Across seven persona formulations per scenario—holding game structure, payoffs, and action space constant—scenarios produce 2.2–2.4 distinct outcomes on average. Some reach up to five.

Different descriptions of the same role shift which equilibrium the agents select. A Kruskal-Wallis test finds no significant difference in variability across model families ($p = 0.27$): the prompt sensitivity is universal, not model-specific.

The persona formulation is an independent lever over equilibrium outcomes.

This is not noise. This is systematic variability from surface-level wording changes.

You cannot audit it from outputs alone. Practitioners cannot detect role-consistent behavior from system outputs—a point Casper et al. (2024) raised about black-box audits more broadly.

## Why This Matters

Multi-agent LLM systems are being deployed for policy simulation, economic modeling, and governance. The practitioners running these simulations often treat persona assignment as a design convenience—just a way to make the agents feel distinct.

This paper shows that convenience is a confound with teeth. A single binary choice shifts equilibrium attainment by up to 90 percentage points.

That choice is rarely logged, tested, or treated as a variable.

The assumption that multi-agent LLM simulations neutrally reflect strategic dynamics does not hold. Equilibrium outcomes are shaped by embedded normative assumptions, not neutral predictions.

The scenario set is imbalanced—41 Green-dominant vs. 12 Tragedy-dominant—and the game is single-shot with binary actions and fixed agent ordering. Whether these findings hold for larger models, richer action spaces, or repeated interaction remains untested. Only four open-source models (7B–32B parameters) were evaluated.

But the core finding does not depend on effect size: representational choices shift equilibria, making them governance decisions regardless of whether the magnitude replicates at scale.

Identity overrides incentives—unless you deliberately strip it away.
