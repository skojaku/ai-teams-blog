*TL;DR: When multiple AI agents collaborate on research, how they talk to each other matters more than how smart each one is — and the wrong topology turns a team of experts into a fragile mess. A controlled, head-to-head comparison of parallel subagents versus sequential specialist teams reveals a sharp stability-versus-depth trade-off.*

## Squeezing the Same Lever

Three AI agents walk into a research lab.

Over 50 rounds, they squeeze the MLP expansion ratio from $4\times$ down to $0.75\times$ — and barely touch anything else.

That's not a thought experiment. It's what actually happened when the authors ran a parallel subagent architecture for automated ML research. The system kept hammering the same dimensional lever because, in a parallel topology, no one is there to say "maybe try something different."

The paper that caught this behavior — *An Empirical Study of Multi-Agent Collaboration for Automated Research* by Shen et al. — does something the field has been conspicuously avoiding.

It puts different multi-agent topologies under the same microscope and measures what actually breaks.

## Nobody Benchmarked This

Automated research is moving fast.

Karpathy's `autoresearch` loop — propose a patch, train, accept or reject — is the minimalist starting point: one agent, one GPU, trial and error.

The AI Scientist (Lu et al., 2024) extended the idea to the full scientific lifecycle, but kept a linear single-agent pipeline.

EvoScientist (Lyu et al., 2026) added persistent experience memory across runs.

Bilevel AutoResearch (Qu and Lu, 2026) wrapped an outer meta-loop to break deterministic search patterns.

What none of these did is ask the basic organizational question: *when you go from one agent to many, what's the right way for them to coordinate?*

Practitioners were choosing topologies — parallel workers, specialist roles, discussion chains — based on intuition, not evidence.

## How They Built a Fair Fight

The authors needed a testbed where the only variable that changes is the collaboration topology. Every confound gets neutralized.

Git worktree isolation ensures agents don't step on each other's files.

A strict Search/Replace patch contract prevents agents from rewriting entire source files.

Preflight validation catches syntax errors before they burn GPU time.

And an explicit global memory file records what worked and what crashed, so agents don't hallucinate their own history.

The task: optimize `train.py` — a GPT-style Transformer pretraining script adapted from Karpathy's autoresearch — on next-token prediction over a fixed dataset, measured by validation bits per byte (val_bpb).

The hardware: a single RTX 3090.

Each candidate training job runs for either 300 or 600 seconds.

Then they race three architectures:

- **Single-agent baseline** — one `glm-4.6v` worker, the vanilla loop.
- **Subagent mode** — three parallel `glm-4.6v` workers explore independently in isolated worktrees; a `glm-4.7` coordinator merges promising patches.
- **Agent teams** — three specialist roles (architecture, optimizer/schedule, efficiency/memory) edit the same file in a six-turn sequential relay, with a `glm-4.7` engineer as a crash fallback.

Same benchmark. Same validation metric. Same preflight rules. Same GPU.

The only difference is who talks to whom, and when.

## Stability or Depth: Pick One

Under the tight budget ($T = 300$s), the results are unambiguous: subagents win on raw improvement count. Seven effective improvements over 50 rounds, versus only three for agent teams.

![Bar chart comparing effective improvement counts for three architectures under T=300s, with subagents at 7 and both single-agent and agent teams at approximately 3]({{ "/assets/figures/f71c04e8e901/fig_005_01.png" | relative_url }})

The subagent system generates more valid proposals, crashes less often, and spends less overhead on deliberation. It operates as what the authors call "a highly resilient, high-throughput search engine" — fast, reliable, and shallow.

Shallow is the keyword.

The subagents got stuck in what the paper terms "a distinct lack of diversity." The MLP expansion ratio example is emblematic: the system kept squeezing the same parameter because no mechanism exists to redirect collective attention.

![Line plot tracking validation bits per byte over 50 rounds for three architectures under T=300s, with subagents showing the steepest improvement curve]({{ "/assets/figures/f71c04e8e901/fig_010_02.png" | relative_url }})

Agent teams, by contrast, produced "significantly higher diversity and structural complexity."

In a single iteration, the team proposed a unified patch that simultaneously flipped the attention window pattern (SSLL → SLSL), adjusted the learning rate warmdown ($0.50 \rightarrow 0.30$), and changed the value embedding vocabulary size.

Under the longer budget ($T = 600$s), this deliberative advantage started to close the cumulative-improvement gap.

![Line plot tracking validation bits per byte over rounds for three architectures under T=600s, with agent teams converging toward subagent performance]({{ "/assets/figures/f71c04e8e901/fig_010_03.png" | relative_url }})

But agent teams paid a steep stability tax.

Sequential text handoffs between specialists compound errors: incompatible tensor dimensions, missing imports, logical contradictions. The preflight and crash rates are noticeably higher.

More deliberation, more things that can go wrong.

## Read the Failure Modes

The single-agent baseline tells its own cautionary tale. It "plateaus quickly" because once the obvious hyperparameter tweaks are exhausted, there's no structural mechanism to escape the local optimum.

Linear trial-and-error is a blunt instrument.

Subagents avoid that particular trap — three independent workers explore different directions simultaneously — but they fall into a different one: *convergent myopia*. Each worker optimizes greedily, and the coordinator merges the best individual gains. No one is thinking about the architecture as a whole.

Agent teams think holistically but execute fragilely. The "multi-author code generation" problem is real.

When an architecture expert changes a tensor shape, an optimizer expert adjusts the learning rate, and an efficiency expert trims memory — all editing the same script through text diffs — the error surface explodes. The engineer fallback absorbs some crashes, but not all of them.

## So What Do You Build?

A controlled, execution-based comparison of multi-agent topologies for automated ML research yields one actionable design principle: **use subagents for broad sweeps, switch to specialist teams for deep refactoring.**

The right topology depends on the task. Fast hyperparameter search? Spin up parallel workers with isolated contexts. Coupled architectural changes that need coherent reasoning? Assemble a specialist relay — but budget for the crash rate.

The authors also give the community a reusable testbed. Git worktree isolation, structured patch contracts, preflight validation, and explicit memory — these are infrastructure decisions that future multi-agent research systems can adopt directly.

## Honest Limits

Two budgets, one GPU, one task.

The trade-offs observed here — on a Transformer pretraining script running an RTX 3090 — may shift with stronger models or different base tasks. Both multi-agent architectures use `glm-4.6v`/`glm-4.7` as backbones; a more capable LLM might change the stability calculus for agent teams, or the diversity calculus for subagents.

And the task, optimizing a single training script, is simpler than what full-blown automated research requires.

The paper is upfront about this: it's a controlled comparison of collaboration topologies, not a claim about absolute best performance. What it gives is a principled starting point — and a warning that topology is a first-class design decision, not an afterthought.

The smartest agents in the world won't save you from the wrong conversation topology.
