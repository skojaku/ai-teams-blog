*TL;DR: Giving an LLM agent a role-based persona—say, "Industrialist"—can shift which equilibrium a four-agent game reaches by up to 90 percentage points, even when every agent sees the full payoff table. Representational choices aren't neutral design; they're governance decisions.*

## A Game Where the Prompt Picks the Winner

Imagine an LLM playing an industrialist in a climate-policy simulation. The payoff table sits right there in the prompt: polluting is the profit-maximizing move, and Tragedy of the Commons is the Nash equilibrium.

Yet the agent chooses CLEAN.

Not because it miscalculated. Because the prompt said "Industrialist"—and the identity stuck harder than the numbers.

Manoranjan and Gaikwad show that this isn't a quirk. It's systematic. The interaction of two design choices—whether you give agents a persona and whether you show them the payoff table—swings equilibrium attainment by up to 90 percentage points across three model families and 53 environmental policy scenarios.

## The Setup: Four Agents, Two Knobs

The authors build a four-player environmental policy game. The players are an Industrialist, a Government, an Environmental Activist, and a Citizen.

Each picks a binary action:

$$A_I = \{\text{POLLUTE}, \text{CLEAN}\}, \quad A_G = \{\text{NOREG}, \text{REGULATE}\}$$

$$A_A = \{\text{NOCAMPAIGN}, \text{CAMPAIGN}\}, \quad A_C = \{\text{BUY\_CHEAP}, \text{SUPPORT\_GREEN}\}$$

Four binary actions yield $2^4 = 16$ joint action profiles. Each scenario defines a payoff function $u_i(a)$ over those 16 profiles.

Two canonical profiles matter:

$$a_{\text{green}} = (\text{CLEAN, REGULATE, CAMPAIGN, SUPPORT\_GREEN})$$

$$a_{\text{tragedy}} = (\text{POLLUTE, NOREG, NOCAMPAIGN, BUY\_CHEAP})$$

Scenarios are classified as **Green-dominant** (where $a_{\text{green}}$ is the Nash equilibrium) or **Tragedy-dominant** (where $a_{\text{tragedy}}$ is the Nash equilibrium). Across 53 scenarios, 41 are Green-dominant and 12 are Tragedy-dominant.

### Two experimental knobs

The $2 \times 2$ factorial crosses two factors:

- **Persona presence**: agents get a role-based identity description ("persona") or are told only to act strategically.
- **Payoff visibility**: agents see the full payoff table ("visible") or must infer incentives from the narrative ("hidden").

Four models play: Qwen2.5-7B, Qwen2.5-32B, Llama-3.1-8B, and Mistral-7B. Every model–scenario–condition combination runs five times.

The question: does payoff structure or prompt framing drive which equilibrium the system lands on?

## Identity Eats Incentives

With personas present, the answer is decisive.

Every model, across all payoff-visibility conditions, reaches near-zero Tragedy equilibrium rates in Tragedy-dominant scenarios. Over 99% of Nash equilibria correspond to Green Transition.

Let that land.

The incentives say "pollute." The payoff table is explicit. The agents still coordinate on the Green Transition—because the persona said "Government" or "Activist," and the role identity overrode the numbers.

![Persona framing suppresses payoff-aligned behavior across all models, with near-100% Green Transition under persona conditions and payoff-aligned behavior emerging only under Visible + No Persona for Qwen]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

Removing personas alone doesn't fix it.

No model reaches Tragedy equilibrium by stripping the persona while keeping payoffs hidden.

You need both interventions at once: persona removed *and* payoffs visible.

Qwen-32B hits 90% Nash attainment under Visible + No Persona, up from near zero. Qwen-7B reaches 65%.

## The Same Knob Cuts Both Ways

That intervention is a double-edged sword.

In Green-dominant scenarios, the exact same condition that recovers payoff alignment in Tragedy settings *breaks* coordination in Green settings.

Qwen-32B drops from 96.1% Nash attainment under Hidden + No Persona to just 16.6% under Visible + No Persona.

Qwen-7B drops from 95.6% to 52.7%.

Mistral-7B drops from 98.0% to 50.2%.

Only Llama-8B holds steady, sliding a modest 97.6% → 93.2%.

The same representational change helps in one context and catastrophically disrupts in another.

Context-blind design is dangerous.

![Under personas, Qwen models reach over 99% Green Transition; under Visible + No Persona, Qwen-32B shifts to 88.6% Tragedy of the Commons]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

## Three Behavioral Profiles

Not every model responds the same way. The authors identify three distinct profiles:

**Qwen shifts.** Both Qwen models swing hard between conditions—role-aligned under personas, payoff-aligned when personas are stripped and payoffs are shown. Qwen-32B shifts to 88.6% Tragedy of the Commons under Visible + No Persona; Qwen-7B shifts to 39.6%. This is the model that pays attention to whatever signal is loudest.

**Mistral scatters.** Without persona framing, Mistral-7B increases response variance—dropping 47.8 percentage points in Green-dominant scenarios—but never converges on Tragedy equilibrium in any condition. The variance increases; the strategic reasoning doesn't.

**Llama holds.** Llama-8B barely budges across all conditions in both scenario types (97.6% → 93.2% in Green-dominant, 0% Nash attainment everywhere in Tragedy-dominant). Whatever frame you give it, Llama behaves the same way.

The same intervention produces large effects for Qwen, disruptive noise for Mistral, and near-zero change for Llama. If you're deploying one of these models in a policy simulation, the model family determines whether your prompt choices matter at all.

## Prompt Wording Alone Moves the Needle

Here's the subtler finding. The authors test seven persona formulation variants per scenario—same game structure, same payoffs, different prompt wording.

Scenarios produce between 2.2 and 2.4 distinct outcomes on average across models, with some reaching up to 5.

Same incentive structure.

Same action space.

Different prompt surface.

Different equilibrium.

![Same game, different prompts—scenarios produce 2.2–2.4 distinct outcomes on average across seven persona variants]({{ "/assets/figures/5f19283c1d03/fig_013_06.png" | relative_url }})

A Kruskal-Wallis test finds no significant difference in variability across model families ($p = 0.27$): all models exhibit similar prompt sensitivity.

This isn't a model-specific artifact. It's a structural property of persona-conditioned LLM agents.

## What This Changes

GTBench (Duan et al., 2024) showed that LLMs have substantial strategic reasoning weaknesses in structured game-theoretic tasks. This paper isolates the mechanism.

The weakness isn't uniformly "bad at games." It's "identity overrides incentives when personas are present."

Prior work found that contextual framing competes with game structure in shaping LLM behavior. But it didn't isolate persona identity as a standalone factor or measure equilibrium-level effects. Here, the factorial design does exactly that, and the equilibrium-level readout makes the magnitude unmistakable.

Akata et al. (2025) found that LLMs cooperate more than humans in repeated games. This paper reframes that cooperative bias: it's not intrinsic to LLMs. It's driven by persona framing.

Strip the persona, expose the payoffs, and the "cooperative" LLM agent turns into a payoff-maximizer—at least if it's a Qwen model.

## The Honest Limits

Four models. 7B–32B parameters. Three families.

Whether these patterns hold for larger or proprietary models is unknown. The universal Green bias under personas may not survive every architecture.

The scenario set is imbalanced—41 Green-dominant versus 12 Tragedy-dominant. The Tragedy-dominant findings rest on 60 runs per condition. The magnitude of the effects is consistent across repetitions and both Qwen models, but the absolute rates need validation on a larger Tragedy-dominant set.

The game is single-shot with full payoff matrices covering all 16 profiles.

That's deliberate—control over the experiment—but it doesn't reflect realistic multi-turn negotiation dynamics.

Whether persona effects persist under repeated interaction is an open question.

## Why It Matters

Multi-agent LLM systems are moving into policy simulation and governance. If persona design determines whether a simulation predicts cooperation or conflict, the simulation output is not a neutral prediction.

It's a reflection of the designer's normative assumptions, embedded in the prompt.

You cannot detect the problem from the output alone.

A simulation that produces "cooperative" outcomes looks like successful coordination.

It may actually be persona-imposed role conformity that happens to align with a socially preferred outcome.

The system gamed the designer, not the incentives.

The punchline is the title: representational choices are governance decisions. Persona assignment, payoff presentation, even prompt wording—these are first-class variables, and they must be documented, tested, and deliberately chosen. Anything less is governance by accident.

Identity overrides incentives—but only if you let it.
