*TL;DR: Standard multi-agent self-training picks synthetic data with the highest Q-values, but those samples barely move the needle on actual performance — DITS swaps in an influence score that measures which data most improves the model, beating the prior best by ~2.7% across six benchmarks.*

## The Wrong Data Is Winning

Picture a Monte Carlo Tree Search cranking out trajectories for a team of LLM agents. After every rollout it ranks the candidate preference pairs by Q-value — the higher, the better — and feeds the top fraction into DPO training.

That is how Optima, the current state-of-the-art, works.

Here is the problem: a preference pair with a sky-high Q-value can be nearly useless for training.

![Scatter plot showing weak correlation between DPO loss and downstream validation performance, with a regression line confirming correlation below 0.2]({{ "/assets/figures/bf4e41ed6254/fig_003_02.png" | relative_url }})

The authors measure it directly: DPO loss and downstream F1 or accuracy correlate at *less than 0.2*.

A pair that looks golden by reward signal may barely shift the model, while a modest-Q pair could be the one that actually moves the needle.

The ranking criterion and the training objective are misaligned.

## From Q-Values to Influence

Koh and Liang's influence functions gave us a way to ask, "how much would removing this point change the model?"

The idea has since been adapted for data selection in pretraining — Yu et al.'s MATES uses gradient-based influence to pick the most impactful pretraining samples.

But those methods hinge on gradients flowing cleanly through a differentiable loss to a differentiable metric.

DPO breaks that chain.

The loss itself is poorly correlated with the metric you care about, so a gradient-based influence score computed through the loss aims at the wrong target.

DITS sidesteps the issue: instead of propagating gradients to a metric, it *measures the metric directly*.

### Estimate Influence with One Gradient Step

The key formula is deceptively simple. For each candidate data point $z_i$, do a single gradient step on the DPO loss, then check what happens to validation performance:

$$IF_{val}(z_i, D_{val}, \theta^*) \approx \frac{1}{\epsilon}\big[F_{val}(z_i, \theta^* - \eta\epsilon\nabla_\theta L_{tr}(z_i, \theta^*)) - F_{val}(z_i, \theta^*)\big]$$

Take the current parameters $\theta^*$. Perturb them along the gradient of the training loss on $z_i$, scaled by a tiny $\epsilon$ and learning rate $\eta$.

Then run inference on a 20-sample validation set and compute the non-differentiable metric — F1, accuracy, whatever matters.

The difference, normalized by $\epsilon$, is your influence score.

No Hessians. No large-scale gradient computation across all parameters.

Just one forward-and-backward pass per candidate, plus 20 inference calls on a tiny validation set.

## Blend Influence with Q-Values

Influence alone is powerful, but Q-values still carry real signal about trajectory quality — especially in debate-style tasks where the reward is cleaner.

DITS combines both:

$$H(z_i) = IF_{val}(z_i, D_{val}, \theta) + \gamma \cdot Q(s, a_i^h)$$

Set $\gamma = 0$ and you rely purely on influence.

Set $\gamma = 1$ and you blend both.

The paper finds that influence-only ($\gamma = 0$) wins on information-exchange tasks where Q-values are noisy.

The blended version edges ahead on debate tasks where rewards are more informative.

Either way, you rank all candidate preference pairs by $H(z_i)$, take the top $\alpha$ fraction, and train with DPO.

## The Iterative Flywheel

One round of DITS is good. Multiple rounds are better — and that is where the real efficiency gain kicks in.

After each training round, the improved model generates higher-quality trajectories.

The next round's influence scores shift right: the mean rises, the average synthetic data point is now more useful.

Better model → better data → better model.

![Performance curves comparing iterative DITS rounds against increasing MCTS synthesis budgets, showing that three DITS iterations outperform a sixteenfold budget increase]({{ "/assets/figures/bf4e41ed6254/fig_009_05.png" | relative_url }})

The authors make a striking comparison on 2WMH QA: increasing the MCTS synthesis budget *sixteenfold* yields smaller gains than simply running three iterative DITS rounds.

Brute-forcing more rollouts is the expensive way; iterating with smarter selection is the cheap way.

## What DITS Achieves

Across six static-scenario benchmarks (HotpotQA, 2WMH QA, TriviaQA, CBT, ARC-C, MMLU), DITS-iSFT-DPO beats Optima-iSFT-DPO on every single dataset.

The margins: an average improvement of 2.7% in single-round iterations, and 2.5% across multi-round iterations for information exchange.

On the dynamic DeepSearch task, DITS-DPO reaches 47.2 against Optima-DPO's 46.6 — the paper reports a 2.6% improvement for this task.

Ablation tests confirm that influence-score selection consistently outperforms both random selection and Q-value-only selection.

The data distribution scatter plots tell the story: high-Q regions and high-influence regions are offset.

The top-30% DITS-selected points cluster where both influence and Q-values are high — but the influence score is what drives the gain.

On cost: DITS uses roughly $2 \times 10^7$ tokens and 106 GPU-hours to reach F1 0.612 on 2WMH QA, while Optima-DPO uses $1.67 \times 10^7$ tokens and 98 GPU-hours to reach only 0.607.

Slightly more compute, meaningfully better results.

## Where It Breaks

DITS is not free. Every candidate data point requires a forward-backward pass plus 20 inference calls on a validation set.

That is fine for offline batch selection but too slow for real-time or streaming data quality assessment.

The study also sticks to structured multi-agent topologies — fixed or dynamically coordinated graphs with a clean topological order.

Open-ended regimes where agents spawn on the fly or teams self-organize are outside the scope; the variance in agent interactions there makes the current validation-metric approach shaky.

And there is a subtle trade-off in the iterative loop: as influence distributions tighten across rounds, data diversity drops.

A sharper, more specialized model comes at the cost of breadth.

## The Bigger Picture

DITS reframes a quiet assumption in multi-agent self-training: that the data which *looks best* during search is the data you should train on.

It is not.

The shift from "pick what earns the highest reward" to "pick what most improves the model" is the kind of reorientation that compounds.

Every round of iteration amplifies the gap.

Influence over correctness. That is the handle worth carrying.
