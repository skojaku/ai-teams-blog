*TL;DR: Multi-agent debate methods for stance detection burn 7–12× more API calls than simple prompting—yet lose by 6.5 percentage points on average. The first apples-to-apples study of five methods across 15 LLMs shows that Auto-CoT and StSQA dominate, and scaling past 32B barely helps.*

## The Expensive Debate That Doesn't Pay Off

Imagine spinning up seven LLM agents to debate whether a tweet supports or opposes a target—linguists, domain experts, stance advocates, a judge—only to get a worse answer than a single well-crafted prompt would give you.

That is not a hypothetical.

Across every model with complete results, the best prompt-based method beats the best agent-based method by an average of 6.5 percentage points.

Agent methods burn 7 to 12 times more API calls per sample.

## Why Nobody Could Compare Them

Stance detection—figuring out whether a text author is for, against, or neutral toward a specific target—has attracted a range of LLM approaches.

Some use zero-shot prompts. Some chain reasoning together. Some orchestrate multi-agent debates.

The problem: nobody was comparing them fairly.

Different papers used different data splits, different base models, and different metrics. One evaluates on the full dataset; another uses only the test split. One uses GPT-3.5; another uses GPT-4.

You could not tell whether COLA's role-infused debate actually worked better or just looked better because of an easier setup.

Dai, Chen, Yang, and Zhang fix this. They run five methods—Direct Prompting, Auto-CoT, StSQA, COLA, and MPRF—across four datasets with 14 subtasks, using 15 LLMs from six families, all at temperature 0 on the same test splits.

Same models. Same data. Same metric. Finally, an apples-to-apples fight.

## How the Methods Stack Up

The five methods fall into two camps, and the cost difference is dramatic:

| Method | Category | API calls/sample |
|--------|----------|-------------------|
| Direct | Prompt | 1 |
| StSQA | Prompt | 1 |
| Auto-CoT | Prompt | 2+ |
| COLA | Agent | 7 |
| MPRF | Agent | 12+ |

Direct Prompting just asks the model for a stance label. No examples, no reasoning.

Auto-CoT generates chain-of-thought demos via clustering, then uses them.

StSQA feeds the model a single verified reasoning example. One shot, one call.

COLA runs three expert agents, then three stance advocates, then a judge.

MPRF fires four reasoning paths, scores them, refines low-scoring ones, and fuses everything through weighted voting.

More agents, more calls, more orchestration. Surely that helps?

## What the Scores Say

Here are the average $F_{\text{avg}}$ scores across all 14 subtasks and 15 models:

- **Auto-CoT: 73.7%** (2+ calls)
- **StSQA: 73.6%** (1 call)
- **COLA: 68.9%** (7 calls)
- **Direct Prompting: 61.7%** (1 call)
- **MPRF: 62.9%** (12+ calls)

MPRF—twelve times more expensive than Direct Prompting—actually scores *worse* on some models. On DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-7B, MPRF manages only 19.7% on its completed subtasks. The multi-path reasoning pipeline and small reasoning-distilled models are fundamentally incompatible.

![Cost-performance trade-off showing that StSQA achieves high F1 with a single API call while COLA and MPRF need 7–12+ calls for lower results]({{ "/assets/figures/ff643bbc81b3/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

The single-call StSQA method nearly matches Auto-CoT's peak while using half the inference budget or less. One verified reasoning example, one call, done.

![Overall results across 15 models showing prompt-based methods systematically outperform agent-based methods]({{ "/assets/figures/ff643bbc81b3/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

## Model Scale: The Real Lever

Method choice matters, but model scale matters more—at least up to a point.

Using the Qwen2.5 family as a clean gradient from 7B to 72B, scaling from 7B to 32B lifts Auto-CoT from 68.6% to 76.4% and StSQA from 66.6% to 76.0%.

Then the gains flatten.

Auto-CoT actually *drops* from 76.4% at 32B to 72.5% at 72B. StSQA inches up just 0.7 points from 32B to 72B ($76.0\% \rightarrow 76.7\%$).

![Performance scaling across Qwen2.5 model sizes (7B–72B), showing gains plateau around 32B]({{ "/assets/figures/ff643bbc81b3/fig_005_04.png" | relative_url }})

The practical takeaway: grab a 32B model and stop there. The remaining 40 billion parameters barely move the needle.

## Reasoning Models Don't Help Either

DeepSeek-R1-Distill models—built for step-by-step reasoning—should, in theory, excel at stance detection, which requires interpreting implicit attitudes.

In practice, at 7B the reasoning-distilled model trails Qwen2.5 by 10.1 points on Auto-CoT (58.5% vs. 68.6%). At 32B the gap shrinks to near zero, but Qwen2.5 never falls behind.

Stance detection may depend more on world knowledge and social understanding than on chain-of-thought reasoning—the exact thing general instruction-following models are trained to leverage.

## Where Agents Almost Work

There is one bright spot for the agent camp. COLA achieves its best relative performance on P-Stance, a binary political-stance dataset.

With Llama-3.3-70B, COLA hits 86.5% on P-Stance targets—the highest agent-method score in the entire study.

Multi-perspective debate seems to help when the target is a well-known political figure and the labels are simply FAVOR or AGAINST. But even here, StSQA on the same model reaches 77.8% with a single call, and Auto-CoT hits 78.8%. The agent overhead still doesn't justify itself.

## The First Fair Fight

This is the first unified benchmark that lets you compare any new stance detection method against a known baseline under identical conditions. All code, prompts, and per-target results for 15 models × 5 methods × 14 subtasks are publicly released.

The headline finding is unambiguous: for LLM-based stance detection, simpler prompting beats multi-agent orchestration on both accuracy and cost. Use Auto-CoT or StSQA with a 32B+ model and you are done.

## The Honest Limits

All four datasets are in English. Whether these patterns hold for Chinese, Arabic, or multilingual stance detection remains open.

Supervised fine-tuning—arguably the strongest baseline—was not compared at all.

And some model–method combinations have incomplete results, particularly MPRF on smaller reasoning-distilled models, where the pipeline simply fails too often for reliable evaluation.

The multi-agent approach might still earn its cost on tasks that genuinely benefit from diverse perspectives—political argumentation, legal reasoning, multi-party negotiation. Stance detection just isn't one of them.

More orchestration does not mean more insight. Sometimes one good prompt is worth twelve agents.
