*TL;DR: A survey of 77 studies on LLM-based trading agents finds that the 19 most rigorous ones almost never report data splits, transaction costs, or reproducible code — making cross-paper comparison effectively impossible.*

You would think a field racing to put language models in charge of real money would be meticulous about evaluation.

You would be wrong.

Xia et al. audit 77 studies on LLM-based trading agents and find that even the 19 studies passing a minimum bar — they actually emit trades *and* evaluate them in a closed loop — barely report the evaluation fundamentals. Time-consistent data splits? Nearly absent. Transaction-cost models? Almost zero. Survivorship handling? Effectively missing. The result is a literature that looks busy but can't compare itself to itself.

## The Evaluation Gap

LLM trading agents are proliferating. Systems like FinAgent and TradingAgents now perceive markets, store memories, reason over decisions, and execute trades — a full cognitive loop, not just a prediction model.

But proliferation without shared evaluation protocols is just noise. You cannot tell whether a new architecture improves outcomes or simply ran a different — and probably leakier — backtest.

Wang et al.'s widely-cited general agent survey offers a useful taxonomy for LLM agents broadly, but it sidesteps finance-specific first-class concerns: transaction costs, execution semantics, temporal data structure. FinBen (Xie et al., 2024) benchmarks financial LLMs on task coverage, but doesn't require end-to-end closed-loop agentic evaluation. Ding et al.'s LLM-trading survey discusses execution but treats it as implicit rather than auditable.

None of these frame the evaluation gap as the central problem.

This survey does.

## How They Audited

The authors screen 92 candidate records down to 77 included studies, then draw a hard line.

To enter the primary empirical subset, a study must satisfy two conditions: **Action Output** (the system emits tradable actions — orders, position changes, or portfolio allocations) and **Closed-Loop Evaluation** (those actions are evaluated in backtesting, simulation, or live trading, not just by predictive accuracy). Under that rule, 19 studies qualify. The other 58 provide background and design context but don't enter the protocol denominators.

Then comes the audit. Each primary study is coded against protocol-critical fields: time-consistent data splits, transaction-cost models, universe and survivorship handling, execution timing and semantics, and a reproducibility tier from R0 (no artifacts) through R3 (fully reproducible pipeline).

The numbers are stark:

- **2 out of 19** studies report extractable time-consistent split protocols.
- **1 out of 19** specifies an explicit transaction-cost model.
- **1 out of 19** documents universe or survivorship handling.
- **11 out of 19** report execution timing or semantics.
- **15 out of 19** land in R0 — the lowest reproducibility tier. **Zero reach R3.**

![Protocol reporting rates across the 19 primary studies, showing near-zero compliance on data splits, transaction costs, and survivorship handling]({{ "/assets/figures/8e7dba5c5e19/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

That is the paper's central finding: *protocol incomparability*. The primary subset lacks the shared evaluation foundations necessary for meaningful cross-paper comparison.

## Reframing the Agent

The survey doesn't just count what's missing — it reframes what these agents actually are.

Xia et al. argue that LLM trading agents are expert-system decision pipelines.

Perception acquires knowledge from prices, news, and market signals.

Memory stores and retrieves it.

Reasoning runs the inference engine.

Execution maps decisions to tradable actions, under market frictions.

Risk controls and compliance filters constrain the path from inference to action.

Logs and human oversight provide auditability.

This framing makes the evaluation problem explicit. A trading agent can look architecturally sophisticated while being empirically weak if the evaluation omits temporal splits, transaction costs, universe construction, or execution semantics.

The expert-system lens forces you to ask: can I *audit* this pipeline?

## Three Axes of Comparison

To organize the evidence, the authors introduce a working analytical framework they call **Architecture–Capability–Adaptation** (A-C-A).

- **Architecture** — *where* knowledge is acquired, stored, inferred, and acted upon: perception, memory, reasoning, execution.
- **Capability** — *what* trading function the architecture supports: alpha generation, portfolio management, execution, risk control.
- **Adaptation** — *how* the pipeline changes under feedback: from in-context learning (behavioral) to self-evolution (structural).

![The Architecture–Capability–Adaptation framework, mapping perception, memory, reasoning, and execution across three analytical dimensions]({{ "/assets/figures/8e7dba5c5e19/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

Prior frameworks conflate these. FinAgent gets labeled "Multi-modal Agent" — perception-centric, with execution underplayed. AlphaAgent is "Multi-agent" — organization-centric, with its regularized exploration mechanisms invisible.

TradingAgents is "Multi-agent Framework" — collapsing hierarchical role architecture, distributed capability allocation, and feedback-based adaptation into one bucket. The A-C-A lens pulls them apart.

The authors are explicit: A-C-A is an exploratory analytical lens, not an externally validated taxonomy. Its categories may evolve. But they give researchers a shared vocabulary for comparing architectures that previously lacked one.

## The Oracle Fallacy

One of the sharpest observations in the paper concerns episodic memory.

An agent retrieves a past trading episode to guide its current decision. That episode tuple looks like $(S_t, A_t, O_{t+k}, \tau)$ — state, action, outcome, timestamp. But $O_{t+k}$ contains information *from the future relative to* $t$. If the agent retrieves this episode at time $t' < t + k$, it is peeking at an outcome that hasn't happened yet.

![How episodic memory retrieval can introduce implicit look-ahead bias by exposing future outcomes to past decision points]({{ "/assets/figures/8e7dba5c5e19/fig_011_05.png" | relative_url }})

The authors call this the **Oracle Fallacy**. It creates implicit look-ahead bias in the agent's own memory store.

The fix they propose is an *Outcome Embargo*: the outcome field in an episode recorded at $t$ cannot be exposed to retrieval until $t_{\text{now}} \geq t + k$.

This is the kind of protocol-level detail that disappears when you treat execution as an afterthought.

## The Missing Protocols

The survey identifies clear, actionable gaps.

**Temporal alignment in perception.** News timestamps record publication time, not the moment a trading pipeline can act on the information. Using retrospectively cleaned data in backtesting introduces data leakage — and most papers don't address it.

**Survivorship and universe handling.** Only 1 in 19 studies documents how the stock universe is constructed. Without it, you can't tell whether results reflect genuine alpha or just picking winners after the fact.

**Transaction costs.** Only 1 in 19 specifies a cost model. In live trading, costs can erase perceived alpha entirely.

**Reproducibility.** 15 of 19 studies provide no reproducible artifacts. Zero reach the highest tier. A field building autonomous financial agents can't verify its own results.

## Honest Limits

The primary empirical subset is small — 19 studies. The findings reflect the current literature's reporting gaps, not a comprehensive evaluation of agent performance.

The A-C-A lens is explicitly exploratory; its categories haven't been externally validated and may shift as the field matures.

But the audit doesn't need a large sample to make its point. When 17 of 19 studies can't tell you how they split their data, the problem is the protocol, not the sample size.

## What This Changes

The authors provide reporting checklists — minimum expectations for splits, costs, universe handling, execution semantics, and reproducibility artifacts — that could become community standards. The evidence ledger and reproducibility audit are the first of their kind for this space.

Meaningful auditability depends on grounded, time-stamped tool calls, data snapshots, and execution logs that can be independently verified. Current LLM trading systems largely lack these artifacts.

If you can't audit it, you can't trust it.
