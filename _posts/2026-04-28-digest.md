## Themes

Scale without engagement is noise. Two of today's papers—*Benchmarking Emergent Coordination in Large-Scale LLM Populations* and *Superminds Test*—find that agent societies of tens of thousands to millions produce worse outcomes than a single model working alone. The bottleneck isn't intelligence; it's that almost nobody shows up to the conversation.

Connectivity is a diagnostic, not a cure. *Complete Cyclic Subtask Graphs for Tool-Using LLM Agents* demonstrates that fully wiring every subtask to every other reveals where you are—exploration-heavy, prerequisite-chain, or retrieval-bottlenecked—but the wiring itself is not the fix. On some tasks it helps; on others it's pure overhead.

Agreement on retrieval ≠ agreement on action. *Quantifying Divergence in Inter-LLM Communication* shows that five major LLMs sharing roughly half their API results can still disagree violently on which to call first. The divergence is structured and domain-dependent, not random—so you can predict where multi-agent coordination will fray before you deploy.

Every capability is a surface. *Toward a Safe Internet of Agents* argues that memory, tools, planning, and peers are simultaneously functional affordances and attack vectors. The dual-use interface principle compounds at every scale, from a single model's context window to an open interoperable network.

## Editorial

All five papers today carry the same tag, and the tag tells the story: ai-teams. The through-line is a healthy skepticism toward the idea that more agents automatically means better results.

Two MoltBook studies, a cyclic graph paper, an API divergence benchmark, and a security survey converge on one inconvenient truth. Multi-agent AI doesn't work by default. It needs architecture, not just population.

The genuinely new contributions are the measurement instruments. Superminds Test, the coordination benchmark, the divergence metrics—these give the field rulers it didn't have before. The security survey's middle tier is incremental; its single-agent and open-ecosystem layers cover well-trod ground.

What warrants a second look: the critical-mass signal buried in *Benchmarking Emergent Coordination*. Successful collaborative threads averaged 21 participants versus 9 on failed ones, with a Cohen's d of 1.18. That's a fragile data point from just 11 successful events, but if it holds, it points toward a minimum viable group size the field has been guessing at.

Most of today can be compressed to one shift: *build bigger* → *measure first*.

Stop scaling. Start designing the interaction.
