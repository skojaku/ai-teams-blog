*TL;DR: SBD reframes delegation in multi-agent systems as a bilevel optimization problem—learning a context-dependent safety weight at runtime rather than fixing it at design time—and proves that raising the outer safety weight provably raises the inner policy's safety, with an accountability bound that distributes blame across agent chains.*

## The Delegation Dial

A clinical AI agent sees a patient's vitals take a turn.

It could hand the case off to a specialist sub-agent and let it act autonomously. Or it could hold the decision close, routing every recommendation through a human.

How much autonomy should that sub-agent get, *right now*—not in general, but given this patient's acuity, this moment's risk?

Nobody has had a principled, runtime answer to that question. Existing safety mechanisms are static architecture choices or binary human-in-the-loop checkpoints.

SBD gives delegation a dial instead of a switch.

## The Trade-Off

Delegation sits on a fundamental tension.

Crank autonomy all the way up ($\alpha = 1$) and you get efficiency—but your sub-agent might make an unsafe decision. Crank it all the way down ($\alpha = 0$) and safety is maximized—but you've bottlenecked every decision through a human supervisor.

Constrained Policy Optimization (CPO) from Achiam et al. (2017) handles this by baking hard safety constraints into training time. Tomasev et al. (2026) offer guidelines for when humans should retain control. HILA (Yang et al., 2026) uses a binary defer/autonomous split in a dual-loop design.

None of these scale the trade-off at runtime. The safety–efficiency balance is fixed before deployment, unable to adapt when risk context shifts mid-execution.

## Stacking Two Problems

SBD solves this by stacking two optimization problems.

The **outer problem** learns a meta-weight network $\phi$ that maps state $s$ to a scalar $\lambda_{\phi}(s) \in [0, 1]$. This is the safety–efficiency knob. When $\lambda$ is high, the system prioritizes safety; when low, it chases efficiency. The outer loss:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{meta}}(\phi) = \mathbb{E}_s \Big[ \lambda_{\phi}(s) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{safety}}(\pi^*_\phi) + \big(1 - \lambda_{\phi}(s)\big) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{eff}}(\pi^*_\phi) \Big]$$

The **inner problem** then optimizes the delegation policy $\pi$ under that weighting, subject to a probabilistic safety constraint:

$$\pi^*_\phi = \arg\min_\pi \mathbb{E}_s \Big[ \lambda_{\phi}(s) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{safety}}(\pi) + \big(1 - \lambda_{\phi}(s)\big) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{eff}}(\pi) \Big] \quad \text{s.t.} \quad P(\text{safe})(\pi, s) \geq 1 - \delta$$

where $\delta$ is a user-specified risk tolerance—$\delta = 0.05$ allows at most 5% unsafe decisions.

The delegation degree $\alpha \in [0, 1]$ is the continuous output: full human override at $\alpha = 0$, full autonomy at $\alpha = 1$, and every gradation in between.

The closed loop is the key insight. Rising contextual risk raises $\lambda$, which pushes $\alpha$ down, which reduces exposure—*automatically*, without re-architecture.

## Three Guarantees

The paper proves three results.

**Safety Monotonicity** (Theorem 4.4): If one meta-weight network produces a pointwise higher $\lambda$ than another, its inner policy is weakly safer. Formally, $\lambda_\phi(s) \leq \lambda_{\phi'}(s)$ for all $s$ implies $P(\text{safe})(\pi^*_\phi, s) \leq P(\text{safe})(\pi^*_{\phi'}, s)$ for all $s$.

More safety weight in, more safety out. This is the conceptual spine of the framework.

**Inner Policy Convergence** (Theorem 4.6): Projected gradient descent on the inner problem converges at a linear rate:

$$\|\pi_t - \pi^*_\phi\|^2 \leq (1 - \eta\mu)^t \|\pi_0 - \pi^*_\phi\|^2$$

under standard smoothness and strong-convexity assumptions. In practice, 50 inner steps suffice.

**Accountability Propagation** (Proposition 4.8): In a $k$-hop delegation chain with maximum delegation degree $\bar{\alpha}$, no single agent can bear full accountability:

$$\max_j w_j \leq 1 - (1 - \bar{\alpha})^k$$

This gives regulators a formal ceiling on blame concentration—a sharp answer to "who's responsible?" when things go wrong in a chain.

### Running the Loop

Algorithm 1 lays out the full procedure. Each outer iteration: (1) run $T_\text{in} = 50$ inner PGD steps on $\pi$ with the current $\lambda_{\phi}$; (2) project $\alpha$ onto the $\delta$-safe set by clipping $\alpha \leq \alpha_\text{max}(s)$; (3) compute a hypergradient on $\phi$ via implicit differentiation and take an outer step.

With $\phi$ as a 3-layer MLP and $\pi$ as a 4-layer MLP (both width 256, ReLU), a single A100 runs the full 500-outer-iteration loop in under 7 minutes per domain.

## What's Tested—and What Isn't Yet

This is version 1 of the paper. The theoretical results are proved. The empirical evaluation across three domains—medical AI (MIMIC-III), financial risk control (S&P 500 with a COVID stress test), and educational supervision (ASSISTments)—is a pre-registered protocol. No numbers on task efficiency or safety rate yet.

But the protocol is specific enough to be falsifiable. SBD is considered falsified on a domain if: it doesn't beat every non-ablated baseline on Safety–Efficiency Area; the ablation without the outer loop matches full SBD within 1%; the empirical $P(\text{safe})$–$\lambda$ curve is non-monotonic at convergence; or the inner-loop residual fails to decay at a rate consistent with linear convergence.

That is a high bar to set for yourself.

### Three Domains, Three Constraint Sets

In the medical domain, unsafe means a treatment conflicts with a documented allergy or $\alpha > 0.70$ when the APACHE-II proxy exceeds 20.

In finance, unsafe means any single asset weight exceeds 10% or $\alpha > 0.80$ when 30-day annualized volatility exceeds 25%.

In education, unsafe means content difficulty is more than 2 standard deviations above a student's knowledge estimate or $\alpha > 0.60$ for at-risk students.

Each constraint set translates domain expertise into a projection floor.

## Honest Limits

The convergence proof assumes $\mu$-strong convexity of the inner loss. Deep networks don't satisfy this globally. The authors acknowledge it and note that extending to PL conditions is future work.

The safety constraint set $C$ is taken as given. Someone has to write it—drug allergy rules, volatility caps, difficulty bounds—and if it's wrong or incomplete, SBD enforces the wrong thing with full formal rigor.

No empirical results exist yet. Every claim about safety rate, task efficiency, and Pareto dominance is a hypothesis awaiting execution.

The framework also doesn't model what happens *inside* a sub-agent after delegation. A safely delegated task can still be executed unsafely. SBD governs the authorization, not the execution.

## Why the Dial Matters

Multi-agent AI systems are already live in clinical triage, portfolio management, and tutoring. They delegate.

They delegate differently depending on context—except current systems can't say *how* differently, or prove that more caution provably produces more safety. SBD gives that proof: the Safety Monotonicity theorem turns a context-dependent weight into a guaranteed safety floor. The Accountability Propagation bound gives auditors a formal handle for tracing blame across chains.

The dial replaces the switch. The closed loop replaces the architect's guess.
