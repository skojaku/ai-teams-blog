*TL;DR: The total stock of high-quality human text is 9–27 trillion tokens, and frontier models are already training on 10–15 trillion of them—this survey maps the entire LLM landscape as an orbital system where hitting the scaling wall forces a shift to test-time compute, pure RL reasoning, sparse architectures, and small specialized models.*

## The Data Is Running Out

Somewhere between 9 and 27 trillion tokens—books, papers, news, Wikipedia, filtered web content—that is the entire stock of high-quality public text on the internet.

Frontier models already train on 10–15 trillion of those tokens.

The Chinchilla scaling laws tell us that compute-optimal training needs roughly 20 tokens per parameter. A trillion-parameter model wants 20 trillion tokens just to hit its sweet spot. Under current trends, the data wall hits between 2026 and 2028.

![Projected data exhaustion showing training dataset sizes for major LLMs against the 9–27 trillion token stock of available human text, with the exhaustion window at 2026–2028]({{ "/assets/figures/d2bba19f23cb/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

This is not a distant concern. It is the central fact that reshapes every other assumption about where AI goes next.

## Three Crises, One Wall

LLMOrbit, a sprawling survey from Patro and Agneeswaran at Microsoft, organizes the entire large language model landscape into eight "orbital dimensions" that orbit around a gravitational center: the scaling wall.

That wall has three faces.

**Data scarcity.** We just covered this. The stock is finite. Overtraining—reusing tokens at 50–200× per parameter—buys time but introduces diminishing returns and overfitting risks.

**Cost explosion.** Training costs went from \$3.3M for GPT-3 (2020) to \$84.5M for GPT-4 (2023) to \$110.7M for DeepSeek-V3 (2025). That is a 34× increase in realized costs over five years, with next-generation models projected at \$300–500M—pushing toward a 100× arc when projected costs land. Hardware amortization accounts for 70–80% of the bill. Cloud rental adds a 2–4× markup on top of ownership costs.

![Stacked bar chart of training cost evolution from GPT-3 ($3.3M) through DeepSeek-V3 ($110.7M), showing 34× realized cost growth across three eras]({{ "/assets/figures/d2bba19f23cb/fig_003_02.png" | relative_url }})

**Energy.** GPT-4's training run consumed roughly 6,154 MWh—enough to power 570 US households for a year. That is a 22× increase from GPT-3's 280.8 MWh. Scaling naively is not just expensive. It is unsustainable.

Three crises, one conclusion: you cannot brute-force your way past this wall.

## Mapping the Orbit

Rather than treating these crises as the end of the story, LLMOrbit treats them as the center of gravity around which everything else orbits. The survey maps 50+ models across 15 organizations into eight interconnected dimensions: scaling wall analysis, model taxonomy, training methodology, architecture evolution, alternative scaling, agentic AI, benchmarking, and economics.

![The LLMOrbit circular taxonomy showing eight orbital dimensions arranged around a central scaling wall, from model taxonomy and training methodology through agentic AI and economic considerations]({{ "/assets/figures/d2bba19f23cb/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The organizing metaphor is a circle, not a line. The scaling wall is not a cliff you fall off—it is a gravitational center that bends every other dimension of progress toward efficiency, post-training innovation, and agentic reasoning instead of raw parameter count.

The survey's key insight: the limitations of brute-force scaling are not a dead end. They are *what forces every alternative paradigm into existence*.

## Six Ways Through the Wall

The paper identifies six paradigms that break through—or bypass—the scaling wall.

**Test-time compute.** OpenAI's o1 spends inference compute on extended chain-of-thought reasoning, achieving 83.3% on AIME versus GPT-4o's 13.4%. One hour of test-time reasoning can match the impact of $10^{6}\times$ more training compute. The old assumption—"train bigger, deploy once"—flips to "train smaller, think longer."

**Sparse architectures.** DeepSeek-V3 packs 671B total parameters but activates only 37B per token via Mixture-of-Experts routing. That is an 18× efficiency gain. Its Multi-head Latent Attention compresses the KV cache by 8×, fitting massive-context inference on hardware that standard attention would choke on.

**Quantization.** Post-training compression achieves 4–8× model size reduction with less than 1% perplexity degradation. The scaling laws of quantization are now predictable enough to plan around.

**Edge computing.** Distributed device networks can deliver 10× cost reduction by offloading inference to consumer hardware.

**Model merging.** Combine specialized models—no retraining needed—and get synergistic capabilities from the union.

**Small specialized models.** Microsoft's Phi-4 runs 14B parameters and matches models ten times its size. The trick is data quality over data quantity—the same lesson Chinchilla taught, applied to the post-training era.

## The Post-Training Takeover

Here is the shift that matters most.

The paper documents what many have suspected: GPT-4's capability gains are roughly 70% attributable to RLHF rather than raw scale—though the exact proportions remain proprietary and unconfirmed by OpenAI.

DeepSeek-R1 takes this further. It achieves 79.8% on the MATH benchmark through *pure reinforcement learning*—no supervised fine-tuning at all. The RL recipe uses verifiable rewards (the answer is either right or wrong) and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO).

No human preference labels. No SFT warmup. Just a base model, a reward signal, and enough scale to discover reasoning strategies on its own.

The three conditions for reasoning emergence, according to the survey: (i) pre-training scale exceeding $10^{11}$ tokens, (ii) RL with verifiable feedback, and (iii) test-time search.

Miss any one, and the model does not reliably reason. Hit all three, and you get the discontinuous performance jumps we see in o1 and DeepSeek-R1.

This is the Chinchilla lesson inverted. Hoffmann et al. showed that smaller models trained on *more* data beat larger undertrained ones. The new lesson: smaller models trained efficiently, then fine-tuned with RL and allowed to think at inference time, beat larger models trained the old way.

## Open Source Catches Up

Llama 3 scores 88.6% on MMLU. GPT-4 scores 86.4%.

That is the first time an open model surpasses a flagship closed model on a major benchmark. The margin is thin, and GPT-4's architecture remains undisclosed—so the comparison comes with caveats. But the trajectory is real.

Where open-source models once lagged by 10–15 points, Llama 3's combination of 15T training tokens, Grouped-Query Attention (16× KV cache reduction), and a 128K vocabulary tokenizer closes the gap.

The cost dimension is equally striking. Inference pricing has dropped below \$0.30 per million tokens for GPT-4-class performance—a 100× cost reduction from where things stood two years ago.

## From Text to Agents

LLMOrbit traces the arc from passive text generation to autonomous agents through a clear progression: chain-of-thought prompting (CoT), tree-of-thoughts (ToT), tool-using reasoning (ReAct), retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), and multi-agent coordination.

Each step moves the model from generating text to *doing things*.

The key enabling insight is that reasoning techniques like CoT and ToT are not just prompt tricks—they are the scaffolding that makes tool use coherent. You cannot call an API reliably if you cannot plan a sequence of steps first. Multi-agent systems add coordination on top of that, with specialized agents decomposing tasks and communicating results.

But the survey is honest about the limits of this trajectory: the leap from "model that reasons in chain-of-thought" to "reliable autonomous agent" remains an open research problem. Tool use is brittle. Coordination is ad hoc. The gap between demo and deployment is wide.

## What the Survey Leaves Out

Covering 50+ models across eight dimensions means depth is traded for breadth. Individual model analyses are concise to the point of omitting training details and ablation studies that practitioners would need.

Many of the headline numbers—like the "70% of GPT-4's gains from RLHF" claim—cite proprietary or unreleased sources. GPT-4's architecture is still officially undisclosed, making cost estimates speculative.

The circular taxonomy is a useful mental model, but it is a map, not the territory. The real connections between scaling walls, architecture choices, and agent capabilities are messier than the orbital metaphor suggests.

And the data-exhaustion projections, while grounded in Epoch AI's work, depend on assumptions about data quality filtering and synthetic data viability that the survey acknowledges but does not resolve.

The wall is real. The orbits around it are still being drawn.
