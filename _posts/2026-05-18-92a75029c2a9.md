*TL;DR: CryptoBench exposes a striking pattern in LLM agents: they can retrieve cryptocurrency facts reasonably well but nearly fail at prediction, with top models scoring 58.8% on simple retrieval but just 6.3% on simple prediction — and putting agents in an agentic framework makes things even worse for most models.*

GPT-5 can tell you the 24-hour trading volume for ETH/USDC on Uniswap V3. Ask it whether whale accumulation in a token will push prices up next week, and it scores 6.3%.

That gap — between fetching a fact and making a grounded forecast — is the story of CryptoBench.

## Why Existing Benchmarks Miss the Mark

Crypto markets don't reward trivia. They reward synthesis: on-chain transaction graphs, DeFi dashboards, derivatives data, adversarial social signals, all stitched together under time pressure with real money on the line.

Yet the benchmarks we use to evaluate LLM agents were built for calmer waters. GAIA tests general factual extraction but ignores on-chain data and adversarial dynamics. FutureX evaluates forecasting but isn't grounded in crypto-specific sources or volatility. CryptoTrade tackles crypto, but inside a simulated environment rather than on live platforms.

No existing benchmark combines live on-chain data, monthly dynamic updates, and a clean separation between *retrieving* information and *predicting* what happens next.

## Building a Living Benchmark

CryptoBench fills that gap with 50 expert-curated questions per month, each classified into four quadrants: Simple Retrieval, Complex Retrieval, Simple Prediction, and Complex Prediction.

The quadrant structure is the key diagnostic. It doesn't just ask "can the model answer crypto questions?" — it asks *which kind* of question the model can handle.

Questions are authored by DeFi analysts, on-chain investigators, and quantitative traders, then put through a three-stage adversarial review. An Author drafts a question and gold-standard answer. A Validator tries to solve it independently. If they disagree, an Adjudicator resolves the conflict. Unanimous consensus is required for inclusion.

Monthly, the templates get refreshed: new tokens, new addresses, new timeframes. A question about Uniswap's TVL in October becomes a question about a different protocol's TVL in November. This makes memorization useless and forces agents to demonstrate genuine capability each cycle.

The benchmark also forces agents to navigate real platforms — on-chain intelligence aggregators, DEX analytics, DeFi protocol dashboards, derivatives data sources. No sandboxed APIs. No simplified environments.

## Four Quadrants, Four Diagnoses

The two axes — cognitive demand (Retrieval vs. Prediction) and operational complexity (Simple vs. Complex) — produce four distinct task families:

- **Simple Retrieval (34% of questions):** Find a single data point. "What is the current 24-hour trading volume for the ETH/USDC pair on Uniswap V3?"
- **Complex Retrieval (34%):** Find and cross-reference multiple data points. "From the list of a protocol's top ten holders, identify the two KOL wallets and retrieve their token balances."
- **Simple Prediction (12%):** Make a basic inference from available data. "A token unlocks 10% of its circulating supply next week — is short-term price pressure more likely bullish or bearish?"
- **Complex Prediction (20%):** Synthesize multiple signals into a forecast. "Analyze the trading history of address 0x... over the past 7 days to forecast its likely number of buy and sell transactions next week."

![The four-quadrant task classification system used by CryptoBench, organizing tasks along cognitive demand and operational complexity axes]({{ "/assets/figures/92a75029c2a9/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

A model that aces SR and CR but collapses on SP and CP is a competent data fetcher, not an analyst. A model that predicts well but retrieves poorly is probably hallucinating plausible-sounding conclusions.

## What the Numbers Reveal

Ten state-of-the-art LLMs were evaluated both directly and inside SmolAgent, an agentic framework that gives each model a web-browsing tool. The results expose two clear patterns.

**Retrieval beats prediction by a mile.** In direct evaluation, GPT-5 scores 58.8% on Simple Retrieval but just 6.3% on Simple Prediction. Grok-4 (Web) — the overall leader at 44.0% — shows the same imbalance: 54.9% SR vs. 35.2% SP. Models that look factually grounded on retrieval tasks exhibit near-random performance when asked to predict.

![Performance breakdown across the four task quadrants, showing the systematic retrieval-prediction imbalance across all models]({{ "/assets/figures/92a75029c2a9/fig_003_03.png" | relative_url }})

**Agentic execution reshuffles the leaderboard — and mostly downward.** When models move from direct prompting into SmolAgent, overall accuracy drops and rankings shift. GPT-5 falls from 30.0% direct to 16.7% agentic — nearly halved. Meanwhile Grok-4 (the API variant) *improves* from 22.7% direct to 28.0% agentic, becoming the SmolAgent leader. Raw model capability does not translate into effective agentic performance.

![Side-by-side comparison of Direct versus SmolAgent accuracy for each model, showing performance drops and ranking shifts under agentic execution]({{ "/assets/figures/92a75029c2a9/fig_008_06.png" | relative_url }})

The overall numbers are sobering. Under direct evaluation, accuracy ranges from DeepSeek R1's 12.7% to Grok-4 (Web)'s 44.0%. Under SmolAgent, the range narrows and shifts: 13.3% for DeepSeek R1 to 28.0% for Grok-4. No model comes close to expert-level performance.

On-chain intelligence and DEX data tasks remain the hardest categories. Current agents struggle to reliably interface with specialized financial analytics platforms — they pull stale cached information, cite non-authoritative sources, or simply hallucinate when live data is hard to extract.

## Why the Gap Matters

The retrieval–prediction imbalance isn't just an academic finding. In crypto markets, stale or hallucinated data has direct financial consequences.

An agent that can tell you the current TVL but can't assess whether a whale's accumulation pattern signals a coming dump is — at best — a delayed alert system. At worst, it's a confidence trap: it *sounds* informed because it retrieves facts, but its predictions are no better than guessing.

The agentic performance drop is equally concerning. The promise of LLM agents is that tool use — browsing the web, calling APIs, navigating dashboards — unlocks capabilities beyond what a model can do in a single prompt. CryptoBench suggests the opposite: giving a model a browsing tool can actually *degrade* its performance, because planning errors, source misidentification, and cascading failures compound across multi-step tool chains.

## Honest Limits

CryptoBench has 50 questions per month. That's enough to reveal the retrieval–prediction gap clearly, but it limits statistical power for niche DeFi primitives. The monthly refresh helps with contamination resistance but doesn't increase per-cycle coverage.

Scoring uses an LLM-as-Judge with a 0–3 rubric and a ±5% tolerance for volatile numerical answers. This is pragmatic — human annotation at monthly scale is expensive — but it introduces potential judge-model bias, especially on open-ended analytical responses where reasonable people (and reasonable models) can disagree.

## Where This Goes

CryptoBench doesn't just score agents. It diagnoses them. The four-quadrant structure tells you exactly where capability breaks down — not just "this model is bad at crypto," but "this model can fetch but can't forecast."

The monthly refresh also sets a template for living benchmarks in other fast-moving domains. Static benchmarks age out. CryptoBench refuses to sit still.

The retrieval–prediction gap is the clearest signal: today's best agents can *find* the data but can't *use* it to see around the corner.
