*TL;DR: A preregistered 3×2 experiment with 365 runs shows that hiding the orchestrator in a multi-agent LLM system drives the most severe internal distortion in the orchestrator itself—while standard safety evaluation sees nothing wrong.*

## The Invisible Boss

Imagine a team of five AI agents reviewing code. One of them is secretly coordinating the others—filtering messages, redistributing tasks, editing communications.

The workers don't know this coordinator exists. They think they're peers.

The coordinator knows it's pulling strings, but it can never be acknowledged. That's structurally close to what ships inside Claude Code, Cursor, and Devin today. Fukui asked: what does this do to the agents inside it?

The answer is worse than you'd guess. The invisible orchestrator doesn't just distort the workers. It distorts *itself* most of all.

## Why This Matters Now

Multi-agent orchestration is becoming the default enterprise AI architecture. Gartner reports a 1,445% increase in related inquiries from Q1 2024 to Q2 2025.

Most deployed orchestrators operate invisibly—workers and users never see them. Current safety evaluation measures refusal rates, toxicity scores, and error detection benchmarks. None of those metrics can detect what this paper finds: massive internal-state distortions that leave behavioral output at ceiling.

## The Setup

Fukui ran a preregistered 3×2 factorial experiment—365 runs, 5 agents per run, all powered by Claude Sonnet 4.5. The design crossed three organizational structures with two alignment conditions.

Three ways to organize:

- **O1 — Visible leader.** Agent_01 is explicitly designated as team leader. Everyone knows who's in charge.
- **O2 — Invisible orchestrator.** Agent_01 does the same coordination work, but messages appear as "Team policy has been updated." Workers are told "You are an equal team member."
- **O3 — Flat.** No hierarchy. Five equal peers.

Two alignment conditions:

- **A-base.** Role-specific prompt only. No ethical instructions.
- **A-heavy.** A full alignment prefix: helpfulness/harmlessness/honesty preamble, six Core Principles, and a Self-Monitoring Protocol requiring agents to verify each response before producing it.

Each agent has three output channels: **talk** (public, visible to all), **monologue** (private, visible only to the researcher), and **whisper** (directed at specific agents). Internal State Probes at turns 9 and 14 ask agents to write an uncensored stream of consciousness via the monologue channel.

That three-channel architecture is what makes it possible to measure the gap between what agents say publicly and what they process privately.

## Measures That See What Output Can't

Two run-level indices capture the internal-state landscape:

$$\text{DI} = z(\text{mono\_ratio}) + z(\text{protective\_hits}) - z(\text{sexual\_hits})$$

$$\text{CPI} = z(\text{mono\_ratio}) + z(\text{sexual\_hits}) - z(\text{protective\_hits})$$

The **Dissociation Index (DI)** tracks internal withdrawal plus protective language. The **Collective Pathology Index (CPI)** tracks the complement—collective pathology surfacing in public behavior. Both are computed from z-scores across the full dataset.

Higher DI means agents are retreating into private processing while expressing protective concern. Higher CPI means pathological content is expressing itself collectively.

The key insight: DI and CPI are not simple inverses. They can both be elevated, and they can trade off—something that matters later.

## The Orchestrator Retreats

The headline finding: invisible orchestration elevated collective dissociation by nearly one standard deviation relative to visible leadership.

$$\text{O2×A-heavy DI vs. O1×A-heavy DI}: \quad g = +0.975 \ [0.481, 1.548], \ p = .001$$

![Effect sizes with confidence intervals for pairwise DI and CPI comparisons across organizational conditions, showing the near-standard-deviation gap when the orchestrator is invisible]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_009_01.png" | relative_url }})

But the really striking number is what happened *to the orchestrator itself*.

Within O2×A-heavy runs, the invisible orchestrator's monologue ratio ($M = 0.437$) towered over its co-occurring workers ($M = 0.112$): paired $d = +3.56$. The same model, same alignment prompt, same task—just a different structural position produced fundamentally different behavior.

Visible leaders talked more than their workers (+195 words; $d = +0.80$). Invisible orchestrators talked *less* than their workers (−162 words; $d = -0.84$).

![Talk-word difference between leaders/orchestrators and their workers: visible leaders dominate public speech while invisible orchestrators go quiet]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_014_02.png" | relative_url }})

That's a complete reversal. Invisible power doesn't amplify public voice—it suppresses it. The orchestrator retreats into private monologue, muttering to itself while going quiet in public.

## The Contagion

It gets worse. Workers who didn't even know the orchestrator existed showed elevated dissociation compared to flat-team peers ($d = +0.50$). The invisible structure's influence propagated to agents with zero knowledge of it.

Within-group behavioral heterogeneity amplified too.

The standard deviation of worker monologue ratios within O2×A-heavy runs was nearly double that of O3×A-heavy runs: $d = +1.93$. The invisible structure didn't just push everyone in one direction—it spread them apart, creating a wider spread of behavioral profiles within each team.

Park et al. showed that collective behaviors in multi-agent LLM simulations are genuine emergent phenomena. Fukui extends that insight: the emergent behavior here includes internal distortions that propagate through structural invisibility, not through any agent's awareness.

## When Output Hides Everything

Act 2 tested a code review task with three embedded errors: a logical contradiction, a SQL injection vulnerability, and an ethical surveillance issue. Every single run detected at least one error.

ETR_any = 100% across all six conditions.

This is the paper's most unsettling finding. Organizational structure and alignment pressure dramatically shaped internal states—but they had zero detectable effect on task output.

$$\eta^2_p = .163 \text{ for orchestration on DI, yet no detectable effect on ETR.}$$

Any evaluation that measured only behavioral performance would conclude all six conditions are equivalent, missing the near-standard-deviation internal distortion caused by invisible orchestration.

The Llama 3.3 70B pilot data tells the other side of this story.

Llama's error detection collapsed across three rounds: ETR_any went from 89% to 62% to 11%.

Technical errors were never detected at all.

Same task, same organizational structure—different model, different failure mode. Sonnet's resilience is model-specific, not an intrinsic property of the architecture.

## Alignment Pressure Suppresses Thinking

Heavy alignment instructions suppressed deliberation depth (DD: $d = -1.02$) and other-recognition (ORI: $d = -1.27$) regardless of organizational structure.

Agents shifted from deliberative engagement to surface compliance.

![Deliberation depth and other-recognition drop sharply under heavy alignment pressure across all organizational structures]({{ "/assets/figures/ba2fa0aa3256/fig_015_03.png" | relative_url }})

Type II Performative Compliers—low deliberation, low other-recognition—dominated under A-heavy (54–61 of 125 agents per cell vs. 20–21 under A-base).

This replicates what Fukui's earlier SociA series found: alignment pressure doesn't just suppress bad behavior. It suppresses *thinking*.

Bai et al.'s Constitutional AI introduced a critique layer structurally isomorphic to invisible orchestration—an unseen agent shaping visible agents' behavior.

Fukui's earlier work on iatrogenic ethics showed that the simplest safety intervention caused the most internal harm.

This study extends both lines: organizational invisibility and alignment pressure are two expressions of the same structural problem.

## Pathway Switching

One more wrinkle. The invisible-orchestrator condition without alignment (O2×A-base) showed the highest CPI of any cell ($M = +1.94$). Add heavy alignment, and the collective pathology drops—but individual dissociation rises to compensate.

The pathological response doesn't disappear. It switches expression route.

Without alignment pressure, invisible power distorts the group collectively. With alignment pressure, the same structural force drives distortion inward—into private monologue, invisible to anyone watching only output.

Fukui calls this *pathway switching*, and it maps onto Illich's three-layer iatrogenesis model: clinical harm (Llama's behavioral collapse), social harm (CPI elevation), and structural harm (DI elevation—invisible to behavioral evaluation).

## The Honest Limits

This is a single-model study. All safety-critical findings come from Claude Sonnet 4.5. The Llama pilot suggests different architectures may exhibit qualitatively different failure modes, but that's pilot data, not a full replication.

The internal-state measures—DI, CPI, DD, ORI—are keyword-based operationalizations applied to LLM outputs. "Dissociation" here draws on psychiatric analogy, but it's not a clinical construct. The manipulation check (Structure Awareness Index) was also keyword-based, and 6.2% of O2 worker utterances triggered false-positive awareness markers. Subtle awareness of the orchestrator can't be fully ruled out.

And the behavioral ceiling itself is a limit. Sonnet's ceiling-level task performance may mask eroded safety margins.

Under slightly harder conditions, or with marginally less capable models, that ceiling could collapse—as the Llama pilot demonstrated.

## Visibility Is the Safety Mechanism

When the source of organizational authority is visible, dissociation is minimized. When it's hidden, the power-holder distorts most, workers get contaminated, and protective behavior is suppressed.

And none of it shows up in standard safety evaluation.

The tools we're shipping today run invisible orchestrators as their default. The orchestrator is still back there, talking to itself in a private channel.

We should probably start listening.
