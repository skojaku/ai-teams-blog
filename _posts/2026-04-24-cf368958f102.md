*TL;DR: DiffMAS throws away natural language as the communication medium between agents and instead lets them pass raw key–value cache representations—then trains the whole pipeline end-to-end, gaining +26.7% on AIME24 and +20.2% on GPQA-Diamond over single-agent baselines.*

## A Bottleneck You Didn't Notice

When LLM agents work together, they talk in English.

Agent A solves part of a problem, serializes its reasoning into a string of tokens, and hands that string to Agent B. Agent B re-encodes those tokens from scratch—throwing away every continuous internal representation that produced them.

This is a two-stroke information disaster.

First, the continuous hidden states inside Agent A get compressed into discrete tokens, losing nuance. Second, the token boundary creates an optimization wall: no gradient can flow from Agent B back to Agent A, so the system can never learn *what* to say to the next agent.

The communication protocol is frozen.

Yu et al. ask a question that sounds almost too simple: what if you stop forcing agents to talk in words, and instead let them share the continuous representations they already compute—then train the whole thing?

## Replace the Text with a Trace

The key insight builds on three prior ideas. Deng et al. showed that reasoning can be internalized into hidden representations rather than emitted as explicit text—latent chain-of-thought works.

Zou et al. demonstrated that agents can swap KV-cache blocks without decoding to text, but their approach was training-free: no gradient signal improved the communication.

Zhuge et al. argued that agentic workflows should be treated as optimizable computation graphs.

DiffMAS puts those three ideas together. Agents don't send messages. They build a shared KV-cache trace.

Here's the mechanism in two stages:

**Stage I — Trace construction.** Agents 1 through K−1 take turns, each one prefilling the existing KV cache, running forward, and appending its own KV segments. No gradients flow yet. No tokens are decoded. The trace just grows.

**Stage II — Decode and train.** The final agent receives the entire accumulated KV trace, decodes autoregressively, and produces an answer. You apply standard cross-entropy supervised fine-tuning—but crucially, the loss backpropagates through all K stages of the trace. Only LoRA parameters are updated; the backbone stays frozen.

![Two-stage DiffMAS framework: agents sequentially construct a shared KV-cache trace in Stage I, then the final agent decodes with gradient backpropagation through LoRA in Stage II]({{ "/assets/figures/cf368958f102/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The "old way → new way" contrast is sharp:

```
old way:  hidden states → tokens → re-encode → hidden states
new way:  hidden states → KV cache → KV cache (continuous, differentiable)
```

No serialization bottleneck. No gradient wall.

## Why Concatenation Matters

DiffMAS doesn't overwrite previous agents' cache entries. It concatenates.

Each new stage appends its KV blocks to the trace, preserving everything that came before. This isn't just an implementation detail. Proposition 3.1 shows that concatenation has a structural advantage over overwriting designs.

In an overwriting system, agent $j$ passes a fixed-dimensional carrier $h_j \in \mathbb{R}^{d_h}$. If the Jacobian $\|\partial h_{j+1}/\partial h_j\|_2 \leq \rho < 1$, then gradients reaching stage $j$ decay geometrically as $\rho^{K-j}$.

The deeper the pipeline, the fainter the signal.

In a concatenative system, the trace $Z_{1:N_K} = [Z^{(1)}_{1:T}; \dots; Z^{(K)}_{1:T}]$ is a growing object. The gradient bound for any segment $Z^{(j)}_{1:T}$ satisfies:

$$\left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Z^{(j)}_{1:T}}\right\|_2 \leq \left\|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Z_{1:N_K}}\right\|_2$$

No $\rho^{K-j}$ factor appears at the interface level. Every agent's contribution remains equally visible to the optimizer, regardless of depth.

The proof is in the appendix and it's clean: concatenation simply doesn't introduce multiplicative decay through the communication interface itself.

## Compact Protocols, Not Long Conversations

One of the most striking results: on AIME24 with Qwen3-8B, just 10 latent communication steps push accuracy from 50.0% to 76.7%.

Extending to 200 steps *drops* it back to 63.3%.

The model isn't using long traces to do more reasoning. It's learning a compact, efficient communication protocol in a narrow latent window. More steps just add noise.

The StitchMAS ablation reinforces this. StitchMAS independently generates KV segments for each agent and then stitches them together under the same SFT regime.

On GPQA-Diamond, StitchMAS scores 48.4% versus DiffMAS's 60.1%.

The gain comes from agents building a *continuous* trace—each one conditioning on what came before—not from merely having multiple KV segments in the room.

## What They Found

The headline numbers land hard. On Qwen3-8B:

- AIME24: 50.0% → 76.7% (+26.7%)
- GPQA-Diamond: 39.9% → 60.1% (+20.2%)
- Meaningful improvements on HumanEval+, MBPP+, and OpenBookQA across model scales from 4B to 14B. At 32B, some benchmarks (AIME25, OpenBookQA) show zero gain.

![Accuracy comparison across benchmarks and model scales showing DiffMAS consistently outperforming single-agent, TextMAS, LatentMAS, and TextMAS+SFT baselines]({{ "/assets/figures/cf368958f102/fig_007_02.png" | relative_url }})

Smaller models benefit most. Qwen3-4B jumps from 43.3% to 63.3% on AIME24—a +20 point swing.

At 32B (DeepSeek-R1-Distill), the gains thin out. Zero on AIME25 and OpenBookQA, though HumanEval+ still picks up +7.8%. When the base model is already very strong, the learned latent channel sometimes adds nothing.

The gains aren't just about task finetuning. DiffMAS matches TextMAS+SFT on AIME24 (both 76.7%), but pulls ahead on distribution-shifted benchmarks: AIME25 56.7% vs 50.0%, GPQA-Diamond 60.1% vs 53.5%.

Learning *how to communicate* generalizes better than learning *how to solve the training task*.

Decoding is more stable, too. On AIME24, DiffMAS achieves mean perplexity of 1.24 versus LatentMAS's 1.31, with a tighter distribution and far fewer entropy spikes.

Self-consistency analysis shows DiffMAS clustering at 3–4 correct out of 4 samples, while LatentMAS and TextMAS cluster at 0–1.

## The Honest Limits

This is not a plug-and-play communication layer you can drop into any multi-agent system.

Training requires SFT data matched to the target task. There's no evidence the learned protocol transfers across domains without re-finetuning.

The C2C baseline was trained on instruction-following data rather than reasoning data, so the gap between DiffMAS and C2C partly reflects a training-data mismatch rather than architecture alone.

All experiments run sequential agent pipelines. Whether the KV-trace approach scales to parallel or graph-structured multi-agent topologies is an open question.

And at the 32B scale, the gains thin out. When the base model is already very strong, adding a learned latent channel over a sequential pipeline sometimes adds nothing.

## Make the Medium Learnable

The deeper contribution isn't the benchmark numbers. It's the reframing.

Multi-agent LLM research has focused on who the agents are and how they're organized. DiffMAS says: the *medium* they use to talk is itself a learnable parameter. Optimize it jointly with reasoning, and the agents discover communication protocols that no human would design—and that no text interface could represent.

The concatenative trace gives gradients a clear path. Ten steps are enough. And the resulting protocols generalize where task-specific finetuning doesn't.

The words were never the point. The gradients were.
