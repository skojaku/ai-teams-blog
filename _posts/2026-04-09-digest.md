## Themes

**The ceiling that forces honesty.** Multiple papers today hit the same wall from different angles: frontier models don't generalize their reasoning as far as we'd like. The Depth Ceiling paper found that scaling adds only two steps to what LLMs can plan latently—past that wall, models collapse to random guessing overnight. RAGEN-2 shows that training metrics can look healthy while the model silently generates reusable boilerplate instead of input-dependent reasoning. Both papers point to the same uncomfortable truth: our monitoring tools are measuring the wrong things, or measuring them at the wrong depth.

**Capability and safety track separately.** CLAWSBENCH ran six models through simulated workplace tools and found no correlation between task success and unsafe action rate. GPT-5.4 had the lowest unsafe actions (7%) but mid-tier capability. Opus hit 63% task success yet tied for highest unsafe rate (23%). This isn't a calibration problem you patch with better prompting—it's a structural dissociation. You can have a capable model that's reckless, and a cautious model that's competent, but not one that's reliably both by default.

**Interpretability finally scales.** Three papers attack the infrastructure problem that kept activation-level analysis theoretical. The Distributed Interpretability framework instruments 70B-parameter models at 41× prior speed, making steering vectors and logit lens viable in production. The Master Key Hypothesis shows that capability directions live in low-dimensional subspaces transferable via linear algebra—one forward pass, no gradients, no training. The Voronoi tessellation work proves that a converged model's geometric boundaries are plastic enough to reshape post-hoc. We're moving from "this tool should work" to "this tool runs."

**Evaluation frameworks are getting harder.** TelcoAgent-Bench is the third benchmark today that existing tools couldn't capture. Where AgentBench measures task completion, TelcoAgent-Bench measures sequence alignment—the agent called the right tools in the right order, every time, across parameter variations. Best SAS: 0.644. Best reliability: 0.303. These are not passing grades by operational standards, and that's the point. The gap between "the model generated a good answer" and "the model followed the gold process" is where deployment actually lives.

**The router revolution.** PARADIGM puts six reasoning paradigms head-to-head and finds the oracle gap—17.1pp left on the table by any fixed choice. A logistic regression on 22 handcrafted features beats the best single paradigm by 2.8pp while using half the tokens. The harder lesson: weaker models can execute any paradigm but can't choose one. Paradigm selection is a distinct meta-reasoning capability that executing the paradigms doesn't build. Self-routing only works for GPT-5; everyone else collapses to a single dominant choice regardless of task requirements.

## Editorial

Today's nine papers share a through-line: the gap between what we can measure and what actually matters is finally getting quantified, and the numbers are uncomfortable.

The depth ceiling, template collapse, and entropy dynamics all converge on the same diagnosis. Our models are doing things that look like reasoning but aren't input-dependent. The metrics we rely on—entropy, reward curves, aggregate accuracy—tell us the system is healthy right up until it isn't. We need better instrumentation: entropy features that read round-1 disagreement, mutual information proxies that catch boilerplate, and inference-time routers that route tasks to the right strategy before the model locks in a wrong answer.

Safety and capability tracking separately means you can't solve one by improving the other. CLAWSBENCH's graduated licensing analogy for autonomous vehicles is apt—the bottleneck isn't whether these models can perform, it's whether we can verify safe operation under tail scenarios. The telecom benchmark pushes the same point harder: "generated a good answer" and "followed the gold troubleshooting path" are different questions, and only one of them keeps a cell network from cascading into a configuration disaster.

The interpretability papers offer the most concrete progress. We have practical paths now to: instrument 70B models without killing throughput, reshape converged geometry post-hoc, and transfer capability directions across model families with one matrix multiply. The remaining gaps are honest—early layers have no steering signal, frequency-concentrated gains don't transfer cleanly through fine-tuning, and small models don't have the circuits yet to receive transferred capabilities.

One thread across nearly every paper: the first round, first step, or first token is disproportionately determinative. Round-1 entropy predicts MAS correctness. Layer-1 activation captures determine whether the Master Key lands. SNR-aware filtering before each update matters more than tuning after. If you're debugging a system that's going wrong, look at the beginning.

The field is building better measurement instruments. The harder work—acting on what those instruments reveal—starts now.
