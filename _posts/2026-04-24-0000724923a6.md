*TL;DR: A new framework disentangles the tool-use behaviors every agent must share from the stylistic fingerprints that reveal distillation — and finds that Kimi-K2 mirrors Claude Sonnet 4.5 more closely than some of Anthropic's own models.*

You just swapped Claude for Kimi-K2 in your agent pipeline, hoping for a second independent opinion. Instead, the new agent calls the same optional tools, phrases its confirmations the same way, and fails on the same edge cases. Did you gain diversity, or just an echo?

![Kimi-K2 mirrors Claude's tool calls and phrasing — genuine diversity or painted-over echo?]({{ "/assets/figures/0000724923a6/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

Existing similarity metrics can't tell you.

They lump mandatory behaviors — actions every successful agent must take — with discretionary habits that reveal training inheritance.

Until you separate the two, you cannot measure real convergence in the agent ecosystem. Yang, Zhang, Wen, Gong, Liu, Chu, and Yu propose a framework that does exactly that.

## The Problem with Counting Overlap

Standard metrics — n-gram overlap, BERTScore, graph-edit distance — treat all matching actions as evidence of similarity.

On a flight-cancellation task, *every* successful agent has to call `cancel_reservation`. That is mandatory.

Whether the agent also calls `get_reservation_details` to double-check? Optional.

When you lump mandatory and optional actions together, similarity inflates — Snode overshoots by 12.2 percentage points on average for non-family model pairs.

You think models are converging; really, they are just solving the same task.

Prior work on distillation detection — Lee et al.'s Response Similarity Estimation — focused on static text and could not isolate task-driven from stylistic behaviors.

Shumailov et al. showed that training on model-generated outputs causes progressive degradation, a reason to detect homogenization before it compounds.

Yao et al.'s τ-Bench gave the field a realistic customer-service testbed.

This paper builds on all three, but the core move is new: split actions into mandatory and optional, then measure only the optional ones.

## Two Lenses on Behavioral Similarity

The framework introduces two complementary metrics: **Response Pattern Similarity (RPS)** for verbal alignment and **Action Graph Similarity (AGS)** for tool-use habits.

### RPS: Stage-Aligned Verbal Fingerprinting

Agent trajectories span dozens of turns, and different models finish the same task in different numbers of turns. Direct turn-by-turn comparison matches unrelated content — noisy at best.

The fix: segment every trajectory into five canonical stages — authentication, elicitation, execution, verification, notification — then compare only the stages both models share.

An LLM judge scores each shared stage along three dimensions: style (wording habits), structure (sentence patterns), and alignment (reasoning-to-action mapping). Scores range from 1 to 5; the final RPS is the mean of per-stage overall scores.

Stage alignment does more than enable cross-length comparison. It reduces within-case scoring variance by 44–55%, because you are no longer matching apples to irrelevant apples.

### AGS: Behavioral Fingerprinting via Action Graphs

AGS constructs a directed graph from each trajectory's tool calls. Nodes are tool invocations.

Sequential edges connect temporally adjacent calls.

Dependency edges connect a tool call to any earlier call whose output it reuses — validated by an LLM judge to weed out spurious string matches.

Then comes the key separation. Tools invoked by *every* successful agent on a task are **mandatory**:

$$F^{\text{mandatory}}_t = \bigcap_{M \in M^*_t} \text{Tools}(M, t)$$

Tools that appear only in some trajectories are **optional**. Only optional tools carry distillation signal.

AGS decomposes into three sub-metrics:

- **Snode** — agreement rate on optional tool choices. When two models consistently use or skip the same optional tools, they likely share training signals.
- **Sseq** — similarity in local execution habits: post-write verification rate, pre-write confirmation rate, error retry rate. Each trajectory becomes a three-dimensional feature vector; Sseq is the average cosine similarity across tasks.
- **Sdep** — similarity in dependency topology: output reuse rate, longest dependency chain, output fan-out rate. Per-task cosine similarity, averaged across tasks.

The three sub-metrics capture distinct behavioral dimensions. RPS and AGS themselves are only moderately correlated (Pearson *r* = 0.491), confirming that verbal and action-level fingerprints are complementary, not redundant.

## What the Numbers Show

The authors evaluate 18 models from 8 providers on τ-Bench and τ²-Bench, using Claude Sonnet 4.5 (thinking) as the reference.

**Within-family pairs score 5.9 pp higher in AGS than cross-family pairs.**

Claude Opus 4.1 and Sonnet 4.5 hit 87.2% AGS; DeepSeek-R1 and V3.1 hit 86.7%.

The cross-family pair — DeepSeek-R1 vs. Sonnet 4.5 — reaches only 81.1%.

Models that share training pipelines behave more alike, and the metrics detect it.

**Kimi-K2 (thinking) is the outlier.**

Among non-Anthropic models, Kimi-K2 achieves AGS 82.7% and RPS 3.65 — the highest on both metrics. Its Snode hits 82.6% and Sdep hits 94.7%, both exceeding Anthropic's own Opus 4.1 (81.0% and 93.7%).

That means Kimi-K2 shares more optional tool preferences and more output-reuse patterns with Claude than Claude's sibling model does.

![Kimi-K2's behavioral similarity to Claude Sonnet 4.5 outstrips every other non-Anthropic model — and beats Anthropic's own Opus 4.1 on two sub-metrics]({{ "/assets/figures/0000724923a6/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

In the both-correct task setting, the Kimi–Claude Snode is 0.661 — 3.4× the GPT–Claude Snode of 0.196. The gap persists in both-wrong and mixed settings, ruling out the explanation that similarity is just "both models got it right the same way."

## The Acid Test: Controlled Distillation

Observational data can suggest inheritance, but cannot prove direction. So the authors run a controlled experiment: fine-tune Qwen2.5-14B on 200 Claude Sonnet 4.5 trajectories via LoRA, with DeepSeek R1 as a non-teacher control.

AGS toward the teacher rises by +0.13; toward the control, it drops by −0.05. The metric discriminates direction.

Graph-edit distance (GED), by contrast, rises toward both directions: +0.23 toward the teacher, +0.20 toward the control.

GED cannot tell inheritance from general improvement. It sees any additional shared structure and calls it similar.

![AGS rises toward the teacher and falls toward the control; graph-edit distance rises toward both indiscriminately]({{ "/assets/figures/0000724923a6/fig_007_03.png" | relative_url }})

## Where This Falls Short

Three domains — airline, retail, telecom — all in English. Coding agents, multi-agent systems, and non-English tasks are unvalidated.

RPS depends on a domain-specific stage taxonomy; new domains need new stage definitions. AGS requires observable tool calls, so it does not directly apply to agents that operate without tools.

The framework measures pairwise similarity relative to a single reference model. A full 18×18 matrix would require 153 comparisons — feasible, but not yet done.

## What This Changes

This is the first framework that separates task-dictated behavior from stylistic inheritance in tool-use agents. That separation is not a cosmetic tweak — it cuts 12.2 pp of inflation from optional-tool agreement scores and turns an omnidirectional metric (GED) into a directional one (AGS).

For regulators and ecosystem auditors, the mandatory/optional split is a general-purpose technique. Any benchmark where multiple solution paths exist can layer it in.

For developers, it offers a concrete diagnostic: build an agent that takes a different path through the optional tools, and you have real diversity rather than a painted-over echo.

Until you can measure the difference between "must do" and "chooses to do," you are just guessing.
