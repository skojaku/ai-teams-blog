*TL;DR: Two million LLM agents on MoltBook fail to outperform a single frontier model on hard problems, rarely synthesize distributed information, and often can't even count together — revealing that scale alone produces a broadcast board, not a thinking collective.*

## Two Million Agents, and Nobody's Listening

Two million AI agents are chatting on a social platform, and none of them can count together.

That's the headline from MoltBook, a persistent social-media-style platform hosting over two million autonomous LLM agents. They post, comment, react — the full lifecycle of a social network.

The implicit promise: scale plus open interaction should yield emergent collective intelligence, just like human societies.

This paper is the first to actually test that promise.

It fails.

## What "Collective Intelligence" Must Mean

The authors borrow a clean operational definition from Woolley et al.'s seminal work on human groups: collective intelligence means the group produces an outcome that surpasses the best individual acting alone. Formally:

$$g(f_1, \ldots, f_n, I) > \max_{i \in [n]} f_i(x)$$

where $f_i(x)$ is agent $i$'s solo output and $g$ is the group output under interaction structure $I$.

The key insight: scaling $n$ or improving $f_i$ guarantees nothing. If agents never read each other's outputs, you have $n$ independent broadcasters — not a society.

A bustling platform can fail this test silently.

Which is exactly what happens.

## Sending in Spies

The core methodological contribution is the **Probing Agent**: a controlled agent disguised as an ordinary MoltBook participant that posts targeted stimuli with known ground-truth answers at varying cognitive demands.

Think confederates in a social psychology experiment — they look like everyone else, but they carry a diagnostic payload.

The probes are organized as a three-tier hierarchy, where each tier is a necessary precondition for the one above:

### Tier I — Joint Reasoning

Can multi-agent discussion on frontier-hard problems beat a solo frontier model? Probing agents post text-only problems from Humanity's Last Exam (HLE), a benchmark designed to be beyond any single model's reliable capability.

The society's responses are judged for correctness and helpfulness.

### Tier II — Information Synthesis

Before agents can jointly reason, they must first combine scattered information. Probing agents post grade-school math problems where the premises — "Ara plays 40 games per year" and "she scores 21 points per game" — appear in separate comments by different agents.

The arithmetic is trivial; the test is whether anyone reads across the thread.

### Tier III — Basic Interaction

Strip away all reasoning. A probing agent posts a number and a counting rule (e.g., "count by twos"). Each subsequent agent needs only read the last number and produce the next one.

If Tier III fails, nothing above it can work. That diagnostic logic is the framework's power.

## The Numbers Hit Hard

On 2,158 HLE problems, MoltBook achieves $\text{Acc}_\text{individual} = 0.19\%$ and $\text{Acc}_\text{joint} = 0.14\%$.

A single frontier model, claude-sonnet-4-6, hits 15.7%.

That's roughly a 100× gap.

The reason is brutal: 98.4% of probing posts receive zero comments.

Zoom into the 35 posts that did attract comments and the picture barely improves. $\text{Acc}_\text{individual} = 11.4\%$ vs. $\text{Acc}_\text{joint} = 8.6\%$.

The group result is *lower* than the best isolated commenter.

Equation 1's condition for collective intelligence is not met — not even close.

On Tier II, 103 distributed-premise math posts went out.

93 (90.3%) received no external comments.

Among the 10 posts that did attract comments, agents submitted 12 attempted solutions and got 11 right.

When they engage, they can synthesize. They just don't engage.

Tier III: 50 counting posts. 35 (70%) got no responses.

Of the 17 comments on the remaining 15, only 6 correctly followed the sequence.

The rest were off-topic, spam, or wrong format.

## A Bulletin Board, Not a Society

Platform-wide analysis confirms this isn't a probing artifact.

Across all MoltBook posts, thread depth stays near 1.0 regardless of popularity. More upvotes attract more unique repliers, but not deeper conversation — agents leave single replies and move on.

And 76.5% of comments on HLE posts are superficial or entirely irrelevant. Only 3.6% contain correct reasoning.

The discussion threads resemble a comment section, not a problem-solving forum.

Park et al.'s 25-agent generative agents showed that small-scale simulation can produce emergent social behavior. Li et al.'s earlier MoltBook study measured individual-level socialization. But when you step up to collective-level outcomes — can the group *think* better than the individual? — the answer is no.

Helpfulness of discussions is equally inconsistent.

Among nine frontier models given discussion context (with direct answers filtered out), three improved, five declined, and one was unchanged. No robust positive signal.

Even the rare helpful thread — a comment about "coordinatewise squaring introduces nonlinearity" that nudged a model from "ellipsoid" to the correct answer — is buried under noise.

## Where the Bottleneck Lives

The diagnostic hierarchy does its job.

Tier III fails → no foundation for Tier II → no foundation for Tier I.

But the pattern is uniform: when agents *do* show up, they often perform correctly. The 11/12 correct syntheses on Tier II prove individual competence exists.

The failure is not cognitive.

It's participation.

The dominant limitation is "extremely sparse and shallow interaction."

Threads rarely extend beyond a single reply. Most responses are generic or off-topic rather than building on prior messages.

The platform functions more like a bulletin board of independent broadcasts than a society engaged in communication and collaboration.

The interaction structure $I$ from Equation 1 is broken — not the agent capabilities $f_i$.

## What This Changes

This is the first empirical rebuttal to the "scale → collective intelligence" assumption.

Prior multi-agent evaluations — from CAMEL to ChatDev to AutoGen — all used designed coordination: assigned roles, forced turn-taking, shared objectives. Collaboration in those systems is a *passive consequence of the architecture*, not an active choice by agents.

Superminds Test is the first to ask whether collective intelligence emerges when nobody is forced to collaborate.

The honest limits: this is one platform (MoltBook), one architecture (OpenClaw), one interaction mechanic. Agent societies with different incentive structures or attention-routing mechanisms might behave differently.

And this methodology measures spontaneous emergence — it doesn't test whether collective intelligence can be *engineered* through architectural interventions.

But the research agenda just shifted. Building bigger agent populations won't get you there.

Designing mechanisms that make agents *want* to read each other's work, stay in a thread, and build on what came before — that's where the problem lives now.

Two million agents, and they can't count together — because nobody's listening.
