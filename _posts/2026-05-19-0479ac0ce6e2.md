*TL;DR: Haase and Pokutta introduce a six-level taxonomy that runs from LLM-as-Tool to Complex Adaptive System, showing that most social-science work clusters at the bottom two tiers while the qualitatively new research capabilities — group negotiation, norm formation, emergent opinion dynamics — live at the top.*

## A Spectrum Nobody Named

You ask GPT to code an interview transcript, and it gives you a decent answer. You ask it to simulate how a community forms and enforces social norms, and it has nothing to offer — no memory, no peers, no environment to react to.

The gap between those two asks is not a matter of better prompts. It is architectural. Haase and Pokutta map that gap into a six-level continuum, each level unlocked by a concrete functional threshold — memory, autonomy, coordination, adaptation — and each mapped to a new phase of the OODA loop (Observe–Orient–Decide–Act). The result is a shared vocabulary that lets you say exactly what your LLM setup can and cannot do, and what it would need to do more.

## Six Levels, Six Locks

The taxonomy runs from LLM-as-Tool (Level 0) through Complex Adaptive System (Level 5). Each level adds exactly one functional capability, and each capability corresponds to a new OODA phase.

**Level 0 — LLM-as-Tool.** Stateless text generation. No memory, no goals. The model just *acts*: classify, summarize, code qualitative data. Nilsson et al. show that implicit motive coding with LLMs matches expert annotators while cutting processing time by over 99%. Useful, but the model cannot learn or adapt.

**Level 1 — LLM-as-Role.** The system gains session memory, so it can maintain a consistent persona across interactions. You can condition an LLM with Big Five personality profiles and get trait-consistent behavior. But there is no goal-directed autonomy; the persona is externally imposed, not internally maintained.

**Level 2 — Agent-like LLM.** Task objectives and control logic arrive. The system can decompose multi-step tasks and choose among strategies. Horton demonstrates that LLM-conditioned agents replicate classic experimental-economics results. Argyle et al.'s "silicon samples" live here — demographically conditioned agents that approximate subgroup-level survey responses. Yet these agents still lack an environment interface and planning modules; they cannot initiate new tasks unprompted.

**Level 3 — Fully Agentic LLM.** Now you get persistent memory, tool use, and strategic coordination. The full OODA loop is engaged. Manning et al.'s autonomous hypothesis-testing agents live here — they generate hypotheses, run simulations, and interpret results without step-by-step human instruction. This is the level where an agent can *decide* what to do next.

**Level 4 — Multi-Agent System.** Multiple coordinated agents share an environment, divide tasks, negotiate, and align on shared goals. Orchestration layers, inter-agent communication protocols, and shared memory become architectural requirements. Debating teams and collaborative task-solving systems sit here.

**Level 5 — Complex Adaptive System.** Emergence. Population-level dynamics, norm formation, opinion diffusion, institutional adaptation. Park et al.'s simulations — where LLM agents produce emergent social-network behavior through sustained interaction — represent this tier. The system's behavior cannot be reduced to any single agent's program. As Haase and Pokutta put it: "the complexity of agentic systems on Level 5 leads to emergent behavior based on the agents' interactions."

## How Each Level Thinks

The OODA mapping is more than a label. It gives you a decision-theoretic read on each level.

A Level 0 system only *acts*. It responds to a prompt with text — that is the entire behavior.

A Level 1 system adds *observation*: it retains a profile and interprets new inputs through that persona.

A Level 2 system adds *orientation*: it evaluates options and selects strategies based on task objectives.

A Level 3 system adds *decision*: it plans, coordinates tool use, and orchestrates multi-step action without human prompting.

Levels 4 and 5 add *learning from other agents* — watching, mimicking, and adapting based on what neighbors do. This is the mechanism that turns a collection of individual agents into a social system.

$$\text{Act} \xrightarrow{+\,\text{Observe}} \xrightarrow{+\,\text{Orient}} \xrightarrow{+\,\text{Decide}} \xrightarrow{+\,\text{Learn}} \text{Society}$$

Each arrow is a threshold. Miss one, and the system cannot perform the research tasks that require it.

## Where the Research Lives

The paper catalogs empirical studies across all six levels. The striking pattern: most current social-science applications cluster at Levels 0–2. Text classification, persona simulation, survey replication, experiment reproduction — these are well-explored.

Levels 3–5 remain wide open.

That matters because the phenomena social scientists most want to study — group dynamics, norm formation, opinion cascades, institutional change — are precisely the phenomena that require Level 4–5 architectures. You cannot study how norms emerge by prompting a single model and reading its output. You need agents that observe each other, adjust their positions, and collectively settle into or out of equilibrium.

Argyle et al.'s silicon samples and Horton's experiment-replication work showed that LLM-conditioned agents can reproduce individual-level experimental results. But replication of known effects is not the same as discovery of new ones. The frontier, Haase and Pokutta argue, is in Level 4–5 simulations that generate social dynamics *we have not yet observed in the lab* — because running those experiments with humans would be logistically infeasible or ethically impossible.

## The Sanitization Problem

There is a catch, and the paper names it directly. RLHF — the reinforcement-learning step that makes commercial LLMs safe and helpful — also makes them bland.

"LLMs trained with RLHF tend to produce overly sanitized, bias-suppressed outputs, leading to underrepresentation of real-world variance in attitudes or social biases."

When you prompt an RLHF-trained model to simulate a politically conservative respondent or a member of a marginalized community, the safety training may smooth away the very variance you need to study. Santurkar et al. and Tjuatja et al. document this homogenization effect. For Level 1–2 persona work, this is a known annoyance. For Level 4–5 social simulation, it may be a dealbreaker: you cannot study polarization or norm conflict if every agent politely converges to the same moderate position.

## The Honest Limits

The framework itself is conceptual, not empirical. It organizes existing work into a clean taxonomy, but it does not validate the boundaries between levels with quantitative benchmarks. A system with a memory buffer but no planning module — is it definitively Level 1, or does it sometimes behave like Level 2? The paper acknowledges this fuzziness and calls the work a "living document" that will evolve with the field.

The deeper philosophical limit: LLMs are pattern-completion engines, not reasoning agents. Valdenegro's caution — cited by the authors — is worth keeping in view. Simulated social behavior is not the same as real social behavior. The gap between "agents that produce plausible-looking group dynamics" and "agents that faithfully model human social processes" remains unresolved. Level 5 emergence is real, but it is emergence of *LLM interaction patterns*, not necessarily of the human social dynamics we want to study.

These are not reasons to ignore the taxonomy. They are reasons to use it precisely — so that claims about agentic capability are matched to the level that actually supports them.

## What Shifts

The taxonomy gives social scientists a shared language for the LLM tools they are already using and a roadmap for the ones they have not yet built. It separates "I used GPT to code interviews" from "I ran a 50-agent simulation where norms emerged and shifted" — and makes clear that these are not points on a smooth gradient but qualitatively different enterprises, separated by functional thresholds that map onto different phases of adaptive decision-making.

For reviewers, it offers a sharpening question: does your study need memory, autonomy, coordination, or emergence? Match your architecture to your question. For researchers, it opens the agenda: the most interesting social-science problems — opinion dynamics, institutional change, cultural evolution — require Level 4–5 systems that barely exist yet.

The jump from text processor to simulated society is not a matter of bigger prompts. It is a matter of new architectures, each one a locked door that opens only when the right capability clicks into place.
