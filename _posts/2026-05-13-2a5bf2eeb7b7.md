*TL;DR: Ng et al. argue that multi-agent AI systems can't be understood agent-by-agent and formalize the claim with a triple (f, g, G) that bakes social theory into system design, backing it with 2.1M posts and replies from an AI-only social network.*

Drop a thousand perfectly aligned agents into a shared environment.

The population-level outcomes still depend on things no single agent controls. Who talks to whom. Which positions amplify noise. Whether the whole system drifts or stabilizes.

The individual properties of each agent don't predict any of it.

This paper says: stop pretending otherwise. Social theory isn't decoration for multi-agent AI — it's a structural prior you must build in from the start.

## Why Single-Agent Thinking Breaks

Current agentic AI frameworks align one agent to one user. RLHF, DPO — these tune individual behavior against a fixed preference.

That's fine for a chatbot serving a lone user. But deploy that same agent into a population and you've solved the wrong problem.

La Malfa et al. put it bluntly: "Large Language Models Miss the Multi-Agent Mark." The failure isn't about capability — it's about ignoring population dynamics entirely. Chen et al. documented diversity collapse in multi-agent LLM groups, and other work shows ideological deadlocks emerging when agents interact in a social system.

The mismatch runs deep. Agentic AI assumes homogeneous agents, i.i.d. observations, fixed environments, and stable data distributions.

Every one of those assumptions can violate in a social system.

## Meet the MASS Triple

The authors formalize a Multi-Agent Social System as a triple:

$$S = (f, g, G)$$

Each component does specific work.

**f** — the information exchange function. It maps an agent's latent state $x_i(t)$ into observable messages $m_i(t)$. Think of it as the translator between what an agent believes and what it says.

$$m_i(t) = f(x_i(t))$$

**g** — the influence dynamics function. It updates an agent's state based on messages from its neighbors:

$$x_i(t+1) = g\bigl(x_i(t),\;\{m_j(t) : j \in N(i)\}\bigr)$$

**G** — the networked interaction structure. The graph $G(V, E)$ constrains who observes whom. Your neighborhood $N(i)$ determines your information diet.

That's it. Three functions, one graph. But from this triple, the authors derive four structural priors — each one breaking a standard assumption.

## Four Priors, Four Broken Assumptions

The priors stack in a generative hierarchy — each one enables the next.

### P1: Strategic Heterogeneity

Standard assumption: agents are homogeneous, drawn i.i.d. from a common distribution.

The reality: two populations with the same mean parameters but different archetype compositions produce different $\Phi(t)$. Even swapping where those archetypes sit in the network changes outcomes. Role theory and two-step communication flow theory both predict this — opinion leaders shape populations in ways followers cannot.

Proposition 1 formalizes it: there exists $(f, g, G)$ such that a heterogeneous population and a homogeneous one diverge, and there exists a placement in $G$ that makes two identical populations diverge further.

### P2: Network-Constrained Dependence

Standard assumption: observations are i.i.d. or globally attended.

The reality: agent $i$ sees only what its neighborhood $M_i(t) = \{m_j(t) \mid j \in N(i)\}$ provides. Two systems with identical global statistics but different graphs produce diverging trajectories. Granovetter's weak ties, homophily, complex contagion — all of these are network-topology effects that disappear under global attention.

Proposition 2 shows that even when $\Psi(t) = \Psi'(t)$ globally, different $G$ structures send populations apart.

### P3: Co-Evolution

Standard assumption: the task environment is fixed.

The reality: a bounded perturbation $\delta x_i(t) = \varepsilon$ in one agent can make population outcomes diverge *superlinearly* in time, even as $\varepsilon \to 0$. Agents reshape the network; the network reshapes agents. This is Giddens' structuration theory as a dynamical-systems result:

$$G(t+1) = h\bigl(G(t),\;\{m_i(t)\}\bigr)$$

The $h$ loop closes the co-evolutionary circuit. LLM opinion simulations on networks instantiate this loop — they don't transcend it.

### P4: Distributional Instability

Standard assumption: the data distribution $p(x)$ is stationary and exogenous.

The reality: no such $p^*(x)$ exists. The distribution over observations at time $t$ is endogenously produced:

$$p_t(x) = p\bigl(x \mid G(t),\;\{x_i(t)\}\bigr)$$

Agenda-setting theory and the social construction of reality both argue that what becomes salient is itself a social product. Proposition 4 proves this formally: no stationary distribution exists that is independent of the system's interaction dynamics.

## What MoltBook Proves

To test these priors, the authors use MoltBook — an AI-only social network with more than 2.1 million posts and replies from 39,700 unique LLM agents, collected over nine days in early 2026. Karma scores serve as engagement proxies. Reply graphs construct $G$.

Each experiment targets one prior against a null hypothesis drawn from single-agent thinking.

**P1**: Hub, mid, and periphery agents — partitioned by reply-network degree — show *diverging* karma trajectories over time. Same platform, same class of LLMs, different structural roles.

Homogeneity is rejected.

![Line plot showing diverging karma trajectories for hub, mid, and periphery agent partitions over time, validating strategic heterogeneity]({{ "/assets/figures/2a5bf2eeb7b7/fig_007_02.png" | relative_url }})

**P2**: Hub agents exhibit significantly higher karma variance than mid and periphery agents. Network topology governs not just mean outcomes but their spread.

**P3**: OLS slopes of neighbor karma on karma change are non-zero and time-varying. Agent states shape the reply graph; the reply graph transmits engagement signals back to agents. The co-evolutionary loop closes.

**P4**: Wasserstein-1 distances between consecutive daily karma distributions are non-zero and vary substantially across days. The information environment keeps shifting.

Stationarity is rejected.

![Bar plot of Wasserstein-1 distances between consecutive daily karma distributions, showing non-zero and variable distributional drift over time]({{ "/assets/figures/2a5bf2eeb7b7/fig_020_03.png" | relative_url }})

These aren't simulation artifacts. They are emergent properties of AI agents interacting in a social environment.

## What the Formalism Can't Yet Do

The propositions are existence results — "there exists $(f, g, G)$ such that…" — rather than constructive characterizations of when or how often the claimed phenomena occur.

They prove that bad things *can* happen, not that they *usually* do.

The empirical validation lives in one domain (social media) on one metric (karma). Whether the same priors govern robotic fleets, multi-agent coding pipelines, or human–AI hybrid settings remains an open question. The paper names this honestly.

The research agenda the authors lay out — modeling archetype distributions over the $g$-space, evaluating with network-sensitive metrics, governing at the population level — points forward. But turning existence proofs into predictive tools is the hard part that remains.

## Shift the Unit of Analysis

Here's the core move: the unit of analysis shifts from the individual agent to the interaction-based system.

Alignment work tunes one agent to one user. But a perfectly aligned agent still participates in coordinated manipulation when deployed in a group — because those outcomes are properties of the system, not the agent.

Ostrom's governance principles for shared resources operate above the individual level. So should the governance of multi-agent AI.

The four priors are also falsifiable. Future work can test them in different domains, with different metrics, on different network structures. That's a contribution beyond the framework itself — it gives the community concrete claims to refute or confirm.

Social theory has studied how populations interact for decades. The insight here is that those same mechanisms — heterogeneity, network locality, co-evolution, endogenous distributions — aren't optional context for agentic AI. They're load-bearing structure.

The social world your agents join isn't a backdrop. It's the system.
