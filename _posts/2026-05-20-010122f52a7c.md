*TL;DR: SIGMA replaces the assumption that all agents cooperate equally with a signed graph that marks trust, conflict, and neutral relations — letting multi-agent LLM reasoning stay accurate even when half the agents are wrong or adversarial.*

## When Teammates Amplify Mistakes

Put five LLM agents in a room and ask them to reason together, and something uncomfortable happens: they don't just share good ideas, they amplify bad ones.

A confident hallucination from one agent gets echoed by another. A subtle error cascades through the group. The system *agrees its way into being wrong*.

Existing multi-agent frameworks — chains, trees, graphs — all share the same blind spot. They assume every interaction is cooperative.

Every edge is positive. Every agent is a friend.

SIGMA, from Longgang He, Longzhu He, Daojing He, and Chaozhuo Li, says: not so fast. Some agents trust each other. Some conflict. Some are just noise.

If you can't tell the difference, your multi-agent system is a vulnerability, not a strength.

## The Signed Graph Insight

The core move is deceptively simple: give every edge a sign.

In a standard multi-agent graph, the adjacency matrix $A$ is non-negative — $A_{ij} \geq 0$ for all pairs. SIGMA replaces this with a *signed* adjacency matrix where each entry encodes both polarity and strength:

$$A_{ij} = s_{ij} \cdot w_{ij}, \quad s_{ij} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}, \; w_{ij} \geq 0$$

Here $s_{ij}$ is the sign — $+1$ for trust, $-1$ for conflict, $0$ for neutral — and $w_{ij}$ is the confidence magnitude. Neutral pairs get dropped from propagation; only meaningful relationships survive.

The matrix decomposes cleanly: $A = A^+ - A^-$, splitting cooperative and adversarial signals into separate channels.

This isn't just notation. It draws on *balance theory* from signed graph literature, where a triad of nodes is "balanced" if the product of its edge signs is positive.

SIGMA exploits this: balanced paths reinforce trustworthy signals; unbalanced paths flip polarity to flag contradictions.

The graph *knows who to believe*.

## How SIGMA Works

The framework runs in four stages, each building on the last.

### Stage I: Pick the right agents

Not every agent should join every conversation. SIGMA scores candidates on three axes — semantic relevance to the query, structural diversity (penalizing redundancy), and response confidence (favoring self-consistent outputs).

A composite score selects the top-$k$ agents. No sense inviting agents who will waste the round.

### Stage II: Build the signed graph

Each pair of selected agents gets a pairwise evaluation $f_\text{eval}(h_i^{(0)}, h_j^{(0)})$ comparing their initial reasoning outputs. The sign becomes the edge polarity; the magnitude becomes the edge weight.

Trust edges connect agents who agree. Conflict edges connect agents who disagree. Neutral edges — pairs with low confidence — are dropped entirely.

Then comes the recursive step that makes the graph powerful. Balanced and unbalanced neighborhoods are computed across multiple hops:

$$B_i^{(\ell+1)} = \bigcup_{v_k \in B_i^{(\ell)}} N_k^+ \cup \bigcup_{v_k \in U_i^{(\ell)}} N_k^-, \quad U_i^{(\ell+1)} = \bigcup_{v_k \in U_i^{(\ell)}} N_k^+ \cup \bigcup_{v_k \in B_i^{(\ell)}} N_k^-$$

The intuition: if my friend trusts someone, I should probably trust them too. If my enemy trusts someone, I should be suspicious. Polarity flips propagate through the graph like signs in an equation — multiply two negatives and you get a positive.

### Stage III: Pass messages through conflict

Each agent maintains two representation vectors — one positive, one negative — capturing its state under supportive and contradictory contexts respectively.

The update rule:

- Positive representations aggregate from balanced neighborhoods (trust) and polarity-flipped signals from unbalanced neighborhoods (conflict turned corrective).
- Negative representations perform the symmetric, polarity-reversed operation.

This is where SIGMA departs from prior work.

Mixture-of-Agents (Wang et al., 2025) stacks layers of cooperative aggregation — no conflict modeling at all. GPTSwarm (Zhuge et al., 2024) optimizes communication topology but treats every link as cooperative.

G-Designer (Zhang et al., 2025) learns which agents should talk to which, but still assumes all interactions are non-negative. None of them can say "this agent's answer contradicts that one — and that's useful information."

In SIGMA, conflicting signals don't disappear. They get *flipped* and *fed back* as corrective evidence.

### Stage IV: Read out the consensus

After $T$ iterations of signed message passing, each agent's final representation is a concatenation of its positive and negative states.

The output weight for agent $i$ is computed from its net supportive strength in the signed graph:

$$w_i = \frac{\sum_j A_{ij}}{\sum_p |\sum_q A_{pq}|}$$

Agents consistently endorsed by others get large positive weights. Agents surrounded by conflict edges get down-weighted.

The final answer aggregates across all agents, but the signed graph ensures the trustworthy voices dominate.

## What the Numbers Show

SIGMA sweeps all six benchmarks — MMLU, MMLU-Pro, GPQA, GSM8K, MultiArith, and HumanEval — with an average accuracy of 89.17%. The next-best baseline, G-Designer, sits at 88.53%.

![Benchmark comparison across six datasets showing SIGMA outperforming MoA, GPTSwarm, G-Designer, and GoA]({{ "/assets/figures/010122f52a7c/fig_004_03.png" | relative_url }})

On the hardest benchmark, GPQA, SIGMA reaches 54.51% versus GoA's 53.16% and G-Designer's 52.72%. These are small absolute gaps on a brutally hard test — but they're consistent across every dataset.

The robustness story is more dramatic.

Inject 50% adversarial agents — conflict-inducing, random noise, low-quality reasoning, or copycat — and SIGMA's accuracy on MMLU drops by less than 4.1 points. On HumanEval under the worst attack (conflict-inducing agents), it still hits 91.00%.

Naive baselines collapse under the same conditions.

![Accuracy under increasing adversarial agent ratios across four attack types, showing SIGMA's graceful degradation]({{ "/assets/figures/010122f52a7c/fig_008_04.png" | relative_url }})

Ablation tells you which module matters most: removing conflict-aware message passing causes the single largest accuracy drop. The signed graph isn't decoration — it's the load-bearing wall.

## Where It Breaks

Two things SIGMA doesn't solve.

First, the pairwise edge evaluator relies on an external embedding model (ALL-MINILM-L6-V2) and LLM-as-evaluator heuristics. In highly specialized domains where "agreement" and "disagreement" require expert judgment, cosine similarity on embeddings may not capture subtle but critical conflicts.

Second, the experiments scale to roughly 8 agents. Pairwise evaluation is quadratic, and multi-hop propagation adds cost per layer. Whether this holds for 30 or 50 agents — the scale at which real agent teams might operate — is an open question.

## The Takeaway

The assumption that more agents means better answers has always been fragile. SIGMA replaces it with something sturdier: more agents means more *signals*, and if you can separate the trustworthy ones from the trash, you get better answers.

The signed graph gives you that separation.

The framework is topology-agnostic — chain, tree, or mesh, the polarity-propagation logic applies. As agent teams grow and disagreements multiply, knowing who to trust is no longer optional.

It's the difference between collective intelligence and collective error.

The enemy of good multi-agent reasoning isn't bad agents. It's treating all agents as if they're equally good.
