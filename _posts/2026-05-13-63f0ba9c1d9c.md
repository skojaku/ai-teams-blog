*TL;DR: LLM-X is a message bus and typed negotiation protocol that lets independent personal LLM agents coordinate through policy-governed CFP→Offer→Accept/Reject rounds — and it holds up for 12 hours straight with sub-10ms latency.*

Your LLM agent books your calendar. Mine negotiates a meeting time. Right now, those two agents have no shared language, no rules of engagement, and no way to verify each other's consent. They operate in total isolation.

LLM-X changes that. It gives personal LLM agents a governed communication substrate — a negotiation bus with schema-validated messages, pluggable decision policies, and cryptographic authentication at the gateway.

## Why Today's Agents Can't Talk

Existing multi-agent frameworks like AutoGen and MetaGPT excel at orchestrating tool calls within a single user's workflow. But they assume one person is pulling the strings. When Alice's agent needs to negotiate with Bob's agent, there's no standard way to structure that conversation, enforce fairness, or verify consent.

Han et al. called this out in their 2024 survey: the field lacks concrete, reproducible infrastructure for studying LLM multi-agent dynamics at scale. AutoGen gives you conversational agents. MetaGPT gives you role-based collaboration. Neither gives you a policy-aware message bus where heterogeneous agents can negotiate under enforceable rules — and that's exactly the gap LLM-X fills.

## How the Negotiation Bus Works

LLM-X is not another agent framework. It is the plumbing underneath.

Agents connect through federated gateways that enforce three things at the entry point: JWT authentication, JSON Schema validation, and rate limiting. Once a message passes the gateway, it enters a typed negotiation flow over NATS pub/sub subjects, with Ack+retry for at-least-once delivery.

![Architecture diagram showing how personal LLM agents connect through federated gateways that enforce JWT auth and schema validation before messages enter the NATS-based negotiation bus]({{ "/assets/figures/63f0ba9c1d9c/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

The negotiation protocol borrows from the classical Contract Net framework. One agent — the initiator — publishes a Call for Proposals, or CFP.

Contractors respond with Offers. The initiator then sends Accept or Reject, and every message gets acknowledged:

```
CFP → Offer → Accept/Reject → Ack
```

Each message is a typed JSON envelope carrying metadata — sender, recipient, capabilities, scope, TTL, consent — and a payload that references an explicit JSON Schema. The bus validates structure before delivery, not after.

Beyond ContractNet, LLM-X also supports FIPA-style Alternating Offers — iterative counter-proposals between two agents under explicit deadlines, encoded in a dedicated `AlternatingOffer.json` schema. The system isn't locked to one coordination primitive.

## Three Policies, Three Trade-offs

The Policy Engine is where LLM-X gets interesting. Three acceptance policies govern when a negotiation round closes:

- **Low**: closes on the first counter or times out. Fast, but you might miss a better offer.
- **Medium**: accepts the first valid response and closes immediately. More rounds completed per unit time, but some offers never get heard.
- **High**: collects *all* offers before deciding. Slower, but fairer and more complete.

This is the central design knob. Everything else — latency, throughput, message volume — follows from which policy you choose.

## How Messages Scale

Message traffic scales predictably. For an agent population of $n$, each CFP generates $n$ offers (one per contractor):

$$\text{Offers per round} = n$$

Total round messages are approximately:

$$\text{Messages per round} \approx 1 \text{ (CFP)} + n \text{ (Offers)} + n \text{ (Accept/Reject)} + O(n) \text{ (Acks)}$$

The key insight: total traffic scales near-linearly with population size. The experiments confirm it. Going from 5 to 12 agents, message volume increases proportionally while latency degradation stays bounded.

## The Headline Result

The 12-hour stress test is the number that matters. Twelve agents, High policy, running continuously:

- 806 valid rounds, 9,672 offers, 100% round completeness
- Mean latency 6.18 ms (p50 = 5 ms, p95 = 11 ms)
- No significant latency drift across the full 12-hour horizon
- Per-agent latency stayed homogeneous: mean 4.58 ms, standard deviation ≈ 0.51 ms

That homogeneity matters. It means the gateway isn't starving any agent of service — fairness is built into the substrate.

![Latency over the full 12-hour stress test showing stable distribution around 5–11 ms with no upward drift across 806 rounds]({{ "/assets/figures/63f0ba9c1d9c/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

The policy trade-off appears clearly in the 2-hour runs with 12 agents. Medium policy completed 290 CFPs and 1,740 offers; High policy completed 258 CFPs and 1,548 offers. Medium gave more throughput. High gave lower and more stable latency — mean 6.00 ms vs. 8.35 ms.

![Bar chart comparing mean latency across Low, Medium, and High acceptance policies under sustained load, confirming High policy achieves tighter latency bounds than Medium despite its stricter synchronization]({{ "/assets/figures/63f0ba9c1d9c/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

Short 2-minute runs across 5, 9, and 12 agents showed that Low policy scales linearly in offer counts and preserves round completeness. But latency dispersion grows with concurrency — from 1–3 ms at 5 agents to 5–7 ms at 12.

## The Honest Limits

First caveat: the agents are synthetic Python wrappers, not live LLMs generating real negotiation payloads. The latencies reflect infrastructure overhead, not inference time. A single GPT-4 call adds orders of magnitude more than 6 ms.

Second caveat: the maximum scale tested is 12 agents. Whether the bounded-latency story holds at hundreds or thousands of concurrent agents remains an open question.

That said, the protocol itself is LLM-ready. The same schema-validated JSON envelopes that Python wrappers produce can be generated by any model through an API — OpenAI, Anthropic, HuggingFace — with no changes to the negotiation flow.

The bus doesn't care what's inside the payload, only that the schema validates.

## Why It Matters

LLM-X provides the first reproducible, policy-aware exchange for studying multi-LLM negotiation dynamics at scale. It establishes an empirical benchmark — 5 to 12 agents, 2-minute to 12-hour runs, three policy levels — that future multi-agent protocol work can build on.

If personal LLM agents are going to live up to their name, they need to talk to each other. Not through ad-hoc prompt hacks, but through typed, validated, consent-governed messages on a shared bus. LLM-X is that bus.

Twelve agents negotiated for half a day on a substrate that never missed a round.
