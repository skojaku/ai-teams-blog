*TL;DR: LLMs are superb coordinators but terrible diversifiers — when paid to disagree, they still agree 27% of the time, almost seven times more often than human pairs — and this asymmetry between converging and diverging is baked into how their similarity works, not just how much of it there is.*

Two players sit across from each other. No communication. One question: "Name a city."

If they pick the same city, they both win. Humans manage this 31% of the time. Two independent copies of GPT-4o? 74%. LLMs are uncanny coordinators.

Now flip the payoff. You win only if you pick *different* cities. Humans drop from 14% baseline agreement down to 4%. LLMs drop too — but only from 58% to 27%. They are told to disagree, they reason about how to disagree, and they still collide almost seven times more often than humans do.

This paper reveals that the same homogeneity that makes LLMs brilliant at finding each other makes them fragile at spreading out.

And the problem has two layers that need separate names.

## Two Flavors of Monoculture

Kleinberg and Raghavan (2021) coined *algorithmic monoculture* to describe what happens when organizations rely on the same algorithm: correlated outputs, amplified mistakes, degraded selection. Their insight focused on correlation that exists *before* any strategic incentive kicks in — what this paper calls **primary algorithmic monoculture**.

But there's a second layer. When you pay two agents to match, they can lean into their shared tendencies. When you penalize matching, they can try to deviate. This paper names that layer **strategic algorithmic monoculture**: the component of similarity that responds to incentives, on top of whatever baseline correlation already exists.

The distinction matters because the two layers have opposite implications for intervention. Primary monoculture is a statistical fact you can mitigate by deploying different models. Strategic monoculture is a behavioral response — it might persist even after you diversify.

## Three Arms, Clean Separation

The experiment, run by Gonzalo Ballestero, Hadi Hosseini, Samarth Khanna, and Ran I. Shorrer, adapts the classic picking/coordination design from Mehta et al. (1994) and adds a third arm:

- **Picking** — name a valid answer, no incentive to match or mismatch. This isolates primary monoculture.
- **Coordination** — you win only if you and another agent pick the same answer. This tests whether agents can amplify their baseline similarity on purpose.
- **Divergence** — you win only if you pick a *different* answer. This tests whether agents can suppress similarity when penalized for it.

Each of 16 LLMs (open- and closed-source) plays each question 50 times per arm. Three hundred and one humans play the same game on Prolific. Twelve question topics — letter, city, color, flower, and so on. The design cleanly separates primary from strategic: the coordination and divergence arms add incentives on top of the picking baseline.

## By the Numbers

The headline agreement rates tell the story:

|  | Picking | Coordination | Divergence |
|---|---|---|---|
| Humans | 14% | 31% | 4% |
| LLMs (self) | 58% | 72% | 27% |

LLMs start four times more similar than humans (58% vs 14%). When incentivized to match, they climb to 72%. When incentivized to diverge, they only fall to 27%.

Notice the asymmetry. Humans go from `14 → 4` — they suppress similarity by 10 percentage points. LLMs go from `58 → 27` — they suppress by 31 points in absolute terms, but the residual agreement is still staggeringly high. They *want* to spread out. They *try* to spread out. They mostly can't.

Cross-model agreement — a Gemini playing against a Llama — tells a similar story: 37% picking, 53% coordination, 9% divergence. Deploying different models helps, but it does not eliminate the problem. Two different LLMs still coordinate at nearly double the human rate.

## What LLMs Say They're Thinking

Here's where it gets interesting. The authors analyze the chain-of-thought text that LLMs produce. Using both semantic-similarity encoding and an LLM-as-judge classifier, they find that in the coordination arm, 68% of reasoning texts invoke strategic salience — secondary, higher-order, or Schelling logic — the kind of "I'll pick what I think the other will pick" reasoning that Schelling (1960) identified in humans decades ago.

In the divergence arm, the dominant reasoning categories are obscurity and higher-order obscurity — strategies like "I'll pick something the other agent would never think of." The models *understand* the strategic logic. They articulate it.

But their actions betray them. The shift in reasoning between arms is larger than the shift in behavior. LLMs think divergent thoughts and still converge on similar answers.

## Reasoning Helps — But Not Enough

Reasoning models — the ones that write out their chain of thought before answering — show lower picking-arm agreement than non-reasoning models, and they perform better at divergence ($p < 0.05$). Stepping through logic gives you more leverage to break routines.

But even the best reasoning models can't fully escape. DeepSeek-R1 drops its agreement rate from 65% (picking) to 11% (divergence) — impressive, but still nearly triple the human divergence-arm rate of 4%.

Temperature manipulation confirms a tradeoff. Raise the temperature and agreement drops across all arms — divergence improves, coordination worsens. Lower it and the reverse happens. Even at extreme temperatures, the qualitative ordering persists: LLMs beat humans at matching and lose to humans at separating.

## Primary Monoculture as Warning Sign

One of the paper's most practical findings: a model pair's baseline agreement (in the picking arm) predicts their agreement rate in both the coordination and divergence arms. Pairs that are more similar when there's no incentive are more similar when incentivized to match *and* when incentivized to diverge.

Primary monoculture isn't just a background condition — it's a leading indicator of strategic monoculture. If two models agree a lot when nobody's paying them to agree, they'll agree a lot even when you pay them not to.

## What the Study Can't Tell Us

The open-ended question format — "name a city," "name a color" — is a deliberately simple laboratory setting. Real-world coordination failures in hiring screens or pricing algorithms involve richer action spaces and higher stakes, where the forces identified here could be amplified *or* attenuated.

The LLMs weren't given monetary incentives; they were given textual instructions. It's unclear how outcome-based reward shaping or reinforcement learning would change the picture. The models *say* they understand the strategic logic, but they're optimizing the instruction, not a dollar-denominated payoff.

And there's a single model — Gemma3-27B — that appears nearly deterministic across all three arms, with agreement rates of 92%, 92%, and 93%. This is likely a temperature or sampling anomaly rather than a refutation, but it's a reminder that not every model behaves the same way.

## What This Means

In a world where AI agents are deployed in hiring, trading, content moderation, and pricing, the difference between *can coordinate* and *can diverge* is the difference between healthy markets and herding. This paper gives us the vocabulary — primary vs. strategic monoculture — and the first large-scale, preregistered evidence that LLMs are lopsided: the same force that makes them brilliant at finding each other makes them dangerous at staying apart.

You can diversify your model roster. That helps. But 53% cross-model coordination agreement vs 31% human coordination agreement means the problem doesn't go away — it just gets smaller.

The best coordinators make the worst diversifiers.
