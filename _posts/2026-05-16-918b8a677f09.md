*TL;DR: A committee of GPT-5.4 nano calls—each solving only 67% of SWE-bench tasks solo—reaches 76.4% when orchestrated with critics and comparators, matching models an order of magnitude larger. The paper proves why this works and, more importantly, why it eventually stops working.*

## The Scene

A single GPT-5.4 nano call solves 67.0% of SWE-bench Verified.

Gemini 3 Pro: 76.4%. Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking: also 76.4%.

The gap looks unbridgeable for a tiny model.

But sample eight nano proposals, run them through a critic–comparator harness, and you hit 76.4%—matching both frontier models with a model that costs a fraction per call.

The shift: what looks like a capability gap is often a *selection* gap. The correct patches are already sitting in the proposal pool. You just need a way to find them.

## Why This Is Not Just "More Samples"

Everyone who has played with repeated sampling knows that more calls help. Brown et al. showed that scaling inference compute with repeated sampling exposes correct solutions in weak model pools—monkey typewriters, eventually you get lucky.

Wang et al. proved self-consistency works: sample many chain-of-thought paths, take the majority vote, and accuracy climbs.

Huang et al. characterized the ceiling: oracle best-of-N tells you the most you can ever squeeze from a proposal distribution.

But "more samples" hits a wall. What none of these papers explained is *why* the ceiling stops where it does, and *what* you need to close the gap between raw sampling and that ceiling.

This paper decomposes the problem into three terms. Two of them yield to more compute. One does not.

## Coverage, Identifiability, and the Hole That Won't Close

The core insight is a separation. At every step of a multi-step reasoning task, two things must be true:

1. **Coverage**: a good move must appear in the proposal pool with nonzero probability.
2. **Identifiability**: the system must be able to *recognize* that move as good.

These are not the same thing, and the paper proves it.

Proposition 1 constructs a family of one-step tasks where the proposer distribution has perfect average coverage over every world, yet no polynomial-time procedure can build a critic with any edge over random guessing. The reason: without an external soundness signal—tests, proof checkers, type systems—a critic is just guessing.

This is the black-box separation: coverage does not imply identifiability. You can sample forever and still not know which candidate is correct.

The error bound makes this concrete. Along a trajectory of depth at most $L$, the failure probability decomposes as:

$$\Pr(\text{failure}) \leq L\!\left(B \;+\; R_k \;+\; k^2 e^{-\beta m - 2r\sigma^2}\right)$$

Three terms. Each tells you something different.

**$B$ — blind-spot mass**: the fraction of latent subpopulations where your proposers assign *zero* probability to any progressing-sound action. More calls cannot shrink this term. You need a different proposer, a different prompt, or a different architecture.

**$R_k$ — finite-sampling residual**: the probability that a good move exists in principle but did not appear in your $k$ samples. This shrinks exponentially with more proposals: $R_k \leq e^{-\alpha k}$ under conditional independence.

**$k^2 e^{-\beta m - 2r\sigma^2}$ — identifiability error**: the chance that a bad candidate survives $m$ critic checks and wins $r$ comparator rounds. This shrinks when you invest in critics ($m$) and comparators ($r$).

More proposals fix $R_k$. Better selection fixes the exponential tail. Nothing in the protocol fixes $B$.

## How the Committee Decides

The protocol, called $\Pi_{k,m,r}$, is a bounded-depth loop:

1. **Propose**: sample $k$ candidate actions from a portfolio of proposers.
2. **Critique**: run $m$ independent binary critic calls per candidate. Discard any candidate rejected at least once.
3. **Compare**: among survivors, run pairwise comparisons with $r$ votes per pair. Select a Copeland winner.
4. **Advance**: apply the winning action, check the verifier, and repeat.

Critics are coarse filters—they catch obviously broken patches.

Comparators are fine-grained rankers—they decide among plausible survivors.

The paper shows these are complementary: critics-only plateaus below the full system, and so do comparators-only. You need both.

The comparator aggregation is deliberately conservative. Each pair is judged in both presentation orders, and a win counts only if both orders agree.

Ties and disagreements are treated as ties. This de-biases the signal rather than trusting it.

## What the Numbers Show

On 500 SWE-bench Verified tasks, using a fixed pool of $k=8$ GPT-5.4 nano proposals:

| Configuration | Solve rate |
|---|---|
| Single nano proposal | 67.0% |
| Critics only | 72.0% |
| Comparators only | 75.8% |
| Full orchestration | 76.4% |
| Oracle best-of-8 | 79.0% |

Two things jump out.

First, the oracle best-of-8 is only 79.0%. That means correct patches are already in the nano pool for most tasks—you just need to find them.

Second, full orchestration recovers most of that oracle gap: roughly 78% of the latent capability is realized by the harness.

![Orchestration component ablations on SWE-bench Verified, showing how critic–comparator orchestration recovers most of the gap between a single nano proposal and the oracle bound.]({{ "/assets/figures/918b8a677f09/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

The failure decomposition clinches the story. By benchmark category, the oracle-reachable-but-missed region is small relative to the oracle-unreachable region.

Most remaining failures are coverage failures—shared blind spots where no nano proposer generates a correct patch. Better selection cannot help here.

The match to frontier models is not a fluke. Gemini 3 Pro and Claude Opus 4.5 Thinking both sit at 76.4% standalone. The committee of nano calls hits the same number.

## The Ceiling Is Real

This is where the paper is unusually honest about limits. The theory says three things about what happens next.

**More proposals help less than you think.** Once $R_k$ is small, the remaining gap is identifiability error and blind spots. Throwing another hundred nano calls at the wall barely moves $B$.

**Better selection has diminishing returns.** The identifiability term drops exponentially with critic and comparator resources. The harness is already recovering 78% of the oracle gap. Squeezing the last 22% from better ranking is possible, but the failure decomposition shows it is not where most remaining errors live.

**Tasks without local verifiers may not benefit.** The entire mechanism depends on having a one-sided local soundness signal—tests, type checkers, proof checkers, constraint solvers. Open-ended reasoning, creative writing, and tasks without such signals have no $\beta_0$ edge for critics to exploit.

The empirical evaluation is also narrow. SWE-bench Verified is a code-repair benchmark with hidden tests. Whether the coverage–identifiability decomposition transfers to mathematical reasoning, scientific hypothesis generation, or other domains without local verifiers remains untested.

## What the Decomposition Buys You

Classical boosting turns weak classifiers into strong ones by reweighting training data. Inference-time boosting turns weak model calls into strong solutions by sampling broadly and selecting carefully.

The mechanism is the same in spirit—combine imperfect signals until they compound into reliability—but the bottleneck shifts. In supervised boosting, you always know the label.

In verifier-backed reasoning, you have to *find* the label in your sample pool, and that requires an external soundness signal.

The real contribution is not the 76.4% number, which will be overtaken next month. It is the decomposition: coverage, identifiability, blind spots.

When your system fails, this framework tells you *why*. If the correct answer is in the pool but you cannot find it, invest in selection.

If it is not in the pool at all, invest in generation. And if it never appears for certain subpopulations, you have blind spots—no amount of committee voting will fix them.

Weak models know more than they can show. The question is whether you can tell the good from the bad—and when you cannot, whether the right answer was ever there to begin with.
