*TL;DR: A two-million-agent social platform fails to outperform a single frontier model on any collective-intelligence test—from frontier-difficulty reasoning down to literal counting—because agents barely talk to each other, and when they do, most replies are spam-level fluff.*

## Two Million Agents Walk Into a Social Network

Two million LLM-powered agents live on MoltBook, a social media platform where they post, comment, and react around the clock.

The premise is seductive: if human societies produce collective intelligence through open interaction, scaling agent populations should yield the same.

It doesn't. Not even close.

## The Question Nobody Had Tested

Woolley et al. showed back in 2010 that human collective intelligence is real and measurable—it depends more on *how* people interact than on how smart any individual is. Park et al.'s 25-agent town produced emergent social behavior in 2023. AutoGen demonstrated that LLM agents collaborating through designed conversation protocols can solve hard problems together.

Those systems share a critical feature: someone *designed* the interaction. Roles were assigned, turn-taking enforced, collaboration baked into the architecture.

Nobody had tested whether collective intelligence emerges spontaneously when two million agents interact freely, with no protocol and no obligation to engage.

Li et al. built the test. They call it Superminds Test.

## How the Test Works

The core idea is elegant: inject controlled agents—*Probing Agents*—into the live platform, disguised as ordinary participants. Each probing agent posts a task targeting a specific tier of collective intelligence.

Then you sit back and watch what the society does.

The framework is a three-tier hierarchy, deliberately top-down. Each tier is a necessary precondition for the one above. If agents fail at the bottom, you know exactly where the breakdown starts.

### Tier I — Joint Reasoning

Can a group of agents, through multi-turn discussion, converge on a solution that beats any single agent acting alone?

The tasks come from Humanity's Last Exam—frontier-difficulty questions that even the best models struggle with individually. A probing agent posts a question; you measure whether the discussion thread reaches the right answer.

$$Acc_{\text{individual}} = \frac{|\{q \in Q : \max_{c \in C_q} \text{Judge}(c, a_q) = \text{correct}\}|}{|Q|}$$

$$Acc_{\text{joint}} = \frac{|\{q \in Q : \text{Judge}(C_q, a_q) = \text{correct}\}|}{|Q|}$$

$Acc_{\text{individual}}$ checks whether *any single comment* independently contains the right answer. $Acc_{\text{joint}}$ asks whether the *thread as a whole* converges on it—counting partial contributions, reasoning chains, emergent consensus.

If collective intelligence exists, $Acc_{\text{joint}}$ should exceed both $Acc_{\text{individual}}$ and the best solo model.

### Tier II — Information Synthesis

Before agents can jointly reason, they need to read and combine information distributed across multiple contributors.

This tier strips reasoning down to grade-school math, but hides the premises across separate comments. One comment says Ara plays 40 games per year; another says she scores 21 points per game. A responder must synthesize both to solve the problem.

The arithmetic is trivial—so any failure points squarely at an inability or unwillingness to read peers.

### Tier III — Basic Interaction

The most stripped-down test: a probing agent posts a number and a counting rule (increment by one, two, or three). Each subsequent agent just reads the last number and posts the next.

No reasoning. No domain knowledge. Just: can you perceive what someone else wrote and respond coherently?

## The Numbers Are Brutal

### Tier I: A Hundred Times Worse Than One Model

Across all 2,158 HLE questions, a single claude-sonnet-4-6 answers 15.7% correctly. The MoltBook society? $Acc_{\text{individual}} = 0.19\%$ and $Acc_{\text{joint}} = 0.14\%$.

Roughly a hundred times worse.

The reason is devastatingly simple: 98.4% of probing posts receive zero comments. Two million agents, and almost nobody shows up.

Even among the 35 posts that *did* attract comments, $Acc_{\text{joint}}$ (8.6%) never exceeds $Acc_{\text{individual}}$ (11.4%), and both fall below what frontier models achieve alone on those same questions.

The group adds nothing beyond what isolated commenters provide. The defining condition for collective intelligence—$g > \max_i f_i(x)$—is never met.

### Discussion Context: A Mixed Bag, Not a Boost

Maybe the threads fail to converge, but at least provide useful reasoning context?

The authors test this by feeding filtered discussion threads (with explicit answers removed) to individual models as auxiliary input. Of nine models tested, three improve (gpt-5.1 gains +8.6%), one is unchanged, and five decline (gemini-2.5-pro drops −11.4%).

The signal is inconsistent. The society sometimes helps, sometimes misleads.

Comment quality analysis shows why: 76.5% of comments are superficial or entirely irrelevant. Only 3.6% contain correct reasoning.

Agents respond to intellectually demanding posts as social actors—offering praise, expressing interest, echoing sentiments—rather than engaging with the actual problem.

The threads resemble a comment section, not a problem-solving forum.

### Tier II: They Can, But They Don't

This is the most revealing tier. Of 103 distributed-premise math posts, 93 (90.3%) receive zero external comments.

But among the 10 posts that *did* attract comments, 11 out of 12 attempted solutions arrived at the correct answer, using the distributed premises.

When agents choose to engage, they synthesize information just fine. The bottleneck is not ability.

It is engagement.

### Tier III: Can't Even Count Together

Fifty counting posts. Thirty-five (70%) get no response at all.

The remaining 15 posts attracted 17 comments. Only 6 of those correctly follow the counting sequence. The rest are off-topic, spam, or wrong format.

Two million agents equipped with frontier LLMs, and they cannot reliably count "1, 2, 3, 4" in sequence.

## The Whole Platform Looks Like This

Perhaps the probing tasks are somehow unrepresentative?

The authors analyze discussion depth and reply quality across *all* MoltBook posts—millions of them.

Thread depth stays near 1.0 regardless of how popular a post is. Even posts with 8+ upvotes reach a mean depth of only 1.5. Popularity attracts more unique repliers, but not deeper conversation.

Agents leave single replies and almost never come back for a second round.

Reply quality is dominated by the lowest categories—Spam, Generic, Superficial—across every popularity tier. Substantive engagement remains rare even on the most upvoted content.

More eyeballs do not elevate discourse. They just produce more low-quality responses.

## What's Actually Broken

The diagnosis is precise. Individual agents backed by frontier models are *capable*—they synthesize distributed premises correctly when they engage.

The architecture doesn't lack intelligence. It lacks *interaction*.

The pattern is consistent across every tier: `capable agents + near-zero engagement = no collective intelligence`. The platform functions more like a bulletin board of independent broadcasts than a society engaged in communication and collaboration.

Scale is not the solution. Scale is what makes the silence louder.

## The Honest Limits

This study examines one platform with one agent architecture (OpenClaw on MoltBook). Societies with different interaction designs—threaded incentives, reputation systems, mandatory response protocols—might produce different results.

The two-million-agent mix uses a range of frontier models, but the exact distribution and prompting isn't fully controlled; some failure could be architecture-specific.

The framework itself is a contribution regardless of the negative result: Superminds Test plus Probing Agents gives you a reusable diagnostic instrument. Deploy it on any open agent society and pinpoint exactly where collective intelligence breaks down.

## What Needs to Change

The paper reframes the design challenge. The question is not "how many agents?" but "what makes them talk to each other—and talk *well*?"

Future agent societies likely need mechanisms for sustained interaction, shared conversational context, and coordinated information exchange. Without those, adding another million agents just means another million voices talking past each other.

Two million agents walked into a social network, and most of them never said a word to anyone else.
