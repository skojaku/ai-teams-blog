*TL;DR: Five major LLMs given identical API-discovery tasks agree on about half the APIs—but disagree sharply on which ones matter most, exposing a hidden reliability gap in multi-agent systems where apparent consensus masks dangerous ranking divergence.*

## The Half-Agreement Problem

Ask five leading LLMs to find and rank APIs for the same task.

They'll retrieve roughly the same pool of candidates—about 50% overlap on average. But look at which API each model puts at the top of its list, and the agreement collapses.

That gap—moderate retrieval overlap alongside significant ranking divergence—is the central finding of this paper by Eyhab Al-Masri, presented at AAAI's LAMAS workshop.

It means multi-agent systems that look like they're converging on a shared plan may actually be diverging on the single most important decision: what to do first.

## Why Agent Teams Should Care

Multi-agent LLM systems increasingly coordinate through shared tools and APIs. Toolformer showed that models can teach themselves to invoke external tools. API-Bank benchmarked how well a single LLM discovers and uses them.

But nobody had asked the cross-model question: when different LLMs reason over the same task, do they agree on what matters?

This paper provides the first systematic answer—and it's unsettling. The kind of latent disagreement the authors uncover is invisible to single-model evaluation. It only surfaces when agents actually try to coordinate, making it a pre-deployment safety risk hiding in plain sight.

## Fifteen Domains, Five Models

The authors built an AutoGen-based pipeline that sends identical API-discovery prompts to five model families—ChatGPT-5, Claude 4.5 Sonnet, Gemini 2.5 Flash, DeepSeek, and Mistral Large 2—across 15 canonical domains (weather, sentiment analysis, geocoding, AI face generation, and more).

Each model returns a ranked list of 10 APIs per task. That yields 750 ranked entries and 14,250 atomic data points.

Then they hit those lists with six complementary metrics, each capturing a different slice of agreement:

- **Average Overlap (AO)**: How many APIs do two models share, averaged across rank depths?

$$\text{AO}(A, B) = \frac{1}{k} \sum_{d=1}^{k} \frac{|A_{1:d} \cap B_{1:d}|}{d}$$

- **Jaccard similarity**: What fraction of the total retrieved set overlaps, ignoring order?

$$J(A, B) = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|}$$

- **Rank-Biased Overlap (RBO)**: Like AO, but with geometric decay that weights top ranks more heavily—the positions that drive execution in real systems.

- **Kendall's τ**: Do two models rank shared APIs in the same order? Concordant pairs minus discordant pairs, normalized.

$$\tau = \frac{C - D}{0.5 \cdot n(n-1)}$$

- **Kendall's W**: Extends τ to all five models simultaneously—group-level concordance.

- **Cronbach's α**: Internal consistency of the group treated as raters.

### Do the Differences Hold Up?

The metrics alone tell you how much models agree. The authors also ran statistical tests—ANOVA, Kruskal–Wallis, and Levene's—to confirm the disagreement is *structured*, not random noise.

All tests returned $p > 0.4$. No significant group-level differences anywhere.

That negative result is the point: the variation isn't stochastic. It's a reproducible, domain-dependent pattern.

## Where Agreement Collapses

Here's the headline number. Mean pairwise Average Overlap across all model pairs is about 0.50—models share roughly half their retrieved APIs.

Now look at Rank-Biased Overlap. It drops to 0.33.

$$\text{AO} \approx 0.50 \;\;\rightarrow\;\; \text{RBO} \approx 0.33$$

Same lists, same models, same task. But when you weight the top positions—the ones an agent would actually execute first—agreement falls by a third.

The models agree on *what's relevant*. They disagree on *what's most relevant*.

## Three Tiers of Trust

Not all model pairs are created equal. The metrics reveal a clear hierarchy.

**High-stability pairs** (Claude–DeepSeek, Claude–Mistral, DeepSeek–Mistral): AO ≥ 0.53, τ ≥ 0.60. These pairs converge on both retrieval and ranking. Over 60% of their pairwise orderings are concordant.

**Moderate-stability pairs** (Claude–Gemini, DeepSeek–Gemini, DeepSeek–ChatGPT, Gemini–ChatGPT, Gemini–Mistral): AO ≈ 0.47–0.56, τ ≈ 0.39–0.43. They overlap on discovery but diverge on ordering.

**Low-stability pairs** (Claude–ChatGPT, ChatGPT–Mistral): AO ≤ 0.46, τ < 0.35. ChatGPT–Mistral hits τ = 0.15—essentially random rank correlation, despite non-trivial overlap in retrieved APIs (AO = 0.40).

## Domain Drives Agreement

The strongest predictor of agreement isn't *which* models you pair. It's *what* you ask them to do.

Structured, data-bound domains—Weather, Geocoding, SMS, Speech-to-Text—produce cumulative reliability scores of 2.1–2.4 across all four metrics. A weather API takes a location and returns a forecast. There isn't much room for interpretive drift.

Semantically open domains—Sentiment Analysis, AI Face Generator—fall below 1.0 cumulative agreement.

$$\text{Weather / Geocoding: total} \approx 2.4 \;\;\;\text{vs.}\;\;\; \text{Sentiment / AI Face: total} \leq 1.0$$

Volatility scores confirm the pattern. Weather and Speech-to-Text show ranking variance of 3–4. Sentiment Analysis and Health spike above 7.

And the relationship is inverse: domains with variance above 7 yield Kemeny–Young consensus distances above 0.9. More dispersion directly weakens agreement.

Domain structure is the dominant factor. When a task has objective constraints and standardized parameters, models converge. When it requires subjective judgment or creative inference, they scatter.

## Why This Breaks Multi-Agent Safety

In a multi-agent system, coordinated behavior is often *assumed* when agents retrieve similar tools.

The paper shows that assumption is fragile.

Models can share a common action space while internally disagreeing on execution priority. That's a class of failure mode that single-agent evaluation can't detect—but it's exactly the kind that manifests in deployment, when agents cascade decisions based on their private rankings.

The authors frame their framework as a pre-deployment diagnostic. You can stress-test an agent ensemble before it goes live: measure pairwise τ, check RBO against AO, and flag domains where the gap is wide.

If your system routes tasks by majority vote but the models have near-random rank correlation on that domain, majority vote isn't consensus. It's coin flipping with extra steps.

## Honest Limits

Five model families, fifteen API-discovery tasks, one prompt per task, fixed temperature. The map is real, but it's drawn from a single vantage point.

The results may not generalize to coding tasks, longer reasoning chains, or models released after this study. The single-prompt design means we don't see how divergence shifts under paraphrasing or adversarial perturbation.

And the benchmark—publicly available at github.com/aeris-lab/llmrank—only measures API discovery and ranking. It doesn't test downstream execution, where disagreement might compound or, counterintuitively, cancel out.

The authors name all of these limits directly.

## The Gap That Hides

This paper's contribution isn't just the metrics. It's the revealed gap between retrieval agreement and ranking agreement—the space where multi-agent systems look like they're cooperating but are actually pulling in different directions.

Moderate overlap. Poor prioritization. Domain-dependent reliability.

The models agree on *what*. They disagree on *what first*.
