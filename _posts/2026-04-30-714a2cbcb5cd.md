*TL;DR: An 8B open-source agent model trained on just 16% of trajectory steps matches GPT-4.1 on a hard multi-step benchmark—because CSO learns to train only on the steps that actually matter and verifies them by outcome, not by noisy reward estimates.*

## Most Steps Don't Matter

An 8B-parameter agent model trained with CSO hits 49.5% on GAIA-Text-103. GPT-4.1 scores 45.6%.

The twist: CSO provides supervision at only 16% of trajectory steps.

The other 84%? Gone. The model barely notices.

This shouldn't shock us. Wang et al. showed that only a small fraction of high-entropy tokens drive RL gains in reasoning. CSO takes that insight — sparsity of meaningful learning signal — and moves it from token-level entropy to agent-level critical decision steps.

The result is a post-training pipeline that is leaner, sharper, and far less noisy than anything before it.

## The Credit Assignment Mess

Training LLM agents after supervised fine-tuning is a credit assignment nightmare.

You have two bad options.

Outcome-based methods like ETO label entire trajectories as good or bad. When a trajectory fails, every step gets penalized — including the ones that were perfectly fine. Coarse but reliable.

Step-level methods like Step-DPO score every intermediate action with a process reward model. Fine-grained, but noisy. The PRM guesses what's good without running the trajectory to completion.

The hybrid approach, IPR, tries both: Monte Carlo rollouts from every step to estimate per-step rewards. That's expensive. On complex multi-step tasks, the rollout budget explodes.

CSO sidesteps the whole mess. No dense reward at every step. No coarse reward smeared across the full trajectory.

Just sparse, outcome-verified signal at the steps that flip failure into success.

## How CSO Works

The pipeline has four moves: fail, screen, branch, verify.

**Fail.** Run the policy model on its training tasks. Keep only the failed trajectories. Starting from the policy's own failures keeps training data on-policy and targets the model's actual weaknesses.

**Screen.** For each step in a failed trajectory, sample $k = 5$ alternative actions from a stronger expert model (Claude 3.7 Sonnet). Score both the policy's original action and the expert alternatives with a PRM. A step becomes a *candidate critical step* if:

$$r^{\text{policy}}_t < \gamma_{\text{low}} \quad \text{and} \quad \max_j r^{\text{expert}}_{t,j} > \gamma_{\text{high}}$$

With $\gamma_{\text{low}} = 0.45$ and $\gamma_{\text{high}} = 0.65$, this screens for steps where the policy clearly erred and an expert alternative looks promising. The PRM is fast and coarse — it just narrows the search.

![CSO pipeline: failed trajectories enter, PRM identifies candidate critical steps, expert proposes alternatives, branch rollouts verify outcomes, and verified pairs feed DPO training.]({{ "/assets/figures/714a2cbcb5cd/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

**Branch.** At each candidate step, swap in the high-scoring expert alternative and continue the rollout *with the policy model itself* until the task ends. Every subsequent action $a'_\ell \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot | s'_\ell)$ for $\ell > t$ comes from the policy, not the expert.

**Verify.** Check whether the branched trajectory actually succeeds ($y' = 1$). Only verified successful branches become training data.

The PRM suggests candidates, but *outcome verification* confirms them. Noisy reward estimates are replaced by ground-truth task outcomes.

The verified pairs feed standard DPO:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{CSO}}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}_{(s_t, a^+_t, a^-_t)} \left[ \log \sigma \left( \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(a^+_t | s_t)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a^+_t | s_t)} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(a^-_t | s_t)}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(a^-_t | s_t)} \right) \right]$$

Where $a^+_t$ is the verified expert alternative and $a^-_t$ is the policy's original failed action, both conditioned on the same state $s_t$.

## Why Outcome Verification Is Everything

Drop verification, and CSO falls from 49.5% to 43.6% on GAIA-Text. That's a 6-point cliff.

The PRM alone cannot tell you whether an alternative action actually leads to task success — it can only guess.

Drop the PRM screening instead, and performance stays at 48.5%. Respectable. But you need nearly 3× more preference pairs to get there: 1,967 instead of 671.

The PRM's job is efficiency, not correctness. It prunes the candidate set so the expensive verification rollouts only run on promising steps.

![Ablation results: full CSO at 49.5%, without PRM selection at 48.5%, and without outcome verification dropping to 43.6%.]({{ "/assets/figures/714a2cbcb5cd/fig_009_03.png" | relative_url }})

Put together: PRM screening + outcome verification = both high quality and high efficiency.

## The Numbers

The results on GAIA-Text-103 tell a clear story.

| Method | GAIA-Text-103 | Δ over SFT |
|--------|--------------|------------|
| CK-Pro-8B (SFT) | 35.9% | — |
| + RFT | 34.9% | −1.0 |
| + ETO | 38.9% | +3.0 |
| + Step-DPO | 38.9% | +3.0 |
| + IPR | 44.6% | +8.7 |
| + CSO | 49.5% | +13.6 |

CSO outperforms every post-training baseline by nearly 5 points or more. On XBench-DeepSearch, the pattern holds: 29.0% versus the SFT baseline's 23.0%, a 26% relative gain.

![Bar chart comparing CSO's 49.5% on GAIA-Text-103 against baselines including ETO, RFT, Step-DPO, IPR, and GPT-4.1, with CSO matching or exceeding GPT-4.1.]({{ "/assets/figures/714a2cbcb5cd/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

The preference data composition matters too. Expert successes paired with policy failures hits 49.5%.

Expert successes paired with expert failures: 46.6%. Policy successes paired with policy failures: 42.7%. The best signal is a contrast between what an expert would do and what the policy actually did, at a step where that difference changes the outcome.

Iterative training is where CSO really pulls away. By Round 2 on GAIA-Text-L1, CSO reaches 61.5% and stays there.

ETO degrades below the SFT baseline by Round 3 — trajectory-level penalties poison correct behaviors. IPR plateaus at 56.4%. Focusing only on verified critical steps avoids the noise that accumulates in other methods.

The critical steps themselves are diverse: 26.1% tool invocation errors, 25.1% reasoning errors, 13.0% task understanding mistakes, 11.7% information extraction failures.

CSO isn't just catching one type of mistake.

## Honest Limits

Outcome verification requires running trajectories to completion. On complex tasks with long horizons, this is slow.

Early stopping heuristics or parallel execution could help, but CSO's current offline pipeline would need rethinking for online RL.

The PRM and expert action generator both rely on Claude 3.7 Sonnet. A closed-source model sits inside the loop, which means no joint optimization of the PRM and policy.

As open-source models catch up, this constraint loosens — but for now, it's real.

## The Step That Matters

Most of what an LLM agent does on a long horizon is forgettable. Tool invocation, routine retrieval, obvious next steps — these don't need training signal.

CSO's contribution is simple and sharp: find the steps that flip outcomes, verify them by running them, and train on exactly those.

16% of steps, 37% relative gain, an 8B model matching GPT-4.1.

The sparsity was already there. CSO just learned to see it.
