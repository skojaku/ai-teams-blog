*TL;DR: Every multi-agent LLM architecture loses about a third of its output quality when prompts carry semantic stress — yet all of them simultaneously produce a measurable, structured stress signal that a future adaptive system could learn from. CAFE is the first detector for that signal.*

## The Punch and the Counterpunch

Stress a multi-agent LLM system with contradictory claims, overloaded context, ambiguous references, or stale data, and it bleeds quality. Across five architectures — flat, hierarchical, debate, meta-adaptive, ensemble — average judged quality drops by roughly one third. The ensemble architecture, which does best on clean inputs, actually degrades the most.

That is the punch. Here is the counterpunch: every one of those same architectures expands its observed stress distribution relative to what was designed. The stress that wrecks output quality also opens up structured variation an adaptive mechanism could exploit.

We call this an *antifragility-compatible regime*, and the paper introduces CAFE — a statistical framework that measures whether one exists.

## Why Robustness Is Not Enough

Most multi-agent LLM evaluation asks one question: does performance survive perturbation? That is a robustness question.

Taleb and Douady formalized antifragility differently back in 2013 — not as "the system stays the same" but as "the system's response to dispersion is convex." A convex response means stress expands the distribution in a useful direction.

CAFE adapts this idea from operational stress to semantic and epistemic stress in language agents. The distinction matters. A system can be non-robust in immediate quality while still exposing learnable stress structure. Without a measurement layer that detects this, you risk optimizing against noise rather than against signal.

## Four Flavors of Stress and Five Ways to Handle Them

The authors define a stress vector with four semantic dimensions:

$$\psi = (\psi_{\text{conflict}},\ \psi_{\text{load}},\ \psi_{\text{ambiguity}},\ \psi_{\text{drift}})$$

Conflict injects contradictory banking-risk claims. Load piles on extra caveats and supervisory notes.

Ambiguity scrambles entity references. Drift makes the reporting windows temporally inconsistent.

Each dimension is sampled from a clipped Gaussian and scaled to $[0, 1]$.

Five architectures process these stressed prompts:

- **A0 Flat** — single-agent baseline, no coordination
- **A1 Hierarchical** — top-down delegation to credit, market, liquidity, and operational-risk specialists
- **A2 Debate** — bull, bear, and arbitrator
- **A3 Meta-Adaptive** — debate with a meta-controller that can request stricter synthesis
- **A4 Ensemble** — three independent analysts and a consensus builder

All five are asked to reason over the stressed input as given, handle contradictions explicitly, and produce concrete risk recommendations. The benchmark uses 50 clean prompts × 10 perturbed variants = 500 stressed prompts.

## How CAFE Works

The framework has three stages.

### Stage 1: Measure the Response

A multi-dimensional judge scores each output on four signals: coherence, grounded novel inference, contradiction resolution, and structural preservation.

These are kept as a vector, not collapsed into a single quality score, because multiple signals constrain the reconstruction better than one.

### Stage 2: Reconstruct the Observed Stress

For each architecture, we fit a multi-output polynomial response surface mapping centered stress intensities to judge signals:

$$\hat{S}_{a,k}(x) = \theta_{0,a,k} + \sum_{i=1}^{4}\theta_{i,a,k}\,x_i + \sum_{i=1}^{4}\alpha_{i,a,k}\,x_i^2 + \sum_{1 \le i < j \le 4}\gamma_{ij,a,k}\,x_i x_j + \delta_{12,a,k}\,x_1 x_2^2 + \delta_{34,a,k}\,x_3 x_4^2$$

Linear terms capture direct stress sensitivity. Quadratic terms capture marginal curvature.

Bilinear terms capture cross-stressor interference. Two selected cubic terms let conflict modulate load and ambiguity modulate drift.

Then comes the inverse problem: given an observed judge vector, what stress vector best explains it? The reconstructed stress vectors form each architecture's *observed effective stress distribution*.

![Three-stage CAFE pipeline: stress perturbation through response-surface fitting and inverse reconstruction to the distributional Jensen Gap]({{ "/assets/figures/abda3af36e7a/fig_017_04.png" | relative_url }})

### Stage 3: Compute the Jensen Gap

CAFE compares the expected stress distribution (what was designed) with the observed stress distribution (what the architecture actually experienced) using a distributional Jensen Gap under a convex potential $\phi(x) = \|x\|_2^2$:

$$G_a = \mathbb{E}_{X \sim P_X}[\phi(T_a(X))] - \phi(\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P_X}[T_a(X)]) - \left(\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P_X}[\phi(X)] - \phi(\mathbb{E}_{X \sim P_X}[X])\right)$$

A positive $G_a$ means the observed distribution expanded — the architecture exposes structured variation under stress. Near-zero means resilience. Negative means fragile compression.

This is not a generic distance between distributions. The directionality matters: expansion under a convex potential is exactly the signature that antifragility theory predicts for systems that can benefit from volatility.

## The Results Cut Both Ways

The findings are deliberately two-sided.

**All architectures lose quality under stress.** Relative drops range from 31.8% (Flat) to 35.4% (Ensemble). No one gets better at answering just because the prompt is harder.

**All architectures show positive Jensen Gaps with bootstrap 95% CIs strictly above zero.**

| Architecture | Jensen Gap | 95% CI |
|---|---|---|
| A4 Ensemble | 0.0975 | [0.0822, 0.1146] |
| A1 Hierarchical | 0.0867 | [0.0719, 0.1014] |
| A3 Meta-Adaptive | 0.0809 | [0.0667, 0.0956] |
| A0 Flat | 0.0787 | [0.0661, 0.0935] |
| A2 Debate | 0.0780 | [0.0647, 0.0927] |

![Jensen Gap by architecture with bootstrap 95% confidence intervals, all strictly above zero]({{ "/assets/figures/abda3af36e7a/fig_009_02.png" | relative_url }})

Quality degradation coexists with antifragility-compatible stress geometry. The ensemble has the largest gap *and* the largest quality drop — exposing the most stress variation does not mean producing the best outputs.

## How Each Architecture Bends Stress

Different coordination strategies expand different stress dimensions.

Debate amplifies conflict spread — consistent with adversarial disagreement surfacing contradictions. Meta-adaptive control amplifies drift spread — consistent with its coherence-and-drift monitoring role.

The ensemble amplifies load spread, plus broad expansion across conflict, ambiguity, and drift. Flat and hierarchical architectures each expand ambiguity most within their own stress profiles.

![Radar plot showing how each architecture expands different stress dimensions: debate amplifies conflict, meta-adaptive amplifies drift, ensemble amplifies load]({{ "/assets/figures/abda3af36e7a/fig_010_03.png" | relative_url }})

This is exactly the kind of structure an adaptive system needs to know about. If you want to route high-conflict cases somewhere useful, you need a debate architecture that amplifies that signal. If you want to detect temporal drift, meta-adaptive is your exposer.

## Where CAFE Falls Short

CAFE is a measurement layer, not a learning system. It detects *where* antifragile adaptation might work, but it does not close the loop.

The observed stress is reconstructed, not directly measured. The inverse problem is solved with moderate $R^2$ fits (roughly 0.45–0.49 mean across architectures). That makes CAFE a distributional diagnostic, not a per-sample oracle.

The evaluation is single-domain: banking risk. Banking combines contradictory, stale, overloaded, and ambiguous information by nature, which makes it a natural stress-test domain.

But the stress geometry could look different in medical, legal, or software settings.

The paper does not demonstrate that acting on the detected opportunity actually improves outcomes. It identifies the signal. Whether future systems can learn from it remains an open question — one the authors frame as a concrete research program: *detect → adapt → verify*.

Prior work like Reflexion showed that LLM agents can use verbal feedback to adapt across trials. But Reflexion measures success by final task performance. CAFE targets the prerequisite condition — whether stress produces structured variation worth adapting to in the first place.

## Why This Matters

This paper separates robustness from antifragility-compatible opportunity.

That separation opens a new evaluation axis for multi-agent architectures: not just "how well does it perform on clean inputs?" or "how much does it degrade under stress?" but "does its response to stress contain learnable structure?"

If you are building systems for banking-risk analysis, medical triage, or legal reasoning — domains where contradictory, stale, overloaded, and ambiguous inputs are the operating environment, not edge cases — you want to know whether your architecture is in a regime where adversity can become signal. CAFE gives you a way to check.

The stress that breaks your output quality might be the same stress that teaches your next system how to handle it.
