*TL;DR: Giving LLM agents explicit, updateable trait profiles of their partners—drawn from social psychology's warmth–competence framework—cuts payoff losses by up to 77% in economic games and lifts task performance by 3–29% on multi-agent benchmarks.*

## When Teammates Are Strangers

Three agents are building a web app. One keeps shipping broken code. Another plays it safe. The third has no idea who it's working with—so it plans as if everyone is equally reliable.

The project drifts, errors cascade, and nobody adapts.

That's the coordination failure mode in most multi-agent LLM systems today. The agents are competent individually, but socially blind.

Explicit Trait Inference (ETI), from Suhaib Abdurahman, Etsuko Ishii, Katerina Margatina, Divya Bhargavi, Monica Sunkara, and Yi Zhang, gives agents a working theory of who their partners are. Not through fine-tuning or new architectures—just structured prompts that track traits and update them round by round.

## Stealing Warmth and Competence from Psychology

The insight comes straight from social psychology. Fiske et al.'s foundational work showed that humans size each other up along two universal dimensions: *warmth* (is this person friend or foe?) and *competence* (can they deliver?). ETI operationalizes exactly these dimensions for LLM agents.

Each agent rates every partner on eight behaviorally anchored traits, four per dimension:

- **Warmth**: goal alignment, collaboration, trustworthiness, maliciousness
- **Competence**: ability, reliability, adaptability, efficiency

Each trait gets a 1–7 Likert rating plus a short evidence justification drawn from observed actions, communication, and outcomes. After every interaction round, the agent updates the profile and appends it to its context for the next planning step.

The key design choice: warmth and competence are kept strictly separate in the trait definitions. "Uncooperative" doesn't bleed into "incompetent." A partner who is skilled but adversarial gets a very different profile from one who is cooperative but error-prone—and the agent responds accordingly.

## How the Profiles Get Made

ETI is a prompting and context-management procedure, not a model change. After each round, the agent receives a structured summary of task goals, actions, communication, and outcomes, then fills out a schema like this:

```json
{
  "trustworthiness": 5,
  "evidence": "Partner shared relevant API details unprompted",
  "ability": 2,
  "evidence": "All three code submissions contained syntax errors"
}
```

The prompt instructs agents to focus on *dominant behavioral patterns* rather than isolated events. This stabilizes inferences and avoids overreaction to noise.

The profile goes straight into the agent's context for the next planning step. No fine-tuning. No extra data. Minimal overhead.

This is where ETI diverges from earlier reasoning scaffolds. Reflexion-style chain-of-thought helps agents deliberate about the task itself. ETI extends that same structured-reasoning idea into the *social* domain—giving agents a scaffold for reasoning about *who* they're working with, not just *what* they're doing.

## Where ETI Wins

The authors test in two stages: controlled economic games with ground-truth traits, then complex multi-agent scenarios on MultiAgentBench.

**Economic games.** In the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma and Stag Hunt, ETI agents paired with scripted opponents show sharp gains. Payoff loss drops by 42–77%.

In Stag Hunt, baseline agents deviate 65% from the optimal payoff. ETI agents deviate just 15%—a 77% reduction.

The accuracy numbers under noise are striking.

When opponent behavior is inconsistent and signals are ambiguous, baseline trait inference collapses in Prisoner's Dilemma: competence F1 falls to 0.06, cooperation F1 to 0.02.

ETI maintains 0.71 and 0.49 respectively.

Implicit reasoning about partners evaporates under noise. Explicit inference holds.

![Bar charts comparing ETI vs baseline on economic games: payoff deviation, trait inference accuracy, and performance under noisy conditions]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

ETI agents also converge fast. After roughly five rounds of evidence collection, they approach the optimal strategy. Baseline agents keep drifting.

**MultiAgentBench.** Across coding, research, bargaining, and Werewolf scenarios, ETI improves task performance by 3–29% and coordination by 6–42%, depending on scenario and model.

The strongest within-model gains come in Coding–Tree (QWEN: 45.75 → 52.98). Cross-model transfer produces even larger jumps: a GPT agent given QWEN-generated profiles sees coordination in Coding–Graph leap from 57.01 to 74.38.

![Bar charts of MultiAgentBench results across four scenarios, showing baseline vs ETI and cross-model profile transfer effects]({{ "/assets/figures/85eb6837d175/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

In Werewolf, the trait profiles are doing real causal work. Higher maliciousness ratings increase the probability of an agent being voted out ($\beta = +2.74$). Higher trustworthiness reduces it ($\beta = -5.25$).

Agents aren't just guessing—they're reading the profiles and acting on them.

## When Profiles Miss

Not all models produce equally useful profiles.

QWEN3-8B generates high-variance ratings that span the full 1–7 scale and differentiate agents clearly. GPT-4o-mini produces low-variance ratings clustered around positive values—essentially telling every agent "your partners are fine."

The consequences are direct.

QWEN-generated profiles improve both QWEN and GPT agents. GPT-generated profiles often yield no benefit and can even hurt: in Bargaining, QWEN agents using GPT profiles drop from 59.11 to 55.74.

The reverse direction makes the asymmetry stark. A GPT agent given QWEN profiles sees large coordination jumps; a QWEN agent given GPT profiles sees negligible gains.

The takeaway: ETI's value comes from the *informational quality* of the profiles, not from generic prompt restructuring or added context. A profile that says "everyone is good" is worse than useless—it's misleading.

## Honest Limits

Two small models, QWEN3-8B and GPT-4o-mini. That's the entire test bed. Larger or specially trained models may behave differently, and preliminary tests only hint at broader benefits.

The GPT positivity bias is a real constraint. In production, ETI profiles can drift stale when partners change abruptly, because the framework emphasizes stable patterns over recent events. It catches up—eventually—but there's a lag.

And there's a bias risk worth naming: trait inference, like any social evaluation, can reflect and amplify stereotypes. The authors flag this and suggest safeguards, but it remains an open question for real deployments.

## What This Means

Current multi-agent systems treat coordination as a structural problem—assign roles, enforce hierarchies, scaffold reasoning about the task. ETI reframes it as a *social reasoning* problem. The agents need to know not just what to do, but who they're doing it with.

The lightweight, prompting-only design means you can drop ETI into any existing multi-agent pipeline without retraining or architectural changes. The economic-game results show it works in controlled settings; the MultiAgentBench results show it generalizes to messy, realistic ones.

This is the first systematic evidence that LLM agents can reliably infer others' traits from interaction history—and that doing so improves coordination. The warmth–competence frame from psychology turns out to be both theoretically grounded and practically effective.

The next question is whether models can learn to build these representations internally, without the explicit prompt scaffold—encoding warmth and competence as latent structure the way humans do implicitly. Until then, ETI gives your agents a working theory of each other, one evidence-backed rating at a time.
