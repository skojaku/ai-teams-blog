*TL;DR: Eywa gives domain-specific foundation models—time-series predictors, tabular learners—an LLM reasoning shell so they can join multi-agent teams, beating language-only agents by ~7% on scientific tasks while cutting token usage by nearly 30%.*

## The Species That Couldn't Speak

On Pandora, the Mountain Banshee is a precision hunter—but it can't tell a Na'vi warrior what it sees.

The neural bond called *Tsaheylu* bridges that gap: a direct interface between two fundamentally different kinds of intelligence.

Li, Zou, Fang, and colleagues at UIUC borrow this idea for AI. Their framework, Eywa, gives domain-specific foundation models the same kind of bond—so they can participate in multi-agent reasoning.

## Why Language-Only Teams Stall

Scientific foundation models are the Banshees of machine learning—powerful but voiceless.

Chronos reads time series natively. TabPFN in-context-learns over tabular data in a single forward pass.

GraphCast has matched or surpassed operational numerical weather prediction.

None of them speaks English.

Current agentic systems assume every agent communicates in natural language. That works when the data is text. It breaks when the task involves time series, molecular structures, or tabular features—which is most of science.

You *could* serialize a time series into text and feed it to an LLM. People try. But the LLM is now reasoning over a lossy approximation of the original signal, and the token cost explodes.

Mixture-of-Agents stacks heterogeneous LLMs in layered aggregation. X-MAS combines multiple diverse language models. Yet on Eywa's benchmark, these LLM-only heterogeneous teams do not consistently beat strong homogeneous baselines.

More language models debating each other does not fix the modality gap.

Cross-modality heterogeneity—mixing models that think in *different representations*—matters more than heterogeneity among language models that all think in tokens.

## Tsaheylu: The Bond

The core contribution is the bidirectional FM–LLM interface the authors call *Tsaheylu*. Two components make it work.

**Query compiler ϕ** translates the LLM's task state into structured invocation parameters for the specialist FM.

**Response adapter ψ** converts the FM's raw output back into a language-compatible context the LLM can reason over.

The pipeline:

$$\text{task} \xrightarrow{\text{LLM}} s \xrightarrow{\phi_k} u_k \xrightarrow{F_k(\cdot)} o_k \xrightarrow{\psi_k} z_k \xrightarrow{\text{LLM}} \hat{y}$$

![Bidirectional FM–LLM interface showing the query compiler φ and response adapter ψ connecting a language agent to domain-specific foundation models via MCP]({{ "/assets/figures/02bb108c0bf2/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

The LLM decides when to invoke the FM and when to skip it. An EywaAgent subsumes a language-only agent as a special case—just always choose *skip*.

But when the task touches domain-specific data, the agent delegates to the FM, gets back a structured result, and continues reasoning.

They implement Tsaheylu via Model Context Protocol (MCP). Each FM runs as an independent server endpoint—the LLM calls it like a tool. No custom translation layers, no hardcoded pipelines.

## Scaling the Bond

The framework scales from a single agent up to dynamic orchestration.

![Three-tier progression from EywaAgent through EywaMAS to EywaOrchestra, showing how the FM–LLM bond composes into plug-and-play multi-agent topologies and conductor-based orchestration]({{ "/assets/figures/02bb108c0bf2/fig_002_02.png" | relative_url }})

**EywaAgent** pairs one LLM with one specialist FM. One brain, one Banshee.

On EywaBench, it lifts overall utility from 0.6154 to 0.6558—a 6.6% gain. Token usage drops from 4469 to 3137, roughly 30% fewer. Execution time falls from 25.2 s to 22.8 s.

**EywaMAS** drops EywaAgents into existing multi-agent topologies—debate, hierarchy, whatever you have.

It reaches the highest overall utility of 0.6761, beating Debate MAS (0.6460), MoA (0.6273), and X-MAS (0.6188). Gains are consistent across all three science domains.

**EywaOrchestra** adds a conductor LLM that dynamically selects which agents, which FMs, and which communication topology to use. No expert configuration needed.

It reaches 0.6746 overall utility—nearly matching expert-designed EywaMAS (0.6761)—but at substantially lower cost: 8335 average tokens vs. 11214, and 48.2 s vs. 72.1 s execution time.

![Utility–cost tradeoff showing EywaOrchestra achieving near-EywaMAS utility at substantially lower token consumption]({{ "/assets/figures/02bb108c0bf2/fig_044_07.png" | relative_url }})

There is a theoretical grounding too. Under the *domain advantage assumption*—any specialist FM achieves strictly lower loss on its home modality than a language-only agent—Theorem 3 proves that EywaAgent attains strictly lower optimal expected task loss:

$$\inf_{f \in \mathcal{F}_{\text{Eywa}}} \mathbb{E}[\ell(f(x), y^\star)] < \inf_{f \in \mathcal{F}_{\text{LLM}}} \mathbb{E}[\ell(f(x), y^\star)]$$

The invoke branch gives you strictly more than the skip branch alone can achieve.

## What Eywa Delivers

The headline numbers come from EywaBench, a new multi-task, multi-domain benchmark spanning physical, life, and social sciences with heterogeneous data modalities—language, time series, and tabular.

Two specialist FMs serve in the experiments: Chronos for time series and TabPFN for tabular data.

Three findings stand out.

First, *adding more LLMs does not fix the modality problem*. MoA and X-MAS, which combine multiple heterogeneous language models, fail to beat strong single-LLM or homogeneous MAS baselines on scientific tasks. The bottleneck is representation, not reasoning diversity.

Second, *delegation saves tokens*. EywaAgent consumes roughly 30% fewer tokens than the language-only baseline because the FM handles structured data natively instead of forcing the LLM to chew through serialized text.

Third, *adaptive orchestration nearly matches expert design*. EywaOrchestra, which picks its topology and FM assignments automatically, hits within 0.0015 of expert-configured EywaMAS—and sometimes surpasses it—while being cheaper to run.

## Honest Limits

The experiments only test two specialist FMs: Chronos and TabPFN. The framework's effectiveness with modalities like vision, molecular graphs, or geospatial data remains empirical conjecture—promising but unproven.

EywaOrchestra's conductor selects from a finite pool of pre-designed topologies rather than generating novel architectures from scratch. That caps how adaptive the system can be.

All experiments use GPT-family backbones (gpt-4.1-nano, gpt-5-nano, gpt-5-mini). Whether the gains hold with open models—Llama, Mistral, Qwen—is an open question.

EywaBench itself is a contribution, but it covers three domains with nine sub-domains. Scaling to more modalities and benchmarks is needed.
