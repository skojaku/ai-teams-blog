*TL;DR: Randomly picking training tasks to optimize how LLM agents talk to each other yields fragile communication graphs — active learning via ensemble Kalman inversion selects the right tasks, fixing the worst runs and boosting accuracy under adversarial attacks.*

## The Quiet Failure of Random Task Selection

You train six GPT-4.1-nano agents to collaborate on MMLU questions. You optimize their communication graph on a handful of randomly sampled tasks, following the standard recipe from Cut the Crap (Zhang et al., 2025).

Mean accuracy barely moves. But look at the worst quarter of runs.

Some of those runs are catastrophically bad.

The standard deviation across runs is 2.19 points on MMLU. All because you picked the wrong training tasks.

This isn't a niche corner case. Under a limited training budget, the choice of tasks *dictates* the quality of the learned communication structure.

Random sampling is standard practice, but it produces unstable results that vary significantly across runs. Yang, Dong, Negrut, and Wu (UW–Madison) ask: what if you chose those tasks on purpose?

## From Random to Intentional

The shift is simple. Stop drawing training tasks at random; start selecting the ones that will change the communication graph the most.

That's an active learning problem. You want tasks that maximize *information gain* — the KL divergence between your prior over graph parameters $p(\mathbf{z})$ and your posterior after seeing the task $p(\mathbf{z} \mid q, y)$. A task that barely nudges the distribution is uninformative.

A task that reshapes it? That's the one you want.

But there's a catch. The LLM multi-agent system is a triple-nested black box: the MAS rollout is non-differentiable, the structure optimizer sits on top of it, and task utility must be measured through the *change* the task induces in the optimized graph.

Gradient-based proxies blow up under all that noise. Monte Carlo methods are too expensive when each evaluation requires multiple agent rollouts burning real tokens.

## The EKI Trick

Yang et al. reach for ensemble Kalman inversion (EKI), a derivative-free method introduced by Kovachki and Stuart (2019) for inverse problems. Here's how it works in this setting.

You maintain an ensemble of $J$ particles $\{\mathbf{z}^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^J$, each a candidate set of graph logits. For a task $q$ with answer $y$, you forward-evaluate every particle through the MAS:

$$g^{(j)} = \mathcal{G}(q; \mathbf{z}^{(j)}, \omega), \quad l^{(j)} = \phi(g^{(j)}, q, y)$$

Then you update the ensemble in one step:

$$\mathbf{z}^{(j)}_{\text{post}} = \mathbf{z}^{(j)} + C_{zl}(C_{ll} + \Gamma)^{-1}(l^* - l^{(j)})$$

where $C_{zl}$ and $C_{ll}$ are empirical cross-covariances, $\Gamma$ is observation noise, and $l^*$ is the target score. No gradients through the MAS required.

The update moves each particle toward where the task says it should be.

Information gain is then the KL divergence between the prior ensemble and the posterior ensemble, approximated via Gaussian fits:

$$I(q) \approx D_{\text{KL}}\bigl(\{\mathbf{z}^{(j)}_{\text{post}}\} \;\|\; \{\mathbf{z}^{(j)}\}\bigr)$$

Rank tasks by this quantity, pick the top-$k$, and train on those.

One EKI step is all you need. The authors show a single update is sufficient to distinguish informative tasks from uninformative ones — the directional shift that matters is captured in one shot.

## Shrinking the Pool

Enumerating information gain for every task in a large pool is still too expensive. The method uses two stages to bring costs down.

**Representative selection.** Embed all tasks with a sentence transformer, then greedily pick the farthest points from the center. This shrinks, say, 1,000 candidates down to 50 without a single MAS rollout. Diversity is preserved; cost is near zero.

**Informative selection.** From those 50, estimate information gain via EKI. A surrogate model learns the mapping from task embeddings to information gain, then Thompson sampling in batches picks the final training set — avoiding the need to evaluate every candidate.

The ablation confirms: representative selection mainly prunes the pool; informative selection drives the accuracy and robustness improvements.

## What Active Learning Changes

The numbers tell a clear story across two settings — benign and adversarial (three of six agents compromised).

**Benign setting.** Active learning reduces standard deviation from 2.19 to 1.35 on MMLU and from 0.54 to 0.28 on GSM8K. The worst 25% of runs improve by +1.30 points on MMLU and +0.71 on GSM8K.

The main effect is *stability*: fewer bad runs, more predictable outcomes. Mean accuracy edges up modestly, comparable to random training's gain over no training at all.

![Worst-25% accuracy under benign conditions: active learning raises the floor on both MMLU and GSM8K and narrows the variance across runs]({{ "/assets/figures/2a153ab8809b/fig_009_04.png" | relative_url }})

**Adversarial setting.** Active learning shines when agents mislead on purpose. Mean accuracy jumps +1.45 on MMLU and +0.93 on GSM8K over random training.

The worst-25% gains are dramatic: +4.25 on MMLU and +1.24 on GSM8K. When agents are actively attacking the system, picking the right training tasks matters far more.

![Worst-25% accuracy under adversarial attacks: active learning dramatically raises the floor, especially on MMLU where the gap widens to +4.25 points]({{ "/assets/figures/2a153ab8809b/fig_029_07.png" | relative_url }})

Under a matched 8× token budget, active learning (which spends part of the budget on selection) outperforms spending the entire 8× on random training. More data doesn't help if it's the wrong data.

EKI is competitive with the strongest baseline — Fisher coreset with determinantal criterion — while avoiding explicit coreset construction over the entire pool.

## Where It Breaks Down

Small ensembles relative to parameter dimension could make EKI unreliable. The paper's sensitivity analysis supports the method at the budgets tested, but the guarantee doesn't extend to extreme regimes.

The current formulation assumes labeled task–answer pairs are already available. Extending to unlabeled tasks — where you'd need expected information gain over possible labels — adds computational cost that hasn't been worked out yet.

## Beyond This Paper

The principle is general: when you're optimizing a black-box system under a tight budget, *what* you train on matters as much as *how* you train. This is the first paper to show that active learning stabilizes LLM-MAS communication-structure optimization. The EKI-based estimator is derivative-free, so it generalizes to any setting where the forward map is noisy and non-differentiable — which is most real multi-agent deployments.

G-Designer (Zhang et al., 2024) also learns communication topologies, and like other methods in this space it presumably draws training tasks at random. The same instability would apply. This method plugs directly into that pipeline: keep your optimizer, swap random selection for informed selection, get better graphs.

Random training → active training. Same budget, fewer catastrophic runs.
