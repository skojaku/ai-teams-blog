*TL;DR: Randomly sampling training tasks to optimize how LLM agents talk to each other produces unstable results — replacing that random draw with active learning cuts worst-case failures and hardens the system against adversarial agents.*

## The Random-Sampling Trap

You have a six-agent LLM team.

You want to learn which agents should talk to which, so you pick a small set of training tasks at random and optimize the communication graph.

Run it with different random seeds.

On MMLU, the standard deviation across runs is 2.19 points — the worst quarter of runs lands at 77.26% accuracy while the mean sits at 79.69%.

Same budget, same method — wildly different outcomes.

That's the instability Yang, Dong, Negrut, and Wu diagnose at the top of this paper.

Scaling up the random training set doesn't fix it — more tasks give diminishing accuracy returns while token cost grows linearly.

The real issue isn't how many tasks you train on. It's *which* ones.

## Why the Graph Matters

Communication structure in LLM-MAS is a double lever.

Redundant edges burn tokens on messages that don't help.

Unrestricted edges let hallucinations and adversarial content propagate freely.

Cut the Crap (Zhang et al., 2025) showed that pruning the interaction graph can reduce token cost while holding accuracy.

AgentDropout (Wang et al., 2025) took a related path, learning which agents to silence entirely.

G-Designer (Zhang et al., 2024) used graph neural networks to architect communication topologies.

All three relied on random task subsets for training.

This paper shows that's the weak link.

## Active Learning Enters the Picture

The core move: treat task selection for graph optimization as an active learning problem.

Instead of random draws, choose training tasks that maximize information gain about the communication-structure parameters.

Formally, the utility of a task set $S$ is the KL divergence between the posterior and prior over the graph logits:

$$U(S) = I(S) = D_{\text{KL}}\big(p(\mathbf{z} \mid S) \,\|\, p(\mathbf{z})\big)$$

A task that shifts the parameter distribution more is more informative.

The question is how to estimate that shift.

Three nested black-box layers stand in the way: the MAS rollout is non-differentiable, the structure optimizer sits on top of it, and task utility requires seeing through both.

## The EKI Trick

The answer: ensemble Kalman inversion (EKI).

Maintain an ensemble of particles $\{\mathbf{z}^{(j)}\}_{j=1}^{J}$ representing uncertainty over the graph logits.

For each candidate task, run one forward evaluation per particle, collect performance scores, and update the ensemble in a single step:

$$\mathbf{z}^{(j)}_{\text{post}} = \mathbf{z}^{(j)} + C_{\mathbf{z}l}\big(C_{ll} + \Gamma\big)^{-1}\big(l^{*} - l^{(j)}\big)$$

where $C_{\mathbf{z}l}$ is the cross-covariance between parameters and scores, $C_{ll}$ is the score covariance, and $\Gamma$ captures forward stochasticity.

The information gain is then approximated by fitting Gaussians to the prior and posterior ensembles and computing their KL divergence.

One step is enough.

A single EKI update suffices to distinguish informative from uninformative tasks.

No gradients through the MAS, no repeated rollouts per candidate — derivative-free, black-box-friendly.

### Shrinking the Candidate Pool

For large task pools, enumerating every candidate is too expensive.

The framework uses a two-stage sieve.

**Stage 1 — representative selection.** Embed tasks with a sentence transformer, then greedily pick farthest-point samples.

No MAS rollouts needed — just embeddings.

**Stage 2 — informative selection.** From the smaller pool, estimate EKI-based information gain.

A surrogate model $\bar{\psi}(\cdot)$ maps task embeddings to information gain, so you don't need to run EKI on every candidate.

Batch Thompson sampling and a prediction-reuse strategy (caching forward evaluations across overlapping subsets) keep the selection cost manageable.

## What Active Learning Changes

Under a matched 8× token budget, active learning (1× training + 7× selection budget) yields higher downstream accuracy than 8× random training.

![Under a matched token budget, active learning achieves higher accuracy than scaling random training — more tasks don't help if they're the wrong tasks]({{ "/assets/figures/2a153ab8809b/fig_024_06.png" | relative_url }})

But the headline story is robustness, not averages.

In the benign setting, active learning tightens the accuracy distribution.

Standard deviation drops from 2.19 to 1.35 on MMLU.

The worst-25% accuracy improves by +1.30 points.

Random training barely moves the mean relative to no training on MMLU — its main benefit is the ~20% token-cost reduction from pruning redundant edges.

Under adversarial attacks — three of six agents intentionally feed wrong reasoning — the difference sharpens.

Active learning raises mean MMLU accuracy from 76.87% to 78.32% and lifts the worst-25% from 72.00% to 76.25%.

The optimized graph learns to isolate unreliable agents — but only when the training tasks are informative enough to expose their harmful influence.

![When three of six agents are adversarial, active learning lifts both mean and worst-25% accuracy well above random training]({{ "/assets/figures/2a153ab8809b/fig_009_05.png" | relative_url }})

EKI-based selection matches the strongest baseline — Fisher coreset with determinant criterion — without needing explicit coreset construction.

That matters for scalability as the candidate pool grows.

## The Honest Limits

Small EKI ensembles relative to the parameter dimension may produce unreliable posterior approximations.

The sensitivity studies support reliability within the budgets tested, but the boundary isn't characterized yet.

The current formulation assumes labeled task-answer pairs are available.

Extending to unlabeled tasks requires wrapping the posterior-update utility inside an outer expectation over possible labels — a fundamentally different expected-information-gain setup.

## Why This Matters

Task selection is not a bookkeeping detail for communication-structure optimization.

It is a first-class component.

When agents might be adversarial and tokens are scarce, the training tasks you pick determine whether your graph learns to defend itself or just learns to be cheap.

Active learning closes that gap.

The right tasks stabilize the graph. The random draw never will.
