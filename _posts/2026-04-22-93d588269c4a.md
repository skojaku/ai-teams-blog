*TL;DR: Adding more LLM agents to a brainstorming team shrinks, not broadens, the space of ideas they explore — and the culprit is how they're wired together, not the models themselves. Independence-preserving interaction designs like Nominal Group Technique can rescue the diversity that dense round-robin debate destroys.*

## Seven Agents, Fewer Ideas Than Three

Scale a multi-agent LLM team from 3 to 7 agents and the absolute number of distinct semantic modes creeps up — but per-agent efficiency more than halves. The Diversity Utilization Ratio, defined as Vendi Score divided by group size, drops from 1.03 to 0.47.

Each additional agent overlaps increasingly with the ones already there.

This is the opposite of what the "more agents, more ideas" intuition promises. Chen et al. call it *diversity collapse*: the interaction meant to broaden exploration instead locks the group into premature consensus.

## Dissecting the Collapse

The authors don't just document the collapse — they dissect it across three levels: model intelligence, agent cognition, and system dynamics. At every level, the same pattern recurs. Interaction that should diversify instead synchronizes.

Start at the model level. Stronger, more aligned LLMs produce higher-quality individual outputs but converge toward similar semantic content. Alignment acts as a global regularizer — it compresses the diversity axis while leaving quality mostly stable.

The consequence: scaling compute does not scale information gain.

Move to the cognition level. Give agents distinct personas — "The Skeptic," "The Interdisciplinarian" — and you'd expect perspectives to diverge. They don't, at least not when authority enters the room.

Junior-dominated horizontal teams achieve a Vendi Score of 8.08; interdisciplinary expert teams collapse to 4.65. The quality gap between them is just 0.6 on a 10-point scale (7.88 vs. 8.50). Those expert roles don't introduce variety — they attract the group toward safe, senior-approved generalities.

Then system dynamics. More agents, denser communication, longer debates — all accelerate convergence on the same narrow region. Wynn et al. identified the same pattern in multi-agent debate: interaction can breed sycophancy and disagreement collapse rather than productive friction.

## Why the Experts Shrink the Space

The density plots tell the story. In leader-led and naive teams, the distribution of semantic distances to the group centroid peaks sharply near zero — a signature the authors call *gravitational collapse*.

Junior agents align their output vectors with the senior authority rather than offering orthogonal critiques.

Horizontal and vertical structures flatten that peak. Vertical teams — mixing senior guidance with junior exploration — land in a "Goldilocks" zone: enough structure to avoid chaos, enough freedom to sustain divergence.

![Junior-driven horizontal teams achieve the highest diversity (Vendi 8.08) while interdisciplinary expert teams produce the highest quality but the lowest diversity (Vendi 4.65), revealing the authority-diversity trade-off.]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

UMAP projections confirm two distinct semantic regimes. Expert-driven structures cluster tightly in a *conservative cluster* of conventional research directions. Junior-driven structures drift to an *innovation frontier* on a separate manifold.

This isn't about the senior persona alone being reductive. Under a flat peer-to-peer topology, senior personas actually produce *higher* diversity than junior ones. It's the combination of expertise and hierarchy — authority-weighted coordination — that triggers the collapse.

## Measuring What Matters

The authors evaluate over 10,000 research proposals across 20 topics, running 50 independent sessions per setting at temperature 0.7. That yields 1,000 proposals per experimental condition.

Four metrics triangulate diversity from different angles. The Vendi Score — Friedman and Dieng's spectral-entropy measure of effective semantic modes — serves as the primary yardstick.

Structural Disorder ($1 - \phi$) diagnoses whether the group has collapsed toward a single centroid. Semantic Dispersion (pairwise cosine distance) measures the magnitude of spread. Lexical Uniqueness catches surface-level rephrasing.

All three embedding-based metrics exceed 80% agreement with human expert annotators on pairwise diversity judgments; the Vendi Score hits 87%. This is not a metric artifact.

## The Topology Intervention That Works

If the problem is structural coupling — agents synchronizing because of how they're connected — then the fix should also be structural. The authors test two alternatives to the standard round-robin debate baseline.

**Nominal Group Technique (NGT)** adds a "blind-writing" phase before any group discussion. Agents independently draft proposals without seeing what others have written. This eliminates production blocking and anchoring — two failure modes Diehl and Stroebe identified in human brainstorming groups decades ago.

NGT launches with the highest early-round semantic diversity.

**Subgroups** partition the communication graph into isolated clusters that debate internally before cross-pollinating. This creates "local pockets of divergence" that prevent the premature rush to agreement. Subgroups sustain the highest and most stable density of constructive conflict in the latter half of the debate.

Both outperform the standard dense topology. The ranking holds across persona variations and replicates on GPT-5.1.

![NGT achieves the highest initial diversity while the Subgroup topology sustains higher diversity through later rounds, both consistently outperforming the Standard round-robin baseline.]({{ "/assets/figures/93d588269c4a/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

### When Structure Backfires

There's a wrinkle. Structural interventions that help standard models like DeepSeek-V3 can *hurt* reasoning-heavy models like o1-mini.

For reasoning models, dense external coordination produces a synchronization effect that overrides the unique perspectives each agent's internal deliberation would have contributed. The same NGT scaffold that organizes a standard model's ideas can clip a reasoning model's deliberative wings.

This is the paper's most subtle finding: the right topology depends on the model's intelligence profile. One size does not fit all.

## Why Rigor Accelerates Collapse

Why does collapse happen fastest in tasks like AI research proposal generation, and slower in physics or pure creative writing? AI research sits at an "edge of chaos" — it demands both broad exploration and logical soundness.

Physics has hard ground-truth constraints that make low diversity appropriate. Creative writing has no constraints at all.

In that hybrid-constraint zone, agents face a tension between exploring novel but unverified paths and converging on superficially rigorous consensus. Under structural coupling, they treat agreement as a proxy for correctness. The very mechanisms designed to ensure quality — collaboration, peer critique — force the system to abandon unconventional ideas to satisfy collective pressure.

Hong and Page's classic result showed that diverse problem-solving groups outperform high-ability groups — but only when independence is preserved. This paper confirms the same principle survives in LLM agents that lack any explicit psychology. The coupling mechanism alone is sufficient.

## What This Changes

The headline reframing: diversity collapse is a *structural* problem, not a model problem. Throwing a bigger or smarter model at the task won't fix it. Neither will assigning fancier persona prompts.

What matters is the interaction graph — who talks to whom, and when.

If you're building a multi-agent ideation system, reach for independence-preserving structures first. NGT's blind-writing phase. Subgroup isolation. Sparse communication when the model already reasons deeply internally.

Reserve dense round-robin debate for tasks where convergence is the *goal*, not the enemy.

## The Honest Boundaries

The evaluation centers on scientific proposal generation. The dynamics may look different in math (strong ground truth) or unconstrained fiction (no ground truth at all).

Primary analyses use DeepSeek-V3 as backbone; cross-model replication on GPT-5.1 and o1-mini, plus heterogeneous ensembles (DeepSeek-V3, GPT-4o, Claude-Sonnet-4), confirms the structural findings generalize — but a broader architectural sweep remains future work.

Temperature is fixed at 0.7 for the main experiments, with robustness checks at 0.3 and 1.0.

Quality scores come from an LLM-as-Judge protocol, inheriting the standard biases of automatic evaluation. The human validation covers the diversity axis only.

More agents shouldn't mean fewer ideas — but without the right wiring, they do.
