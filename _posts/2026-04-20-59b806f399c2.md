*TL;DR: The authors introduce Instruction Inference, a 20-scenario benchmark that tests whether LLM agents can interpret incomplete and ambiguous instructions in a shared grid world—and find that GPT-4o with few-shot chain-of-thought prompting matches or exceeds 52 human participants on intent accuracy, making a strong case that LLMs can wield theory of mind in goal-directed collaboration.*

## "Can you get that?"

A teammate points at a key and says, "Can you get that?"

You don't just fetch the key. You infer which key, which door it opens, and which gem lies beyond—because you model what your teammate wants.

This kind of pragmatic reasoning is second nature for humans.

The question Saad, Murukannaiah, and Singh ask: can LLM-based agents do the same, and can we measure it?

## From Vignettes to a Grid World

Theory of Mind benchmarks have proliferated, but they almost all sit in one spot: a vignette, a question, a score.

Strachan et al. found GPT-4 outperforms humans on irony, hinting, and strange stories—but only in conversational vignettes detached from action.

Kosinski showed LLMs can attribute beliefs in structured cognitive tests—but again, no environment, no decisions, no consequences.

The gap is clear: *knowing* what someone believes is not the same as *acting* on it.

Saad, Murukannaiah, and Singh build on Zhi-Xuan et al.'s Doors, Keys, and Gems environment—a grid where a principal and an agent cooperate to collect a gem.

The principal gives an instruction; the agent must interpret it and act: fetch keys, unlock doors, hand things off.

The twist: the instruction might be clear, incomplete, or ambiguous.

They ground these categories in Grice's Cooperative Principle.

A clear instruction violates no maxim.

An incomplete one violates the Quantity maxim—it omits critical details, like asking for "a red key" when two red keys exist and only one unlocks the right path.

An ambiguous one violates the Manner maxim—it admits multiple interpretations, like "unlock that door" when the principal's gesture could point to more than one door.

![Three grid-world scenarios illustrating the three instruction types: clear, incomplete, and ambiguous, each requiring different levels of pragmatic inference]({{ "/assets/figures/59b806f399c2/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The resulting dataset has 20 scenarios: 8 clear, 6 incomplete, 6 ambiguous, across 16 grid configurations.

Each scenario demands that the agent read the principal's movements, parse the instruction, and infer an unspoken goal—precisely the kind of ToM reasoning that story-based benchmarks never test.

## Enter Tomcat

Tomcat is an LLM-based agent with three prompt components: a common-ground section (rules and objectives), a response-generation prompt, and demonstration exemplars.

The authors compare two variants:

- **Fs-CoT** (Few-shot Chain-of-Thought): seven input-output exemplars with structured reasoning, plus explicit rules like "apply theory of mind principles to infer the human's desired gem."
- **CP** (Commonsense Prompt): two problem walkthroughs with full grid-state descriptions, matching what the 52 human participants received.

Both variants run on GPT-4o, DeepSeek-R1, and Gemma-3-27B.

The human study recruited 52 STEM-background participants, each completing 10 scenarios under identical information conditions as the CP variant.

The evaluation is fine-grained.

Intent accuracy captures whether the agent identified the correct goal gem.

Action feasibility and optimality measure whether the agent's proposed moves work in the grid.

Plan feasibility and optimality extend the lens to the *entire* collaborative sequence—what the agent does and what the principal does next.

This distinction matters: a plan that includes the principal's subsequent steps reflects deeper modeling of the principal's mental state.

## The Numbers

The headline: GPT-4o with Fs-CoT achieves 85% intent accuracy, edging past human participants at 80.38%.

On plan optimality—the metric that matters most for real collaboration—GPT-4o Fs-CoT hits 75%, roughly matching humans at 71.15%.

DeepSeek-R1 Fs-CoT is close behind at 80% intent accuracy and 70% plan optimality.

![Bar chart comparing intent accuracy across all models and prompting strategies, with GPT-4o Fs-CoT at 85% versus humans at 80.38%]({{ "/assets/figures/59b806f399c2/fig_010_03.png" | relative_url }})

Then there is the prompting story.

Few-shot chain-of-thought *consistently* outperforms commonsense prompting across all three LLMs and all five metrics.

For GPT-4o, the gap is dramatic: 85% vs. 50% on intent accuracy—a 35-percentage-point gain significant at $p = 0.039$ (McNemar's exact test).

For DeepSeek-R1, Fs-CoT lifts plan optimality from 55% to 70%.

Even Gemma-3-27B, which struggles badly, goes from 5% to 30% on intent accuracy when you swap CP for Fs-CoT.

![Grouped bar chart showing plan feasibility and plan optimality, with GPT-4o Fs-CoT at 75% plan optimality matching humans at 71.15%]({{ "/assets/figures/59b806f399c2/fig_014_05.png" | relative_url }})

Gemma-3-27B is the clear laggard.

Its Fs-CoT variant manages only 30% intent accuracy and 30% plan optimality—well below human performance.

The gaps on action feasibility (75%) and action optimality (51.25%) suggest the model can navigate the grid but cannot reliably infer goals from ambiguous instructions.

## Why Prompting Matters So Much

The contrast between Fs-CoT and CP is not just a methodological detail—it is part of the finding.

The CP variant gives the model two detailed walkthroughs of solved scenarios, complete with grid states.

The human participants received the same walkthroughs (as animated GIFs) and performed well.

The Fs-CoT variant strips the grid states but adds seven exemplars of structured reasoning plus explicit ToM-prompting rules.

The result: Fs-CoT beats CP not because it shows more examples, but because it teaches the *form* of reasoning.

The seven exemplars demonstrate how to classify an instruction, infer a goal from movement, and lay out a plan.

The additional rules—like "infer the human's desired gem based on their movement, instruction, and grid configuration"—turn a general-purpose LLM into a goal-inference engine.

This mirrors the broader lesson from the LLM reasoning literature: you do not just need data, you need scaffolding that makes the reasoning visible.

## What This Changes

Existing ToM benchmarks are reading comprehension. Instruction Inference is *collaboration under uncertainty*.

The agent has to do something with its inference—fetch the right key, unlock the right door, plan for what happens next.

The results also put a stake in the ground: top-tier LLMs with structured prompting can reach human-level ToM performance in cooperative task settings.

That strengthens the case for LLM-driven teammates in mixed-initiative workflows.

If an agent can infer that "Can you get that?" means "fetch the red key so I can unlock the red door and then the yellow door to reach the gem at (7, 0)," we are somewhere genuinely new.

## The Honest Bounds

The grid world is deliberately simple—discrete actions, small maps, short horizons.

Real collaboration involves richer language, continuous sensorimotor dynamics, and much longer planning chains.

The human benchmark comes from 52 STEM-background participants in a controlled study; how well these numbers generalize to broader populations remains open.

And Gemma-3-27B's poor showing is a reminder that capability is nowhere near uniform across models—the ToM gap between frontier and mid-tier LLMs is stark.

The task also limits instruction types to incomplete (Quantity violations) and ambiguous (Manner violations).

It deliberately excludes invalid and irrelevant instructions—deception, sarcasm, and conversational breakdown—which are harder and arguably more realistic stress tests for collaborative agents.

Still, the move from "can the model answer a belief-attribution question?" to "can the model infer an unspoken goal and act on it?" is exactly the right step.

"Can you get that?" turns out to be a harder question than it sounds—and a more revealing one.
