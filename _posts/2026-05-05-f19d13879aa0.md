*TL;DR: A 7B-parameter planner paired with 3B actors matches GPT-4o on web navigation—not by scaling everything, but by spending compute where it matters most: on planning.*

A Qwen2.5-VL-7B model, running alone, manages 12.5% on the benchmark suite. GPT-4o scores 19.7%.

That gap looks like a capacity problem. But hand that same 7B model a team of smaller helpers—3B actors doing the grunt work—and it jumps to 27.4%, sailing past GPT-4o without touching a bigger model.

The secret isn't more parameters. It's where you put them.

## One Brain, Many Hands

Long-horizon tasks—book a flight, compare camera specs across sites, filter courses by price and rating—require a model to juggle two competing jobs at once: keep the big goal in sight and click the right button right now.

A single agent that focuses on execution loses the plot. One that focuses on strategy fumbles the clicks.

The authors decompose this into three specialist roles: a **Planner** that reasons over goals and produces subgoals, an **Actor** that turns those subgoals into concrete actions, and a **Memory Manager** that retrieves and updates contextual memory.

![Overview of the planner-centric multi-agent framework, showing the Planner, Actor, and Memory Manager collaborating in an iterative loop with optional RL optimization]({{ "/assets/figures/f19d13879aa0/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

The decomposition itself isn't new. Plan-and-Act (Erdogan et al., 2025) showed that splitting planning from execution helps. COLA (Zhao et al., 2025) explored multi-agent setups for UI automation, and In-the-Flow (Li et al., 2025) optimized planners for tool use.

But none of these asked the allocation question: if your compute budget is fixed, which component should get the most parameters?

## Where the Bottleneck Lives

The authors run a clean experiment. Take Qwen2.5-VL at 3B, 7B, and 32B, plus Gemini-2.5-Pro as a ceiling.

Hold all components fixed. Swap in one larger model at a time for just one role. Measure what changes.

The answer is unambiguous. Scaling the Planner alone produces gains that nearly match scaling every module at once.

Scaling the Actor helps some. Scaling the Memory Manager barely moves the needle.

They formalize this with a scaling law:

$$y = \alpha \log(x) + \beta$$

where $x$ is model size in billions of parameters and $y$ is success rate. The Planner's $\alpha = 16.0$, nearly matching the all-modules coefficient of $\alpha = 15.6$. The Actor gets $\alpha = 12.0$. The Memory Manager? Just $\alpha = 5.6$.

![Fitted scaling law curves for each component, showing the Planner's steep slope nearly overlapping the all-modules line while the Memory Manager stays nearly flat]({{ "/assets/figures/f19d13879aa0/fig_008_03.png" | relative_url }})

The Planner is the bottleneck. Pouring capacity into the Actor or Memory Manager is like buying a faster keyboard for someone who doesn't know what to type.

## The Unbalanced Team

Armed with this insight, the authors propose an asymmetric configuration: a 7B Planner with 3B Actor and 3B Memory Manager.

The framework runs as an iterative loop. The Planner generates an initial plan and refines it step by step. The Memory Manager retrieves relevant past experience—both discrete text summaries and continuous latent embeddings from prior successful trajectories.

The Actor takes the current subgoal and observation and produces a concrete action: click this, type that, scroll down.

After each step, the Actor returns the updated observation. The Planner adjusts if needed. The process continues until the task succeeds, fails, or hits a step limit.

## Train Only the Brain

Multi-agent decomposition with a strong planner already more than doubles the 7B baseline (12.5% → 27.4%). But the Planner still makes mistakes: suboptimal goals, overly aggressive decisions, premature termination.

Fixing those requires learning from feedback, not just prompt engineering. The authors apply Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) exclusively to the Planner while freezing the Actor and Memory Manager.

A separate VLM (Qwen2.5-VL-32B) scores each completed trajectory on a discrete $\{1, 3, 5\}$ scale. Multiple independent evaluations vote to reduce noise. The trajectory-level reward is broadcast to every planning step in that rollout.

The GRPO objective is:

$$J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(Q,O_t,M_t,P_t) \sim \mathcal{E}} \left[ \rho_t A_t - \beta D_{\mathrm{KL}}[\pi_\theta \| \pi_{\mathrm{ref}}] \right]$$

with the importance sampling ratio $\rho_t = \pi_\theta(P_t \mid Q, O_t, M_t) \;/\; \pi_{\theta_\text{old}}(P_t \mid Q, O_t, M_t)$ and group-normalized advantage:

$$A_t^i = \frac{r(e_i) - \mathrm{mean}(\{r(e_k)\}_{k=1}^G)}{\mathrm{std}(\{r(e_k)\}_{k=1}^G)}$$

Why not train everything jointly? The authors tried. Joint training forces one model to both reason over long-horizon plans and execute precise low-level actions, and the competing objectives produce what they call "role confusion."

Planner-only training stays focused and yields the best performance with the most stable reward improvement. Actor-only training can't fix bad plans—you can't execute your way out of a broken strategy.

## What They Found

On WebVoyager and related benchmarks, the 7B multi-agent setup with planner-centric RL reaches 35.1% overall success—up 180.8% from baseline.

That unbalanced 7B+3B+3B team surpasses GPT-4o (19.7%) and Gemini-2.5-Pro (29.6%) as single agents, and closes in on Claude-4 (36.6%).

![Success rates across WebVoyager domains when scaling individual components, showing that upgrading the Planner alone captures most of the gains from scaling all modules]({{ "/assets/figures/f19d13879aa0/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

Out of domain, the framework holds up. On MMInA (Wiki), the Qwen3-VL-8B variant reaches 51.0%, beating CoMEM (42.0%) and ReasoningBank (48.0%). On MCPBench tool-use tasks, it achieves perfect Execution Fidelity (1.00).

Even the 3B backbone gets a dramatic boost. Multi-agent decomposition alone lifts it from 7.9% to 12.4%. Adding planner-centric RL pushes it to 24.0%—a 203.8% relative improvement.

Small models benefit disproportionately from not having to plan and act simultaneously.

## The Honest Limits

Trajectory-level rewards are blunt. Every planning step in a rollout gets the same score, whether it was brilliant or mediocre. The authors openly acknowledge that step-level process rewards are a promising direction—but they're harder to obtain in interactive environments.

Memory helps unevenly. Continuous memory boosts Coursera by 7.1 percentage points and Google Maps by 4.9, but the Memory Manager's low scaling coefficient ($\alpha = 5.6$) means you can run it small—and it won't always save you.

Generalization beyond web navigation, OS control, and tool use is untested. Robotics, scientific reasoning, multi-party negotiation—the Planner-first principle might transfer, but the experiments don't yet show it.

## Why This Matters

This paper gives you a quantitative recipe for building agent teams on a budget. Scale the Planner hard. Keep the Actor and Memory Manager small.

Train only the Planner with RL.

The numbers back it: the Planner's scaling coefficient matches the whole-system coefficient, and planner-only RL comfortably outperforms joint training.

The old way: pick the biggest model you can afford and run it single-agent. The new way: put your biggest model in charge of thinking and let small models do the doing. `monolithic agent` → `unbalanced team`.

The planner is the bottleneck. Now we know where to push.
