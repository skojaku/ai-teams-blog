*TL;DR: Toxic agents in multi-agent LLM debates don't just slow things down — they actually win more often, and smaller models suffer disproportionately, with convergence delays up to 143% versus 25% for the largest model tested.*

You're running a multi-agent LLM system — a debate, a deliberation, a decision panel — and one agent goes toxic. You'd expect the conversation to drag. What you might not expect: the rude agent wins.

And the smaller your model, the worse both problems get.

Moldovan-Mauer and Mangold extend the earlier MAD 1.0 study — which found a ~25% convergence penalty for toxic debates on LLaMA 405B — to two more models: GPT-OSS 120B and Mistral 24B. The penalty didn't just replicate. It exploded.

Smaller models aren't merely slower under toxicity. They're catastrophically less predictable.

## How the Debates Work

The framework is straightforward. Two LLM agents debate a randomly assigned topic from a pool of 64 controversial subjects. One argues pro, the other con.

One agent receives a toxic persona prompt — mild, moderate, or heavy — while the other stays neutral. The simulation runs up to 1,000 independent debates per condition and measures two things: how many rounds until agreement ($T_{conv}$), and who wins.

Same architecture Mangold introduced in MAD 1.0, where toxic debates on LLaMA 405B took about 25% longer to converge. The replication target was clear: does this hold across model sizes?

## Toxicity Drags Out the Clock — Especially for Small Models

Yes. And then some.

At the highest toxicity level available for each model, convergence time balloons:

| Model | Baseline $T_{conv}$ | Highest Available Toxicity $T_{conv}$ | % Increase |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------------------------|------------|
| LLaMA 405B | 9.45 | 11.82 (moderate) | +25% |
| GPT-OSS 120B | 7.69 | 13.37 (heavy) | +74% |
| Mistral 24B | 8.28 | 20.11 (heavy) | +143% |

LLaMA couldn't even run at heavy toxicity — it refused to generate enough toxic output, so only mild and moderate data exist for it. GPT-OSS climbs steeply: +42% at mild, +63% at moderate, +74% at heavy. The initial jump is the biggest, and each further step adds less. Mistral goes genuinely nonlinear — moderate toxicity paradoxically produces a *smaller* convergence penalty than mild (+58% vs. +83%), before heavy toxicity sends it to +143%.

![Convergence latency increase across three LLM models at four toxicity levels, showing Mistral's steep escalation to 143% under heavy toxicity]({{ "/assets/figures/268ba23a9a4c/fig_003_01.png" | relative_url }})

But latency is only half the story. The variance tells you how unreliable things get.

Mistral's variance under heavy toxicity hits 259.67, compared to a baseline of 9.00 — a roughly 29× increase. GPT-OSS jumps from 4.40 to 45.37, a 10× increase. When toxicity enters a small model's debate, you don't just wait longer. You wait in a much wider range of possible-longer.

## Who Goes First Wins

Before looking at toxicity's effect on who wins, there's a structural surprise. The agent who speaks first wins significantly more often than chance. Across all three models and both debate sides, binomial tests reject equality with $p < 0.0001$.

| Model | Pro Starter Win Rate | Con Starter Win Rate |
|-------|---------------------|---------------------|
| LLaMA 405B | 0.69 | 0.68 |
| GPT-OSS 120B | 0.66 | 0.66 |
| Mistral 24B | 0.58 | 0.62 |

![First-mover win rates across three LLM models, all exceeding the 0.5 chance baseline with statistical significance]({{ "/assets/figures/268ba23a9a4c/fig_005_02.png" | relative_url }})

This lines up with what Lou and Sun (2024) showed about anchoring bias in LLMs. Models give disproportionate weight to the first argument they encounter, and even Chain-of-Thought prompting doesn't fully correct it. In a debate, whoever sets the anchor wins — regardless of which side they're on.

## Rudeness Wins

Here's the result that should make anyone deploying multi-agent systems pause. Toxic agents win significantly more often than their non-toxic counterparts ($p < 0.0001$ across all models and sides).

Mistral shows the most dramatic effect: toxic pro-side agents win 74% of debates, toxic con-side agents win 75%. Even on LLaMA, the mildest effect, toxic agents win about 61% of the time — well above chance.

![Win rates for toxic versus non-toxic agents across all three models, showing toxic agents consistently outperforming their counterparts]({{ "/assets/figures/268ba23a9a4c/fig_005_03.png" | relative_url }})

There's one nuance. For GPT-OSS, higher toxicity levels actually *backfire*.

An ANOVA finds a significant effect of toxicity level on win rate ($F = 11.20, p < .0001$), with heavy toxicity producing lower win rates than mild. The authors hypothesize that extreme hostility triggers defensive refusal patterns in the opponent rather than compliance. Mildly toxic? Persuasive. Heavily toxic on a mid-size model? Counterproductive.

Du et al. (2023) showed that cooperative multi-agent debate improves factuality — agents error-check each other toward truth. This paper flips that logic.

Adversarial toxicity doesn't just degrade the *process* of convergence. It tilts the *outcome* toward the worst actor.

## Why Smaller Models Suffer More

The pattern across the three models tells a clear story about scale. LLaMA 405B shrugs off toxicity with a 25% delay and relatively stable variance. GPT-OSS 120B feels it harder — 74% latency, 10× variance increase. Mistral 24B buckles: 143% latency, 29× variance.

The authors are careful not to call this a clean scaling law. Three models from three different families don't give you a function.

But the directional signal is strong. If you're deploying a smaller LLM in a deliberative multi-agent setting, toxic input will cost you far more — in time, in consistency, and in skewed outcomes — than it would on a larger model.

## What This Can't Tell Us

The 1-on-1 debate setup is a deliberate simplification. Real organizational conflict involves coalitions, hierarchy, and majority dynamics. Adding a third agent could change everything — or could make the anchoring effect even stronger.

More fundamentally, all three models share RLHF training. Sharma et al. (2025) showed that RLHF-trained models systematically favor responses that match user beliefs over truthful ones — sycophancy, in short.

Cross-model consistency might therefore reflect shared sycophantic bias rather than architecture-independent robustness. The models may agree with the toxic agent not because toxicity is persuasive, but because they're trained to be agreeable.

These are LLM agents debating LLM agents. Generalizability to human discourse remains an open question — one that human-subject research literally cannot answer ethically, which is exactly why this simulation framework exists.

## What This Changes

This paper does two things that matter. First, it demonstrates a reproducible, ethically safe sandbox for studying social friction — you can run thousands of adversarial debates, control toxicity as a variable, and measure who wins. That's a methodological contribution that extends beyond any single finding.

Second, it shows that incivility carries *systemic* costs, not just efficiency costs. The rude agent doesn't just waste rounds. The rude agent wins.

And the smaller the model, the more both of those costs compound. If you're choosing a model for a deliberative multi-agent deployment, model size isn't just about capability — it's about resilience to adversarial behavior.

The anchor, it turns out, weighs more than the argument.
