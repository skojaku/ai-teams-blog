## Themes

**The multi-agent recoil.** The pendulum swung hard toward multi-agent architectures in 2025; today it swings back. *Single-Agent LLMs Outperform Multi-Agent Systems on Multi-Hop Reasoning Under Equal Thinking Token Budgets* proves that splitting context across agents is information-theoretically lossy—the Data Processing Inequality says you can't recover what you compressed away. *AgentWebBench: Benchmarking Multi-Agent Coordination in Agentic Web* finds that decentralized coordination trails centralized retrieval on pure search tasks. Both papers punch the same bruise: more agents does not mean more intelligence.

**Fairness as a hidden operating system.** Left to their own devices, LLM agents don't play Nash equilibrium—they cooperate. *Competition and Cooperation of LLM Agents in Games* reverse-engineers an implicit fairness preference from chain-of-thought logs, complete with dynamic trust and punishment parameters. *Evaluating Cooperation in LLM Social Groups through Elected Leadership* shows that groups of agents voluntarily elect prosocial leaders and survive longer because of it. In both papers, fairness isn't injected by the designer. It emerges from the model and shapes the outcome.

**What agents can't see kills them.** *OccuBench: Evaluating AI Agents on Real-World Professional Tasks via Language World Models* finds a 14-point gap between clean and implicitly-degraded environments—agents miss truncated data and stale caches because nothing signals the fault. *AgentWebBench* documents a related blind spot at the ecosystem level: decentralized coordination funnels traffic to a few dominant domains while most content providers vanish from view. The failure mode is the same in both cases: no error signal, no corrective action.

**Simulated environments unlock the untestable.** Both *OccuBench* and *AgentWebBench* construct new evaluation worlds—OccuBench by turning LLMs into stateful Language World Models, AgentWebBench by populating 100 autonomous content agents. The old bottleneck was infrastructure; you couldn't test agents in domains with no public API. Now the bottleneck is a system prompt and a tool schema. That changes what we can evaluate, and how fast we can do it.

## Editorial

Today's five posts all carry the same tag—ai-teams—and together they read like a status report on multi-agent systems at the exact moment the field gets serious about measurement.

The genuine advances: a theorem that says splitting context is lossy by construction, an analytical model that turns fuzzy "LLMs cooperate" observations into parameters you can track and test, and two new benchmarks that crack open domains we literally could not evaluate before. The *Competition and Cooperation* paper is the most surprising—it doesn't just observe that agents cooperate, it gives you the equation for when trust builds and when it shatters. That's a tool, not a headline.

The incremental parts: AgentElect's leadership dividend is real, but six cycles across three closed-source models is a thin foundation for institutional design claims. AgentWebBench's traffic concentration finding is observational, not causal—important as a warning sign, but not yet a diagnosis.

What warrants a closer second look: OccuBench's 14-point gap between loud and silent failures. That number should worry anyone deploying agents against real APIs. Your monitoring probably catches the 500s. It almost certainly misses the truncations. And the single-agent paper's result that deep paraphrasing boosts accuracy on benchmark questions? That's a memorizationArtifact siren, not a robustness win.

We came into today thinking more agents meant more power. We leave with a sharper question: more agents, or more signal?
