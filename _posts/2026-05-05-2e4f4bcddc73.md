*TL;DR: When LLM agents work as coordinated teams, the thing you need to train isn't just each agent—it's the orchestration trace that decides who spawns, who delegates, and when to stop. This survey reframes multi-agent LLM training around that trace, reveals that credit assignment and reward design are underserved at precisely the levels that matter most, and identifies a gaping hole: nobody has an RL method for learning when to stop.*

## A Trace, Not a Trajectory

Kimi K2.6 runs 300 sub-agents for up to 4,000 coordinated steps.

Anthropic deployed sixteen parallel Claudes to jointly build a C compiler. OpenAI's Codex orchestrates parallel software-engineering agents from a command center.

These are not single agents with longer context windows. They are teams—and the thing that makes a team work or fail is not any one agent's policy but the *orchestration*: who gets spawned, who talks to whom, how partial results merge, and when the whole thing winds down.

This survey reframes the problem. Stop analyzing these systems as per-agent trajectories. Start analyzing them as *orchestration traces*: temporal interaction graphs whose vertices are events (spawn, message, tool call, return, aggregation) and whose structure is itself the output of a learned policy.

That shift exposes three coupled problems: reward design, credit assignment, and learning to orchestrate.

## Why Single-Agent RL Breaks in Teams

GRPO dominates single-agent LLM post-training. Normalize a group of $K$ rollouts from the same prompt, use the group mean as a baseline, and update.

It works beautifully when there is one trajectory. Apply it naively to a multi-agent trace and the signal dissolves.

Two structural pathologies explain why.

First, *credit diffusion*: under a shared terminal reward, uniform allocation across $n$ credit-bearing units makes each unit's signal shrink toward noise as the trace grows longer.

This is the same problem that motivated COMA's counterfactual baselines and QMIX's value decomposition in classical MARL. But it hits harder here—LLM traces are long, asynchronous, and populated by agents whose very existence is a policy decision.

Second, *non-identifiability of same-prefix counterfactuals*. When an orchestrator decides to spawn a sub-agent, the un-taken branch—what would have happened without spawning—never appears in on-policy data.

You cannot compute $\mathbb{E}[R \mid G_{\leq t}, \text{spawn}] - \mathbb{E}[R \mid G_{\leq t}, \text{no-spawn}]$ from realized traces alone. Classical Q-learning requires coverage of every action at comparable states; here the counterfactual trace structurally does not exist.

Dr. MAS already documented GRPO instability in multi-agent rollouts. This survey pinpoints the structural cause: uniform credit over ever-longer traces makes per-unit signals fragile, and orchestrator decisions are inherently counterfactual.

## Promoting the Trace to First-Class Object

The central formal contribution is the *dynamic Dec-POMDP* ($M^+$), which extends the classical Dec-POMDP with a time-indexed agent set $I_t$, spawn/despawn actions, and role-conditioned action spaces.

A rollout under $M^+$ produces an *orchestration trace*:

$$G = (V, E, \ell_V, \ell_E)$$

where $V = V_{\text{orch}} \cup V_{\text{spawn}} \cup V_{\text{msg}} \cup V_{\text{tool}} \cup V_{\text{ret}} \cup V_{\text{agg}}$—orchestrator decisions, sub-agent spawns, messages, tool calls, returns, aggregations—each vertex labelled by agent, role, and content.

![Orchestration trace as a temporal event graph showing spawn, message, tool call, and return events forming a structured dependency graph across coordinated agents]({{ "/assets/figures/2e4f4bcddc73/fig_006_01.png" | relative_url }})

A classical Dec-POMDP trajectory is the degenerate case: no spawn events, fixed $n$, linear structure. The interesting case grows and shrinks its vertex set during rollout.

Because the joint action dimension changes with spawn/despawn, the value function must be conditioned on the trace prefix rather than a fixed-shape state:

$$V^\pi(G_{\leq t}) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\left[\sum_{\tau \geq t} \gamma^{\tau - t} r_\tau \;\middle|\; G_{\leq t}\right]$$

This is a bookkeeping identity, not a convergence proof. But it makes precise why classical CTDE critics with fixed-size inputs cannot directly transfer: $G_{\leq t+1}$ may contain more vertices than $G_{\leq t}$.

The authors are explicit that $M^+$ is an organizing abstraction for taxonomy and auditability, not a new MARL solution concept. Concrete algorithmic forms—what sub-graph features suffice, how to amortize $V$ across variable-shape inputs—remain open problems.

## Three Axes of the Gap

The survey organizes the literature along three axes: reward design, credit assignment, and orchestration learning.

### Reward Design

Eight families. Individual rewards tag one agent's outcome.

Shared rewards give the whole team the same signal. Debate rewards score argument quality.

Role rewards target planner/executor/critic performance. Process rewards judge intermediate steps.

Verifier rewards come from an external checker. Orchestration rewards target system-level properties like parallelism speedup, split correctness, and aggregation quality. Hybrid rewards combine multiple families.

![Taxonomy of eight reward design families for LLM-based multi-agent systems, from per-agent individual rewards to system-level orchestration rewards]({{ "/assets/figures/2e4f4bcddc73/fig_017_02.png" | relative_url }})

The cross-tab tells the real story. Of 84 curated entries, only 7 explicitly target orchestration rewards—and 6 of those assign credit at the orchestrator level. Orchestration-level credit comes almost exclusively from orchestration-type rewards.

Most methods still default to agent-level shared or hybrid rewards. The orchestration layer, where the most consequential decisions live, is nearly unrewarded.

### Credit Assignment

Eight credit-bearing units, from coarse to fine: team → agent → role → turn → message → tool call → step → token.

Agent-level credit dominates with 23 entries. Role-level is growing with 10. Orchestrator-level is emerging at 8.

![Hierarchy of eight credit and signal assignment units, ranging from coarse team-level granularity down to fine-grained token-level attribution]({{ "/assets/figures/2e4f4bcddc73/fig_024_03.png" | relative_url }})

Message-level credit—the granularity at which inter-agent arguments and delegations actually happen—has exactly two entries in the entire pool. Only C3 estimates message-level counterfactual credit.

This is the survey's sharpest finding: the unit where agents influence each other is almost untouched.

### Orchestration Learning

Five sub-decisions: O1 (when to spawn), O2 (whom to delegate to), O3 (how to communicate), O4 (how to aggregate), O5 (when to stop).

As of May 4, 2026, the authors found no explicit RL training method for O5—stopping. This gap is alarming.

A team that cannot learn when to stop either wastes compute on redundant iterations or terminates before convergence.

## The Industrial–Academic Gap

This survey bridges three deployed systems to academic methods—a move most surveys skip.

Kimi K2.5 trains an orchestrator with PARL across up to 100 sub-agents and 1,500 coordinated steps. K2.6 scales to 300 sub-agents and 4,000 steps.

Kimi is the clearest public anchor for *trained* orchestration: they disclose the reward decomposition and training regime.

OpenAI's Codex and Anthropic's Claude Code are different. Both document deployment shape—parallel workflows, harness boundaries, dynamic spawn—but neither discloses whether multi-agent coordination is an RL training target or pure prompting.

The survey treats them as *deployment-envelope* evidence, not training evidence. The distinction matters: we know these systems orchestrate at scale; we do not know how they learned it.

### Rollout Cost Gates Everything

One engineering constraint threads through the paper: rollout cost dominates wall-clock training time, and the slowest sub-agent gates the whole trace.

An orchestrated team of 300 agents running 4,000 steps is not a minor variant on single-agent RL. It is a systems problem whose cost structure constrains which algorithms are even feasible.

This is why harness boundaries—where the frozen infrastructure ends and the learnable policy begins—are a training design decision, not a deployment detail.

## The Reward–Credit Dual

Dense rewards and strong credit decomposition are *duals*.

A process reward that fires at every step places the signal right where the decision happens; you need minimal credit decomposition because the reward already picked the layer. A sparse terminal reward shifts the entire burden onto credit assignment—you need COMA-style counterfactuals, Shapley attribution, or role-level critics to figure out who earned what.

Both can work.

The taxonomy reveals that current methods cluster at two extremes: hybrid rewards with agent-level credit (the mainstream), and orchestration-targeted rewards with orchestrator-level credit (the frontier). The middle—message-level credit with process rewards—remains nearly empty.

## What This Survey Doesn't Do

This is a taxonomy paper, not an experimental evaluation. No new algorithm, no new benchmark.

The claims about credit diffusion and non-identifiability rest on informal arguments and a structured literature audit, not on formal proofs or controlled experiments.

The industrial evidence is drawn from public blogs and technical reports, not from independent reproduction. The authors flag this explicitly: Kimi's numbers are a deployment envelope, not a verified training trace.

And the taxonomy itself reveals the gap it describes. Eight families of reward, eight levels of credit, five orchestration decisions—and the coverage matrix is sparse at exactly the points that matter most. Orchestration rewards, message-level credit, and the stopping decision are where the holes cluster.

## Why It Matters

LLM agents are moving from solo tool-users to coordinated teams whether we are ready or not.

Kimi is training orchestrators at 300-agent scale. Codex and Claude Code ship multi-agent orchestration in production. But the RL methods we have were built for single trajectories.

This survey gives the field its first shared vocabulary—orchestration trace, credit-bearing unit, orchestration sub-decision—to talk about what breaks when you scale up, and where the open problems live. The fifteen open problems it lists, from counterfactual orchestrator credit to MAS-native benchmarks to steerability under adversarial agents, are real structural gaps, not wishlist items.

The trace, not the trajectory, is the object we need to learn.
