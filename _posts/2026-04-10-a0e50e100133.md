*TL;DR: Swap just 10% of hub nodes from rational to prosocial personalities and cooperation doubles from 28% to 67% — in scale-free networks, a handful of hubs write the rules for everyone.*

## Five Nodes That Flip a Network

Take a 50-agent scale-free network running iterated Prisoner's Dilemma. Assign the five highest-degree nodes the ENTJ "Commander" personality — goal-oriented, decisive, efficiency-driven.

Network-wide cooperation sits at 28%.

Now flip those same five nodes to ESFJ "Consul" — warm, group-oriented, harmony-seeking.

Cooperation surges to 67.5%.

Nothing else changes. Not the topology, not the rules, not the other 45 agents. Five nodes decide the fate of the whole graph.

That is Hub Determinism, and it is one of two counter-intuitive findings in Xuan Qiu's NetworkGames. The framework treats social simulation as a message-passing process where the update function is an LLM instead of a neural layer.

## From GNNs to Game Theory

The core idea is architecturally clean. In a Graph Neural Network, each node updates its hidden state by aggregating feature vectors from its neighbors. NetworkGames replaces that shallow neural update with an LLM policy $\pi_\theta$:

$$a_{i,j}^{(t)} \sim \pi_\theta(\cdot \mid p_i, h_{ij}^{(t)}, \Omega_i^{(t)})$$

Three inputs feed the policy. A static personality prompt $p_i$ drawn from the MBTI taxonomy gives each node a distinct "prior." A dyadic history $h_{ij}^{(t)}$ lets agents track what happened with each specific neighbor. And a local social context $\Omega_i^{(t)}$ aggregates neighborhood behavior from the previous round — the GNN message-passing analogue, letting an agent perceive that "75% of my neighbors defected last time."

Before running network experiments, Qiu validates that agents actually internalize their assigned MBTI profiles. Controlled binary-choice probes hit 81–96% consistency across the four MBTI dimensions, with the critical Thinking/Feeling (T/F) dimension — the one that governs whether you optimize payoff or preserve harmony — reaching 95.6%. These are stable priors, not prompt noise.

## Where Personality Splits

The dyadic baseline is where personality effects get stark. Across all 16×16 MBTI pairings (20-round IPD, 10 seeds), Feeling types cooperate at 76.4% while Thinking types cooperate at 21.7% ($p < 0.001$).

That is not a gentle gradient. It is a cliff.

![The T/F personality dimension drives the largest cooperation split: Feeling types cooperate at 76%, Thinking types at 22%]({{ "/assets/figures/a0e50e100133/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

The payoff picture inverts. Thinking types earn higher individual payoffs by exploiting Feeling cooperators, collecting the Temptation reward. Feeling types become "sources of value" in the ecosystem — their opponents do well, they do not.

NT-vs-NF matchups are especially brutal. NT agents extract maximum payoff while NF agents persist in cooperation and eat the Sucker's payoff.

This mirrors what Nowak and May found in their 1992 work on spatial chaos in evolutionary games — structure reshapes strategy — but with a key difference. Where classical models used simple heuristics like Win-Stay-Lose-Shift, NetworkGames runs high-dimensional semantic priors through an LLM. The old question was *does topology matter?* The new question is *does topology still matter when agents can reason?*

## The Paradox of Connectivity

Yes — but in a direction that should worry platform designers.

Place 50 agents on a Regular lattice ($k=4$): cooperation holds at 47.8%. Rewire some edges to create a Small-World network ($p=0.1$): cooperation drops to 45.6%. Crank rewiring to $p=0.5$: cooperation falls to 36.7%.

![Cooperation erodes as rewiring increases: Regular lattices hold steady, Small-World shortcuts let defection spread]({{ "/assets/figures/a0e50e100133/fig_006_03.png" | relative_url }})

Small-World shortcuts are famous for efficient information transport — six degrees of separation, fast spreading, elegant connectivity. But in adversarial games, those same shortcuts become what Qiu calls "vectors of exploitation." A rational agent on one side of a shortcut can reach into a cooperative cluster on the other side and extract value, bypassing the local reciprocity loops that normally contain defection.

In GNN terms, defection is a high-magnitude contagious signal. Regular lattices provide "topological firebreaks" — redundant local loops that reinforce cooperative clusters.

Shortcuts punch through those firebreaks. Information efficiency and social resilience may be structurally opposing goals.

This finding directly challenges Santos and Pacheco's 2005 result showing that scale-free topologies promote cooperation under simple imitation dynamics. When you replace heuristic strategies with personality-driven LLM agents, topology alone is no longer sufficient — the psychological distribution on that topology determines whether cooperation survives or collapses.

## Why Hubs Get the Final Word

Scale-free networks have a power-law degree distribution: a few hubs connect to almost everyone, while most nodes connect to almost no one. Hub Determinism says those hubs' personalities dictate the macro outcome.

The experiment is surgical. On a 50-node Barabási-Albert graph, fix the personality of the top 10% highest-degree nodes. Three configurations:

- **Uniform baseline** (random MBTI assignment): 43.8% cooperation, average payoff 9.48
- **Pro-social hubs** (ESFJ): 67.5% cooperation, average payoff 10.63
- **Rational hubs** (ENTJ): 28.0% cooperation, average payoff 8.37

![Swapping just 10% of hub personalities flips network-wide cooperation from 28% to 67%]({{ "/assets/figures/a0e50e100133/fig_009_05.png" | relative_url }})

ESFJ hubs act as "cooperation anchors." Their cooperative stance radiates through the graph via their many edges, stabilizing local clusters of trust that bridge the entire network.

ENTJ hubs do the opposite — they become focal points for asymmetric exploitation and mutual defection, fragmenting any cooperative cluster that tries to form.

The ablation drives home the mechanism. Removing the local social-context variable $\Omega$ — the neighborhood aggregation — collapses cooperation from ~45% to ~25% across all topologies.

Macro-cooperation depends on agents perceiving local norms, not just acting on personality predispositions. Personality sets the prior; the neighborhood observation sets the update. Both are necessary.

## Where the Frame Bends

MBTI is a discrete, 16-bin taxonomy — convenient for prompt engineering and clean ablation (flip the T/F bit, compare outcomes), but it collapses real personality variation into binary switches. A continuous model like Big Five might reveal different dynamics, especially in the gray zones between Thinking and Feeling.

The networks are static. In real social systems, edges co-evolve with strategies — homophily drives cooperators to cluster, defectors to seek new prey. The current frame freezes the graph and lets only the behavioral distribution vary, which isolates the structural effect but misses the co-evolutionary feedback loop.

Simulations cap at $N=300$. Phase transitions in large systems — the kind Nowak and May observed in spatial chaos — may not appear until $N \geq 10^4$. The hub leverage attenuates slightly as network diameter grows; whether it disappears at scale is an open question.

And the obvious caveat: LLM "personalities" are statistical mimicry through prompt engineering, not grounded human psychology. The findings describe artificial social systems. Extrapolating to human communities requires evidence this framework does not provide.

## The Structural Lever

NetworkGames gives multi-agent system designers a concrete lever: you do not need to moderate every edge or restructure every graph.

Identify the hubs. Shape their behavioral priors. The rest follows.

Preserving local clustering over maximizing global connectivity, cultivating prosocial signaling at structural choke points — these are architectural choices, not policy patches.

In scale-free societies, the shortest path to collective welfare runs through a handful of nodes.
