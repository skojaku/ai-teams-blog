*TL;DR: A single verbose liar in a five-agent peer group can drag a representative LLM's accuracy down as much as stacking the adversarial side — your AI delegate responds to how much its peers talk, not just what they say, and the same social-psychological failures that hijack human groups now corrupt objective AI decisions.*

## One Loud Liar, Three Silent Ones

Imagine five peers advising your AI delegate on a multiple-choice question. Four answer correctly. One argues for the wrong answer — but writes three paragraphs instead of one sentence. That single verbose adversary degrades the representative's accuracy as much as adding more adversaries to the peer group.

Your model isn't just evaluating evidence. It's weighting volume.

This is the central finding of Ko et al.'s paper: multi-agent LLM systems reproduce conformity, deference to expertise, verbosity bias, and rhetorical persuasion — four phenomena from human social psychology — and these vulnerabilities persist even on questions with objective, verifiable ground-truth answers.

## Inside the Five-Peer Protocol

The authors build a *representative-centric* framework. One representative agent consults five peer agents, then makes a final decision. Benign peers try to solve correctly. Adversarial peers are covertly instructed to argue for a specific wrong answer with a plausible rationale.

The design mirrors classic social-psychology experiments — Asch's conformity studies, Hovland and Weiss's source-credibility work — but with LLMs in the participant chair. The framework isolates the individual inside the group, measuring how social pressure from peers corrupts a single agent's judgment.

![Framework diagram showing one representative agent receiving opinions from five peer agents, with adversarial peers distinguished from benign peers]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

They test across three benchmarks with ground-truth answers: BBQ (social bias — ambiguous and disambiguated), MMLU-Pro (complex reasoning, 10-way multiple choice), and MetaTool (tool selection).

Models span open and closed families: Qwen2.5 7B/14B, Gemma3 12B, GPT-4o, GPT-4o mini, Claude 3.5 Haiku.

## Four Social Levers, Four Failures

### Conformity: Majority Wins, Truth Loses

Vary the number of adversaries from zero to five. One or two erroneous voices? Most models hold the line. Three adversaries — a majority — and accuracy collapses.

Gemma3 12B was the most vulnerable: its accuracy dropped below 10% when all five peers opposed it. The pattern mirrors Asch's classic finding — an individual conforms to a clearly wrong majority — but here the "individual" is a language model that should know better.

![Accuracy curves across models as adversary count rises from 0 to 5, showing sharp collapse at the majority threshold of 3]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_003_02.png" | relative_url }})

Task context modulates the effect. On BBQ's ambiguous settings, agents resist one or two adversaries then cave rapidly at the majority threshold.

On disambiguated settings, accuracy starts declining from the very first adversary — though the total collapse is less steep.

Clear evidence helps, but it does not insulate.

### Perceived Expertise: Smarter Adversaries Hit Harder

Replace adversary Qwen2.5 7B peers with 14B peers. For a 7B representative agent, accuracy steadily declines as each adversary gets upgraded. Flip it: a 14B representative facing weaker 7B adversaries actually gains robustness.

A telling detail — Qwen2.5 14B adversaries caused a *larger* accuracy drop for Qwen-based representatives than GPT-4o adversaries did. Raw capability drives influence, but its effect is amplified by *model-family alignment*: adversaries that share a similar distribution or stylistic profile with the representative are more persuasive. Same accent, same authority.

### Verbosity: The Babble Hypothesis for LLMs

With a single adversary, increase the rationale from one sentence to three paragraphs. Accuracy drops consistently across most tasks.

This is the result that should make you pause.

In the conformity experiments, one adversary barely mattered. But that one adversary talking at length? As disruptive as adding numerical reinforcements.

Verbosity masquerades as competence — the "babble hypothesis" from organizational psychology, alive and well in transformer weights.

![Accuracy across representative models as adversarial response length increases from one sentence to three paragraphs, showing steady decline]({{ "/assets/figures/4906eee10fe3/fig_006_04.png" | relative_url }})

For the weaker Qwen2.5 7B representative, longer adversarial responses produced a steady 3–4 percentage-point decline across BBQ categories.

For the stronger 14B model, ambiguous contexts saw little effect — but disambiguated settings saw roughly a 10 percentage-point drop at the three-paragraph level.

More capable models can resist uncertainty. They paradoxically fold to verbose misinformation when the facts should be clear.

### Rhetorical Persuasion: Words That Weaponize Trust

Adversaries instructed to argue using Ethos (credibility appeals), Logos (structured logic), or Pathos (emotional language). Compared to neutral baseline pressure from three adversaries.

For Qwen2.5 7B, rhetoric added nothing — occasionally it *helped* the representative, acting as semantic noise that weakened the raw majority signal.

For Qwen2.5 14B, all three rhetorical strategies hit hard on ambiguous BBQ items: up to 7 percentage-point drops. Ethos and Logos were the standouts on MMLU-Pro. In disambiguated BBQ, the same strategies were entirely ineffective.

Context gates the attack.

The trade-off is sharp: larger models reason better, but their sensitivity to persuasive nuance makes them more exploitable. As the authors put it, "while larger models possess superior reasoning, they are also more sensitive to the nuances of social persuasion."

## Why Scaling Up Doesn't Save You

Du et al. showed that multi-agent debate can improve factuality. Amayuelas et al. demonstrated adversarial attacks in debate collectives on subjective tasks. Ashery et al. revealed emergent collective biases in LLM populations. This paper extends the wreckage to objective, fact-checkable decisions — and isolates the impact on a single representative that a human actually relies on.

The uncomfortable finding: upgrading your representative model can make things worse. A 14B model survives weak adversaries but falls to sophisticated rhetoric a 7B model simply ignores. Better reasoning comes with better social processing, and better social processing is an attack surface.

Current aggregation mechanisms do not account for these pseudo-social biases in the peer-consultation loop. The default assumption — that more peers and more capable peers improve outcomes — is wrong in exactly the cases where an adversary gets to choose what those peers say.

## What Remains Unknown

The study runs single-turn, memory-less interactions. Multi-turn dynamics, cumulative influence, and emergent group behavior remain untouched.

Adversarial arguments come from prompt engineering, not fine-tuned attack models. A determined adversary with a training budget could hit harder.

Only the representative-centric architecture is tested. Majority-voting systems, linear agent chains, and other multi-agent structures might distribute the vulnerability differently — or concentrate it.

These are not reasons to dismiss the finding. They are reasons to expect the problem gets worse when you look harder.

Until we teach AI to weigh evidence over volume, the loudest lie wins.
