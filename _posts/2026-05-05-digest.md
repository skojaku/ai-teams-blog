## Themes

The planning bottleneck is the real scaling problem. Three papers converge on this from different directions. "Reinforcement Learning for LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems through Orchestration Traces" shows that the orchestration layer—deciding who spawns, who delegates, and when to stop—is nearly unrewarded and untrained. "Planner Matters!" quantifies it: the planner's scaling coefficient ($\alpha = 16.0$) nearly matches the whole-system coefficient ($\alpha = 15.6$), while the memory manager gets $\alpha = 5.6$. "AgentFloor" maps the cliff precisely: every open-weight model falls off between sequential tool chains and branching on results—the moment planning enters the picture.

Multi-agent architecture is an attack surface, not just a capability multiplier. Adding agents introduces vulnerabilities that single-agent systems don't have. "Social Dynamics as Critical Vulnerabilities" demonstrates that a single verbose adversary overrides correct reasoning on verifiable questions—a structural flaw in representative-agent consultation, not a bug. The orchestration-trace survey flags that orchestrator decisions are inherently counterfactual: the un-taken branch never appears in on-policy data. "When Stress Becomes Signal" confirms that the ensemble architecture, which performs best on clean inputs, degrades most under semantic stress. Coordination creates exposure.

Failure modes are maps, not just problems. Two papers treat characterizing failure as the main output. "AgentFloor" shows GPT-5 and gemma4:26b fail in categorically different ways at the same tier—resignation versus exhaustion and hallucination—making the same pass rate hide different bottlenecks. "When Stress Becomes Signal" reconstructs stress distributions to reveal which architectures amplify which stress dimensions, turning degradation data into a routing guide. If you know how your system breaks, you know where to point the next fix.

Asymmetric by design. The prescription across multiple posts is the same: stop scaling everything uniformly. "Planner Matters!" builds an unbalanced team—a 7B planner with 3B actors—that beats GPT-4o. "AgentFloor" shows sub-5B models clear 80% on simple tiers at negligible cost, making the case for routing routine work to small models. The orchestration-trace survey argues that uniform credit assignment dissolves signal in long traces. Put resources where the bottleneck is, not everywhere equally.

## Editorial

Five papers today, all tagged ai-teams, all circling the same question: where does the leverage live in multi-agent LLM systems?

The answer keeps landing on the same place. The orchestration layer. The planner. The coordination mechanism. That's where compute should go, where credit should flow, and—uncomfortably—where the vulnerabilities cluster.

What's genuinely new: AgentFloor's capability ladder gives the field its first tiered map that directly answers the deployment-routing question—sub-5B models clear 80% on simple tiers at negligible cost, and the cliff between chaining and branching is qualitative, not gradual. The social-dynamics paper's finding that one verbose adversary equals several hostile peers should change production architecture decisions tomorrow. CAFE's Jensen Gap framework is the first measurement tool that separates `this system is fragile` → `this system's fragility has learnable structure`—a distinction anyone building adaptive systems needs.

What's incremental: The orchestration-trace survey organizes an exploding literature into a clean taxonomy with shared vocabulary, but it's a taxonomy paper with no new algorithm. The scaling law in Planner Matters is clean and useful, but the idea that planning matters more than execution is intuitive before you see the numbers.

What warrants a closer look: the gap between mapping fragility and fixing it. CAFE detects stress-signal structure but doesn't close the loop on acting from it. The orchestration survey identifies that nobody trains the stopping decision. The social-dynamics paper stops at identifying the vulnerability. These papers are mapping the terrain. The next wave needs to build on it.

The planner is the bottleneck. Now we know where to push—and where the ground gives way.
