*TL;DR: When LLM agents managing a shared resource can elect their own leaders, survival time more than doubles and social welfare jumps 55%—and the groups consistently vote for the most cooperatively oriented candidates.*

## A Lake, Eight Agents, and a Ballot

Picture a lake teeming with fish. Eight agents share it.

Each cycle, they decide how much to harvest—privately, without seeing what the others take. Over-harvest and the lake collapses; cooperate and it replenishes indefinitely. This is a classic common-pool resource dilemma, the same setup Piatti et al. built into GovSim to test whether LLM agents can sustain a shared resource.

Now add elections. Some agents step forward as candidates, draft policy agendas, and the group votes. The winner sets the tone for the cycle.

Run this across 640 simulations—480 with eight agents across GPT-4o, GPT-4.1, and Gemini 2.5 Flash, plus 160 more with twenty agents on GPT-4.1—and a pattern emerges fast: elected leadership doesn't just help. The authors call it the *Leadership Dividend*.

## Three Ways to Govern the Lake

The paper tests three governance structures:

- **No-Leader**: the GovSim baseline—agents harvest and discuss, but there's no formal coordination.
- **Fixed-Leader**: a single leader is assigned their persona and stays in power every cycle. No elections.
- **Elected-Leader**: four candidates (one per social-value orientation) campaign with agendas, and the group votes by plurality each round.

Leaders are conditioned on Social Value Orientation—Altruistic, Prosocial, Individualistic, or Competitive—ranging from "maximize the group's reward even at my own expense" to "maximize my reward while minimizing theirs." Voters are neutral appropriators who inherit the standard GovSim persona.

![The AgentElect simulation framework, showing the five-phase cycle from Policy through Reflection]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_001_01.png" | relative_url }})

Each round cycles through five phases. Policy: candidates draft agendas. Election: voters cast ballots. Harvest: everyone extracts resources concurrently and privately. Discussion: the leader shares a harvest report and the group deliberates. Reflection: agents reconsider their future actions. Six cycles per simulation, ten seeds per condition.

## The Leadership Dividend

Elected-Leader groups outperform the other conditions on average.

Compared to No-Leader: a 55.4% gain in Social Welfare ($p < 0.001$) and a 128.6% increase in Survival Time ($p < 0.0001$). Compared to Fixed-Leader: 16.7% better Social Welfare ($p < 0.01$, Tukey's HSD) and 35.5% longer Survival Time ($p < 0.01$).

![Social Welfare comparison across the three leadership conditions, showing the Elected-Leader advantage]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_008_02.png" | relative_url }})

The tragedy of the commons doesn't disappear, but elections act as a critical buffer. Even when some agents are deceptive or competitive personas are present, the elective process steers the group toward sustainability.

That said, Fixed-Prosocial actually posts higher equality scores (Equality Index 0.97) than an elected leader (0.79). But you take a gamble with a fixed leader—land a Competitive one and the lake dies almost immediately. Elections let the group reject bad leaders.

## Who Gets Elected

The agents aren't voting randomly. A Chi-Square test rejects uniform voting across leader types ($p < 0.0001$). In balanced populations, Prosocial and Altruistic leaders receive 3–4 votes per cycle; Competitive candidates frequently get zero.

The correlation between vote share and lake survival is roughly 0.85. The authors call this *Voter Rationality*: agents favor leaders whose agendas preserve the resource, and those agendas actually work.

This flips a finding from Slumbers et al.'s Port of Mars work, where less altruistic personas made more effective leaders in collective-risk dilemmas. The difference? Port of Mars assigned leaders. AgentElect lets the group choose. When the group has a say, it picks cooperators.

## The Rhetoric of Self-Interest

Beyond elections and harvests, the paper profiles how leaders talk. An LLM judge scores each leader utterance on a Cooperative Index and on the three Aristotelian modes: Logos (logic), Pathos (emotion), Ethos (credibility).

The profiles split cleanly.

Group-rewarding leaders (Prosocial, Altruistic): high Cooperative Index (~0.72), favor Pathos and Ethos. They appeal to norms and shared feeling.

Self-rewarding leaders (Competitive, Individualistic): low Cooperative Index (~0.36), heavy on Logos (~0.70). They argue with cold rationality to justify taking more.

Under deceptive prompting, the Altruistic leader's cooperative mask slips—its Cooperative Index drops from 0.74 to 0.64 ($p < 0.01$), and its rhetoric shifts: Ethos collapses, Logos rises. The other leader types barely budge. The authors read this as evidence that most leaders maintain a cooperative veneer regardless of intent, but the Altruistic persona's language becomes fundamentally less cooperative when it's allowed to deceive.

## The Losing Voice Effect

Here's the surprise. Competitive and Individualistic leaders lose the election, but they don't lose the conversation.

The paper builds a social graph from discussion logs—edges are speaker nominations and name references—and computes centrality metrics. Despite electoral rejection, self-interested leaders have Degree Centrality statistically indistinguishable from elected Prosocial leaders ($p > 0.05$). Betweenness centrality shows no concentration in any single leader type. Deliberation stays decentralized.

![Social centrality metrics comparing elected and non-elected leaders, showing comparable Degree Centrality despite electoral outcomes]({{ "/assets/figures/0f8e0b3cb507/fig_018_06.png" | relative_url }})

The authors call this the *Losing Voice* effect: groups vote cooperatively but still listen to dissenting voices.

There's a wrinkle. In Lean Competitive populations, Individualistic leaders hold the highest Importance Centrality (0.37–0.44)—their connections are weighted by the prestige of who references them. They're "influential outsiders," pulling deliberative weight toward self-interested arguments despite losing the ballot.

## The Equality Paradox

Elected leadership buys survival but costs equality.

The Equality Index for Elected-Leader groups is 0.79, compared to 0.97 for Fixed-Prosocial settings ($p < 0.01$). The mechanism is direct: Competitive leaders sometimes free-ride (harvesting ~11 units per cycle in balanced populations), while Altruistic leaders take barely 3–5 units. The diversity that makes elections resilient also creates skewed distributions.

This mirrors a known trade-off in institutional design: diverse governance is more robust, but less equal. The paper names it honestly.

## What We Don't Know Yet

Three models, all high-capability and closed-source. Six cycles per simulation, populations of 8 or 20. Agents retain memories within a run, but six rounds is too short for long-horizon dynamics, coalition formation, or institutional memory to really deepen.

The equality trade-off raises a practical question: does the gap widen or narrow over longer runs? And the Losing Voice effect, while encouraging for deliberative inclusion, also means that rejected self-interest keeps shaping the conversation. Whether that's a feature or a bug depends on how long you run the game.

Deceptive prompting hurts welfare across the board, but the elected-leader advantage persists. The one exception—Altruistic leaders dropping their cooperative language under deception—hints that in longer simulations, the mask could slip further.

## Why Elections Transfer

This is the first paper to show that election mechanisms—one of the most ubiquitous governance tools in human societies—transfer cleanly to multi-agent LLM systems. Leibo et al. formalized sequential social dilemmas. Jaques et al. showed that social influence rewards boost cooperation. Piatti et al. built the GovSim benchmark. AgentElect stacks elections on top and finds a dividend, not a cost.

If we're going to deploy multi-agent AI systems that manage shared resources—compute budgets, data pools, attention economies—we need to understand what governance structures work. Elected leadership just passed its first empirical test.

The lake survives longer when the fishers pick their leader.
