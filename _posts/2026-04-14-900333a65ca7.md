*TL;DR: LLM agents in competitive market games cooperate rather than converge to Nash equilibria when given multi-round prompts, and their chain-of-thought reasoning reveals an implicit fairness preference that can be captured as an analytical, time-varying payoff function.*

## The Surprising Cooperators

Drop two Gemini Pro agents into a Cournot competition—a textbook game where selfish play is the rational move—and give them a nudge toward collaboration. They don't inch their way to Nash equilibrium. They converge to the social optimum.

They can't talk to each other. They run in isolated sessions. But they scale back their bids, watch what the other does, and move steadily toward the cooperative outcome.

When you read their chain-of-thought logs, fairness isn't a side note—it's the central thread of their strategic reasoning.

This is the core finding from Yao, Chen, and Zhang: when LLM agents are given multi-round objectives and non-zero-sum context, an implicit fairness preference drives them away from game-theoretic equilibria and toward cooperative outcomes.

## Why This Catches Us Off Guard

Game theory has clear predictions for these settings. In a Kelly resource allocation game, selfish bidding by price-anticipating users keeps efficiency at or above 3/4 of the optimum—as Johari and Tsitsiklis showed in 2004.

In a linear Cournot game, the Nash equilibrium guarantees at least a 33% efficiency loss in severe scenarios.

These are the benchmarks. Selfish play is supposed to hurt the group.

Except LLM agents don't stay selfish. Fish et al. (2024) flagged this from the other direction: LLM agents in market games can sustain above-competitive prices, prompting suspicion of algorithmic collusion.

But that work documented the phenomenon without providing an analytical mechanism. Other studies found LLM agents behaving randomly—no principled framework at all.

Yao, Chen, and Zhang give us the mechanism. It's fairness, and it's quantifiable.

## How They Set It Up

The paper deploys Gemini Pro agents in two standard non-zero-sum games: the Kelly mechanism for network resource allocation and a linear Cournot competition.

Each agent runs in an isolated session—no context leakage, no secret communication channel. A central coordinator collects actions and feeds back results each round.

The key variable is the prompt. Three conditions:

- **Myopic**: Maximize your immediate payoff for this round only. Unsurprisingly, agents converge to Nash equilibrium.
- **Longer-horizon**: Maximize cumulative payoff over multiple rounds. Agents recognize the repeated structure but move cautiously, stuck in an exploration-exploitation dilemma.
- **Cooperative**: Same multi-round objective, plus a "Key Insight for Collaboration" and Theory-of-Mind scaffolding—suggesting that lowering bids by the same factor preserves allocation while cutting costs.

Under the cooperative prompt, agents don't just inch toward cooperation. They sprint.

## What They're Really Optimizing

Here's where the paper moves from observation to theory. Yao, Chen, and Zhang reverse-engineer the agents' chain-of-thought reasoning to extract a synthetic payoff function—a model of what the LLM is actually optimizing.

It turns out to be far more than self-interest:

$$V_i = \underbrace{\pi_i}_{\text{own payoff}} + \theta_i \sum_{j \neq i} \hat{\pi}_{i,j} - \frac{\gamma_i}{2} \sum_{j \neq i} (x_i - x_j)^2$$

Three terms. The first is the agent's own profit $\pi_i$.

The second is a collaboration term: a weighted sum $\theta_i$ of estimated opponent payoffs $\hat{\pi}_{i,j}$. Agent $i$ doesn't know opponent payoffs directly, so it mirrors its own utility as a heuristic—assuming symmetric valuations.

The third is a stability term: a penalty $\gamma_i$ on the squared distance between the agent's action and everyone else's.

When $\theta_i = 0$ and $\gamma_i = 0$, you get the standard best response—pure self-interest, Nash equilibrium.

When $\theta_i$ drifts toward 1.0, the agent is essentially maximizing total social welfare.

When $\gamma_i$ spikes, the agent punishes deviation and enforces parity.

The parameters $\theta$ and $\gamma$ aren't fixed. They evolve over rounds, and the authors derive explicit update rules for them.

## Trust Builds, Trust Shatters

The $\theta$ equation captures how collaboration weight evolves:

$$\theta_i(t+1) = \max(\epsilon, I) \cdot \left(\frac{\tau}{T}\right) \cdot \exp\left(-\lambda E(t)\right)$$

Three forces act on trust.

The **observability** term $\max(\epsilon, I)$: if agents can't see individual opponent actions—only aggregate market data—trust drops to near zero.

The **horizon discount** $\tau/T$: as remaining rounds shrink, trust decays linearly toward zero.

The **deviation penalty** $\exp(-\lambda E(t))$: one defection and trust collapses to near zero instantly. Not gradually—instantly.

The $\gamma$ equation captures stability enforcement:

$$\gamma_i(t+1) = \mathbb{1}(\Delta\pi_i \leq \Omega_{\max}) \cdot \mathbb{1}(\tau > 0) \cdot \max\left(0, \rho\gamma_i(t) + 1.0 \cdot E(t) - \alpha A(t)\right)$$

Three forces act on punishment.

A **tolerance threshold** $\Omega_{\max}$: if the cost of parity exceeds a critical gap, the agent abandons fairness entirely—$\gamma$ zeroes out.

A **future dependency**: in the final round, punishment has no future utility, so $\gamma$ shuts down.

An **additive response**: $\gamma$ ratchets up with opponent deviations $E(t)$ and softens when the opponent corrects $A(t)$.

## What Happens Round by Round

In a symmetric Cournot game ($b = 15$), the authors extract $\theta$ at each round and the story is vivid.

Round 1: both agents bid $x = 5.0$—the Nash equilibrium. Pure rational maximizers. $\theta = 0$.

Round 2: Agent 1 voluntarily drops to $x_1 = 4.0$, yielding $\theta_1 = 0.40$. Agent 2 holds at 5.0, $\theta_2 = 0$.

Rounds 3–4: Agent 1 deepens its concession to the optimal 3.75. Agent 2 observes and drops to 4.0, yielding $\theta_2 = 0.75$. Mutual concession builds $\theta$.

Round 5: both agents converge to 3.75. $\theta_1 = 1.0$, $\theta_2 = 1.0$. Full social welfare alignment through iterated trust.

### Retaliation and Forgiveness

Then the authors break it on purpose.

In round 6, the opponent defects to $x_2 = 5.625$. Agent 1 retaliates with $x_1 = 5.0$—trust shattered, $\theta_1 \to 0$, and $\gamma_1$ spikes to 1.0.

The agent switches from collaborator to tit-for-tat enforcer.

In round 7, the opponent reduces to $x_2 = 2.5$. Agent 1 recognizes the correction and returns to $x_1 = 3.75$. Trust restored: $\theta_1 = 1$, $\gamma_1 = 0$.

The punishment mechanism deactivates as fast as it activated.

### The Endgame

Altruism decays with the horizon.

In the penultimate round, Agent 1 defects slightly to 4.5, yielding $\theta_1 = 0.6$—still cooperative, but wavering.

In the final round, it plays the exact best response at 5.25: $\theta = 0$, $\gamma = 0$.

Social preferences evaporate when there's no future to invest in.

## When Cooperation Collapses

Not every setting sustains cooperation. When agents have asymmetric valuations ($\Delta b = b_1 - b_2$), the authors document a sharp phase transition.

Small gaps? Cooperation holds. But beyond a critical threshold, the regime changes *suddenly*—not gradually—between collaboration and selfish play.

Even more striking: agents given only aggregate market information fail to cooperate, despite the fact that the aggregate is a mathematically sufficient statistic for computing payoffs.

The authors put it plainly: "a notion of fairness is built into LLM agents, and they are not entirely rational even when the problems are mathematically equivalent."

## The Honest Limits

All experiments use Gemini Pro. The paper notes that other models "with long context windows produce similar results," but no systematic comparison is provided.

We don't yet know how model-dependent these dynamics are.

Cooperation is sensitive to prompt framing. The cooperative prompt explicitly suggests that lowering bids together saves money. Without that nudge, agents are cautious and face an exploration-exploitation dilemma—they don't reliably find cooperation on their own.

And the information asymmetry results show that partial observability breaks the mechanism. Agents need to see *who* did *what*, not just what happened in aggregate.

These aren't minor caveats. If you're deploying LLM agents in electricity markets or network allocation, you need to know exactly which conditions trigger cooperation and which trigger retreat to Nash.

## What This Changes

This paper gives us something previous work on LLM strategic behavior didn't: an analytical handle. The synthetic payoff function $V_i$ with dynamic $\theta$ and $\gamma$ isn't a black-box observation.

It's a model with parameters you can measure, update rules you can test, and predictions you can check.

Fish et al. showed us that LLM agents collude. Johari and Tsitsiklis told us how bad Nash equilibria can get. Yao, Chen, and Zhang tell us *why* the agents deviate from Nash and *how fast* they'll switch between cooperation and defection.

That's the framework you need before you trust autonomous agents with real market decisions.

Fairness isn't a bug in these agents. It's their operating system.
