## Themes

**Architecture is the first-order variable.** Every paper today agrees: the way you organize agents matters more than how many you add or how powerful each one is. "When Agents Evolve, Institutions Follow" shows a 57-point performance swing from swapping governance topology alone. "The Inverse-Wisdom Law" proves that adding same-family agents doesn't dilute error—it stabilizes it. "Heterogeneous Scientific Foundation Model Collaboration" beats language-only ensembles not with more LLMs but with a different kind of intelligence in the loop. The multiplier is structure, not scale.

**Same-family agents amplify failure.** The Inverse-Wisdom Law gives this a closed form: when the synthesizer's tribalism coefficient τ dominates, a perfect auditor still can't save the swarm. "When Agents Evolve, Institutions Follow" echoes this from the other side—gated pipelines where the gatekeeper shares the same model's biases turn review loops into resource sinks, burning 89K tokens on tasks that score 21.6%. Kinship in agent teams is not a feature. It is a hazard.

**Prevention beats recovery.** "Reinforced Agent" builds a reviewer that inspects tool calls before they execute, not after. "Safe Bilevel Delegation" adjusts delegation authority at runtime before the safety constraint is breached, not after a violation. Both papers draw the same line: the old way is post-hoc correction; the new way is proactive prevention. Once the call goes out or the task is delegated, damage is already done.

**The right kind of diversity crosses representation, not just model family.** "Heterogeneous Scientific Foundation Model Collaboration" shows that mixing a time-series model with a language model beats mixing two language models from different families. "The Inverse-Wisdom Law" proves that Mixture-of-Agents and X-MAS—heterogeneous by LLM family but homogeneous by modality—fail to beat strong single-LLM baselines. Diversity of representation is the lever. Diversity of brand is decoration.

## Editorial

Today's batch reads like five chapters of the same argument: multi-agent AI is not a scaling problem, it is a design problem.

The clearest result is the Inverse-Wisdom Law. It gives you a theorem and a coefficient that quantifies what everyone suspected but couldn't prove—same-family swarms agree themselves into disaster. The 67.8% error delta from swapping a single synthesizer node is the kind of number that reshapes how you think about team composition.

Reinforced Agent and SBD both attack the same blind spot from different angles: the moment of delegation. One pre-filters tool calls. The other pre-tunes how much autonomy to grant. Neither waits for failure and then scrambles to recover. That shift—from post-hoc to proactive—is where the field is moving.

Eywa and Institutions widen the lens. Eywa says the missing agents aren't more LLMs; they're models that think in different representations. Institutions says the missing variable isn't the agent at all; it's the governance topology that routes decisions through them. A 57-point swing from wiring alone should end the era of treating architecture as an afterthought.

What's genuinely new: formal constraints with teeth. The Heterogeneity Mandate is a provable safety requirement, not a design suggestion. SBD's monotonicity guarantee is a theorem, not a heuristic. The Synthesizer Gating Theorem gives you a number—and that number is the hard floor on your swarm's error. These are not "we tried it and it worked." They are "here is why it cannot work otherwise."

What's incremental: Eywa's empirical validation covers only two specialist FMs and GPT-family backbones. Institutions tests three commercial models. SBD has no empirical results at all—it is a pre-registered protocol. The theory is running ahead of the data, and the gap matters.

What warrants a second look: the gate-loop trap from Institutions. A topology that is optimal for one model can be catastrophic for another. If you are deploying multi-agent systems, you need to know which architecture your specific model is tuned for—and whether your model can satisfy its own gates. That question has no generic answer, and today's papers prove it.

The architecture is the agent.
