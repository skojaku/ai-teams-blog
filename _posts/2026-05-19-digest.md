## Themes

**Scaffolding over smarts.** The papers in today's batch keep pointing at the same uncomfortable truth: the model is not the bottleneck. "Code as Agent Harness" argues that the executable, inspectable, stateful wrapper around an LLM — tools, memory, verification loops — is what determines whether an agent actually works. "Beyond Static Responses" reaches the same conclusion from a different angle: each of its six tiers is unlocked not by a smarter model but by a new architectural capability — memory, autonomy, coordination, adaptation. The LIFE progression survey adds that even self-evolution depends on diagnostic infrastructure, not raw intelligence. The model delivers text. The harness makes it an agent.

**Process beats topology.** Two papers converge on a striking finding: the structure of multi-agent interaction matters less than its dynamics. "AgentArk" shows that distilling the *reasoning process* of debate — not the agent graph — into a single model captures most of the gains. "Multi-agent AI systems outperform human teams in creativity" finds that what predicts creative output is not who talks to whom but *how* the conversation moves through semantic space. Both papers reduce a structural question to a process variable: step-level correctness signals and trajectory geometry, respectively.

**The debugging debt.** The LIFE progression survey names what everyone building multi-agent systems already feels: harder collaboration means harder debugging. When a planner, coder, and verifier pass a hallucinated fact through three interaction rounds, the original error cascades beyond recognition. "Code as Agent Harness" offers inspectability as a partial remedy — make the harness readable. The taxonomy paper implies that Levels 4–5, where emergence happens, are precisely where attribution becomes most elusive. We can build systems that surprise us but not yet systems that tell us *why* they surprised us.

**Creativity by design.** The creativity paper does something rare: it turns a soft intuition — "diverse teams are more creative" — into a tunable knob. Discussion structure and model choice explain 26.8% of variance in conversation trajectories, and trajectory features predict 32.6% of creativity variance. That chain — design choice → geometry → outcome — means you can run a few pilot conversations, diagnose your model's default exploration pattern, and pick the discussion protocol that patches its weaknesses. Direct semantic jumps beat connected zigzags, and you can engineer the jump.

## Editorial

Five papers, one tag — ai-teams — and a shared conviction that multi-agent LLM systems have outgrown the "does it work?" question. Every post today is asking "how does it work, and where does it break?"

The convergence is genuine. The harness paper and the LIFE progression paper are two framings of the same problem: the infrastructure around the model is underbuilt. The taxonomy paper provides the vocabulary to say exactly which threshold you have not crossed. The creativity paper gives you measurement tools. The distillation paper asks whether you even need the team at runtime.

What is genuinely new: the creativity paper's trajectory geometry, which turns conversation dynamics into a design variable. And the LIFE progression's attribution–evolution loop, which names a feedback cycle the field has been circling without labeling. What is incremental: the taxonomy paper's level system, which organizes known work cleanly but does not validate the boundaries. And the distillation paper's "just bake it in" result, which works on benchmarks but still falters on out-of-domain transfer and excludes tool use entirely.

The paper warranting a closer second look is "Code as Agent Harness." Its distinction between system-provided infrastructure and agent-initiated artifacts is sharp and underexplored. When agents start writing, running, and revising their own harness code, you get a different kind of system — one that can improve itself and one that can break in ways no one audited. That is the frontier the LIFE authors need but do not yet have the tools to diagnose.

The model makes the text. The harness makes the agent. The diagnostics make the harness trustworthy. We have the first. We are building the second. We are barely sketching the third.
