*TL;DR: OMAC frames multi-agent LLM system design as five optimizable dimensions—two functional, three structural—and optimizes them all with a single contrastive-reasoning loop that treats performance gaps as supervised signal, no gradients required.*

## The Handcrafted Problem

Seven agents in a fully-connected mesh, every prompt hand-crafted, every output wired to every input. No one optimized any of it.

That's how most multi-agent LLM systems ship today. Prompts are art, wiring is guesswork, team composition is vibes. It works—up to a point.

OMAC asks: what if you could treat the *whole thing*—what agents say, who joins, when they join, and who hears what—as optimizable knobs, and turn them with a single loop?

## Five Knobs, One Machine

OMAC carves the multi-agent design space into five dimensions. Think of the collaboration process as information flowing over a graph: agents are nodes, communication channels are edges. Two dimensions tune the *nodes* (what agents do), three tune the *edges* (how they connect).

The functional dimensions:

- **Fun-1**: Optimize an existing agent's prompt or few-shot examples.
- **Fun-2**: Construct an entirely new agent from scratch and slot it into the team.

The structural dimensions:

- **Str-1**: A controller picks which agents belong on the team for a given task.
- **Str-2**: A controller decides which agents participate at each step—dynamic participation, not static casting.
- **Str-3**: A controller routes outputs: does Agent A's answer reach Agent B, or not?

![Five optimization dimensions of OMAC: two functional (agent prompt optimization, new agent construction) and three structural (candidate selection, dynamic participation, communication routing)]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

DyLAN optimized team composition via unsupervised heuristics. ADAS evolved agent prompts through automated search. AFlow searched workflow graphs with Monte Carlo tree search.

Each tackled one face of the problem—functionality *or* structure, never both together. OMAC unifies all five under one optimization umbrella.

## How the Loop Works

The engine has two moving parts: the *Semantic Initializer* and the *Contrastive Comparator*.

The Semantic Initializer generates a small collection of diverse candidates—agent prompts or controller prompts—by asking an LLM to explore the relevant semantic space with controlled variation. Think of it as a smart brainstorm that seeds the search.

Every candidate then runs through the full multi-agent pipeline on training data and earns a performance score. The top scorers become *positive* examples; the bottom become *negative*.

The Contrastive Comparator receives a positive-negative pair and reasons about *why* one outperformed the other. From that diagnosis, it generates a refined candidate—designed to amplify what worked and suppress what didn't.

![OMAC's iterative optimization loop: Semantic Initializer creates candidate collection, evaluation produces scores, Contrastive Comparator compares positive-negative pairs and generates refinements]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

The refined candidate re-enters the collection, gets re-evaluated, and the cycle repeats. Thresholds $h$ and $l$ define who counts as positive or negative—for all experiments, $h = l = 0.5$, meaning the top half is positive, the bottom half is negative. Initial collection size is 3; the loop runs for a maximum of 3 contrastive iterations.

This is supervised signal without gradient updates. You get "what went right vs. what went wrong" and you reason over the gap.

## The Programmer Who Comments

A concrete example from the paper: optimizing a "Programmer" agent (Fun-1). The Semantic Initializer generates two prompts—both cast the agent as a Programmer for code generation, but the second one explicitly requires code comments.

The second prompt performs better. The Contrastive Comparator identifies commenting as the key differentiator and generates a third prompt that emphasizes comments and explains *how* to use them. The refined agent writes more accurate, more logical code.

Vague "write good code" → targeted "include purposeful comments throughout."

## Turning Multiple Knobs

Tuning one knob helps. Tuning multiple knobs at once helps more—but you can't turn them all simultaneously or the contrastive signal gets noisy. OMAC's strategy: optimize one dimension at a time while freezing the others, then iterate.

Jointly optimizing the two best single dimensions on arithmetic reasoning lifts improvement from 2.9% to 9.6%. The paper also confirms that asking the Contrastive Comparator to reason over multiple changing factors at once produces smaller gains and higher variance—so iterative, one-at-a-time optimization isn't just a practical choice, it's the better design.

![Single-dimension versus multi-dimension optimization gains on arithmetic reasoning, showing joint optimization roughly triples the improvement]({{ "/assets/figures/9de46c6fd740/fig_021_08.png" | relative_url }})

To keep computation tractable, only the dimensions that individually showed the largest gains are selected for joint optimization. Two dimensions over two iterations costs four times what a single dimension costs; selectivity matters.

## The Numbers

All experiments run on gpt-3.5-turbo-1106 with temperature 0.8. The default MAS uses 7 agents for code generation and general reasoning, 4 for arithmetic, starting from a fully-connected baseline.

**HumanEval (code generation)**: OMAC reaches Pass@1 of $89.25 \pm 1.30$ (Fun-1.4), vs. $85.74 \pm 2.83$ for DyLAN, the strongest baseline.

**MMLU (general reasoning)**: OMAC hits $74.22 \pm 2.22$ accuracy (Fun-1.4), vs. $70.06 \pm 2.57$ for AFlow, the strongest baseline.

**MATH (arithmetic reasoning)**: OMAC reaches $35.17 \pm 1.96$ (Fun-1.1), vs. $32.49 \pm 2.62$ for AFlow, the strongest baseline.

The structural dimensions (Str-1 through Str-3) also improve performance across most benchmarks and configurations. Optimizing *who talks to whom* yields gains on top of optimizing *what agents say*, though the margin varies by task and dimension.

An ablation strips out the Contrastive Comparator entirely, leaving only the Semantic Initializer (dubbed OMAC-C). OMAC-C still beats DyLAN across every dimension on arithmetic reasoning—pure semantic-space exploration helps on its own.

But the full loop adds consistent lifts: $35.17$ vs. $34.20$ on MATH Fun-1.1. The contrastive reasoning step earns its keep.

## Honest Limits

All results sit on a single base model: gpt-3.5-turbo-1106. Whether OMAC transfers to open-weight models, larger LLMs, or genuinely massive multi-agent setups remains untested.

Training-time compute grows quickly with multi-dimension iteration. The paper acknowledges exponential cost in the number of jointly optimized dimensions and relies on selective dimension inclusion to stay tractable—but that's a heuristic, not a guarantee.

The fully-connected default starting point means OMAC optimizes *from* a baseline, not *from scratch*. If your initial team composition is poorly chosen, there's only so much prompt-level contrastive reasoning can do.

## Why It Matters

OMAC validates a simple claim that matters: multi-agent system design is not an art—it's an optimization problem with at least five legible dimensions, and you can turn them with one algorithmic loop.

No gradients. No MCTS. Just "show me what worked, show me what didn't, and let me reason about the gap."

Joint optimization delivers roughly triple the gain of any single dimension. The structural dimensions—team selection, dynamic participation, communication routing—are the ones most teams design by intuition. OMAC gives those intuitions a supervisor.

From cobbled-together to systematically tuned—one contrastive pair at a time.
