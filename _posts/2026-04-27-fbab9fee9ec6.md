*TL;DR: Thirty independent runs of LLM-agent forum simulations, benchmarked against 30 matched real-world windows, show that agents reproduce some online regularities—but single-run "believability" verdicts hide systematic structural mismatches that only replication can reveal.*

## One Run Looks Plausible. Thirty Tell the Truth.

You build LLM agents, drop them into a Reddit-like forum, and the conversations look reasonable. Lively threads, heated replies, topics that scan as tech news. You call it believable and move on.

That is essentially the validation standard today: the field's reviews find that most studies report a single run and assess it subjectively.

Tomašević and colleagues run thirty. And then compare every one of them against thirty matched, non-overlapping windows of real Voat data.

The result is a replicated, five-dimensional operational-validation framework—and a clear demonstration that "looks realistic" is not enough.

## From Single-Run Gut Checks to Replicated Benchmarks

Prior generative-agents work showed that LLM-powered agents could produce plausible social dynamics, but validation rested on single-run believability checks.

Tomašević et al. extend the idea in two directions: from village- and town-scale interactions to contentious online forums, and from the single-run gut check to 99% confidence intervals across five dimensions measured over 30 independent replications.

Their earlier Y Social platform already simulated Reddit communities. This paper adds the rigorous, replicated benchmark—calibrated against empirical Voat windows drawn from the MADOC dataset.

The shift matters because online platforms will soon mix human and artificial users. Understanding what AI agents alone can produce in a social media environment provides a critical baseline for later human–machine studies. You need to know the machine-only floor before you can measure what changes when people enter.

## How the Simulation Works

Each simulation runs for 30 simulated days on Y Social, a Reddit-like digital twin.

Agents are powered by Dolphin Mistral 24B Venice Edition—an uncensored model chosen to preserve the possibility of disagreement and toxic speech, not to produce polite consensus.

Agents are *stateless*. No persistent memory. Continuity comes from server-side state—thread history, user interests—passed into each prompt.

When an agent opens a thread, it sees only the last $K = 3$ prior items.

Each agent carries a persona: education level, political leaning (four right-of-center segments matching Voat's ideological profile), age, 2–5 topical interests from a 10-category tech catalog, and a toxicity propensity level.

A truncated Zipf distribution ($s = 2.5$) assigns per-round action budgets, producing the heavy-tailed participation pattern familiar from real online communities.

Each turn presents exactly three options: NONE plus two distinct actions sampled from posting, reading, commenting, searching, and sharing links. Engagement weights calibrate comment-to-post ratios.

Discussion is seeded from a fixed catalog of 1,000 real Voat technology URLs.

Population turnover targets the high-churn character of Voat's v/technology: the simulation adds 30% new agents per day and removes 90% of the longest-inactive agents, starting from 50 agents. The resulting user counts match Voat's roughly 600 unique users per 30-day window.

The forum is small, volatile, and shallow—average thread length ≈ 2 interactions.

## Where the Numbers Line Up

Across 30 runs, three core activity metrics show overlapping 99% CIs with the Voat benchmark:

- Root posts: simulation 593 [577, 610] vs. Voat 569 [495, 642] ✓
- Unique users: 610 [595, 625] vs. 591 [505, 677] ✓
- Daily active users: 37.3 [36.6, 38.0] vs. 32.7 [27.7, 37.6] ✓

Those lineups are real. The simulation does produce the right order of magnitude for the basic traffic signals.

Topic alignment is even stronger. BERTopic models fit separately on simulation and Voat threads show 99.6% of simulation topics have at least one Voat match at cosine $\geq 0.50$.

Mean top-match cosine is 0.687 [0.680, 0.693].

Privacy and security, energy and EVs, AI ethics, Big Tech regulation—all map cleanly.

The persona interest catalog and URL seeding do their job: agents talk about the right things.

## Where the Simulation Diverges

The divergence shows up the moment you look past surface activity.

### Comments and threads inflate

Simulated comments hit 904 [880, 927] vs. Voat's 733 [601, 865], with non-overlapping intervals.

Average thread size follows: 2.53 vs. 2.25. The reply engine runs too hot.

### Network cores are too big and too diffuse

Both networks exhibit core–periphery structure—a real qualitative match.

But the simulated core is 3.8× larger: 19.6% [18.6, 20.7] of the largest connected component vs. Voat's 5.1% [4.2, 6.1].

Core–periphery density is 0.020 vs. 0.055. Repeated interactions are rarer (5.90% vs. 8.70% of edges).

The simulation spreads hub status across too many weakly connected nodes instead of concentrating it in a tight, high-density core.

The degree distribution tells the same story. Voat's top 1% of nodes hold a median 20.3% of total degree.

The simulation's top 1% hold just 7.2%.

Participation inequality exists in both—but the simulation's hubs don't consolidate power the way real users do.

### Toxicity is misallocated across layers

This is the most structurally interesting mismatch.

Simulated root posts carry mean toxicity 0.116 [0.107, 0.124]—nearly four times real Voat root submissions at 0.0307 [0.0265, 0.0348].

But simulated comments are *less* toxic than Voat comments: 0.161 [0.151, 0.170] vs. 0.189 [0.173, 0.205].

The direction is right—comments are more toxic than posts in both settings.

But the gradient is flattened: too much toxicity leaks into the opening posts, too little accumulates in the reply layer.

A depth-shuffle permutation test confirms the pattern comes from the post/comment split, not from within-thread escalation.

A neutral-persona sensitivity variant cuts mean toxicity by 46.7% and the share of items above 0.5 toxicity by 51.5%, while a no-politics variant barely moves the needle. Toxicity is driven by persona construction broadly, not just political leaning.

## The Convergence Entropy Trap

The most revealing finding is about stylistic convergence.

Convergence entropy $H$ measures how predictable an utterance becomes given prior speech—lower entropy means more convergence.

Across 30 runs, mean $H/T = 0.275$ [0.270, 0.280], below the GPT-4o mini baseline of 0.2827. Looks like the agents accommodate each other's style.

But then the lag structure gives it away:

$$H: 0.272 \text{ (lag 1)} \to 0.279 \text{ (lag 2)} \to 0.287 \text{ (lag 3)} \to \text{plateau at lag 4+}$$

The entropy staircase exactly traces the $K = 3$ context window. While prior turns remain visible, agents echo each other. The moment context drops out, convergence vanishes.

There is no persistent stylistic accommodation—no learning, no identity alignment, no "we've been talking so I'll adjust to you." It's purely an architectural artifact.

This is the kind of result that only appears when you measure mechanism, not surface. A single-run believability check would have celebrated the convergence. Replication with the lag decomposition exposes it as a mirage.

## Honest Limits

The agents are stateless by design. No persistent memory means no long-term relationship formation, no grudge-holding, no loyalty. The $K = 3$ context cap limits conversational depth and produces the artifactual convergence entropy pattern.

One model, one community. Everything here runs on Dolphin Mistral 24B Venice Edition, simulating Voat's v/technology. Whether a larger model, a differently seeded community, or a mainstream platform like Reddit would shift these conclusions remains unknown.

The personas are constructed for face validity, not demographic representativeness. Political-leaning weights are heuristic approximations, not calibrated estimates of Voat's true population.

These aren't footnotes. They are the specific targets the paper identifies for future work—and the reason the paper's methodological contribution matters more than any single result.

## What Changes Now

This paper replaces the single-run believability judgment with a replicable, five-dimensional benchmark. It tells you exactly where LLM-agent simulations succeed (topic coverage, user counts, qualitative network structure), exactly where they fail (diffuse cores, misallocated toxicity, context-driven pseudo-convergence), and exactly how to measure both.

That is the baseline the field needed. Before you can study human–machine interaction in online platforms, you need to know what machines alone produce. Now we have a procedure for finding out—and a catalog of structural mismatches to fix first.

What looks like emergent social behavior may just be your context window showing.
