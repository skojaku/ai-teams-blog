*TL;DR: You can cut LLM agent costs by 2.5–3.5× without any training—just by letting a cheap student watch the teacher and self-check its confidence at each step. The method matches or even exceeds teacher accuracy on two benchmarks, and it's ready the moment you finish collecting demonstrations.*

You want to run an LLM agent at scale—a million batch tasks, say. Claude Sonnet 4.5 nails the job, but at $0.059 per episode the bill stings. GPT-4.1-mini costs roughly a tenth per token but face-plants on accuracy: 18% on ALFWorld, 28% on AppWorld.

Fine-tuning the student sounds like the fix. Except it takes days of training, breaks when your agent design changes, and—as the authors discovered—can produce models with 0% task success despite converging training loss. Prompt engineering is brittle, slow, and unreliable.

You're stuck choosing between quality and cost.

This paper says you don't have to choose.

## Two Old Tricks, One New Combination

The insight is almost embarrassingly simple: combine two well-known inference-time techniques and aim them at cost reduction instead of capability improvement.

**Trick one: dynamic in-context distillation.** Run the teacher on a few hundred tasks. Index those trajectories in a vector database. At every step the student takes, retrieve the most relevant teacher demonstration windows—goal, plan, reasoning, action—and stuff them into the student's context. The student learns *why* the teacher did what it did, not just *what* it did.

**Trick two: self-consistency cascades.** Sample the student's action three times per step. If all three agree, execute the student's action. If they diverge, defer that step to the teacher. Agreement signals confidence; disagreement signals the retrieved examples didn't cover this situation.

Neither trick is new. Wang et al.'s self-consistency work and Sarukkai et al.'s per-step retrieval architecture both predate this paper. The contribution is aiming them squarely at the cost problem—with a cascade that routes between student and teacher rather than within a single model, and with teacher-to-student transfer rather than self-improvement. Other cost-reduction efforts require learned routers or model access; this one needs only inference-time mechanisms.

## Wiring It All Together

The full system runs in two phases.

**Collection.** Execute the teacher on a small task subset $T_\text{demo}$. Save every trajectory—plans, observations, reasoning traces, actions. Embed goals, plans, and reasoning with MiniLM-L6-v2. Done.

**Deployment.** For each new task, the student runs a ReAct-style loop. At every step:

1. **Retrieve.** Fetch the top-$k$ most similar teacher windows using multi-key cosine similarity over goal, plan, and current reasoning.
2. **Sample.** The student generates $N=3$ candidate actions conditioned on the retrieved context.
3. **Route.** If all three agree → student acts. If not → teacher takes over that step.

$$a_t = \begin{cases} a_t^{(1)} & \text{if } \forall\, i,j :\, a_t^{(i)} = a_t^{(j)} \\ M_t(g, p, o_t) & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Sampling three student outputs costs $3\times$ the output tokens. But since the student is roughly $10\times$ cheaper per token and input tokens dominate agent costs, the overhead is small. The teacher only fires when the student admits uncertainty.

No weight updates. No prompt tuning. No manual engineering. The system is ready the moment demonstration collection finishes.

## What They Found

On **ALFWorld** (embodied household tasks), the full pipeline hits 96% accuracy—*higher* than the teacher's 89%—at 2.5× lower cost ($0.059 → $0.024 per episode). The boost likely comes from retrieved examples giving the student implicit information about environment dynamics across multiple trajectories.

On **AppWorld** (multi-step API orchestration), the story is harder but still compelling: 66% accuracy at 29% of teacher cost, a 3.5× cost reduction while recovering 79% of teacher accuracy. The boosted GPT-4.1-mini (55% accuracy at 15% cost) even outperforms zero-shot GPT-4.1 (51% at 56% cost)—a smaller model with teacher demonstrations strictly dominates a larger standalone one.

Both tricks pull their weight. In-context learning alone bridges 97% of the teacher gap on ALFWorld. The cascade adds intelligent routing on top.

Random teacher-student mixing and cascade-only baselines are strictly worse on both benchmarks.

### How Many Demonstrations Do You Need?

Surprisingly few. Just 100 teacher demonstrations on ALFWorld yield 94% of teacher accuracy.

The upfront collection cost of $29.50 amortizes after 843 episodes. At a million tasks, cumulative savings exceed $34,900.

### What About Fine-Tuning?

On ALFWorld, fine-tuning GPT-4.1-mini reached 94% accuracy—close to the paper's 96%, and the only baseline that trades blows.

On AppWorld, fine-tuning *failed completely*: API services failed when training on teacher demonstrations, and Llama-3.3-70B trained to converging loss but produced 0% task success.

Complex multi-step agents turn out to be genuinely hard to fine-tune—objective design and debugging become real bottlenecks.

## Where It Breaks Down

The method's ceiling is the demonstration database. On AppWorld's hardest difficulty tier, the student recovers only 43% accuracy versus the teacher's 71%.

An LLM-judge analysis of student failures found that 68.8% trace back to coverage gaps—retrieved examples simply didn't demonstrate the needed API usage, disambiguation strategy, or error handling.

This is the honest limit: your student is only as good as the diversity of behavior in your demonstration pool. If the teacher never showed a certain edge case, the student will flounder and the cascade will fire—but deferring to the teacher costs money, and on hard tasks those deferrals add up fast.

The economics also depend on a wide cost gap between student and teacher. The paper assumes a roughly $10\times$ per-token price ratio; if model pricing narrows, the savings compress. And the $N=3$ sampling overhead, while modest now, is still overhead.

## Why This Matters Right Now

You can deploy this today. Collect a few hundred teacher trajectories, index them, and turn on the cascade. No training run, no prompt iteration, no waiting.

That matters when you're iterating on agent designs in production or running time-sensitive batch jobs where fine-tuning cycles are a non-starter.

The approach generalizes across closed-weight (GPT-4.1-mini) and open-weight (Llama-3.3-70B) students, across embodied reasoning and API orchestration. And for practitioners with difficulty metadata, a simple variant—route hard tasks straight to the teacher—can push accuracy to 78%, recovering 94% of teacher performance at 71% of teacher cost.

The deeper lesson: inference-time levers like retrieval quality, demonstration coverage, and cascade thresholds are real knobs for navigating cost-performance tradeoffs.

You don't always need to retrain to get a different operating point. Sometimes you just need a better librarian.

A cheaper student, taught on the fly, that knows when to ask for help—that's the distillation you can actually deploy.
