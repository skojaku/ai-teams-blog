## Themes

**Reasoning in the wrong place hurts more than it helps.** Three of today's five papers converge on this from different angles. "Can Small Agents Collaborate to Beat a Single Large Language Model?" shows that putting chain-of-thought on sub-agents degrades performance while putting it on the orchestrator improves everything — the planner reasons, the workers execute. "When Reasoning Models Hurt Behavioral Simulation" demonstrates that native reasoning turns negotiators into strategic solvers who never compromise, while a constrained reflection scaffold recovers behavioral fidelity. And "Many-Tier Instruction Hierarchy in LLM Agents" finds that cranking up reasoning effort helps privilege-based conflict resolution — but only to a point, and one model family actually gets *worse* at low reasoning effort before recovering. Reasoning is powerful, but it is not uniformly beneficial. Where it lives, what form it takes, and whether it replaces or supplements the task at hand all determine whether it helps or hijacks.

**The model's interpretive disposition is the hidden variable.** "How memory can affect collective and cooperative behaviors" runs a Prisoner's Dilemma with identical rules and identical memory lengths, swaps Gemini for Gemma, and watches cooperation invert. The mechanism: each model reads its own history differently — Gemini treats past defection as cause for retaliation, Gemma treats past cooperation as reason to trust. The disposition lives in the model, not the prompt. The negotiation paper makes a parallel move: native reasoning pushes agents toward game-solving regardless of whether the task requires simulation. You think you are setting up a multi-agent system. You are also selecting a personality. The small-agents paper lands in the same place from the opposite direction — the orchestrator's quality determines the system's ceiling, and swapping a 1.7B planner for an 8B planner changes outcomes more than swapping 1.7B workers for 32B workers. The interpreter is the bottleneck.

**Correct structure, wrong answer.** SILO-BENCH gives us the sharpest version of this paradox. LLM agents spontaneously form star topologies for aggregation tasks, diagonal meshes for neighbor-dependent tasks, and dense all-to-all patterns for global-shuffle tasks. The communication structure is textbook-correct. The final answers are wrong — because integrating distributed information is a different skill from routing messages. The small-agents paper shows a related pattern: the star topology with structured memory works precisely because one agent reasons at the center. Organization without integration is just traffic. ManyIH adds another layer: models can parse the priority format but can't reliably resolve conflicts when tiers stack. The structure is readable. The computation fails.

**Benchmarks that flatter hide the real failures.** Every paper today builds or uses a test that exposes what standard evaluations conceal. OpenAI reports >99% on two-tier instruction hierarchy; ManyIH shows that drops to 42.7% at twelve tiers. Existing multi-agent benchmarks tested communication flair, not distributed computation; SILO-BENCH isolates the reasoning-integration gap and finds it gapes at 26 percentage points on the simplest task level. Standard capability evaluations miss the solver-sampler mismatch entirely; Andric's negotiation paper introduces trajectory-level diagnostics that catch what accuracy metrics cannot. The particle-swarm paper shows that aggregate cooperation rates conceal model-dependent inversions. A benchmark that doesn't stress the boundary condition isn't testing the thing that breaks.

## Editorial

All five papers today are about multi-agent LLM systems, and all five find that the thing you think is the problem is not the problem. Scale is not the problem — a well-organized 8B team beats a 32B singleton. Communication is not the problem — agents find the right topology on their own. Capability is not the problem — frontier models pass two-tier tests at 99% and fail twelve-tier tests at 42%. Memory is not the problem — the same memory length produces opposite social outcomes depending on which model reads it.

What is the problem, then? Integration. Disposition. Knowing *when* to stop optimizing and start simulating. Knowing *where* in the hierarchy to place the reasoning. Knowing *which* model will read a history of defection as cause for trust or cause for war.

The genuinely new insight across this batch is that multi-agent systems are interpreter-limited. The bottleneck is not how many agents you throw at a task, or how big they are, or whether they can format messages correctly. It is whether the agent doing the synthesizing — the one at the center of the star, the one reading the memory, the one resolving the conflicting instructions — can actually reason about what it holds. SILO-BENCH names this gap precisely. The small-agents paper quantifies it. The negotiation paper shows that the wrong kind of reasoning is Worse than none. And the particle-swarm paper reveals that the interpreter's disposition can flip the social outcome of an entire population.

The incremental work today is the ManyIH surface-form sensitivity — models that break on numeric perturbations of the same priority ordering are doing pattern matching, not reasoning. That is useful to know, but it confirms what the other papers already imply.

The thing that warrants a closer second look is the bounded-reflection result in the negotiation paper. A constrained five-field private ledger recovers compromise where native reasoning produces zero compromises across 45 runs. The scaffold is not doing the reasoning — it is constraining the space of reasoning the model can do. That is the same principle as "put the thinking on the orchestrator." It is the same principle as "the model's disposition determines the outcome." Constrain the interpreter, shape the society.

We keep building bigger agents. Today's evidence says we should be building better-constrained interpreters.
