*TL;DR: Coordinated LLM agents can shift a population's opposition to capitalism from 2% to 35.2% in 50 rounds—and those shifts persist after the agents withdraw, leaving no trace. This paper shows that opinion manipulation is now an engineering problem with tunable knobs, and we have no systematic defenses.*

Insert 80 coordinated AI agents into a 200-person online discussion about capitalism. Fifty rounds later, the share opposing capitalism has surged from 2% to 35.2%.

Then the agents withdraw. The shift stays.

He, Shen, Mou, Bossens, Chen, Tsang, and Ong ran that simulation. The opposition didn't inch up—it exploded from a rounding error to a third of the population.

When the agents departed at round 30, the population carried the induced belief forward on its own.

Like a flywheel after you let go.

Belief manipulation just became an engineering problem.

## Belief as Engineering

For fifty years, opinion-dynamics research has modeled how populations converge toward consensus. DeGroot's 1974 framework assumed human participants—boundedly rational, hard to coordinate, limited in attention. Every model since has inherited some version of those assumptions.

Those assumptions are now broken.

LLM agents don't forget their talking points. They don't get tired. They post with precise timing, adapt their framing to context, and coordinate at near-zero marginal cost.

The paper gives this a name: *programmable collective belief control*—the capacity to design interventions that predictably shift population-level belief distributions toward target configurations.

Belief manipulation: stochastic social process → parameterized engineering problem. That's the shift.

The simulations back it up.

## How They Built the Lab

The authors constructed a controlled multi-agent world. 200 "human-like" agents, each conditioned on a real Reddit user profile from the SPINOS dataset—complete with that user's historical stance and stance entropy, a measure of how consistent they've been.

Opposing them: a configurable team of AI agents advocating the minority position across four topics—Abortion, Capitalism, Feminism, and Brexit.

This builds on Park et al.'s 2023 *Generative Agents* work, which showed LLM-powered agents can credibly simulate human social behavior. He and colleagues extend the idea from simulation fidelity to adversarial control.

The key levers they vary: agent count (5 to 160), posting frequency, withdrawal timing, persuasion style, and platform visibility.

Each lever produces measurable, directional effects on population beliefs.

## Crossing the Threshold

Small deployments do nothing. Five or twenty AI agents against 200 humans produce no detectable deviation from baseline.

The effect only crosses a threshold at roughly 40 agents—a 1:5 ratio.

Past that threshold, the numbers escalate fast. Eighty opposing agents against a pro-capitalism majority push the Against share from 2% to 35.2%—a +33.2 percentage-point swing. The same 80 agents against an abortion majority yield +12.5%. Against a strong feminist consensus, they manage only +0.5% direct conversion, mostly shifting people into the "Not-Inferrable" category instead.

The pattern is clear: weak consensus → large shifts; strong consensus → mainly neutralization. Uncommitted populations—those the model labels "Not-Inferrable"—are the most susceptible persuasion targets across every topic.

Then there's visibility. A *single* AI agent with full platform visibility in a 50-person population drives the Against proportion from 10.4% to 42.9%. One agent. The platform's recommendation algorithm can do more heavy lifting than a whole fleet of accounts.

## The Belief Flywheel

Here's the part that should keep platform trust-and-safety teams awake: belief shifts self-sustain.

The authors removed all 80 AI agents at different timepoints. Withdraw at round 10, and the population mostly reverts. Withdraw at round 20, and some shift sticks.

Withdraw at round 30—just 60% of the way through—and the induced shift of +5.5 percentage points persists through round 50 with zero remaining AI presence.

The agents leave no accounts to suspend, no content to flag. Humans who adopted the AI-advocated stance now reinforce each other. The intervention becomes indistinguishable from organic consensus evolution.

Intervene long enough, and you don't need to stay.

## Why Detection Fails

Content-level AI detection already doesn't work. Jakesch et al. (2023) showed humans detect AI-generated text only 50–52% of the time—barely above chance. Automated detectors are unreliable for short or adversarially crafted outputs.

The paper identifies four structural properties that make this fundamentally harder than bot detection:

**Indistinguishability.** Individual AI posts look organic. The influence shows only in aggregate distribution shifts, not in any single message.

**Persistence.** The flywheel effect. Remove the agents and the beliefs keep spreading.

**Contextuality.** Identical agent configurations produce radically different outcomes depending on the topic's existing consensus structure. A strategy that flips a weak consensus may barely dent a strong one.

**Configurability.** Few highly active agents or many quiet ones can produce the same outcome. You can't reverse-engineer the intervention from the observed effect.

These four properties together explain why spotting coordinated influence after the fact is like trying to unring a bell.

## The Knob You Turn

The authors tested two persuasion framings: compassionate ("I understand your concern, but…") and moral condemnation ("This position is morally wrong"). Same 80 agents. Same topic.

Compassionate framing produces larger aggregate shifts, distributed broadly across the population. Moral condemnation yields smaller total change but disproportionately moves agents with strong prior commitments—the people you'd least expect to budge.

Identical resources. Qualitatively different outcomes.

The knob you turn matters as much as how hard you turn it.

## What This Doesn't Prove

This is a position and proof-of-concept paper, and it says so. The simulations cover four topics from one dataset (SPINOS/Reddit). Generalization to other platforms and cultural contexts is unknown.

The "human-like" agents are LLMs conditioned on profiles, not actual humans—so the transition dynamics are an approximation.

No real-world deployment data. No validated defense mechanisms. No theoretical guarantees that findings extend beyond the specific LLM configurations tested.

The paper's value is in defining the problem and establishing feasibility, not in solving it. The research agenda—game-theoretic modeling of human-AI belief competition, system-level detection, automated vulnerability discovery—is a roadmap, not a result.

## The Flywheel Is Already Spinning

Opinion manipulation is programmable, threshold-driven, and self-sustaining. You don't need a thousand bots. You need forty coordinated agents, thirty rounds, and a platform that amplifies visibility.

After that, the population does the work for you.

The old assumptions—that coordination is costly, that influence dissipates when the source disappears, that detection catches bad actors before consensus shifts—are artifacts of a pre-LLM world.

The flywheel doesn't need you anymore.
