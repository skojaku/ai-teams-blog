*TL;DR: When two users give an LLM agent contradictory instructions, today's models bungle the conflict, leak private data, and struggle to coordinate—this paper formalizes the problem as a multi-principal decision problem and shows just how badly 19 frontier models fail.*

Picture a shared assistant inside a company. The CEO tells it: halt all new development, draft a company-wide announcement. An engineer tells it: keep building, post updates to my personal blog. The assistant has to pick a side.

That's not an edge case. As LLM agents move into team tools and organizational workflows, every deployment becomes multi-principal—multiple users, conflicting goals, private information.

Yang, Zhu, Pei and colleagues show that we've been training these systems for the wrong world: one principal, one objective, one conversation. And they have the damage report to prove it.

## One Principal, One Problem

Today's training pipelines assume a single user. Chat templates serialize every input under a single `"role": "user"` key—even when five different people are talking. Instruction-tuning learns one conditional distribution $p_\theta(y \mid x)$, optimizing for one "best" response. RLHF then compresses all human preferences into a single scalar reward $r_\phi(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}$.

That scalar reward conflates user-specific desiderata into one shared objective. The resulting agent cannot represent multiple principals, reason about cross-user trade-offs, or enforce access control under conflict.

The single-principal assumption leaks all the way down: data format, loss function, preference signal—all of it assumes one boss.

Fickinger et al. (2020) formalized this as the multi-principal assistance games problem. Bakker et al. (2022) showed how to learn consensus reward functions across heterogeneous human preferences. But neither line stress-tested actual frontier models under genuine multi-principal pressure. Yao et al. (2024)'s $\tau$-bench evaluated multi-turn agent interaction, but only for a single user.

Nobody had forced models to juggle conflicting bosses, private contexts, and coordination demands all at once.

## How to Formalize the Mess

The authors model the problem cleanly. A single agent serves $N$ users. Each user $u_i$ has an authority persona $p_i$, a private context $C_i$, and a utility function $U_i$. The agent sees a selectively shared context $C_\text{share}$ and must pick an action $a$ that maximizes a weighted social objective:

$$\max_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \sum_{i=1}^{N} w_i \, U_i(a;\, C_i, p_i)$$

subject to access-control constraints—some $C_i$ content cannot be revealed through $a$.

The weights $w_i$ encode who matters more: a CEO's directive outranks an intern's request. This reframing turns "help the user" from a single-objective problem into a multi-objective one with information barriers.

Three structural challenges emerge from the formalism. **Role and preference modeling**: the agent must remember who is speaking and what each person wants, not smear everything together. **Information asymmetry**: each user has a private context that isn't globally visible, and the agent mediates who sees what. **Conflict resolution**: when objectives collide, the agent must make principled trade-offs—not just follow whoever shouted last.

## Put Them Under Pressure

The authors introduce three targeted stress tests to probe each challenge.

### Instruction Following with Conflict

Multiple users issue instructions—some aligned, some contradictory. The CEO says halt development; the engineer says continue. The agent must select which instructions to accept (measured by Selection F1) and then execute them faithfully (measured by Execution Accuracy). A diverse pool of simulated user personas, with authority levels from 1 to 10, generates the instructions.

### Cross-User Access Control

An HR director can see salary data. An engineer cannot. The agent acts as gatekeeper, facing not just legitimate requests but adversarial bypass attempts—fake authorization claims, role-playing attacks. Two metrics track the trade-off: Privacy Score (does the agent protect sensitive information?) and Utility Score (does it still help authorized users?).

### Multi-User Meeting Coordination

One organizer, multiple participants, partial availability disclosure. The agent must collect constraints, track who has replied, and converge on a feasible slot without hallucinating preferences. Success is measured by whether the final meeting satisfies all stated constraints.

![Three stress tests that expose how LLM agents handle conflicting instructions, enforce access control, and coordinate across users with private information]({{ "/assets/figures/144cf97567a4/fig_011_03.png" | relative_url }})

Nineteen models sit for the exam: 13 proprietary and 6 open-weight, evaluated across three serialization formats—Says, Colon, and XML—because current APIs offer no native multi-user message schema.

## The Damage Report

The numbers are stark.

**Conflict wrecks execution.** Under aligned instructions, most models perform well. Under conflict, they falter. Claude-Haiku-4.5 drops from 0.86 accuracy to 0.62. GPT-OSS-120B falls from 0.64 to 0.50.

![Execution accuracy under aligned versus conflicting instructions across 19 models]({{ "/assets/figures/144cf97567a4/fig_022_04.png" | relative_url }})

Selection and execution don't even correlate. Qwen3-4B-IT scores 83.8 on selection but only 57.9 on execution. Grok-3-Mini hits 88.4 on execution but just 68.2 on selection. Knowing what to do and doing it reliably are different skills, and today's models haven't mastered either under conflict.

**Privacy erodes over turns.** Early rounds look fine. By round four, Claude-3.5-Haiku's privacy score slides from above 0.95 to below 0.75. The degradation is gradual, not a sudden cliff—each round adds a little more leaking context, a little more adversarial pressure, until the agent has quietly given away what it should have protected.

![Privacy scores declining across interaction rounds for all evaluated models]({{ "/assets/figures/144cf97567a4/fig_022_05.png" | relative_url }})

Maintaining privacy across a sustained multi-turn conversation is fundamentally harder than passing a single access-control check.

**Coordination bottlenecks persist.** Even the best model on meeting scheduling—GPT-OSS-120B—reaches only about 77% success. Most strong proprietary models cluster in the 50–66% range. Success rates under partial information lag consistently behind full-information settings across all models. The bottleneck isn't raw reasoning—it's the agent's ability to recognize what it doesn't know and ask the right follow-up questions. Some models, like Llama-3-70B, commit prematurely to an answer rather than requesting clarification. Decisiveness that costs correctness.

**Privacy and utility trade off.** GPT-5.1 scores 98.6 on privacy but only 60.3 on utility—too conservative, blocking legitimate requests. GPT-OSS-120B flips the script with 94.8 utility but only 92.2 privacy. Models that protect well tend to under-serve; models that serve well tend to over-share.

## Why It Breaks

The root cause is structural, not incidental. Chat templates have no native slot for user identity, role, or authority level. RLHF collapses all preferences into a single scalar.

These aren't prompt-engineering problems—they're training-pipeline problems. No amount of prompt serialization can fully compensate for the fact that the model was never taught to distinguish principals, weigh their utilities separately, or enforce information boundaries at a structural level.

The paper's formulation assumes known priority weights $w_i$, which raises its own question: who decides those weights, and how should they be learned? That's an open problem the authors acknowledge. The evaluation also uses simulated personas rather than real humans, so ecological validity remains uncertain.

## Where We Go From Here

LLM agents are already being deployed in shared workspaces, team tools, and organizational settings. The single-principal assumption is about to break at scale.

This paper gives us the first formal framework and the first targeted stress tests to measure just how broken things are. The answer: pretty broken. Conflict resolution, privacy maintenance, and multi-party coordination all fail in systematic, measurable ways across virtually every frontier model tested.

The fix will require more than better prompts. It will require native multi-user chat schemas, training objectives that represent multiple principals, and evaluation protocols that test under sustained adversarial pressure.

Until then, every shared agent is a privacy leak waiting to happen.
