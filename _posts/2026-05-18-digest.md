## Themes

**Perception beats deliberation — and by a lot.** The clearest signal across today's batch is that shoving more reasoning at a multi-agent system often makes it worse. *Context, Reasoning, and Hierarchy* documents a "deliberation cascade" where self-critique loops in sub-agents amplify hedged qualifications instead of sharpening decisions, degrading returns by up to 3.4×. The fix isn't deeper thinking — it's a deterministic Python function that compresses raw observations into a one-line status summary. DMoA echoes the same logic at the routing layer: sparse, confidence-driven agent selection outperforms dense, everyone-talks-every-step orchestration.

**Adaptive wiring replaces the org chart.** Three of five papers share a rejection of fixed multi-agent topologies. DMoA learns a differentiable router that expands and contracts the roster per reasoning step. NEXA reads parallel drafts first, then predicts a communication graph — or predicts nothing at all, letting the empty graph stand as a valid outcome. Both treat "who talks to whom" as a runtime decision, not a design-time commitment. The *Beyond Individual Intelligence* survey formalizes what this flexibility is racing toward: systemic self-evolution, where the topology reshapes itself in response to failure.

**Retrieval works. Prediction doesn't.** CryptoBench isolates the gap with surgical precision: GPT-5 scores 58.8% on fact retrieval and 6.3% on prediction. Agents can find data. They cannot turn it into foresight. The other papers hint at why — deliberation cascades produce confident hedging rather than decisive inference, and fixed topologies route information without routing judgment. Being able to fetch the right fact and being able to see around the corner are different capabilities, and today's agents have only the first.

**The self-improvement loop is still open.** The LIFE survey names the bottleneck directly: multi-agent systems can diagnose failures but almost never reorganize themselves structurally in response. *Context, Reasoning, and Hierarchy* demonstrates the symptom — agents spiral into deliberation cascades with no mechanism to detect and break the pattern. DMoA and NEXA offer patches (learn to route, learn when to skip communication), but they optimize local decisions, not the system's own architecture. We can find what went wrong. We cannot yet make the system rewrite itself so it won't happen again.

## Editorial

Today's five papers land on the same urgent question from different angles: how do you make a team of LLM agents better, and why does the obvious answer — give them more tools, more talk, more reasoning — keep making things worse?

The *Context, Reasoning, and Hierarchy* paper is the day's most uncomfortable finding. More sub-agents, more self-critique, more tokens — all of it hurts in adversarial settings. Not a little. Up to 3.4×. The cheapest lever is a Python function that cleans the input. If you are building multi-agent systems and you haven't invested in deterministic context engineering first, stop what you're doing and fix the perception layer.

DMoA and NEXA offer complementary escape hatches. DMoA routes sparsely, activating only confident agents. NEXA reads the room before deciding whether anyone needs to talk at all. Both beat baselines on accuracy and efficiency. Both treat orchestration as a learned runtime decision, not a design-time assumption. Take note: the org chart is dead weight.

CryptoBench exposes the consequences of overconfident agents. Retrieval scores are survivable. Prediction scores are catastrophic — 6.3% isn't analysis, it's a coin flip with a spreadsheet. And wrapping a model in an agentic framework made things worse for most models, not better. More tools, worse outcomes. We have seen that pattern already today.

The LIFE survey puts a frame around the whole mess. We can build teams. We can diagnose failures. We cannot yet close the loop — agents don't reorganize themselves when they learn what went wrong. DMoA and NEXA are local optimizations. What the field needs is systemic evolution: mechanisms that rewrite the topology, not just skip a message.

The cheap lever won. The org chart is dead. And the loop is still open.
