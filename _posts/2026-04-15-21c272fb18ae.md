*TL;DR: Giving LLM agents stronger reasoning makes them worse at simulating how real people negotiate. The evidence is stark: GPT-5.2 with native reasoning ended in authority-imposed decisions in all 45 runs across three experiments, while the same model with a constrained reflection scaffold recovered compromise outcomes in every environment — though the degree of recovery varied by domain.*

## The Wrong Instrument

Forty-five out of forty-five. That is how many times GPT-5.2 with native reasoning drove a multi-agent negotiation to an authority-imposed decision. No compromise. No concession dynamics. Just strategic convergence on the dominant path. Three experiments, three different environments, fifteen runs each — the same result every time.

Switch that same model to bounded reflection — a short, structured private ledger tracking concessions, opponent assessments, and open issues — and compromise outcomes appear in all three environments. The recovery is partial in two experiments (5 of 15 and 7 of 15) and strong in the third (13 of 15), but it happens everywhere native reasoning failed entirely.

The capability that makes a model a good strategic solver is precisely what makes it a bad behavioral sampler. Sandro Andric calls this the **solver-sampler mismatch**, and this paper is the cleanest demonstration of it we have seen.

## Two Questions, Not One

Simulation and optimization ask different things.

A solver asks: what is the best action sequence under these conditions? A sampler asks: what range of plausible trajectories might boundedly rational agents produce?

These are related but not identical. A model tuned for coherent long-horizon optimization can reinterpret a simulation prompt as a task to be solved. It infers the strategic structure too cleanly, converges on a narrow dominant policy, and collapses the very variation the simulation was supposed to reveal.

This matters because LLMs are increasingly deployed as agents in policy simulations, synthetic societies, and economic games. Park et al.'s generative agents and Horton's "homo silicus" both assume the model can stand in for boundedly rational actors. But if you hand those actors a theorem prover, you are not simulating human negotiation — you are optimizing it.

SPABCBench (Li et al., 2026) already showed that stronger models do not reliably improve behavioral fidelity. CRAFT (Nath et al., 2026) showed that stronger reasoning does not reliably improve multi-agent coordination under partial information. Andric's paper pins down the mechanism: the reasoning itself is the mismatch.

## How They Test It

The experimental design is methodical. Three negotiation environments, each with a different structure.

**Experiment 1.** Fragmented-authority trading limits — multiple parties, ambiguous institutional power, partial convergence.

**Experiment 2.** Unified-opposition trading limits — same institutional grammar, different coalition structure.

**Experiment 3.** Grid-curtailment transfer — a substantive domain shift from financial-market restrictions to emergency electricity management, preserving the negotiation architecture.

Three reflection conditions cut across all three environments:

- **No reflection** — just the prompt, no structured private state.
- **Bounded reflection** — a constrained five-field private ledger (own concessions, opponent concessions, current state, opponent assessment, open issues).
- **Native reasoning** — the provider's built-in reasoning mode enabled.

Two primary model families (Gemini 3.1 Flash Lite Preview, DeepSeek V3.2) plus a direct OpenAI extension (GPT-4.1, GPT-5.2). Fifteen runs per cell. 495 total runs.

The key metrics are not endpoint accuracy. They are trajectory-level diagnostics: **action entropy** (behavioral diversity within a run), **concession arc rate** (whether any agent softens from opposition to support), and **max-turn exhaustion rate** (whether the negotiation burns through its entire turn budget without resolution).

These are falsification tools. Near-zero entropy and zero concession arcs are inconsistent with any plausible account of boundedly rational negotiation.

## The Pattern, Every Time

The pattern is uniform across both primary model families, all three environments, and the OpenAI extension.

**No reflection** produces clean but rigid hardline play. Agents repeat the same dominant action, never concede, and the authority fallback fires every time.

**Bounded reflection** produces diverse, concession-rich trajectories that converge on compromise.

**Native reasoning** does not recover bounded-sampler quality. It remains outcome-rigid and operationally noisy.

The Gemini anchor in Experiment 1 makes the contrast sharpest. No reflection: action entropy 0.041, zero concession arcs, 15/15 authority decisions. Bounded reflection: action entropy 1.040, 100% concession arc rate, 15/15 compromises. Native reasoning: action entropy 0.409, zero concession arcs, 15/15 authority decisions — and a parse success rate that drops from 100% to 26.7%.

![Action entropy and concession arc rate for Experiment 1 across three reflection conditions, showing bounded reflection dramatically outperforming no reflection and native reasoning]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_004_01.png" | relative_url }})

The same pattern replicates in Experiment 2 (different coalition structure) and Experiment 3 (different policy domain entirely). It survives a second model family. It survives a third provider.

And it is not a temperature artifact. Sweeping temperature from 0.3 to 1.0 on Gemini changes action entropy within conditions but does not shift a single outcome: no reflection stays 10/10 authority decisions, bounded reflection stays 10/10 compromises, native reasoning stays 10/10 authority decisions. The bounded scaffold is doing more than injecting randomness.

## Diversity Without Fidelity

Here is the result that should make you reconsider any model-selection shortcut.

DeepSeek native reasoning in Experiment 3 shows action entropy of 1.476 and a concession-arc rate of 0.933. By local metrics, it looks like a reasonably diverse negotiator. Yet all 15 runs end in authority decision.

The local variation is present. The concession behavior is present. The terminal outcome never includes a compromise.

![Max-turn exhaustion and authority decision rates for DeepSeek V3.2 in Experiment 2, illustrating how native reasoning produces variation without shifting terminal outcomes away from authority decisions]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_006_05.png" | relative_url }})

Andric calls this **diversity without fidelity**: the model generates varied within-trajectory behavior but still fails to produce compromise-oriented endpoints. The mechanism appears to be what he terms *game-solving pressure* — native reasoning pushes agents toward coherent strategic resolution rather than plausible boundedly rational enactment.

This pattern is the sharpest falsification of the idea that native reasoning only fails because it suppresses all variation. It does not. It preserves variation while still collapsing outcome-level fidelity. The problem is not less behavior; it is the wrong kind of behavior for a simulation that needs boundedly rational agents.

## How Bounded Reflection Works

The bounded ledger gives agents a cognitively limited private notebook — not a theorem prover, but a short structured memory scaffold. It tracks five things: what you conceded, what they conceded, where things stand, what you think the other side wants, and what issues are still open.

This is not open-ended chain-of-thought. It is a constrained update rule that preserves enough state to support adaptive behavior without pushing the agent into unrestricted strategic optimization.

A sensitivity analysis confirms the effect is not a prompt artifact. A permissive ledger variant still produces compromise outcomes, but a compact ledger that truncates private state too aggressively collapses toward 9/10 authority decisions. The private state must stay bounded *and* sufficiently expressive.

There is an honest normative concern here. A ledger that explicitly tracks concessions and opponent assessments may privilege compromise-relevant state variables over adversarial ones. Andric acknowledges this and does not claim the current ledger is normatively neutral. The claim is narrower: the effect is not tied to one magical prompt, and it survives domain transfer, coalition-structure variation, and provider-family change.

## Same Mismatch, Third Provider

The OpenAI extension runs the same protocol with GPT-4.1 and GPT-5.2. The pattern replicates.

GPT-5.2 with native reasoning: 45 of 45 authority decisions. GPT-5.2 with no reflection: 45 of 45 authority decisions. GPT-5.2 with bounded reflection: compromise outcomes appear in all three environments — partially in the two trading-limits experiments (5/15 and 7/15) and strongly in the grid-curtailment transfer (13/15).

![Compromise rates for GPT-4.1 and GPT-5.2 across all three experiments, showing bounded reflection recovering compromise outcomes while native reasoning remains at zero]({{ "/assets/figures/21c272fb18ae/fig_009_07.png" | relative_url }})

A crossed mechanism check on GPT-5.2 sharpens the story. Combining the bounded ledger with native reasoning does not recover compromise. The 2×2 produces 15/15 authority decisions in both cells where native reasoning is enabled — even when the ledger is present. The scaffold helps only when it replaces, rather than supplements, unrestricted reasoning.

## Qualify Your Model as a Sampler

The practical lesson is direct. If you are building an LLM-based policy simulation, economic game, or synthetic society, you should qualify your model as a **sampler**, not as a solver. The model that gives the best strategic advice may be the worst instrument for simulating the negotiators themselves.

Action entropy, concession arcs, and turn-budget exhaustion can audit simulation pipelines before deployment. These are not nice-to-have diagnostics. They are falsification checks that catch exactly the failure mode this paper documents.

The honest limits are important. Three scenario-based environments with 15 runs per cell is not a benchmark suite. Whether the pattern generalizes to legislative voting, market pricing, or conflict escalation is untested. The bounded-reflection scaffold may structurally favor compromise, and that normative bias has not been resolved. The effect is real, well-diagnosed, and still waiting for broader stress-testing.

More reasoning, worse simulation. Sometimes the smarter model is the wrong instrument.
