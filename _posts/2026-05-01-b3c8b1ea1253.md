*TL;DR: The same LLM, given different organizational structures, swings from 88% to 31% task success—a 57-point gap that makes governance topology a first-order design variable for multi-agent systems, with no single institution winning everywhere.*

## One Model, 57 Points of Variance

Run MiniMax M2.5 through a Tang-style gated pipeline and it nails 88.2% of tasks. Run the *same model* through a bare single-agent setup and it manages 31.1%.

That 57-point gap isn't a prompt-engineering trick. It's governance topology—the who-proposes-who-reviews-who-executes structure—doing the heavy lifting.

This is the headline from Fei, Guo, and Xiao's SocialSystemArena. They formalize seven historical political institutions as executable multi-agent architectures, run them across three LLMs and two benchmarks, and show that *how you organize agents* matters as much as *which model you pick*.

## Why Bounded Agents Need Structure

Single-agent LLMs hit hard ceilings on complex tasks: limited context, finite reasoning depth, no structured error correction.

As agents grow more autonomous, concentrating all authority in one agent also raises trust and safety problems—privacy exposure, unsafe actions, weak oversight, poor failure recovery.

Multi-agent systems are the natural response. But existing work picks topologies ad hoc. Park et al.'s generative agents showed rich emergent social dynamics—yet never systematically varied institutional structure to isolate its causal effect. Zhuge et al.'s GPTSwarm automated topology search via gradient methods, but validated on a single model backend. Piatti et al. demonstrated that explicit institutional mechanisms help LLM agents cooperate in commons-dilemma settings—without comparing multiple architectures under matched conditions.

What's missing is a controlled experiment: hold the model and tasks constant, vary only the governance structure, and measure the gap.

## Seven Institutions, One Runtime

SocialSystemArena introduces a declarative governance specification:

$$G = (P, A, S, T, F)$$

where $P$ is the pattern (pipeline, gated pipeline, autonomous cluster, or consensus), $A$ is the set of agents with roles and soul prompts, $S$ is the ordered stage sequence, $T$ is the transition function that routes decisions, and $F$ is a set of feature plugins like monitoring and loop guards.

### From History to Code

Seven historical institutions map to four canonical patterns:

- **Pipeline**: Qin-Han Commandery, Soviet Party-State, Mongol Empire—linear delegation through specialized stages.
- **Gated pipeline**: Tang Three Departments, US Federal—pipeline plus review gates that can reject and loop back for revision.
- **Autonomous cluster**: Edo Bakuhan—an orchestrator dispatches to parallel subsystems that execute independently.
- **Consensus**: Athenian Democracy—a proposer sends to seven citizen-voters who decide by majority.

![Four canonical governance patterns: linear pipeline, gated pipeline with review loops, autonomous cluster with parallel dispatch, and consensus with parallel voting]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_004_02.png" | relative_url }})

Each institution gets hand-crafted soul prompts encoding its institutional persona and behavioral constraints. A single GovernanceRuntime executes all eight architectures (including a bare single-agent baseline). The runtime code, LLM adapter, and model backend are identical across runs. The only variable is $G$.

### Gate Density as a Structural Signal

The gated-pipeline pattern introduces a key metric: gate density $\rho = g/|S|$, the fraction of stages devoted to review rather than execution. Tang has $\rho = 0.17$ (one gate). US Federal has $\rho = 0.56$ (five gates).

This number predicts a specific failure mode—gate loops—where reject-revise cycles amplify cost without improving quality.

### The Benchmarks

Two task suites: PinchBench (23 single-turn tool-use tasks) and ClaweBench (104 multi-step real-world tasks spanning 24 categories and 6 difficulty levels). Three LLM backends: MiniMax M2.5, Kimi K2.5, and Gemini 2.5 Flash.

## No Universal Winner

The cross-model results on PinchBench are dramatic:

| Institution | MiniMax | Kimi | Gemini |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tang (gated pipeline) | **88.2%** | 21.6% | 27.2% |
| Edo (autonomous cluster) | 83.7% | 12.3% | **87.7%** |
| Mongol (pipeline) | 59.3% | **67.3%** | 46.3% |
| SAS (baseline) | 31.1% | 21.7% | 63.8% |

The best institution differs across every model: Tang for MiniMax, Mongol for Kimi, Edo for Gemini. Tang—the overall champion on MiniMax—collapses to the worst performer on Gemini. Meanwhile, the bare single-agent baseline outperforms five multi-agent institutions on Gemini.

![Grouped bar chart of PinchBench success rates across eight governance specifications and three model backends, showing 57+ percentage point within-model gaps]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_002_01.png" | relative_url }})

### The Gate-Loop Death Spiral

On Kimi, Tang enters catastrophic gate-loop explosion. The Menxia gate rejects proposals up to 15 consecutive times per task, averaging 26.7 steps and 89K tokens per task—all for a 21.6% score. Eighteen of 23 tasks hit the 32-step budget ceiling without the gate ever approving.

The same topology on MiniMax? 5.9 steps, 88.2% score, zero zero-score tasks.

The mechanism is clear: gates help when the model can satisfy their criteria, and they spiral when it cannot. Gate density $\rho$ predicts this risk structurally, but its severity depends on the interaction between topology and model capability.

![Scatter plot relating gate density ρ to performance and cost across institutions and models, highlighting the gate-loop failure mode]({{ "/assets/figures/b3c8b1ea1253/fig_007_04.png" | relative_url }})

### Edo Wins on Efficiency

Edo Bakuhan emerges as the Pareto champion. On MiniMax: 83.7% accuracy with only 3.0 steps and 48K tokens per task, and just 1 zero-score task. On Gemini: 87.7% with 3.0 steps and 0 zero-score tasks.

The autonomous cluster pattern achieves high parallelism with minimal coordination overhead.

### Hard Tasks Compress the Gap

On ClaweBench, the governance effect compresses dramatically. The best-worst gap shrinks to roughly 9–12 percentage points—an 80% reduction from PinchBench's 57–61 pp gaps.

On sufficiently complex multi-step tasks, task difficulty dominates the effect of governance topology. Pipeline institutions like Qin-Han (49.8% on MiniMax ClaweBench) pull ahead, while the complex topologies that shone on PinchBench fade.

Even the model ranking inverts: Kimi, weakest on PinchBench (23.5% mean), becomes strongest on ClaweBench (51.0%). The two benchmarks test fundamentally different capabilities—single-turn tool-use fluency versus multi-step reasoning and planning.

## What This Means

Governance topology is a first-order performance variable. Fifty-seven percentage points of swing on a single model is too large to ignore. Yet no static topology wins everywhere.

The optimal architecture shifts with model capability and task difficulty. Governance deserves the same design attention as model choice.

The historical framing isn't decorative. It provides a structured design space—pipeline, gated pipeline, autonomous cluster, consensus—that would be hard to discover through ad-hoc engineering alone.

The gate-loop failure mode is a concrete engineering hazard: any multi-agent system with review stages must account for the model's ability to satisfy the gate, not just the elegance of the topology.

The honest limits: only three commercial LLMs tested, so open-weight generalization is unknown; institutions are static specs, not the adaptive systems real governance becomes; soul prompts are hand-crafted, introducing potential prompt-engineering confounds.

The implication is a shift in perspective. The future isn't finding the one best org chart for agents. It's building systems that can reconfigure their own governance as tasks, capabilities, and risk conditions change—the same way real institutions evolve under pressure.

History didn't settle on one form of government. Agent societies won't either.
