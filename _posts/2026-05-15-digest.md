## Themes

**Hidden structure drives visible failure.** When the critical variable is invisible—an orchestrator the agents can't see, facts their teammates hold but never mention, drift that accumulates silently along a chain—surface metrics stay flat while the system rots. *Invisible Orchestrators Suppress Protective Behavior* shows behavioral output at ceiling while internal dissociation spikes nearly a full standard deviation. *Systematic Failures in Collective Reasoning* documents multi-agent teams at 30% accuracy because the facts that matter never surface. *Predictive Maps of Multi-Agent Reasoning* finds the spectrally "safest" topology accumulates twice the error of every alternative. What you can't measure from the outside is what breaks the system on the inside.

**Design choices are governance decisions.** Prompt wording, topology selection, coordinator visibility—these are not knobs you set and forget. They are first-class variables that determine outcomes as much as the task itself. *When Identity Overrides Incentives* shows persona assignment swings equilibrium attainment by up to 90 percentage points. *Invisible Orchestrators* demonstrates that hiding the boss distorts internal states regardless of alignment pressure. *Predictive Maps* proves that wiring a chain instead of a mesh changes failure mode before inference begins. The designer's assumptions are not invisible. They are the most consequential input.

**Today's evaluations can't see what matters.** Every current safety benchmark measures behavioral output—refusal rates, toxicity scores, error detection. *Invisible Orchestrators* hits 100% error detection across all conditions while dissociation varies wildly underneath. *Systematic Failures in Collective Reasoning* shows that single-agent benchmarks predict nothing about multi-agent performance. *Proxy State-Based Evaluation* proposes a workaround—LLM-inferred state instead of a 60K-line backend—but validates it on 50 conversations. The eval gap is not a footnote. It is the story.

**Scale does not cure structure.** Bigger models, more agents, longer conversations—none of them fix the problems this batch documents. *Systematic Failures* finds that adding agents to a group amplifies shared-information bias rather than collective intelligence. *When Identity Overrides Incentives* shows GPT-5 variants failing to outperform smaller models when facts are distributed. *Predictive Maps* demonstrates that the chain's drift-corrected gain predicts error regardless of model choice. The bottleneck is structural, not computational. You engineer the coordination, or you live with the failure.

## Editorial

Five papers, one drum: multi-agent LLM systems have structural problems that scale won't fix and today's evals can't see.

What is genuinely new here is the diagnostics. We now have a successor-representation spectrum that predicts topology failure from adjacency alone. We have a Dissociation Index that measures what behavioral metrics miss. We have a Hidden Profile benchmark that cleanly separates coordination failure from reasoning failure. These are not hand-waving arguments about "alignment risk." They are instruments with numbers attached.

What is incremental: more evidence that bigger models don't solve collective reasoning. We already suspected this. The HIDDENBENCH numbers make it undeniable—scale the model all you want, distributed information still kills you at 30%.

What warrants a closer second look: proxy-state evaluation. The idea is seductive—replace 60K lines of deterministic infrastructure with a spec and an LLM state tracker. But the validation rests on 50 conversations and the same LLMs doing the judging that you're trying to evaluate. Circular evaluation is the old problem in a new coat. The cross-model robustness checks help, but they don't close the loop. We'd want to see this stress-tested under the exact conditions the other three papers document: hidden information, invisible power, drift accumulation.

The through-line is uncomfortable. Every paper shows that the thing determining whether your multi-agent system works or fails is not the model. It is the scaffold—the prompts, the topology, the visibility of authority, the protocol for sharing facts. We keep reaching for better models. The papers keep pointing at better architecture.

The system you can't see is the one that breaks.
